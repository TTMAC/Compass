## Capability and Information Fail Across Every Municipal Function

The previous three articles in Part 4 diagnosed [water](/articles/ra-4-1-fixing-water-and-sanitation), [electricity](/articles/ra-4-2-fixing-electricity), and [waste and roads](/articles/ra-4-3-fixing-waste-and-roads) as individual functions. Each produced a function-specific diagnosis with function-specific reform blueprints. But look at the RAARICLE™ scorecards side by side and two elements appear in the critical failure zone across every function: capability and information. Water's capability scores 1. Electricity's scores 2. Waste and roads both score 2. Information scores 2 to 3 across all four. These are not function-specific failures. They are the cross-cutting structural conditions that make every function fail.

## The Capability Crisis in Numbers

This article steps back from individual functions to diagnose these two elements directly. The question is not "why does the water department lack a qualified process controller?" or "why does the roads department lack an engineer?" The question is: why does the municipal institutional architecture systematically fail to attract, deploy, and retain qualified professionals — and why do municipal information systems consistently fail to provide the data that oversight bodies need?

The numbers paint the picture. In the Free State, only 8 of 23 municipalities have a qualified permanent municipal manager. Of 129 senior posts, 73 are vacant — a 57 per cent vacancy rate. **Across all municipalities, an estimated 47 per cent of municipal managers do not meet the basic qualification criteria for their positions** — a figure surfaced during the Zondo Commission's examination of cadre deployment practices, a structural vulnerability that any governing party with unchecked appointment authority could exploit.

The technical staffing crisis is equally severe. **Engineering staff experience a 4.7 per cent annual turnover rate — the highest of all municipal staffing categories.** More than 50 per cent of professionals, technicians, and clerical support workers in municipalities are employed in shortage occupations. Only 141 accredited civil engineers serve across all 257 municipalities. No municipality in the country meets the Municipal Staff Regulations' requirement of a 3&#58;1 ratio of technical to support staff. The best-performing municipalities manage roughly 1&#58;1.

The financial management capability is reflected in the Auditor-General's 2023/24 MFMA outcomes: **only 41 of 257 municipalities — 16 per cent — achieved clean audits.** Seven municipalities received disclaimed opinions for three to eight consecutive years. Municipalities spent R1.47 billion on consultants to prepare their financial statements because they lacked the internal capability to do so. Total unauthorised expenditure reached R31.79 billion. Irregular expenditure: R28.72 billion. The AG identified 446 material irregularities with an estimated financial loss of R8.74 billion.

These are not numbers that describe a few failing municipalities. They describe a system-wide capability collapse. The municipality that cannot attract a qualified engineer cannot maintain its water treatment plant, cannot manage its electricity network, cannot plan road maintenance, and cannot operate a compliant landfill. The municipality that cannot produce clean financial statements cannot tell you how it spent public money — which means it cannot be held accountable for anything.

:::takeaway
The capability crisis is not a function-specific problem. The same vacancy patterns, qualification gaps, and technical staff shortages appear across water, electricity, waste, roads, and financial management. When 47 per cent of municipal managers do not meet basic qualification criteria, when engineering staff turnover exceeds any other category, and when only 16 per cent of municipalities achieve clean audits, the diagnosis is structural — the municipal institutional architecture is systematically incapable of attracting and retaining the professionals it needs.
:::

## Why Capability Keeps Collapsing

The capability deficit has four structural causes, and understanding them is essential because each requires a different kind of fix.

The first cause is *political appointments*. The Zondo Commission documented how cadre deployment practices intervened in appointments at 88 state institutions — a structural vulnerability that could, in principle, arise wherever a governing party's internal selection processes operate ahead of statutory merit requirements. At the municipal level, Section 56 and 57 managers — the municipal manager, the chief financial officer, and other senior managers — are appointed by the municipal council. In theory, appointments must follow the Regulations on Minimum Competency Levels, which require specific qualifications and prescribe that senior managers must hold or obtain SAQA Qualification ID No. 48965 within 18 months of appointment. **In practice, political considerations routinely override professional criteria.** A politically connected candidate who lacks qualifications is appointed over a qualified professional who lacks political connections. The appointment is technically lawful — the new appointee has 18 months to obtain the qualification — but the institution is immediately weaker because the appointee lacks the competence to manage a complex technical operation.

The Municipal Systems Amendment Act of 2022 attempted to address this by preventing senior public officials from holding political office in political parties, requiring national advertising of vacant municipal manager posts, and barring the re-employment of officials dismissed for misconduct. These are important safeguards. But the Constitutional Court in April 2025 struck down the provision restricting municipal employees from holding political party positions, in a challenge brought by SAMWU. **The boundary between political and professional appointments remains contested — and as long as it remains contested, political considerations will continue to influence who runs municipalities.**

The second cause is *salary competition*. Municipal salary scales cannot compete with the private sector for qualified technical professionals. **A water or wastewater engineer in municipal employment earns approximately R260,000 per year. A civil engineer in the private sector earns R650,000. A consulting engineer earns R970,000.** Municipal engineers earn roughly 40 per cent of what their private sector counterparts earn — and 27 per cent of consulting engineer salaries. The gap is structural: SAMWU's five-year wage agreement (2024-2029) delivers CPI plus 0.75 per cent annually, which maintains the real value of existing salaries but does not close the gap with the private sector. National government departments, which can offer somewhat higher salaries and more stable employment, also outcompete municipalities for technical talent.

The third cause is rural isolation. South Africa's most capability-constrained municipalities are rural local municipalities in the Eastern Cape, Limpopo, KwaZulu-Natal, and the North West — places where qualified professionals do not want to live. The household-to-staff ratio reveals the disparity: Tshwane has 40 households per municipal staff member, while Alfred Nzo district in the Eastern Cape has more than 150. But the staffing problem in rural areas is not just about volume — it is about type. Rural municipalities have large numbers of non-technical staff (general workers, administration, security) and almost no technical professionals. A rural municipality that advertises an engineering post may receive no qualified applicants — not because the salary is too low (though it is) but because no qualified engineer wants to live in a remote town with limited schools, healthcare, and career prospects.

The fourth cause is *institutional instability*. Municipal manager employment contracts are fixed-term — a maximum of five years, not exceeding one year after the election of the new council. When a new council takes office after an election, senior managers know their contracts may not be renewed. In coalition municipalities, the instability is worse: changes in coalition composition can trigger changes in senior management, sometimes within months of appointment. **This instability deters qualified professionals who value career stability.** It also creates a rational disincentive to invest in long-term institutional building — why develop a five-year asset management plan when you may be replaced before you can implement it?

:::expert
Research by the Dullah Omar Institute documented the municipal skills crisis in quantitative terms. Annual staff losses in engineering positions reached 4.7 per cent — the highest turnover of any municipal staffing category — with planning professionals at 3.6 per cent and ICT staff at 3.5 per cent. In a single reporting year (2018/19), departures exceeded new hires by at least 338 high-priority managers, 84 professionals, and 249 technicians. The Institute's analysis identified a fundamental mismatch: municipalities recruit from a shrinking pool of technical professionals, cannot match private sector salaries, offer limited career progression, and provide an institutional environment characterised by political interference and instability. The result is a permanent structural deficit in exactly the capabilities that municipal service delivery depends on.
:::

## The Information Black Hole

If capability is the first cross-cutting failure, information is the second. A municipality cannot manage what it cannot measure. And across South Africa's 257 municipalities, the information systems that should enable management, accountability, and oversight are fragmented, incomplete, or non-existent.

The municipal Standard Chart of Accounts was supposed to transform financial reporting. Mandated from 1 July 2017, mSCOA requires all municipalities to transact and report using a standardised chart of accounts that enables consistent financial analysis across municipalities. In practice, implementation has been uneven. Some municipalities have fully transitioned to mSCOA; others have struggled with system migration, particularly for payroll. The City of Tshwane was assessed at only 13 per cent implementation in an earlier review. No single national compliance rate is published — which is itself telling. The data about data systems is incomplete.

Asset registers — the databases that should tell a municipality what it owns, the condition of each asset, its remaining useful life, and its maintenance history — are a persistent audit qualification basis. The AG's 2023/24 findings identified property, plant and equipment as one of the most common reasons for qualified and adverse opinions. A municipality that does not know what it owns cannot plan maintenance, cannot budget for replacement, and cannot report accurately on its infrastructure. Seventy-seven per cent of 113 audited infrastructure projects faced delays, cost overruns, or poor quality. Completed projects often sit unused while existing assets decay.

Municipal performance management systems are supposed to track whether the municipality is delivering on its Integrated Development Plan. In practice, as the LGSETA's 2022-2023 research on municipal oversight found, performance reporting alone is insufficient for oversight — "not everything a department does is reflected in quarterly reports." Councillors lack the technical knowledge to interrogate the data that is presented. Physical on-site assessments are rarely conducted. The information reaches the council pre-filtered by management, and councillors lack the capability to question what they receive.

The information failure has a specific consequence for democratic accountability. Councillors are the elected representatives who are supposed to hold the municipal administration accountable on behalf of citizens. But research published in the Journal of Local Government Research and Innovation found that councillors are "relatively ill-prepared" to conduct oversight — they lack technical knowledge regarding planning frameworks, budgeting processes, and bylaw content. They cannot interpret financial data. They cannot evaluate performance reports. They approve budgets they do not fully understand. They oversee departments whose operations are opaque to them.

This is not a criticism of individual councillors. It is a structural condition. **The municipal institutional architecture places democratic oversight responsibility on part-time elected officials who receive limited training and limited access to the independent technical advice that would enable them to do their jobs.** When the only source of information is the administration itself, and the administration has every incentive to present a positive picture, *oversight becomes a ritual rather than a function.*

:::takeaway
The information black hole has two dimensions. First, municipalities lack the operational data systems — asset registers, performance monitoring, real-time dashboards — needed to manage their own operations. Second, the information that does exist is filtered through management before it reaches councillors, the AG, or the public, creating a structural condition where oversight bodies cannot see what is actually happening. The capability deficit and the information deficit are mutually reinforcing: you need capable people to build information systems, and you need information systems to identify where capable people are most needed.
:::

## The Five Capture Vectors at Municipal Level

The RAARICLE™ framework's anti-capture architecture identifies five vectors through which institutional capture occurs. At the municipal level, all five vectors are active — and the capability and information deficits make municipalities structurally vulnerable to each.

### Political Capture

Political capture at the municipal level operates primarily through appointments. The Zondo Commission documented how cadre deployment practices — which the Commission found to be unconstitutional in their implementation — intervened in appointments across state institutions. The structural vulnerability would be equally available to any governing party with unchecked authority over senior municipal appointments. At the municipal level, the mechanism is direct: the council appoints the municipal manager, who appoints other senior managers. When the council is controlled by a party that prioritises political loyalty over professional competence, the entire senior management team can be captured within a single electoral cycle.

The consequence is not just incompetence (though that follows). It is structural — a politically captured senior management team has no incentive to build the capability or information systems that would make its own performance visible. A qualified municipal manager wants a functional asset register because it enables planning. A politically appointed municipal manager may prefer not to have a functional asset register because it would reveal the state of disrepair.

Tariff suppression — keeping water, electricity, and refuse tariffs artificially low before elections — is the other form of political capture. It sacrifices infrastructure maintenance to buy political support. The result, as documented in the preceding articles, is chronic underfunding of every municipal function that generates tariff revenue.

### Commercial Capture

Commercial capture takes the form of procurement fraud. The SIU has investigated municipal procurement across the country, finding patterns that are consistent and predictable. In OR Tambo District, a consortium received an irregular R70 million contract against a budget of R20 million — without council approval. Payments exceeded invoices. Funds were diverted to unrelated expenditure including a boxing tournament. The SIU filed a civil recovery claim of R25.8 million.

The scale is enormous: R28.72 billion in irregular expenditure across all municipalities in 2023/24. The AG identified 446 material irregularities with an estimated financial loss of R8.74 billion. The Institute for Security Studies has documented how organised corruption at the municipal level follows a three-stage pattern: influence appointments through nepotism and patronage, manipulate procurement systems for personal gain, and protect the arrangement through administrative or violent means. In OR Tambo, a "water mafia" with alleged links to water contracts threatened aid workers from Gift of the Givers.

### Labour Capture

Local government employment grew 45 per cent from 237,000 in 2009 to over 345,000 in 2019. The growth was concentrated in non-technical positions — general workers, administration, and support functions — while technical vacancies persisted. The household-to-staff ratio disparity between metros (Tshwane: 40&#58;1) and rural areas (Alfred Nzo: 150&#58;1) suggests that some municipalities are overstaffed in non-technical roles while understaffed in the technical roles that determine service delivery quality.

Union resistance to performance management compounds the problem. When Sol Plaatje Municipality in the Northern Cape attempted to restructure staffing, SAMWU and IMATU occupied council chambers. The political cost of holding non-performing staff accountable — strikes, disruption, political pressure — is high enough that most municipalities avoid it. The result is an institutional structure that protects non-performing staff while failing to attract the qualified professionals it needs.

### Regulatory Capture

The Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (DCoG) is supposed to monitor and support municipalities. Provincial departments of local government are supposed to provide oversight and intervene when municipalities fail. In practice, both tiers of regulatory oversight are capacity-constrained. DCoG reduced distressed municipalities from 66 to 35 through Municipal Support Intervention Plans, but monitoring 257 municipalities with limited staff produces superficial oversight. Provincial departments face the same capability crisis as the municipalities they are supposed to regulate — they lack the qualified personnel to evaluate whether a municipality is performing.

Section 139 interventions — the municipal equivalent of Section 100 — have been invoked in multiple municipalities. As explored in [When Provinces Fail](/articles/ra-3-3-when-provinces-fail), intervention mechanisms treat symptoms rather than causes. A Section 139 intervention can stabilise an immediate fiscal crisis but does not alter the structural conditions that produced it. The municipality that returns to self-governance after intervention typically returns to the same capability deficit that caused the crisis.

### Information Capture

Information capture at the municipal level is structural, not deliberate. Management controls what the council sees — quarterly reports are prepared by the administration, financial data is compiled by the finance department, performance data is collected by the people whose performance it measures. Councillors who lack technical knowledge cannot distinguish between accurate and misleading information. The AG's annual audit is the most reliable independent information source, but it arrives once a year, months after the financial year ends — too late to influence in-year decisions.

The ISS documented explicit cases of information manipulation: at Madibeng, financial crimes included bank account tampering, tender fraud, illegal investments, and duplicate payments. At the City of Johannesburg, the AG reported collusion between supply chain officials, bid committees, and service providers. These are extreme cases. The routine form of information capture is quieter — a management report that omits the infrastructure condition data, a performance report that emphasises inputs rather than outcomes, a budget presentation that obscures the maintenance deficit.

:::expert
The Public Affairs Research Institute's COMPACT project, reporting in November 2024, assessed the state of municipal oversight and governance. The findings confirmed a systemic gap: statutory mechanisms for oversight exist — councillors have the legal authority to question management, request information, and approve budgets — but the practical capability to exercise this authority is absent. Councillors lack technical training, lack access to independent advisory support, and depend entirely on the administration for the information on which they base decisions. PARI's analysis suggests that the oversight gap is not primarily a question of political will but of institutional design — the system places accountability demands on councillors without providing the tools to meet them.
:::

## The Reform Blueprint for Capability

### Protected Technical Appointments

The most critical reform is *structural separation of technical appointments from political appointments*. The current system allows political authority (the council) to appoint all senior managers, including the technical professionals (engineers, CFOs, planners) whose qualifications and competence determine whether services function. The reform requires:

A binding classification of municipal posts into two categories: **executive positions** (municipal manager, deputy municipal manager) that are appointed by the council and serve fixed terms aligned with the electoral cycle; and **technical positions** (CFO, director of engineering/infrastructure, director of community services, technical directors for water/electricity/waste/roads) that are appointed through an independent professional appointment process.

Technical appointments should be made by an independent Municipal Professional Appointments Panel — a body established by regulation, comprising representatives from the relevant professional bodies (ECSA, SAICA, SACPLAN, SAICE), the provincial treasury, and SALGA. The panel evaluates qualifications, verifies professional registration, and recommends a shortlist. The council appoints from the shortlist but cannot appoint outside it. This preserves democratic oversight while preventing unqualified political appointments.

Technical post incumbents should have protected tenure — they cannot be dismissed except for proven misconduct or incapacity, through a process verified by the professional body and the relevant provincial department. This protection insulates technical staff from the political instability that currently drives turnover. A qualified engineer who knows they will survive a change in council composition is more likely to invest in long-term infrastructure planning.

### Mandatory Professional Registration

Every technical post in a municipality must require professional registration with the relevant body: ECSA for engineers, SAICA or CIGFARO for financial managers, SACPLAN for town planners, HPCSA for environmental health practitioners. The Regulations on Minimum Competency Levels already prescribe qualifications for senior financial managers. The same principle must extend to all technical posts. A municipality cannot appoint an unregistered person to a technical position, and a person who loses their professional registration automatically vacates the post.

### The Shared Services Mandate

For municipalities that cannot attract qualified professionals — the small rural municipalities where no engineer will live, no matter what the salary — the reform must provide an alternative to permanent appointment. The shared services model, proposed in [Fixing Waste and Roads](/articles/ra-4-3-fixing-waste-and-roads), requires district municipalities to employ qualified technical professionals who serve multiple local municipalities within the district.

The model works as follows: the district municipality establishes a District Technical Services Unit staffed with the qualified professionals that individual local municipalities cannot attract — a civil engineer, an electrical engineer, a water process engineer, a financial manager, a town planner. Each professional serves two to five local municipalities within the district, spending defined time in each. The cost is shared across local municipalities based on population and budget. The district professional provides the technical capability that the local municipality lacks — infrastructure condition assessment, maintenance programme design, capital project management, financial statement preparation.

This is not a voluntary arrangement. For municipalities below a defined capability threshold (determined by the provincial treasury based on audit outcomes, technical vacancy rates, and infrastructure condition data), participation in the District Technical Services Unit must be mandatory. A municipality that cannot employ a qualified engineer for more than 24 months must receive engineering services from the district — at its own cost, funded from its infrastructure budget.

### The National Professional Pipeline

Shared services and protected appointments address the deployment of existing professionals. They do not create new ones. The pipeline of qualified municipal technical professionals must be expanded through a national bursary-and-bond scheme modelled on successful programmes in health and defence.

The scheme works as follows: National Treasury funds bursaries for engineering, financial management, town planning, and environmental science degrees at accredited institutions. Recipients sign a return-of-service agreement requiring them to work in a municipality or District Technical Services Unit for a defined period (five years) after qualifying. During the bond period, the professional earns a municipal salary supplemented by a retention allowance funded from the conditional grant. After the bond period, the professional may choose to remain in municipal employment or move to the private sector.

The target is to produce 500 additional qualified technical professionals for municipal employment over a decade — enough to staff the District Technical Services Units in the most capability-constrained areas and begin closing the technical vacancy gap.

## The Reform Blueprint for Information

### Real-Time Operational Dashboards

Every municipality must have a real-time operational dashboard for its trading services — water, electricity, waste, and (where applicable) public transport. The dashboard must display: bulk purchases and distribution volumes (water, electricity), distribution losses, treatment plant performance (water quality, wastewater discharge compliance), revenue billed and collected, and infrastructure maintenance spending as a percentage of asset value.

The technology for these dashboards exists. SCADA systems already monitor water and electricity infrastructure in well-managed municipalities. The mSCOA framework provides standardised financial data. The reform is not about inventing new technology — it is about mandating its deployment and ensuring that the data flows not only to management but directly to the council, the AG, the relevant national department, and the public.

### Mandatory Quarterly Data Publication

Every municipality must publish quarterly performance data for its core functions in a standardised format defined by National Treasury. The data must be published on the municipal website and submitted to a central national database. The format must be consistent across municipalities, enabling comparison and benchmarking.

The data must be published without management intermediation. This is the critical anti-capture design requirement. Current performance reporting flows from operational staff through management to the council — and management has every incentive and opportunity to filter, delay, or spin the data. The reform requires that operational data flows directly from the monitoring systems to the publication platform. Management may add commentary and context, but cannot alter the underlying numbers.

### Independent Technical Advisory Support for Councillors

Councillors cannot exercise oversight if their only information source is the administration they are supposed to oversee. The reform establishes a Municipal Oversight Support Unit within each district — a small team of qualified professionals (an accountant, an engineer, a performance management specialist) who are employed by the district but whose mandate is to support councillors in local municipalities.

The Oversight Support Unit reviews quarterly data publications, identifies anomalies and risks, prepares briefing notes for councillors in plain language, and is available to answer technical questions. It reports to the district council's oversight committee, not to municipal management. This creates an independent information channel that breaks the management monopoly on information to council.

### Automated AG Early Warning

The Auditor-General's annual audit is the most reliable assessment of municipal financial management — but it arrives too late to prevent problems. By the time the AG identifies irregular expenditure, the money is spent. The reform creates an automated early warning system: the real-time dashboards and quarterly data publications are monitored by an AG early warning unit that flags anomalies — sudden increases in expenditure, procurement patterns that match known fraud indicators, infrastructure maintenance spending that falls below defined thresholds — and issues alerts to the relevant provincial treasury and the municipal council.

This is not a new audit. It is an extension of the AG's existing mandate using the data infrastructure created by the dashboard and publication reforms. It shifts the AG from a retrospective assessor (telling you what went wrong last year) to a prospective warning system (telling you what is going wrong now).

:::framework
**The Municipal Capability Audit Framework**

Use these five tests to assess whether your municipality has the capability and information systems to deliver services — before a crisis forces the question:

**Test 1: The professional appointments test.**

Check your municipality's annual report for Section 56 and Section 57 appointments (senior managers required under the Municipal Systems Act). For each post, ask: Is the position filled permanently or by an acting official? Does the incumbent hold the minimum qualifications prescribed by regulation? How long have they been in the role? If more than 30% of senior technical posts are vacant, filled by acting officials, or held by people without prescribed qualifications, the capability element has collapsed.

**Test 2: The vacancy rate test.**

Look beyond senior management. Request the municipality's overall vacancy rate and the vacancy rate for technical positions specifically — engineers, accountants, town planners, environmental health officers, process controllers. A municipality can have a low overall vacancy rate (because non-technical posts are filled) while critical technical functions are hollowed out. The technical vacancy rate is the one that predicts service delivery failure.

**Test 3: The information availability test.**

Pick any municipal function — water, electricity, waste, roads — and try to find current performance data. Is the data published on the municipal website? Is it available through the municipal annual report? Can you find it through National Treasury or the relevant national department? If you cannot find basic operational data for a core function, the information element has failed — and oversight bodies cannot do their jobs either.

**Test 4: The Auditor-General trajectory test.**

Look at your municipality's audit outcomes over the last five years (available on the AGSA website). A municipality moving from unqualified to qualified to disclaimer audit opinions is on a capability decline trajectory. A municipality stuck on disclaimer for three or more years has experienced capability collapse — the institution cannot even account for how it spent public money.

**Test 5: The capture vulnerability test.**

For each of the five capture vectors — political, commercial, labour, regulatory, and information — ask whether structural safeguards exist. Are senior technical appointments protected from political interference? Is procurement data published openly? Are performance management systems actually enforced? If safeguards are absent on three or more vectors, the municipality is structurally vulnerable to capture regardless of who is in charge.

With these five tests, you can produce a capability diagnosis of your municipality that goes beyond anecdotes and identifies the specific structural gaps that need to be fixed — through legislation, regulation, or public advocacy.
:::

## The Municipal Capability Act

The reforms proposed in this article and in the preceding three articles of Part 4 require a legislative vehicle. Some can be achieved through regulation — National Treasury can mandate revenue ring-fencing, the Minister of COGTA can prescribe minimum qualifications, NERSA can attach conditions to licences. **But the structural reforms — protected technical appointments, mandatory shared services, the District Technical Services Unit, the Municipal Oversight Support Unit, automatic enforcement triggers — require a coherent legislative framework.**

The Municipal Systems Amendment Act of 2022 was a step in the right direction but did not go far enough. Cabinet approved a further amendment bill in March 2024. The Framework for the Professionalisation of the Public Sector, approved by Cabinet, establishes five pillars: pre-entry recruitment and selection, induction and onboarding, planning and performance management, continuous learning, and career progression. The Public Procurement Act, stemming from Zondo Commission recommendations, creates a cohesive procurement regulatory framework.

These are valuable components. What is missing is an integrated legislative framework that addresses the municipal capability and information deficits as a single structural problem. The proposed Municipal Capability Act would consolidate the following provisions:

A mandatory classification of municipal posts into executive (political appointment) and technical (professional appointment) categories, with binding appointment processes for each. A requirement for professional registration for all technical post incumbents. Protected tenure for technical appointments. A mandatory District Technical Services Unit for municipalities below a defined capability threshold. A national bursary-and-bond scheme for municipal technical professionals. Mandatory real-time operational dashboards for trading services. Mandatory quarterly data publication in standardised format. A Municipal Oversight Support Unit in each district. Automatic enforcement triggers linked to capability indicators (vacancy rates, audit outcomes, infrastructure condition) and information indicators (data publication compliance, dashboard deployment).

The Act would not replace the Municipal Systems Act or the MFMA. It would supplement them with the structural provisions that address the specific capability and information deficits that this series has diagnosed across every municipal function. As explored throughout Part 4, the reforms proposed for [water](/articles/ra-4-1-fixing-water-and-sanitation), [electricity](/articles/ra-4-2-fixing-electricity), and [waste and roads](/articles/ra-4-3-fixing-waste-and-roads) all depend on the same two cross-cutting conditions: qualified professionals running the technical functions and reliable information flowing to oversight bodies. The Municipal Capability Act creates those conditions in law.

The legislative pathway is a Section 76 bill — affecting local government, requiring National Council of Provinces concurrence. The political feasibility depends on whether the Government of National Unity can build consensus around the principle that professional municipal governance is a prerequisite for service delivery. The evidence presented across the 14 articles of this series makes the case. The question is whether the political will exists to act on it.

As discussed in [Municipal Councils](/articles/3-3-municipal-councils) and [The Municipal Performance Index](/articles/eg-4-1-the-municipal-performance-index), the democratic governance of municipalities is not the problem — it is the solution, provided that democratic governance is supported by the professional capability and information infrastructure that makes oversight meaningful. The Municipal Capability Act does not diminish democratic governance. It enables it — by ensuring that elected councillors have qualified professionals to manage services and reliable information to evaluate performance. Democracy without capability is hollow. Capability without democracy is authoritarian. The reform blueprint requires both.
