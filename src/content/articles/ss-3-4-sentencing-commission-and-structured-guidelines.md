## Sentencing That Is Simultaneously Too Rigid and Too Arbitrary

South Africa's sentencing framework is caught between two failures. On one side: the Criminal Law Amendment Act of 1997 (Act 105) imposed blunt minimum sentences for serious offences — removing judicial discretion without addressing the root causes of sentencing inconsistency. **A magistrate who concludes that a particular case warrants a sentence below the minimum must find "substantial and compelling circumstances" to deviate** — a legal standard that different courts interpret differently, creating inconsistency in the application of a law designed to reduce inconsistency. On the other side: in the absence of structured guidelines for the vast majority of offences not covered by minimum sentencing, magistrates and judges produce wildly inconsistent sentences for similar crimes across different courts and provinces. **The same offence, with similar circumstances, can produce sentences that vary by years depending on which magistrate hears the case and in which jurisdiction.**

## Caught Between Two Failures

The result is a system that is simultaneously too rigid and too arbitrary. Minimum sentences remove the judicial discretion needed for individualised justice in the cases they cover. The absence of guidelines leaves judicial discretion entirely unstructured in the cases they do not. Neither approach produces sentences that are consistently proportionate, constitutionally sound, and aligned with the rehabilitation mandate that the Correctional Services Act establishes as the primary purpose of incarceration.

The consequences of this twin failure land unevenly. Research by the Institute for Security Studies and the South African Law Reform Commission has documented **sentencing disparities of several years for functionally similar offences across different courts and provinces** — not because the cases differ in ways the law recognises, but because the magistrates do. For the Schedule 2 offences that fall under minimum sentencing, the reverse problem applies: *first-time offenders whose circumstances cry out for rehabilitation-focused sentences receive the same fifteen-year term as hardened repeat offenders*, because the "substantial and compelling circumstances" threshold was interpreted too restrictively in the court that happened to hear their case. Two defendants convicted of effectively identical crimes can leave the courtroom with sentences that differ by a decade — a gap that the current framework offers no structured way to close.

A system that produces this much variance for this little reason is not merely inefficient. It undermines the constitutional guarantee of equal protection before the law. The Constitutional Court has repeatedly emphasised that dignity and equality are foundational values of the sentencing framework, yet the framework itself contains no mechanism to ensure that similar cases produce similar sentences. Structured guidelines are the missing mechanism — not as a replacement for judicial judgement, but as the instrument that makes judicial judgement auditable and defensible.

A permanent Sentencing Commission with structured guidelines — presumptive sentencing bands that guide but do not eliminate judicial discretion, anchored in data and constitutional values — could resolve both failures. This is not an untested idea. The US Sentencing Commission has operated since 1984, and despite legitimate criticisms of its implementation, it has produced a framework that structures discretion while maintaining judicial independence. China's structured sentencing grids provide systematic, data-informed guidance that reduces inconsistency without eliminating judicial judgement. The South African Law Reform Commission has repeatedly recommended sentencing reform. What has been missing is the political will to implement it.

## The Current Framework: Act 105 and Its Problems

The Criminal Law Amendment Act of 1997 was a response to public outrage over perceived sentencing leniency for serious crimes, particularly in the context of the post-apartheid crime explosion documented in [From Rainbow Nation to State Capture](/articles/ss-1-2-from-rainbow-nation-to-state-capture). The Act prescribes minimum sentences for specified offences: **life imprisonment for premeditated murder, rape in specified circumstances**, and other offences listed in Part I of Schedule 2; **fifteen years for first offenders** (escalating with repeat offences) for murder, rape, robbery with aggravating circumstances, and other serious offences listed in Parts II, III, and IV.

The Act allows deviation from the minimum when "substantial and compelling circumstances" exist — a standard that the Constitutional Court has interpreted as permitting judicial discretion, but only when the circumstances of the case are sufficiently exceptional to justify a departure. The Constitutional Court, in *S v Malgas* (2001), confirmed that the minimum sentencing framework is constitutionally valid but emphasised that courts retain discretion to impose lesser sentences when the prescribed minimum would be disproportionate.

The jurisprudence that followed *Malgas* has not resolved the underlying tension. In *S v Dodo* (2001), the Constitutional Court upheld the constitutional validity of the minimum sentencing framework but cautioned that a sentence grossly disproportionate to the offence would violate the right to dignity in Section 10 of the Constitution and the prohibition on cruel, inhuman, or degrading punishment in Section 12. The Supreme Court of Appeal in *S v Vilakazi* (2009) applied this principle to a minimum sentencing case and deviated below the prescribed term, signalling that the "substantial and compelling circumstances" standard should not be interpreted so narrowly that it produces constitutionally offensive outcomes.

But these cases are exceptions that prove the rule. Appeal courts can correct egregious outcomes in individual cases; they cannot produce consistency across the thousands of sentences imposed by magistrates' courts every year. The appeal route is slow, expensive, and available only to defendants with legal representation capable of navigating it. **For the majority of sentenced offenders — represented by overworked Legal Aid practitioners, unable to afford private appeals — the sentencing outcome they receive in the court of first instance is effectively final.** The inconsistency that appeal courts occasionally correct at the top of the system persists, unchecked, at the base.

The empirical picture is equally troubling. The South African Law Reform Commission has documented sentencing patterns showing that identical offences — controlling for statutory elements, aggravating factors, and offender history — produce sentences that vary substantially across magistrates' courts in different jurisdictions. The Institute for Security Studies has published analysis of sentencing outcomes in specific offence categories showing comparable variance. Academic researchers, notably Prof Stephan Terblanche at Unisa, have argued for decades that the absence of a structured framework is the root cause — not judicial incompetence or bad faith, but the absence of the institutional infrastructure that other constitutional democracies have built to structure judicial discretion without eliminating it.

The problems with this framework are structural. First, minimum sentences are blunt instruments that cannot account for the vast diversity of circumstances within any offence category. Two murders can be profoundly different in their moral culpability, circumstances, and appropriate response — but the minimum sentencing framework treats them identically unless a court finds "substantial and compelling circumstances" to deviate. Second, the "substantial and compelling circumstances" standard is interpreted inconsistently across courts and provinces — some courts treat it as a relatively low threshold, others as an almost impossibly high one. Third, the minimum sentencing framework applies only to specified serious offences. For the vast majority of criminal matters — common assault, theft, fraud, drug possession, property damage — no sentencing guidelines exist at all. Magistrates exercise entirely unstructured discretion, guided only by their own judgement, the limited precedent available from appeal courts, and whatever informal norms prevail in their jurisdiction.

:::takeaway
South Africa's sentencing framework fails in two opposite directions: minimum sentences for serious offences are blunt instruments that remove judicial discretion without producing consistency, while the absence of guidelines for other offences leaves discretion entirely unstructured. The result is a system that is simultaneously too rigid for cases it covers and too arbitrary for cases it does not. Structured sentencing guidelines — presumptive bands that guide rather than eliminate discretion — would resolve both failures.
:::

## The Constitutional Foundation

Any sentencing reform in South Africa must begin from the constitutional text. Section 35(3) of the Constitution guarantees the right to a fair trial, which the Constitutional Court has interpreted to include a right to a proportionate sentence. Section 12 prohibits cruel, inhuman, or degrading punishment. Section 10 protects the right to dignity. Section 9 guarantees equal protection before the law — the constitutional foundation for consistency in sentencing outcomes. And the Correctional Services Act, read with the constitutional values that shaped it, establishes rehabilitation as the primary purpose of incarceration, not retribution or incapacitation in isolation.

These provisions point in the same direction: sentences must be proportionate to the offence, consistent with how similar offences are sentenced, protective of dignity, and oriented toward rehabilitation where rehabilitation is possible. The minimum sentencing framework of Act 105 satisfies none of these requirements cleanly. It substitutes statutory prescription for proportionality analysis. It produces inconsistency in the application of the deviation standard. It treats dignity as a constraint to be navigated around rather than a positive value to be advanced. And it privileges retribution and incapacitation over rehabilitation in precisely the cases — serious offences with long sentences — where the absence of rehabilitation programming inside prison produces the recidivism problem documented in [Rehabilitation as Constitutional Mandate](/articles/ss-3-5-rehabilitation-as-constitutional-mandate).

Structured sentencing guidelines — designed from the constitutional text outward — would align the sentencing framework with the values the Constitution actually embeds. Presumptive bands built from empirical data on sentencing patterns would advance consistency. Written-reasons requirements for deviations would protect proportionality analysis. Integration of rehabilitation prospects as a formal sentencing input would operationalise the rehabilitation mandate. Integration of victim impact and restorative outcomes would protect dignity on both sides of the courtroom — the victim's and the offender's.

This is not a technocratic exercise. It is a constitutional one. The South African Law Reform Commission, in its sentencing reform work, has consistently framed structured guidelines as the institutional mechanism for giving effect to constitutional values that the current framework honours in principle but cannot deliver in practice. The Constitutional Court itself, in *Dodo* and subsequent cases, has signalled that the existing framework stands on thin constitutional ice — defensible in general terms, but vulnerable to challenge in any individual case where the outcome is grossly disproportionate. A Sentencing Commission with structured guidelines would move the framework from thin ice to solid ground: defensible not only in general but case by case, because each sentence would be anchored in data, reasoning, and a framework built from the constitutional text.

## The Proposed Reform: A South African Sentencing Commission

The reform proposes a permanent South African Sentencing Commission — an independent body, appointed through the judiciary, mandated to develop, publish, and maintain structured sentencing guidelines for all criminal offences. The model draws on the US Sentencing Commission (for institutional design) and China's structured sentencing grids (for the systematic approach to structuring discretion), adapted to South Africa's constitutional value framework.

The Commission would develop *presumptive sentencing bands* for each offence category. A presumptive band defines an expected sentencing range — say, three to five years for a first-time robbery without aggravating circumstances. A magistrate or judge can sentence within the band without providing reasons beyond the normal sentencing considerations. **A sentence below or above the band requires written reasons, creating an auditable record that promotes consistency without eliminating discretion.**

The sentencing bands would be determined by multiple inputs: the crime type and its statutory maximum, the circumstances of the offence (aggravating and mitigating), the offender's prior record, the impact on the victim (as established through victim impact statements or restorative justice processes), and the offender's rehabilitation prospects (as assessed through pre-sentencing reports from probation officers). Each input would be structured — not as a mechanical formula, but as a framework that guides the judicial officer through a consistent analytical process.

Restorative justice outcomes would be a formal sentencing input. When an offender has participated in a restorative justice process — victim-offender mediation, community conferencing, or a compensation agreement — the outcome of that process would be considered by the sentencing court as a factor in determining the appropriate sentence within the presumptive band. This integration of restorative justice into the formal sentencing framework gives restorative processes legal weight and creates an incentive for offenders to participate in them.

**Institutional design.** The Commission's institutional design would determine whether it succeeds or fails. A Commission captured by political authority would produce guidelines that shift with electoral cycles; a Commission ignored by the judiciary would produce guidelines that sit unread on court registrars' shelves. The design must avoid both failure modes. The proposed structure follows the US Sentencing Commission and the Minnesota Sentencing Guidelines Commission — both institutions with multiple decades of operational experience — but adapts their design to South Africa's constitutional context.

The Commission would be constituted through a judicial appointments process similar to the Judicial Service Commission's role in judicial appointments, not through executive appointment. Commissioners would include serving and retired judges and magistrates, academic criminal law specialists, a representative from the South African Law Reform Commission, a representative from the National Prosecuting Authority, a representative from Legal Aid South Africa, and representatives of victim and offender advocacy organisations — a composition that ensures the guidelines reflect both the judicial officers who will apply them and the people whose lives the guidelines will shape. Commissioners would serve fixed, staggered terms — long enough to develop institutional knowledge, staggered to prevent wholesale replacement by any single political administration.

The Commission would be funded through a dedicated parliamentary appropriation, protected from the discretionary budget cuts that have hollowed out other independent institutions. It would report annually to Parliament on sentencing trends, guideline compliance, and proposed amendments — creating a structured accountability relationship that does not compromise judicial independence. It would maintain its own research capacity: a small team of legal and empirical researchers capable of analysing sentencing data, comparing outcomes across jurisdictions, and producing the evidence base for guideline revisions.

**Guideline scope.** The guidelines would cover all criminal offences, not only those currently subject to minimum sentencing. This is a critical design choice. The current framework's most overlooked failure is the absence of guidelines for the vast majority of offences that magistrates' courts handle every day — common assault, theft, fraud, drug possession, property damage. For these offences, the Commission would develop presumptive bands from empirical sentencing data, applied through the same structured analytical framework that applies to serious offences. The reform replaces not only minimum sentencing but the unstructured discretion that currently governs everything else.

:::expert
The South African Law Reform Commission (SALRC) has investigated sentencing reform over multiple project cycles, producing discussion papers and reports that have recommended the establishment of a sentencing information system and structured sentencing guidelines. Their research has documented sentencing disparity across courts and provinces with empirical data, showing that similar offences receive sentences that vary by years depending on the court and the judicial officer. The SALRC has noted that the minimum sentencing framework of Act 105 was intended as a temporary measure — the Act originally included a sunset clause that has been repeatedly renewed rather than replaced with a permanent, structured sentencing framework. Their analysis has consistently recommended a Sentencing Commission as the institutional mechanism for developing guidelines that structure discretion without eliminating it, and their work provides the analytical foundation for the reform proposed here.
:::

## Comparative Models: Lessons From Other Jurisdictions

The proposed reform draws explicitly on three comparative models. Each offers a different lesson about what structured sentencing can achieve and how it can fail.

**The United States Sentencing Commission** was established in 1984 under the Sentencing Reform Act. Its guidelines became effective in 1987 and were initially mandatory — sentencing judges were required to impose sentences within the prescribed range absent specific statutory grounds for departure. In *Mistretta v. United States* (1989), the US Supreme Court upheld the Commission's constitutionality. In *United States v. Booker* (2005), the Court held that the mandatory nature of the guidelines violated the Sixth Amendment right to jury trial and rendered the guidelines advisory rather than mandatory. Post-*Booker*, the guidelines operate as presumptive — judges are expected to consider them, and deviations are reviewable on appeal for reasonableness, but they are not bound by the prescribed range. This is a closer model for South African conditions than the original mandatory framework. It retains structured discretion without raising separation-of-powers concerns.

The US experience also offers cautionary lessons. The original guidelines were criticised for producing racial disparities in drug sentencing, particularly the cocaine/crack sentencing gap that was eventually narrowed by the Fair Sentencing Act of 2010. *The lesson for South Africa is that guidelines built from historical sentencing data inherit whatever biases the historical data reflects.* **A South African Sentencing Commission would need to actively audit its guidelines for disparate impact on Black, coloured, and poor defendants** — not as a political exercise but as a constitutional one, grounded in Section 9's equal protection guarantee.

**The Minnesota Sentencing Guidelines Commission**, established in 1980, is considered the gold standard of state-level sentencing commissions in the United States. Its guidelines are presumptive rather than mandatory, its structure is simple enough to apply consistently, and its evidence-based revision process has kept the guidelines aligned with changing circumstances over four decades. Minnesota's experience demonstrates that a well-designed commission can produce substantial consistency gains without eliminating judicial discretion. It is the closest institutional match for what South Africa should build.

**The United Kingdom Sentencing Council**, established in 2010, offers a third model — more centralised than the US approach, with guidelines that courts must follow unless doing so would be contrary to the interests of justice. The UK Council publishes detailed guidelines for each offence category, with step-by-step sentencing frameworks that walk judges through the analytical process. The detailed-guidance approach is worth studying, but the UK institutional context differs meaningfully from South Africa's — a unitary legal system, a smaller judiciary, and a different relationship between the judiciary and parliament.

**China's structured sentencing reforms**, introduced from 2010 onward, use sentencing grids for common offences — a more prescriptive approach than the Anglophone models. The Chinese approach is not politically transferable to South Africa, but its technical apparatus — the grids themselves, the data infrastructure that supports them, the feedback loops between courts and policymakers — offers useful design references for building a data-informed sentencing system at scale.

:::expert
Professor Stephan Terblanche at the University of South Africa has written extensively on sentencing reform in the South African context, arguing consistently that the absence of structured guidelines is the root cause of the disparities that critics of minimum sentencing attribute to judicial discretion. His analysis draws a distinction between discretion that is structured through data, frameworks, and written reasons — and discretion that is unstructured, unreviewable, and effectively unlimited. Terblanche has argued that the solution to sentencing inconsistency is not to remove discretion (the minimum sentencing approach) but to give that discretion an institutional home — a framework within which it can be exercised visibly, justified in writing, and reviewed by reference to a published standard. His work, together with the sentencing research produced by the SALRC and the Institute for Security Studies, provides the empirical and analytical foundation for the reform proposed in this article. The policy question is no longer "what should a sentencing framework look like" — that has been answered in the academic literature for at least two decades — but "why has the answer not been implemented." The answer to that question, as with most institutional reform in South Africa, is political will rather than technical capacity.
:::

## Integration with Restorative Justice

The Sentencing Commission would give restorative justice processes formal standing in the sentencing framework — building on the reform proposed in [Community Courts and Restorative Justice](/articles/ss-3-3-community-courts-and-restorative-justice). Under the current framework, restorative outcomes exist in an ambiguous legal space. A magistrate can consider a victim-offender mediation outcome in sentencing, but there is no structured requirement to do so, no consistent weighting of restorative outcomes against other sentencing factors, and no appellate review of whether restorative outcomes were properly considered. The result is that restorative processes thrive in some courts and are invisible in others.

The proposed guidelines would integrate restorative justice as a formal sentencing input with structured weight. When an offender has participated in a victim-offender mediation and the victim has accepted the outcome, the sentencing court would be required to consider that outcome in determining the sentence within the presumptive band. The weight of the restorative outcome would not be mechanical — it would be calibrated by the nature of the offence, the sincerity of the offender's engagement, and the victim's informed acceptance — but it would be structured, visible, and reviewable.

This integration serves two purposes. First, it gives restorative justice processes legal weight, which creates an incentive for offenders to engage with them genuinely rather than instrumentally. Second, it protects restorative processes from being dismissed as soft-on-crime by critics who argue that non-custodial outcomes are inappropriate for serious offences. By embedding restorative outcomes in the sentencing framework alongside other factors — offence seriousness, prior record, rehabilitation prospects — the reform demonstrates that restorative justice is not an alternative to accountability but a form of it.

## Real-Time Sentencing Analytics

One of the most powerful features of the proposed framework is real-time sentencing analytics. The Sentencing Commission would maintain a national sentencing database — recording every sentence imposed, by offence, circumstances, judicial officer, and jurisdiction. This database would power a dashboard accessible to judicial officers, showing what sentences similar cases received nationally.

When a magistrate is about to sentence a first-time offender for assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm, the dashboard would show the distribution of sentences for similar cases across the country — the median, the range, the factors associated with higher and lower sentences. The magistrate retains full discretion — the dashboard informs rather than dictates. **But the information makes inconsistency visible. A magistrate who consistently sentences outside the national distribution would be identifiable**, and their deviation would require explanation — not to a political authority, but through the auditable written-reasons requirement.

This transparency serves multiple functions. It promotes consistency by giving judicial officers a reference point. It builds public confidence by making the sentencing system auditable. It supports appeal courts by providing empirical context for assessing whether a sentence is proportionate. And it generates the data needed for the Sentencing Commission to refine its guidelines over time — a feedback loop that makes the system adaptive rather than static.

**Data infrastructure requirements.** The real-time analytics function is only as good as the data that feeds it. Building the national sentencing database would require court reporting systems that capture sentencing outcomes in a structured, machine-readable format — a substantial upgrade from the paper-based and PDF-based reporting that characterises much of the current court system. The database would need to integrate with the NPA's case management system, the Department of Correctional Services' offender records, and the SAPS's criminal history database — integration work that has been attempted before and failed, and that must be budgeted and sequenced realistically this time.

Privacy and data protection considerations are non-trivial. The database would contain detailed information about sentenced individuals, their offences, and their personal circumstances. POPIA compliance would require anonymisation for public reporting, access controls for judicial users, and clear retention policies. The Commission's design must address these requirements from the outset — not as an afterthought added under pressure from the Information Regulator, but as a foundational element of the institutional architecture. The goal is a data infrastructure that produces transparent sentencing information while protecting the dignity and privacy of the individuals whose cases populate it.

## Transitional Provisions: Phasing Out Minimum Sentencing

The reform would not repeal Act 105 immediately. The transition would be structured, phased, and designed to preserve public confidence in the sentencing framework during the changeover. The Sentencing Commission would first be established by statute and given two years to develop initial guidelines through public consultation, judicial input, and empirical analysis. During this development phase, the minimum sentencing framework would remain in force unchanged.

Once the initial guidelines are published, a five-year transitional period would begin. During this period, the minimum sentencing framework would continue to apply, but courts would also be required to consult the Commission's guidelines and to indicate in their sentencing reasons how the guidelines inform the outcome. This parallel operation would allow empirical comparison of guideline-informed outcomes against minimum-sentencing outcomes — generating the evidence base for the final transition decision.

At the end of the transitional period, Parliament would decide — on the basis of the evidence generated — whether to sunset Act 105 and replace it with the guidelines as the primary sentencing framework. The evidence to be considered would include: comparative sentencing outcomes under the two frameworks, consistency gains, impact on recidivism, public confidence measures, and the views of the judiciary, the NPA, and victim advocacy organisations. The final decision would rest with Parliament, not the Commission — preserving democratic accountability for the ultimate policy choice.

This phased approach avoids a disruptive transition. It also builds political support by demonstrating the guidelines in operation before committing to them permanently. The South African Law Reform Commission has noted repeatedly that the minimum sentencing framework was originally intended as a temporary measure — the Act included a sunset clause that has been extended by Parliament rather than allowed to trigger a permanent sentencing reform. The proposed transitional provisions would finally give effect to the temporary intent of the original framework, replacing it with something built to last.

## Risks and Failure Modes

Any reform of this scale carries risks, and the proposal must be honest about them. Four failure modes warrant specific attention.

**Political capture.** A Sentencing Commission with weak institutional protections could become an instrument of whichever political authority appointed its commissioners. The mitigations are the judicial appointment process, the staggered fixed terms, the composition requirements, and the dedicated parliamentary appropriation — all designed to insulate the Commission from executive pressure. But no institutional design is immune to political capture if the political authority is determined enough. The ultimate protection is the constitutional values the Commission must operate within and the appellate review available for sentences that deviate from the guidelines. Capture of the Commission itself cannot capture the Constitutional Court.

**Judicial resistance.** Judges and magistrates who view structured guidelines as an encroachment on judicial independence could simply ignore the guidelines in practice — producing sentences that deviate without meaningful reasons and relying on the fact that appellate review is slow and expensive. The mitigation is to design the guidelines with judicial involvement from the outset, to frame them as structured discretion rather than eliminated discretion, and to pair their introduction with judicial education and data infrastructure that makes guideline compliance easier than deviation. The US Sentencing Commission's post-*Booker* experience suggests that judges will engage with guidelines they perceive as legitimate and ignore guidelines they perceive as imposed.

**Capacity constraints.** The Commission would require trained researchers, functional data infrastructure, and reliable court reporting systems — none of which exist at scale in South Africa's current court system. The mitigation is to phase the data infrastructure rollout alongside the guideline development, to use the five-year transitional period to build capacity, and to accept that the first generation of guidelines will operate with incomplete data. The perfect must not be the enemy of the good.

**Unfunded mandate.** Sentencing reform that is announced but not resourced would produce the worst of both worlds: the appearance of reform without the substance. The mitigation is the dedicated parliamentary appropriation — ring-fenced funding that cannot be reallocated through ordinary budget processes — and clear publication of the Commission's budget and staffing, so that any attempt to starve it of resources becomes visible and politically costly.

## The Sentencing Literacy Guide

Understanding the sentencing framework is essential for anyone who reads about court cases in the media, who has personal experience of the criminal justice system, or who wants to hold the system accountable for its sentencing outcomes.

:::framework
**The Sentencing Literacy Guide**

**How to Understand a Sentence:**
When you read about a sentence in the news, ask: What offence was convicted? What was the prescribed minimum (if any)? Did the court deviate from the minimum, and if so, on what grounds? How does the sentence compare to sentences for similar offences? Was a pre-sentencing report prepared? Were restorative justice outcomes considered?

**Key Sentencing Concepts:**
Minimum sentencing applies only to offences listed in Schedule 2 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act. "Substantial and compelling circumstances" is the legal standard for deviating below a prescribed minimum. A suspended sentence means the custodial portion is not served unless the offender commits a further offence during the suspension period. A fine can be imposed as an alternative to or in addition to imprisonment for most offences. Community service can be imposed as a condition of a suspended sentence or as a standalone sentence for less serious offences.

**Where to Find Sentencing Data:**
NPA Annual Report: includes conviction and sentencing data by crime type and court. Department of Justice annual statistics: includes case finalisation data. Appeal court judgments (available through SAFLII — the Southern African Legal Information Institute at www.saflii.org): provide precedent on sentencing for specific offences. The ISS publishes periodic sentencing analysis that contextualises individual sentences within broader patterns.

**How to Evaluate Consistency:**
If a sentence seems disproportionately high or low, compare it to sentences for similar offences in the same court tier. Look for appeal court guidance on the appropriate sentencing range. Check whether the minimum sentencing framework applies. If the sentence is an outlier, consider whether the circumstances genuinely justify deviation or whether the sentence reflects the kind of inconsistency that structured guidelines would address.
:::
