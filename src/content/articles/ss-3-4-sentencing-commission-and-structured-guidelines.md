## Caught Between Two Failures

South Africa's sentencing framework is caught between two failures. On one side: the Criminal Law Amendment Act of 1997 (Act 105) imposed blunt minimum sentences for serious offences — removing judicial discretion without addressing the root causes of sentencing inconsistency. A magistrate who concludes that a particular case warrants a sentence below the minimum must find "substantial and compelling circumstances" to deviate — a legal standard that different courts interpret differently, creating inconsistency in the application of a law designed to reduce inconsistency. On the other side: in the absence of structured guidelines for the vast majority of offences not covered by minimum sentencing, magistrates and judges produce wildly inconsistent sentences for similar crimes across different courts and provinces. The same offence, with similar circumstances, can produce sentences that vary by years depending on which magistrate hears the case and in which jurisdiction.

The result is a system that is simultaneously too rigid and too arbitrary. Minimum sentences remove the judicial discretion needed for individualised justice in the cases they cover. The absence of guidelines leaves judicial discretion entirely unstructured in the cases they do not. Neither approach produces sentences that are consistently proportionate, constitutionally sound, and aligned with the rehabilitation mandate that the Correctional Services Act establishes as the primary purpose of incarceration.

A permanent Sentencing Commission with structured guidelines — presumptive sentencing bands that guide but do not eliminate judicial discretion, anchored in data and constitutional values — could resolve both failures. This is not an untested idea. The US Sentencing Commission has operated since 1984, and despite legitimate criticisms of its implementation, it has produced a framework that structures discretion while maintaining judicial independence. China's structured sentencing grids provide systematic, data-informed guidance that reduces inconsistency without eliminating judicial judgement. The South African Law Reform Commission has repeatedly recommended sentencing reform. What has been missing is the political will to implement it.

## The Current Framework: Act 105 and Its Problems

The Criminal Law Amendment Act of 1997 was a response to public outrage over perceived sentencing leniency for serious crimes, particularly in the context of the post-apartheid crime explosion documented in [From Rainbow Nation to State Capture](/articles/ss-1-2-from-rainbow-nation-to-state-capture). The Act prescribes minimum sentences for specified offences: life imprisonment for premeditated murder, rape in specified circumstances, and other offences listed in Part I of Schedule 2; fifteen years for first offenders (escalating with repeat offences) for murder, rape, robbery with aggravating circumstances, and other serious offences listed in Parts II, III, and IV.

The Act allows deviation from the minimum when "substantial and compelling circumstances" exist — a standard that the Constitutional Court has interpreted as permitting judicial discretion, but only when the circumstances of the case are sufficiently exceptional to justify a departure. The Constitutional Court, in *S v Malgas* (2001), confirmed that the minimum sentencing framework is constitutionally valid but emphasised that courts retain discretion to impose lesser sentences when the prescribed minimum would be disproportionate.

The problems with this framework are structural. First, minimum sentences are blunt instruments that cannot account for the vast diversity of circumstances within any offence category. Two murders can be profoundly different in their moral culpability, circumstances, and appropriate response — but the minimum sentencing framework treats them identically unless a court finds "substantial and compelling circumstances" to deviate. Second, the "substantial and compelling circumstances" standard is interpreted inconsistently across courts and provinces — some courts treat it as a relatively low threshold, others as an almost impossibly high one. Third, the minimum sentencing framework applies only to specified serious offences. For the vast majority of criminal matters — common assault, theft, fraud, drug possession, property damage — no sentencing guidelines exist at all. Magistrates exercise entirely unstructured discretion, guided only by their own judgement, the limited precedent available from appeal courts, and whatever informal norms prevail in their jurisdiction.

:::takeaway
South Africa's sentencing framework fails in two opposite directions: minimum sentences for serious offences are blunt instruments that remove judicial discretion without producing consistency, while the absence of guidelines for other offences leaves discretion entirely unstructured. The result is a system that is simultaneously too rigid for cases it covers and too arbitrary for cases it does not. Structured sentencing guidelines — presumptive bands that guide rather than eliminate discretion — would resolve both failures.
:::

## The Proposed Reform: A South African Sentencing Commission

The reform proposes a permanent South African Sentencing Commission — an independent body, appointed through the judiciary, mandated to develop, publish, and maintain structured sentencing guidelines for all criminal offences. The model draws on the US Sentencing Commission (for institutional design) and China's structured sentencing grids (for the systematic approach to structuring discretion), adapted to South Africa's constitutional value framework.

The Commission would develop presumptive sentencing bands for each offence category. A presumptive band defines an expected sentencing range — say, three to five years for a first-time robbery without aggravating circumstances. A magistrate or judge can sentence within the band without providing reasons beyond the normal sentencing considerations. A sentence below or above the band requires written reasons, creating an auditable record that promotes consistency without eliminating discretion.

The sentencing bands would be determined by multiple inputs: the crime type and its statutory maximum, the circumstances of the offence (aggravating and mitigating), the offender's prior record, the impact on the victim (as established through victim impact statements or restorative justice processes), and the offender's rehabilitation prospects (as assessed through pre-sentencing reports from probation officers). Each input would be structured — not as a mechanical formula, but as a framework that guides the judicial officer through a consistent analytical process.

Restorative justice outcomes would be a formal sentencing input. When an offender has participated in a restorative justice process — victim-offender mediation, community conferencing, or a compensation agreement — the outcome of that process would be considered by the sentencing court as a factor in determining the appropriate sentence within the presumptive band. This integration of restorative justice into the formal sentencing framework gives restorative processes legal weight and creates an incentive for offenders to participate in them.

:::expert
The South African Law Reform Commission (SALRC) has investigated sentencing reform over multiple project cycles, producing discussion papers and reports that have recommended the establishment of a sentencing information system and structured sentencing guidelines. Their research has documented sentencing disparity across courts and provinces with empirical data, showing that similar offences receive sentences that vary by years depending on the court and the judicial officer. The SALRC has noted that the minimum sentencing framework of Act 105 was intended as a temporary measure — the Act originally included a sunset clause that has been repeatedly renewed rather than replaced with a permanent, structured sentencing framework. Their analysis has consistently recommended a Sentencing Commission as the institutional mechanism for developing guidelines that structure discretion without eliminating it, and their work provides the analytical foundation for the reform proposed here.
:::

## Real-Time Sentencing Analytics

One of the most powerful features of the proposed framework is real-time sentencing analytics. The Sentencing Commission would maintain a national sentencing database — recording every sentence imposed, by offence, circumstances, judicial officer, and jurisdiction. This database would power a dashboard accessible to judicial officers, showing what sentences similar cases received nationally.

When a magistrate is about to sentence a first-time offender for assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm, the dashboard would show the distribution of sentences for similar cases across the country — the median, the range, the factors associated with higher and lower sentences. The magistrate retains full discretion — the dashboard informs rather than dictates. But the information makes inconsistency visible. A magistrate who consistently sentences outside the national distribution would be identifiable, and their deviation would require explanation — not to a political authority, but through the auditable written-reasons requirement.

This transparency serves multiple functions. It promotes consistency by giving judicial officers a reference point. It builds public confidence by making the sentencing system auditable. It supports appeal courts by providing empirical context for assessing whether a sentence is proportionate. And it generates the data needed for the Sentencing Commission to refine its guidelines over time — a feedback loop that makes the system adaptive rather than static.

## The Sentencing Literacy Guide

Understanding the sentencing framework is essential for anyone who reads about court cases in the media, who has personal experience of the criminal justice system, or who wants to hold the system accountable for its sentencing outcomes.

:::framework
**The Sentencing Literacy Guide**

**How to Understand a Sentence:**
When you read about a sentence in the news, ask: What offence was convicted? What was the prescribed minimum (if any)? Did the court deviate from the minimum, and if so, on what grounds? How does the sentence compare to sentences for similar offences? Was a pre-sentencing report prepared? Were restorative justice outcomes considered?

**Key Sentencing Concepts:**
Minimum sentencing applies only to offences listed in Schedule 2 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act. "Substantial and compelling circumstances" is the legal standard for deviating below a prescribed minimum. A suspended sentence means the custodial portion is not served unless the offender commits a further offence during the suspension period. A fine can be imposed as an alternative to or in addition to imprisonment for most offences. Community service can be imposed as a condition of a suspended sentence or as a standalone sentence for less serious offences.

**Where to Find Sentencing Data:**
NPA Annual Report: includes conviction and sentencing data by crime type and court. Department of Justice annual statistics: includes case finalisation data. Appeal court judgments (available through SAFLII — the Southern African Legal Information Institute at www.saflii.org): provide precedent on sentencing for specific offences. The ISS publishes periodic sentencing analysis that contextualises individual sentences within broader patterns.

**How to Evaluate Consistency:**
If a sentence seems disproportionately high or low, compare it to sentences for similar offences in the same court tier. Look for appeal court guidance on the appropriate sentencing range. Check whether the minimum sentencing framework applies. If the sentence is an outlier, consider whether the circumstances genuinely justify deviation or whether the sentence reflects the kind of inconsistency that structured guidelines would address.
:::
