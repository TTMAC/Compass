## The Trap of Comprehensive Reform

South Africa has no shortage of reform plans. The National Development Plan devoted an entire chapter to safety and justice. The White Paper on Policing (2016) articulated a vision for professional, demilitarised police services. The White Paper on Corrections (2005) described rehabilitation-centred incarceration. The Farlam Commission after Marikana produced detailed recommendations on crowd management and police accountability. Parliamentary committees have tabled reports. Civil society organisations have published blueprints. University researchers have produced dissertations.

Almost none of it has been implemented.

The reason is not that the plans were wrong. Many of them were excellent. The reason is that comprehensive reform plans — plans that propose to change everything at once — collapse under their own weight. They require simultaneous political will across multiple ministries, coordinated legislative action across multiple committees, and institutional capacity that the very dysfunction they are trying to fix has eroded. A plan that requires the Minister of Police, the Minister of Justice, the Minister of Correctional Services, and the National Treasury to act in concert, simultaneously, on a dozen legislative amendments while also restructuring their departments — that plan will fail. Not because it is intellectually wrong, but because it is operationally impossible.

This article takes a different approach. The preceding articles in this series have laid out five major structural reforms: a [three-tier policing architecture](/articles/ss-3-1-three-tier-policing-architecture) that distributes responsibility across national, provincial, and municipal levels; an [independent investigating magistracy](/articles/ss-3-2-independent-investigating-magistracy) that separates investigation from prosecution; [community courts](/articles/ss-3-3-community-courts-and-restorative-justice) that create a municipal justice tier; a [sentencing commission](/articles/ss-3-4-sentencing-commission-and-structured-guidelines) that brings consistency and proportionality to sentencing; and [rehabilitation-centred corrections](/articles/ss-3-5-rehabilitation-as-constitutional-mandate) that transforms prisons from warehouses into institutions that actually reduce recidivism.

Each of these reforms is sound. Together, they would transform South Africa's criminal justice system. But attempting all five simultaneously would guarantee that none succeeds. The question is not what needs to change — the preceding articles answered that. The question is: in what order?

## Why Sequence Matters

Reform sequencing is not a bureaucratic detail. It is the single most important determinant of whether reform succeeds or fails. Three principles govern sequencing.

The first principle is prerequisites. Some reforms cannot succeed unless other reforms have already been implemented. You cannot improve prosecution if investigation remains broken, because prosecutors depend on the quality of dockets that detectives produce. You cannot reduce prison overcrowding through sentencing reform if the remand detention crisis — driven by court delays — is not addressed first. You cannot build a three-tier policing system if you have not first established the investigative and prosecutorial capacity that the system requires at each tier.

The second principle is enabling infrastructure. Some reforms are multipliers — they do not directly solve a problem, but they make it possible for other reforms to work. Digital case management is a classic enabler. A unified electronic docket system does not, by itself, reduce crime. But it makes it possible to track cases from report through investigation, prosecution, adjudication, and corrections — which means every other reform in the pipeline becomes measurable, manageable, and accountable. Without that digital spine, each reform operates in an information vacuum.

The third principle is political momentum. Some reforms are easier to achieve than others — because they face less political resistance, because they deliver visible results quickly, or because they can be achieved through executive action or regulatory change rather than requiring contested legislation. Starting with achievable reforms builds the political capital and institutional confidence needed for harder reforms later. Starting with the hardest reform first — say, constitutional amendment for provincial policing — risks exhausting political capital before any tangible improvement has been delivered.

:::takeaway
Reform sequence is determined by three principles: prerequisites (some reforms depend on others being in place first), enabling infrastructure (some reforms are multipliers that make others work), and political momentum (start with achievable changes that build capital for harder ones). Ignoring sequence is how comprehensive reform plans die.
:::

## Phase 1: Investigation and Prosecution Reform (Years 1–3)

The criminal justice system is a pipeline. Crime enters at one end, and consequences — whether conviction, diversion, or acquittal — emerge at the other. The pipeline's performance is determined by its narrowest point. In South Africa, that narrowest point is the handoff between investigation and prosecution: Outcome 3 (investigation quality) and Outcome 7 (prosecutorial effectiveness).

As [The Broken Pipeline](/articles/ss-2-1-the-broken-pipeline) documented, SAPS detectives are overwhelmed, undertrained, and unsupported. Forensic laboratories have multi-year backlogs. Case dockets arrive at the NPA incomplete, poorly compiled, and lacking the evidence needed for successful prosecution. The NPA, itself depleted by years of political interference and institutional decay, declines or withdraws cases not because offenders are innocent but because the evidence is insufficient. The result is a system in which the probability of a reported crime resulting in conviction is catastrophically low — and the deterrent effect of the criminal justice system approaches zero.

Phase 1 targets this bottleneck.

**The Investigating Magistracy Pilot.** The investigating magistracy proposed in [An Independent Investigating Magistracy](/articles/ss-3-2-independent-investigating-magistracy) — Judicial Investigation Officers (JIOs) who direct complex investigations — should be piloted in two or three metropolitan areas during years one to three. The pilot does not require new primary legislation. It can be established through amendments to the Criminal Procedure Act of 1977, specifically provisions governing the appointment and powers of investigating officers, supplemented by new enabling regulations. The Constitutional Court's existing jurisprudence on the right to a fair trial (Section 35) and the independence of the judiciary (Section 165) provides the constitutional foundation. The pilot would focus on Track C cases — serious and complex matters including murder, armed robbery, organised crime, and major corruption — where investigation quality most critically determines prosecution outcomes.

**SAPS Detective Capacity.** Simultaneously, SAPS detective capacity must be upgraded. This does not require legislative change — it requires budget reallocation within SAPS and operational restructuring by the National Commissioner. The specific interventions: ring-fenced detective recruitment (recruiting specifically for the Detective Service, not rotating patrol officers into detective roles), specialised training programmes (forensic awareness, digital evidence, statement taking, docket management), and reduced caseloads through the case-flow triage system described in [Community Courts and Restorative Justice](/articles/ss-3-3-community-courts-and-restorative-justice). When community courts divert 30 to 40 per cent of current magistrates' court matters to Track A, the detective service can concentrate its limited capacity on the cases that actually require skilled investigation.

**NPA Restoration.** The NPA's prosecutorial capacity must be restored — and here the word "restored" is precise, because the NPA was once a capable institution before successive politically motivated appointments and institutional interference hollowed it out. The NPA Act of 1998 requires amendment to strengthen the appointment process for the National Director of Public Prosecutions (insulating it from executive discretion), to ring-fence NPA budget allocations (preventing the Treasury squeeze that has eroded prosecutorial capacity), and to establish clear performance metrics linked to case conversion rates rather than the crude conviction statistics currently used. These amendments are politically achievable because the NPA's decline is widely acknowledged across the political spectrum — restoring NPA capacity is one of the few criminal justice reforms with broad political support.

**Digital Case Management.** The enabling infrastructure for Phase 1 is a digital case management system — replacing SAPS's broken Crime Administration System (CAS) and the NPA's fragmented case tracking with a unified electronic docket that follows a case from report through to final disposition. This is a technology procurement and implementation project, not a legislative one. It requires Treasury funding, competent procurement (a significant risk given the government's history with large IT projects), and change management within SAPS and the NPA. But it is the single most important enabler of everything that follows, because without integrated case tracking, no reform can be measured, no bottleneck can be identified, and no accountability can be enforced.

The case management system need not be built from scratch. Multiple commercial and open-source criminal justice case management platforms exist internationally and can be adapted for South African requirements. The key design decision is interoperability: the system must be designed from the outset to exchange data with the court management systems and corrections management systems that will connect to it in Phases 2 and 3. Building a closed system that serves only SAPS and the NPA would repeat the fundamental error of every previous South African criminal justice IT project — building a silo rather than a platform. The Phase 1 system must be architected as the first module of the eventual Integrated Criminal Justice Information System (ICJS), not as a standalone tool.

:::expert
The Institute for Security Studies (ISS) has researched criminal justice reform sequencing across sub-Saharan Africa, including case studies from Kenya, Rwanda, and Ghana. Their analysis, published across multiple monographs and policy briefs, consistently finds that investigation-prosecution reform must precede structural changes to policing or corrections. The reason is empirical: in every country where policing restructuring was attempted before investigation capacity was built, the new structures replicated the old dysfunction because the core capability — converting crime into evidence into prosecution — had not been addressed. Johan Burger, a senior researcher at the ISS and former SAPS officer, has argued that South Africa's detective service crisis is the "single greatest obstacle to criminal justice effectiveness" and that no amount of visible policing, community engagement, or legislative reform will reduce crime if detectives cannot produce prosecutable dockets. The ISS has recommended that South Africa prioritise detective capacity, forensic science capability, and prosecutor-detective coordination as the foundation for all subsequent reform — a recommendation that aligns precisely with the Phase 1 sequencing proposed here.
:::

## Phase 2: Court Reform and Policing Restructuring (Years 3–5)

With investigation and prosecution capacity strengthened, Phase 2 addresses the institutional architecture — how courts process cases and how policing is structured.

**Community Courts Legislation.** The community courts described in [Community Courts and Restorative Justice](/articles/ss-3-3-community-courts-and-restorative-justice) require amendment to the Magistrates' Courts Act of 1944. The amendment would establish Municipal Community Justice Courts as a formal tier of the judiciary, define their jurisdiction (summary offences with sentences up to two years), create the Community Justice Officer (CJO) profession, and establish the case-flow triage system that routes matters to the appropriate track. This is contested legislation — the legal profession will debate jurisdiction, the magistracy will resist perceived encroachment, and the Treasury will resist the funding implications. But the amendment is achievable because the case for decongesting the courts is overwhelming, the Hatfield Community Court pilot has demonstrated viability, and the Child Justice Act's diversion provisions have already established the legal principle that not every criminal matter requires a full magistrate's attention.

Community courts are sequenced in Phase 2 rather than Phase 1 because they depend on the digital case management system established in Phase 1. The triage system that routes cases to the appropriate track requires integrated electronic records — without them, triage becomes an administrative bottleneck rather than an efficiency gain.

**Sentencing Commission Establishment.** The sentencing commission proposed in [Sentencing Commission and Structured Guidelines](/articles/ss-3-4-sentencing-commission-and-structured-guidelines) can be established through new enabling legislation — a Sentencing Reform Act — without amending the Constitution. The commission would develop structured sentencing guidelines, collect sentencing data to measure inter-court consistency, and make recommendations for minimum sentencing reform. Establishing the commission in Phase 2 is strategic: it takes two to three years for a sentencing commission to develop its first set of guidelines (based on international experience, including England and Wales, where the Sentencing Council took approximately two years from establishment to first guidelines). Starting in year three means guidelines are available by years five to six — precisely when the broader structural reforms are beginning to take effect and consistent sentencing becomes critical.

**Three-Tier Policing Pilots.** The three-tier policing architecture proposed in [Three-Tier Policing Architecture](/articles/ss-3-1-three-tier-policing-architecture) is the most structurally ambitious reform in the series — and therefore the one that requires the most careful sequencing. Phase 2 begins with pilots in two or three provinces, not a nationwide rollout. The pilots require amendment to the South African Police Service Act of 1995 to create the legal basis for provincial police services operating alongside the national service. This is where the constitutional question becomes unavoidable.

## The Constitutional Question

Section 199(1) of the Constitution establishes "a single police service." Section 205(1) provides that the national police service must be structured to function at national, provincial, and — where appropriate — local levels. Schedule 4 of the Constitution lists "police services" as a concurrent competency of national and provincial government.

The conventional reading of Section 199(1) is that it prohibits provincial police services — that the phrase "a single police service" means one unified force under the National Commissioner. This reading has been reinforced by decades of centralised policing practice and by the political dynamics that favour national control.

But the constitutional text is more permissive than the conventional reading suggests. Section 205(1) explicitly anticipates policing structured at provincial and local levels. Schedule 4's inclusion of "police services" (plural) as a concurrent competency implies that provinces have a constitutional role in policing that goes beyond the current arrangement of provincial commissioners who are subordinate to the National Commissioner. The Constitutional Court has not been asked to adjudicate this question directly, but its broader jurisprudence on co-operative governance (Section 41) and provincial powers suggests an interpretation that could accommodate provincial police services within the framework of "a single police service" — provided that the national service retains overall coordination, standards-setting, and priority crime functions.

:::expert
Professor Lukas Muntingh of the Dullah Omar Institute at the University of the Western Cape has written extensively on the constitutional dimensions of policing reform in South Africa. His analysis of Section 199, read alongside Section 205 and Schedule 4, concludes that the Constitution does not require a monolithic national police force but rather a coordinated national police service within which provincial and local components can operate. Muntingh has argued that the phrase "a single police service" refers to the principle of a unified system — common standards, shared intelligence, coordinated operations — rather than a single organisational hierarchy. This interpretation is supported by the constitutional text's own structure: Schedule 4 would not list "police services" as a concurrent provincial competency if the Constitution intended to exclude provinces entirely from policing governance. Muntingh's work suggests that provincial police services could be established through amendment to the SAPS Act and provincial legislation, without requiring constitutional amendment — provided that the national service retains its coordination and standards-setting role and that the provincial services operate within a framework of co-operative governance as contemplated by Chapter 3 of the Constitution.
:::

The practical implication: three-tier policing pilots can proceed through statutory amendment — they do not require the two-thirds parliamentary majority needed for constitutional change. This is a critical sequencing insight, because constitutional amendments are politically almost impossible in South Africa's current parliamentary configuration, while statutory amendments require only a simple majority.

The Phase 2 pilots would establish provincial police services in two or three provinces. The Western Cape and Gauteng are logical candidates, given their existing metropolitan police capacity (the City of Cape Town and City of Johannesburg both operate municipal police services under the South African Police Service Act) and the political appetite for provincial policing in both provinces. KwaZulu-Natal, with its distinct crime profile (political violence, taxi-related violence, rural stock theft alongside urban gang activity) and its history of calls for provincial policing autonomy, is a strong candidate for the third pilot.

The pilots would test the operational model across multiple dimensions. Command relationships: how do provincial police commissioners relate to the National Commissioner, and how do municipal police services integrate with the provincial tier? Resource allocation: how are personnel, vehicles, equipment, and technology distributed between tiers, and how does the funding model work in practice? Intelligence sharing: how does crime intelligence flow between national, provincial, and municipal levels without creating the fragmentation that undermines intelligence effectiveness? Accountability: how are provincial police services held accountable — to provincial legislatures, to IPID, to the Civilian Secretariat for Police, and to the communities they serve? These operational questions cannot be answered theoretically. They require empirical testing — which is precisely why the phased approach pilots before it scales.

## Phase 3: Full Structural Transformation (Years 5–10)

Phase 3 is where the reforms consolidate into a transformed system. It depends on the foundations laid in Phases 1 and 2.

**Nationwide Three-Tier Policing.** Based on the evidence from Phase 2 pilots, the three-tier policing model rolls out to all nine provinces. This requires further amendment to the SAPS Act, new provincial policing legislation in each province, and — critically — the funding model detailed in [Funding the Delta](/articles/ss-4-2-funding-the-delta). The rollout is phased province by province, starting with provinces that have the strongest existing capacity (Gauteng, Western Cape, KwaZulu-Natal) and proceeding to provinces where capacity must be built from the ground up (Limpopo, Mpumalanga, North West, Northern Cape, Eastern Cape, Free State).

**Rehabilitation-Centred Corrections.** The transformation of the correctional system from warehousing to rehabilitation, as described in [Rehabilitation as Constitutional Mandate](/articles/ss-3-5-rehabilitation-as-constitutional-mandate), requires amendment to the Correctional Services Act of 1998. The amendments would mandate individual sentence plans for every inmate, establish minimum standards for educational and vocational programme access, require substance abuse treatment capacity proportional to the inmate population with substance abuse disorders, and restructure the parole and community corrections system to prioritise reintegration support over compliance monitoring. Corrections reform is sequenced in Phase 3 because it depends on reduced prison populations — which depend on the remand detention reduction achieved through court reform (Phase 2) and the sentencing guidelines produced by the sentencing commission (Phase 2). Attempting rehabilitation reform in overcrowded facilities is futile. You must first reduce the population to manageable levels.

**Full Sentencing Commission Implementation.** By year five, the sentencing commission established in Phase 2 has produced its first set of structured guidelines. Phase 3 sees these guidelines implemented across all courts, with monitoring data feeding back to the commission for refinement. The minimum sentencing framework (introduced by Act 105 of 1997 and repeatedly extended) is reformed — not abolished, but restructured to allow judicial discretion within guideline ranges rather than the blunt mandatory minimums that currently distort sentencing.

**Technology Infrastructure Buildout.** The digital case management system piloted in Phase 1 is expanded into a full Integrated Criminal Justice Information System (ICJS) — a unified platform that tracks every case from report through investigation, prosecution, adjudication, sentencing, incarceration, rehabilitation, parole, and post-release monitoring. The ICJS has been proposed and funded multiple times in South Africa's history and has never been successfully implemented, primarily because it was attempted as a standalone technology project rather than as the digital spine of a broader institutional reform. Sequencing it within Phase 3 — after the institutional reforms it is meant to serve have been established — gives it a functional purpose and institutional demand that previous attempts lacked. The ICJS enables the National Law and Order Scorecard described in [Measuring Progress](/articles/ss-4-3-measuring-progress).

## The Legislative Agenda

The reform sequence requires amendments to six existing Acts and at least two new pieces of primary legislation. Here is the map.

**The Criminal Procedure Act, 1977 (Act 51 of 1977).** This is South Africa's foundational criminal procedure legislation. Phase 1 amendments would create the legal basis for Judicial Investigation Officers, establish the case-flow triage system, and modernise provisions governing digital evidence and electronic docket management. Phase 2 amendments would define community court procedure and the rights of accused persons in the community court track. This Act is the legislative workhorse of the reform — the most amendments, the most complexity, and the most contested provisions.

**The National Prosecuting Authority Act, 1998 (Act 32 of 1998).** Phase 1 amendments would strengthen the appointment process for the National Director (requiring parliamentary confirmation and fixed-term protections), ring-fence NPA budget allocations, and establish statutory performance metrics. These amendments address the institutional vulnerability that allowed the NPA to be captured and hollowed out — a vulnerability that, if not addressed, would undermine every other reform in the pipeline.

**The South African Police Service Act, 1995 (Act 68 of 1995).** Phase 2 amendments would create the legal framework for provincial police services, define command relationships between national, provincial, and municipal tiers, establish the intelligence-sharing architecture, and create civilian oversight mechanisms for each tier. Phase 3 amendments would extend the framework nationwide based on pilot outcomes.

**The Magistrates' Courts Act, 1944 (Act 32 of 1944).** Phase 2 amendments would establish Municipal Community Justice Courts, define their jurisdiction, create the Community Justice Officer profession, and establish the appeal pathway from community courts to magistrates' courts. This is the oldest statute in the reform agenda — drafted during apartheid — and its comprehensive amendment is long overdue.

**The Correctional Services Act, 1998 (Act 111 of 1998).** Phase 3 amendments would mandate rehabilitation programming, establish minimum staffing ratios for rehabilitation services, restructure the parole board system, and transform the Community Corrections mandate from compliance monitoring to reintegration support.

**The Private Security Industry Regulation Act, 2001 (Act 56 of 2001).** Phase 3 amendments would strengthen regulatory oversight of the private security industry, establish clearer boundaries between public policing and private security functions, and create mechanisms for integrating private security intelligence into the national crime intelligence architecture. As [The Private Security Paradox](/articles/ss-2-5-the-private-security-paradox) documented, the private security industry's growth is both a symptom of and a response to public policing failure — and its regulation must evolve as public policing capacity improves.

**New Legislation: The Investigating Magistracy Act.** Phase 1 would require new enabling legislation to establish the Judicial Investigation Office, define JIO appointment criteria and independence protections, and create the institutional framework for JIO operations. This could alternatively be achieved through substantial amendment to the Criminal Procedure Act, but a standalone Act would provide clearer institutional identity and stronger independence protections.

**New Legislation: The Sentencing Reform Act.** Phase 2 would require new legislation to establish the Sentencing Commission, define its mandate, composition, and independence, and create the legal basis for structured sentencing guidelines. Again, this could theoretically be achieved through amendment to existing legislation, but the significance of the reform warrants standalone legislation.

:::takeaway
The legislative agenda spans six existing Acts and two new statutes. No constitutional amendment is required — the reform sequence can be achieved through ordinary legislation passed by simple parliamentary majority. The most critical legislative actions are in Phase 1 (Criminal Procedure Act and NPA Act amendments) because they unblock everything that follows.
:::

## Learning from Failed Reform: The National Crime Prevention Strategy

South Africa has attempted comprehensive criminal justice reform before. The National Crime Prevention Strategy (NCPS), adopted in 1996, was an ambitious, evidence-based framework that correctly identified the systemic nature of crime and proposed integrated interventions across policing, prosecution, corrections, and social prevention. It was widely praised by international experts. It achieved almost nothing.

The NCPS failed for precisely the reasons this article's sequencing framework is designed to avoid. It attempted to reform everything simultaneously. It required coordination across multiple government departments that had no institutional mechanisms for coordination. It depended on sustained political attention from a Cabinet that was simultaneously managing the transition from apartheid across every domain of governance. It set ambitious targets without specifying which changes were prerequisites for others, which could be achieved within existing legislation, and which required new law. Within three years, the NCPS had been quietly abandoned — replaced by a series of more politically appealing "tough on crime" initiatives (Operation Crackdown, the Firearms Control Act, the Sexual Offences Act amendments) that addressed individual symptoms without tackling the systemic dysfunction.

The lesson is not that reform is impossible. The lesson is that the approach to reform determines its fate. A sequenced approach — starting with the narrowest, most critical bottleneck (investigation and prosecution), building enabling infrastructure (digital case management), then expanding to broader structural changes (court reform, policing restructuring, corrections transformation) — avoids the trap that killed the NCPS. Each phase delivers measurable improvement before the next begins. Each phase creates the institutional capacity and political momentum for the next. And each phase can be evaluated independently, so that failure in one area does not collapse the entire programme.

The phased approach also manages fiscal risk. Instead of committing to a decade-long programme with a single upfront cost estimate (which will inevitably be wrong), phased reform allows each phase's investment to be evaluated against its returns before the next phase's investment is committed. If Phase 1 delivers the expected improvement in investigation and prosecution outcomes, the fiscal and political case for Phase 2 investment strengthens. If Phase 1 underperforms, the approach can be adjusted before Phase 2 spending is locked in.

:::expert
The Africa Criminal Justice Reform (ACJR) programme, based at the Dullah Omar Institute and funded by multiple international development partners, has studied criminal justice reform sequencing across the African continent. Their comparative analysis of reform efforts in Kenya (2010 constitutional reform), Rwanda (post-genocide justice system rebuilding), Ghana (justice sector reform programme), and Sierra Leone (post-conflict justice reform) identifies a consistent pattern: reforms that began with investigation and prosecution capacity — the system's core throughput function — produced measurable improvements within three to five years, while reforms that began with structural reorganisation (new courts, new police structures, new corrections models) without first building the investigative and prosecutorial foundation took longer to produce results and were more vulnerable to political reversal. The ACJR's findings reinforce the Phase 1 priority proposed here: fix the detective-to-prosecutor handoff first, because that is the point where the system converts crime into consequences, and consequences are what everything else depends on.
:::

## The Risk of Doing Nothing

The reform sequence proposed here is ambitious. It spans a decade. It requires sustained political will, fiscal commitment, and institutional capacity. Critics will argue that it is unrealistic.

But the alternative — doing nothing — is not cost-free. The alternative is a continuation of the current trajectory: a criminal justice system that fails to deter crime, fails to convert investigation into prosecution, fails to deliver timely justice, fails to rehabilitate offenders, and fails to protect citizens. That failure has consequences. It drives the private security industry's relentless growth, effectively taxing citizens for a service the state should provide. It depresses foreign direct investment, because international investors factor crime risk into their capital allocation decisions. It destroys social trust, because communities that cannot rely on the state for justice develop their own — often violent — alternatives. It entrenches inequality, because the wealthy can afford private security and the poor cannot.

The cost of doing nothing is examined in detail in [Funding the Delta](/articles/ss-4-2-funding-the-delta). But the directional conclusion is clear: the status quo is not free, and its cost is rising.

## The Legislative Tracker

If you have read this far, you understand the reform roadmap. The question becomes: how do you, as a citizen, track whether it is being implemented? The Legislative Tracker is a practical framework for monitoring reform legislation through Parliament.

:::framework
**The Legislative Tracker**

**Where to Find Bills:**
Parliament's website (www.parliament.gov.za) publishes all Bills before Parliament, including their status (introduced, referred to committee, passed by one house, passed by both houses, assented to by the President). The Parliamentary Monitoring Group (www.pmg.org.za) provides more accessible tracking, with committee meeting reports, Bill summaries, and submission deadlines.

**Which Committees to Watch:**
The Portfolio Committee on Police (SAPS Act amendments, policing restructuring). The Portfolio Committee on Justice and Correctional Services (Criminal Procedure Act, NPA Act, Correctional Services Act, Magistrates' Courts Act amendments, new Investigating Magistracy and Sentencing Reform legislation). The Portfolio Committee on Finance (budget allocations for reform implementation). The Select Committees in the NCOP mirror these portfolios and handle concurrent competency matters (including provincial policing).

**How to Track Progress:**
For each reform in the sequence, identify the specific legislative vehicle — the Bill or amendment that enables it. Then track that Bill through its stages: introduction, committee referral, public hearings, committee deliberation, committee report, plenary debate, and vote. The Parliamentary Monitoring Group sends email alerts for committee meetings and submission deadlines — subscribe to alerts for the committees listed above.

**How to Make a Submission:**
When a reform Bill is tabled, Parliament is constitutionally required to facilitate public participation. [Making Public Submissions](/articles/4-3-making-public-submissions) provides a detailed guide to writing and submitting comments on legislation. For criminal justice reform Bills specifically, frame your submission around the reform's expected impact on the outcomes documented in this series — investigation quality, prosecution effectiveness, court efficiency, sentencing consistency, rehabilitation, and recidivism reduction.

**What to Watch For:**
Dilution — reform Bills that are amended during committee stage to remove their most important provisions. Delay — Bills that are referred to committee and never scheduled for deliberation. Defunding — Bills that are passed but not allocated the budget needed for implementation. These are the three mechanisms by which reform is defeated without ever being formally rejected. Your role as a citizen watchdog is to identify these patterns and raise them — through submissions, through media engagement, and through the democratic accountability mechanisms that [Your Right to Participate](/articles/4-1-your-right-to-participate) documents.
:::

The reform roadmap in this article is a plan. Plans do not implement themselves. The fiscal dimension — what reform will cost and why it is cheaper than the status quo — is the subject of [Funding the Delta](/articles/ss-4-2-funding-the-delta). The measurement dimension — how you will know whether reform is working — is the subject of [Measuring Progress](/articles/ss-4-3-measuring-progress). Together, these three articles provide the bridge between the system you have and the system the Constitution demands.
