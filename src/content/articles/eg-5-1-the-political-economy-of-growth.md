## The Bottom Line, Up Front

Every economic reform creates winners and losers. This is not a bug in the system — it is the system. And in South Africa, the political management of that redistribution is not a side effect of growth strategy but its central challenge. Resource optimisation threatens mining incumbents who profit from the current export model. Efficiency-driven growth requires regulatory reforms that eliminate rent-seeking opportunities. Infrastructure reform exposes SOE patronage networks. MSME support challenges established firms that prefer less competition. No economic strategy succeeds by pretending these political dynamics do not exist.

If you have followed this series from [The R7,000 Economy](/articles/eg-1-1-the-seven-thousand-rand-economy) through the growth strategy in Part 2, the enablers in Part 3, and the scorecards in Part 4, you now have the full picture of what South Africa needs to do. Part 5 is about why it has not done it, and what would need to change for it to start. This article addresses the question that sits underneath every strategy document, every policy framework, and every budget speech: who wins, who loses, and how do you build coalitions for reform in a democracy where the losers vote too?

The comparator countries that achieved what South Africa has not — Chile, Malaysia, South Korea, Poland — all managed the political economy of reform explicitly. They did not pretend that economic restructuring was a technocratic exercise that could be separated from politics. They built social compacts. They constructed reform coalitions. They phased in changes to give losers time to adapt. They compensated displaced workers and industries. And critically, they ensured that the winners of reform were numerous enough, vocal enough, and politically organised enough to sustain the reform agenda through the inevitable backlash.

South Africa has the technical knowledge to design sound economic policy. It has produced dozens of credible strategy documents — the NDP, the IPAP iterations, the ERRP, the ReIMagine industrialisation framework. What it has lacked is not economic intelligence but political economy management: the disciplined, unsentimental analysis of who benefits, who loses, and how you construct a political coalition that can push reform through against the resistance of those who profit from the status quo.

This article gives you that analysis. By the end, you will have a stakeholder map you can apply to any proposed economic policy — a practical framework for assessing whether a reform has the political conditions to succeed or whether it is destined to join the long list of South African strategies that were technically sound and politically stillborn.

## Economic Policy Is Not a Technocratic Exercise

There is a persistent fantasy in policy circles — not just in South Africa, but globally — that economic reform is fundamentally a technical problem. Identify the right policies, design the right incentives, write the right legislation, and growth follows. This fantasy is appealing because it suggests that if we could just get the experts in the room and keep the politicians out, everything would work.

It is also completely wrong.

Every economic policy is a distributional choice. A decision to reduce tariffs on imported textiles benefits consumers (who pay lower prices) and retailers (who earn higher margins) while harming domestic textile manufacturers (who face cheaper competition) and their workers (who face job losses). A decision to reform electricity generation benefits independent power producers and their investors while threatening Eskom's institutional power and the jobs of workers in coal-fired power stations. A decision to open up telecommunications spectrum benefits new mobile operators and consumers while threatening the market position of incumbents who benefited from the previous regulatory arrangement.

You cannot avoid creating losers. The question is never whether a policy has distributional consequences — it always does. The question is whether those consequences are managed, whether the losers are compensated or given transition support, and whether the beneficiaries of reform are politically organised enough to sustain the policy through the inevitable resistance.

Daron Acemoglu and James Robinson, in their landmark work on why nations fail, make the point with characteristic directness: economic institutions that promote growth are threatening to political elites who benefit from extractive arrangements. The reason poor countries stay poor is not usually that their leaders do not know what good economic policy looks like. It is that good economic policy would redistribute power and resources away from the people who currently control the state. The problem is not ignorance but incentives.

South Africa illustrates this dynamic with painful clarity. The National Development Plan, published in 2012, is a sophisticated and largely sound economic strategy document. It was adopted by Cabinet. It was endorsed across the political spectrum. Its diagnosis of South Africa's economic challenges — unemployment, inequality, low growth — is accurate. Its prescriptions — infrastructure investment, education reform, regulatory simplification, competition policy — are broadly correct. And in the fourteen years since its publication, the vast majority of its economic recommendations have not been implemented. Not because they were technically flawed, but because implementation would have required confronting entrenched interests — in the public service, in state-owned enterprises, in organised labour, in politically connected business — that benefit from the existing arrangement.

The political economy question is not a footnote to economic strategy. It is the strategy. Everything else is a document.

## Mapping the Winners and Losers

To understand why reform stalls, you need to map the specific winners and losers of each major policy area. Abstract discussions about "vested interests" are not useful. What is useful is identifying, with precision, which organisations, industries, and constituencies stand to gain or lose from each proposed reform — and then assessing whether the winners are more politically powerful than the losers.

Let us work through the four major reform areas covered in this series.

### Resource Optimisation: Moving Up the Value Chain

The resource optimisation strategy outlined in [Resource Optimisation](/articles/eg-2-1-resource-optimisation) proposes that South Africa systematically move up the value chain in minerals, agriculture, and energy — processing raw materials domestically rather than exporting them for others to process.

The winners of this strategy are substantial. Downstream processing industries — catalytic converter manufacturers, fuel cell component makers, ferrochrome smelters, food processors — gain new production opportunities and employment. Agricultural value chains gain from investment in cold storage, processing facilities, and logistics infrastructure. Workers gain from the creation of manufacturing and processing jobs that pay more than primary extraction jobs. Government gains from higher tax revenues on value-added products compared to raw material exports. Local communities near processing facilities gain from economic multiplier effects.

The losers are equally identifiable, and they are politically powerful. Mining houses that currently operate an extraction-and-export model face higher costs if they are required to process minerals domestically. The current model is simple and profitable: dig it up, put it on a ship, sell it to a smelter in China or India. Beneficiation requirements add complexity, capital expenditure, and regulatory risk. International trading companies — the Glencores and Trafiguras of the world — profit from buying raw materials cheaply in producer countries and selling processed products at higher prices in consumer countries. They have no interest in South Africa capturing more of the value chain. Foreign governments, particularly China, which has built enormous processing capacity for African minerals, have a strategic interest in maintaining the flow of raw materials.

The political management challenge is acute because the losers are concentrated and well-organised while the winners are diffuse and not yet organised. A mining company that faces beneficiation requirements can hire lobbyists, fund political campaigns, threaten to withdraw investment, and engage in regulatory arbitrage (shifting operations to jurisdictions with fewer requirements). The potential workers in a downstream processing plant that does not yet exist cannot lobby for anything — they do not yet know they are potential beneficiaries.

This asymmetry between concentrated losers and diffuse winners is the central challenge of political economy, first identified by Mancur Olson in his work on collective action. Small groups with large individual stakes in a policy outcome will always organise more effectively than large groups with small individual stakes. A mining company that stands to lose R500 million from beneficiation requirements will spend R50 million on lobbying to prevent them. A million consumers who would each benefit by R50 from lower processed goods prices will not organise at all.

The successful political management of resource optimisation requires several strategies. First, phase in beneficiation requirements over time, giving mining companies predictability and adjustment periods. Chile did not impose copper processing requirements overnight — it phased them in over a decade, allowing the private sector to plan and invest. Second, use tax incentives rather than mandates where possible. A tax credit for domestic processing investment is less threatening than a legal requirement to process a minimum percentage domestically, even if the economic effect is similar, because incentives preserve the perception of choice. Third, build the winner coalition by investing in downstream industries before imposing upstream requirements — create the processing capacity and the jobs first, so that the beneficiaries of reform exist and can advocate for its continuation. Fourth, use the employment narrative relentlessly. In a country with thirty-three per cent unemployment, the argument that beneficiation creates more jobs per unit of mineral extracted than raw export is politically potent — but only if the government can point to actual jobs in actual processing facilities, not projections in strategy documents.

:::expert[Mzukisi Qobo on Mining Reform]
Mzukisi Qobo, professor of international business and strategy at the Wits Business School and former head of the Centre for the Study of Governance Innovation at the University of Pretoria, has argued that South Africa's mineral beneficiation debate has been trapped between two unproductive poles: ideological insistence on processing everything domestically (which ignores comparative advantage) and corporate resistance to any processing requirements (which preserves the colonial extraction model). Qobo's research on South Africa's political economy of trade and industrial policy emphasises that successful resource-based industrialisation requires what he calls "strategic pragmatism" — selecting beneficiation opportunities based on rigorous economic analysis rather than political symbolism, while building genuine state capacity to implement industrial policy rather than relying on regulatory mandates that the state lacks the capability to enforce.
:::

### Infrastructure Reform: The SOE Question

Infrastructure reform is where the political economy challenge is most explosive, because it directly confronts the patronage networks that have developed within state-owned enterprises over the past two decades.

The winners of infrastructure reform are the majority of South Africans. Citizens benefit from reliable electricity, functioning public transport, efficient ports, clean water, and broadband connectivity. Private investors benefit from opportunities to participate in infrastructure delivery through PPPs and competitive procurement. Construction firms benefit from a larger pipeline of bankable projects. The economy as a whole benefits from reduced logistics costs, improved competitiveness, and the multiplier effects of infrastructure investment.

The losers, however, are embedded within the state itself. SOE executives and board members who have used their positions for patronage and self-enrichment lose their power base if SOEs are reformed, restructured, or subjected to genuine competition. Politically connected contractors who have benefited from directed procurement — the no-bid contracts, the inflated prices, the scope creep that turns a R2 billion project into a R8 billion project — lose their privileged access if procurement becomes genuinely competitive. Unions representing SOE workers resist restructuring because restructuring typically means headcount reductions, performance accountability, and the end of arrangements that protect unproductive employment.

The Renewable Energy Independent Power Producer Procurement Programme — the REIPPPP — demonstrates both what is possible and what the political economy constraints look like. The REIPPPP used competitive procurement to bring private investment into electricity generation. It attracted over R200 billion in private investment. It delivered new generation capacity on time and on budget. It created jobs in construction and operations. And it demonstrated that when you design procurement properly — transparent bidding, clear evaluation criteria, independent adjudication — the private sector will invest at scale.

But the REIPPPP also illustrates the political resistance that successful reform provokes. Between 2016 and 2018, the signing of new REIPPPP power purchase agreements was blocked. The stated reasons varied, but the underlying dynamic was that successful independent power production threatened Eskom's monopoly position and the political interests embedded within it. Every megawatt of renewable energy that came online was a megawatt that Eskom did not generate, which was revenue that Eskom did not earn, which was procurement that Eskom-connected contractors did not supply, which was patronage that could not be distributed.

The political management of infrastructure reform requires a combination of ring-fencing, competitive procurement expansion, and managed transition. Ring-fencing means constitutionally or legislatively protecting infrastructure budgets from diversion — making it harder for political actors to redirect infrastructure spending to politically favoured but economically unproductive uses. The REIPPPP model should be expanded to water, transport, and digital infrastructure — not because the private sector should own public infrastructure, but because competitive procurement disciplines both public and private actors. And managed transition means providing genuine retraining and redeployment programmes for SOE workers who are displaced by restructuring — not as an afterthought, but as a central element of the reform design. If you do not offer displaced workers a credible pathway to alternative employment, they will resist restructuring with everything they have, and in a democracy, they have votes.

### MSME and Competition Reform

The MSME and competition reform agenda outlined in this series is perhaps the most politically tractable of the four reform areas, because it has the broadest base of potential winners. But it is not without its political economy challenges.

The winners of competition reform and MSME support are numerous. Aspiring entrepreneurs who currently face barriers to entry — complex licensing requirements, high compliance costs, market dominance by established firms — benefit from a more open competitive environment. Consumers benefit from lower prices, more choice, and better service quality that competition drives. Workers benefit from the job creation that a thriving MSME sector generates — small businesses account for a significant share of employment creation in every successful middle-income economy.

The losers are established firms that benefit from the current barriers to entry. In South Africa's highly concentrated economy — where a handful of firms dominate banking, telecommunications, retail, food processing, and construction — increased competition threatens profit margins and market share. Politically connected businesses that benefit from preferential procurement and regulatory capture lose their competitive advantage when the playing field is levelled. And bureaucrats who control licensing and regulatory approval processes lose their gatekeeping power — and the rent-seeking opportunities that come with it — when regulations are simplified.

The political management of competition reform is comparatively straightforward because the winner coalition is potentially enormous (every aspiring small business owner, every consumer, every unemployed person who might find work in a new enterprise) while the loser coalition is small (a handful of dominant firms and their political connections). The challenge is that the winners are, once again, diffuse and unorganised, while the losers are concentrated and well-resourced. A dominant supermarket chain that faces new competition from township-based retailers can deploy lawyers, lobbyists, and political connections to delay or dilute competition reforms. A thousand aspiring township retailers who would benefit from those reforms do not have a coordinating mechanism to advocate collectively.

The political economy solution is to phase in competition reforms while simultaneously building the organisational infrastructure of the MSME sector. Business associations, cooperative structures, and entrepreneur networks serve as the organisational vehicles through which diffuse winners can become a politically effective coalition. When small business owners see themselves as members of a collective movement with shared interests and shared political voice, rather than as isolated individual actors, the political balance shifts.

Celebrating MSME success stories is not just good communications — it is political economy management. Every visible, successful small business that can point to how regulatory reform or competition policy helped it grow is a piece of evidence that makes the reform coalition larger and more committed.

### Education Reform: The Hardest Political Economy Problem

Education reform is politically the most difficult of the four areas, and not just in South Africa. In virtually every country, education reform provokes intense political resistance because it directly threatens the interests of organised labour in a sector where unions are particularly strong, particularly well-organised, and particularly effective at political mobilisation.

The winners of education reform are students who receive better quality instruction, employers who can hire workers with relevant skills, and the economy as a whole through improved human capital and productivity. These are enormous gains spread across millions of people.

The losers are more concentrated. Teachers' unions resist accountability mechanisms — standardised testing, performance evaluation, consequences for poor teaching — because these mechanisms create the possibility that some teachers will be found wanting and face consequences. This is not unique to South Africa. The American Federation of Teachers, the National Union of Teachers in the UK, and education unions across Latin America have all resisted accountability-driven reform. The difference in South Africa is that the South African Democratic Teachers' Union (SADTU) is a COSATU affiliate, which gives it direct political influence through the tripartite alliance, and that SADTU's organisational power extends into school governance through its influence over the appointment of principals, district officials, and departmental heads.

Education bureaucrats resist decentralisation because centralised systems concentrate power and resources at the provincial and national levels, where bureaucrats control them. Decentralisation — giving schools more autonomy over budgets, hiring, and curriculum implementation — transfers power from bureaucrats to school-level actors. Private sector training providers who currently receive government contracts for skills development resist reforms that might redirect funding toward TVET colleges or university-based training.

The political management of education reform requires a fundamentally different approach from the other three reform areas. You cannot simply compensate the losers, because the losers are the people who are supposed to implement the reform. If teachers' unions oppose accountability reforms, and teachers are the ones who must implement better teaching practices, you have a problem that compensation alone cannot solve.

The successful approaches have combined investment with accountability. When you invest heavily in teacher development — better training, better materials, better working conditions, better pay — alongside accountability mechanisms, you change the political calculus. Teachers who see reform as "the government is coming to punish us" will resist. Teachers who see reform as "the government is investing in us and expects results" are more likely to cooperate. Singapore's education reforms, widely regarded as among the most successful in the world, followed precisely this model: dramatically increased investment in teacher quality (higher salaries, rigorous selection, continuous professional development) combined with high expectations for student outcomes.

Involving unions in reform design — not as a pro forma consultation but as genuine co-designers of the reform — also shifts the dynamic. When SADTU leaders can tell their members "we shaped this reform, and it protects our interests while improving education quality," the political resistance diminishes. When reform is imposed from above without union involvement, resistance is almost guaranteed.

:::expert[Anthoni van Nieuwkerk on Coalition Building]
Anthoni van Nieuwkerk, professor of governance at the Wits School of Governance and senior research fellow at the university's Institute for Pan-African Thought and Conversation, has written extensively on the challenges of policy implementation in the South African state. His research on the gap between policy design and execution highlights a pattern he describes as "consensus without commitment" — the tendency for South African stakeholders to agree on broad strategic direction while resisting the specific implementation steps that would redistribute power and resources. Van Nieuwkerk argues that South Africa's institutional architecture, particularly NEDLAC, was designed for consensus-building in a negotiation culture, but that this consensus orientation becomes a weakness when the required reforms are inherently redistributive and cannot satisfy all parties simultaneously. The challenge, he suggests, is building coalitions willing to accept partial losses in exchange for broader gains — a capability that South Africa's political system has not yet developed.
:::

## The Social Compact: Learning from Others

The concept of a social compact is straightforward: it is a negotiated agreement among government, business, labour, and civil society on the terms of economic transition. It says, in effect: we agree that the economy needs to change, we agree on the broad direction of change, we agree on how the costs and benefits will be distributed, and we commit to supporting the reform process even when it imposes costs on our own constituency.

Social compacts have been the vehicle through which several countries managed difficult economic transitions without either authoritarian coercion or political paralysis. Three cases are particularly instructive for South Africa.

### South Korea's Crisis Compact (1997-1998)

When the Asian financial crisis hit South Korea in late 1997, the economy faced a catastrophic contraction. GDP fell by nearly six per cent in 1998. The won lost half its value. Dozens of major corporations faced bankruptcy. Unemployment tripled. The crisis required wrenching structural reforms — the dismantling of the chaebol (conglomerate) system's most destructive features, the opening of capital markets to foreign investment, the restructuring of the banking sector, and significant labour market reforms.

President Kim Dae-jung, who took office in February 1998, established the Tripartite Commission — bringing together government, business (represented by the Federation of Korean Industries and the Korea Employers' Federation), and labour (represented by the Korean Confederation of Trade Unions and the Federation of Korean Trade Unions). Within weeks, the Commission produced a social compact. Labour accepted greater labour market flexibility, including the possibility of layoffs for economic reasons (previously prohibited). Business accepted stronger workers' rights, including the legalisation of unions' political activities and the establishment of an unemployment insurance system. Government committed to expanding the social safety net, including unemployment benefits and retraining programmes.

The compact was not painless. Union members protested. Some chaebol owners resisted restructuring. The compact partially broke down when the KCTU withdrew in February 1998 after disagreements over implementation. But the framework held sufficiently to enable the reforms that powered South Korea's recovery. Within two years, the economy was growing again. Within five years, GDP per capita had surpassed pre-crisis levels.

The lesson for South Africa is not that a social compact eliminates conflict — it does not. The lesson is that a social compact creates a framework within which conflict can be managed productively. Without the compact, South Korea would have faced either authoritarian imposition of reform (which was politically unacceptable in a newly democratised country) or paralysis (which would have deepened the crisis). The compact provided a third path: negotiated reform with compensatory mechanisms for those who bore the costs.

### Ireland's Social Partnership (1987-2008)

Ireland's transformation from one of Western Europe's poorest countries to the "Celtic Tiger" is often attributed to low corporate tax rates and EU structural funds. Both mattered. But the institutional mechanism that made the transformation possible was the social partnership model — a series of negotiated agreements between government, employers, trade unions, farming organisations, and community/voluntary groups that ran from 1987 to 2008.

The first agreement, the Programme for National Recovery (1987), was negotiated at a time of economic crisis — high unemployment, high debt, emigration. The agreement traded wage moderation (unions accepted below-inflation pay increases) for tax reductions (government committed to cutting income tax rates) and social spending commitments (increased investment in health, education, and social welfare). Subsequent agreements — the Programme for Economic and Social Progress (1991), the Programme for Competitiveness and Work (1994), Partnership 2000 (1997), and others — followed the same structure: negotiated trade-offs among constituencies, with each party accepting costs in one area in exchange for gains in another.

The model worked because it created predictability. Businesses could plan investment knowing that wage costs would follow a negotiated trajectory rather than being subject to disruptive industrial action. Unions could accept wage moderation knowing that their members would benefit from tax reductions and social spending. Government could pursue fiscal consolidation knowing that it had social partners' buy-in for the necessary restraint.

Ireland's model eventually failed — the 2008 financial crisis exposed the extent to which the Celtic Tiger had been inflated by a property bubble, and the social partnership model did not survive the austerity that followed. But the two decades of partnership-facilitated growth transformed Ireland from a country with a GDP per capita below the EU average to one of the wealthiest nations in Europe. The lesson is not that social partnership is a permanent solution but that it can serve as a transitional framework for managing difficult economic restructuring.

### South Africa's NEDLAC: Potential and Performance

South Africa already has an institutional framework for social compact negotiation: the National Economic Development and Labour Council, established by the NEDLAC Act of 1994. NEDLAC brings together organised business (represented by Business Unity South Africa), organised labour (represented by COSATU, FEDUSA, and NACTU), community organisations, and government. It has four chambers — trade and industry, labour market, development, and public finance and monetary policy — that are supposed to serve as forums for negotiating economic policy.

On paper, NEDLAC is precisely the institution that South Africa needs for managing the political economy of reform. In practice, its track record is mixed.

NEDLAC has produced some notable successes. The negotiations that led to the Labour Relations Act of 1995 and the Basic Conditions of Employment Act of 1997 were substantive processes that produced legislation with genuine buy-in from business and labour. The Growth and Development Summit of 2003 produced commitments on job creation, investment, and skills development. More recently, NEDLAC served as the platform for negotiating emergency responses during the COVID-19 pandemic, including the Temporary Employer-Employee Relief Scheme (TERS).

But NEDLAC has also been characterised by what might be called agreement without implementation. The commitments made at NEDLAC summits and in NEDLAC chamber negotiations frequently do not translate into policy action. The reasons are structural. NEDLAC can produce agreements, but it has no enforcement mechanism. If government commits to regulatory reform and then does not follow through, there is no consequence. If business commits to investment and then does not invest, there is no penalty. If labour commits to flexibility and then resists it at the sectoral level, there is no sanction. The social compact is only as strong as the political will to implement it.

There is a deeper problem. NEDLAC's constituency structure reflects the economy of 1994, not the economy of today. Organised labour represents a shrinking proportion of the workforce — union density has declined from over thirty per cent in the mid-1990s to under twenty-five per cent today. The unemployed — who number over eight million on the expanded definition — are not represented at NEDLAC at all. The informal sector, which employs millions, has no seat at the table. Small businesses are nominally represented through BUSA, but BUSA's membership and leadership are dominated by large corporations. The community constituency, which was supposed to represent broader civil society, has been the weakest of the four and has struggled to articulate a coherent economic agenda.

If NEDLAC is to serve as the vehicle for a genuine social compact around economic reform, it needs to be reformed itself. The representivity of its constituencies needs to reflect the actual structure of the economy. The unemployed and the informal sector need meaningful representation. Implementation mechanisms need teeth. And the political leadership needs to treat NEDLAC not as a consultation box to be ticked but as the forum where the difficult distributional trade-offs of reform are genuinely negotiated.

## International Positioning and Trade Diplomacy

Economic reform does not happen in a vacuum. South Africa's domestic economic strategy is constrained and enabled by its international positioning — its trade agreements, its bilateral investment treaties, its membership in multilateral institutions, and its diplomatic relationships.

Trade diplomacy matters for domestic reform for a simple reason: the viability of domestic industries depends on their access to export markets and their exposure to import competition. A beneficiation strategy that produces catalytic converters for export requires trade agreements that give those converters access to the European and North American automotive markets without prohibitive tariffs. An MSME sector that sells processed food products needs phytosanitary standards alignment with target markets. A digital services sector needs data governance frameworks that are compatible with major trading partners' regulations.

South Africa's trade positioning is defined by several overlapping frameworks.

The African Continental Free Trade Area, which entered its operational phase in 2021, is potentially the most transformative trade agreement for South Africa's growth strategy. The AfCFTA creates a single market of 1.3 billion people with a combined GDP of over $3 trillion. For South Africa, which already has the most diversified manufacturing sector on the continent, the AfCFTA represents an opportunity to export manufactured and processed goods to African markets at preferential tariff rates. The beneficiation strategy becomes significantly more viable when you can sell processed minerals, manufactured components, and value-added agricultural products across a continental market without trade barriers.

But the AfCFTA also illustrates the political economy challenge of trade diplomacy. South Africa's manufacturing sector benefits from preferential access to African markets. But African trading partners benefit from access to the South African market, which means increased competition for South African firms in their home market. Nigerian cement producers, Kenyan tea processors, and Egyptian textile manufacturers gain access to South African consumers. The political management of the AfCFTA within South Africa requires demonstrating that the gains from expanded export markets outweigh the losses from increased import competition — and providing adjustment support for domestic industries that face new competitive pressure.

South Africa's relationship with BRICS — now expanded to include several new members — provides diplomatic leverage but also creates trade policy tensions. China is simultaneously South Africa's largest trading partner, its most significant source of manufactured imports, and the destination for much of its raw mineral exports. The trade relationship with China embodies the very dynamic that resource optimisation seeks to change: South Africa exports raw materials and imports manufactured goods. Reforming this dynamic — processing more minerals domestically before export — inevitably creates friction with Chinese economic interests. Managing that friction requires diplomatic sophistication and a willingness to accept some bilateral tension in pursuit of long-term domestic economic restructuring.

The European Union remains South Africa's most significant trading partner for manufactured and processed goods, and the Economic Partnership Agreement (EPA) between the EU and the Southern African Customs Union provides preferential market access. The EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism — which imposes tariffs on carbon-intensive imports — creates both a threat (South Africa's energy-intensive manufacturing faces higher costs when exporting to Europe) and an opportunity (incentivising the transition to renewable energy makes South African exports more competitive under CBAM rules).

The World Trade Organisation constrains certain domestic policy options. Beneficiation mandates that function as de facto export restrictions may face WTO challenges. Local content requirements in procurement must be designed carefully to comply with WTO rules. Industrial subsidies must navigate the WTO's Agreement on Subsidies and Countervailing Measures. This does not mean that industrial policy is impossible — every successful industrialising country has found ways to pursue strategic economic objectives within WTO rules — but it means that policy design must be legally sophisticated enough to achieve economic objectives without triggering trade disputes that South Africa would lose.

The political economy of trade diplomacy intersects with domestic reform in a way that most policy discussions ignore. When a mining company argues against beneficiation requirements, it often cites international competitiveness concerns — "if we have to process minerals here, we will lose market share to producers in Australia or Brazil who face no such requirements." This argument carries weight because it has an element of truth. But it also serves the company's financial interest in maintaining the simpler, cheaper export model. The role of trade diplomacy is to create the international conditions — preferential market access for processed goods, protection against predatory pricing by competitors, bilateral investment frameworks that encourage processing investment — that neutralise the competitiveness argument and make domestic processing genuinely viable.

:::expert[Mills Soko on Trade and Industrial Strategy]
Mills Soko, professor at the UCT Graduate School of Business and former director of the UCT Nelson Mandela School of Public Governance, has published extensively on the intersection of trade policy and industrial strategy in South Africa. Soko's research highlights what he terms the "coordination deficit" in South Africa's economic diplomacy — the disconnect between the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition's industrial policy objectives, the Department of International Relations and Cooperation's diplomatic priorities, and the South African Revenue Service's trade facilitation functions. He argues that successful trade diplomacy requires that these functions operate as an integrated system rather than as bureaucratic silos with competing agendas. South Korea's trade diplomacy success, Soko notes, was built on institutional coordination mechanisms — particularly the Economic Planning Board and its successors — that ensured trade agreements served industrial strategy objectives rather than operating independently of them.
:::

## Parliamentary Accountability: The Missing Link

There is one more political institution that should be central to the political economy of reform but has largely failed to play its role: Parliament.

As described in [The Architecture of the State](/articles/1-1-architecture-of-the-state), South Africa's Parliament is structured with portfolio committees that are supposed to exercise oversight over executive departments. The Portfolio Committee on Trade, Industry and Competition should scrutinise industrial policy implementation. The Portfolio Committee on Mineral Resources and Energy should hold the minister accountable for beneficiation strategy. The Portfolio Committee on Public Enterprises should oversee SOE reform. The Standing Committee on Finance and the Standing Committee on Appropriations should ensure that budgets align with strategic priorities.

The mechanism exists. The Budgetary Review and Recommendation Reports — BRRRs — are the formal instrument through which portfolio committees assess departmental performance against strategic plans and budget allocations. Each committee is supposed to produce a BRRR annually, evaluating whether the department has spent its budget effectively, achieved its targets, and delivered value for money.

In practice, parliamentary oversight of economic strategy implementation is weak for several reasons. First, many committee members lack the technical expertise to interrogate complex economic policy effectively. Understanding the economics of mineral beneficiation, the financial structure of PPPs, or the competitive dynamics of telecommunications regulation requires substantive policy knowledge that many parliamentarians do not have. This is not a criticism of individual members — it reflects the reality that parliamentary careers in South Africa are typically built on political organising rather than policy expertise.

Second, the party-list proportional representation system creates incentives that work against effective oversight. Members of Parliament owe their positions to their party, not to a geographic constituency. A committee member who aggressively interrogates a minister from their own party risks their position on the party list at the next election. The incentive structure favours party loyalty over executive accountability. This dynamic, which you can explore further in [From Rainbow Nation to State Capture](/articles/ss-1-2-from-rainbow-nation-to-state-capture), has been one of the most significant institutional weaknesses in South Africa's democratic system.

Third, committees lack independent research capacity. The Parliamentary Research Unit provides support, but its capacity is limited relative to the complexity of the policy areas it covers. By contrast, the United States Congress has the Congressional Budget Office, the Congressional Research Service, and the Government Accountability Office — substantial independent research institutions that give legislators the analytical firepower to challenge executive claims. South Africa's Parliament has nothing comparable.

The consequence is that economic reform lacks an institutional accountability mechanism. The executive can announce strategies, set targets, and allocate budgets — and then fail to deliver — without facing meaningful parliamentary scrutiny. The NDP's implementation failures have never been the subject of a comprehensive parliamentary investigation. NEDLAC's non-implementation of negotiated agreements has never been systematically reviewed by a committee. The gap between infrastructure commitments and infrastructure delivery — documented in [Infrastructure Planning vs Reality](/articles/eg-4-3-infrastructure-planning-vs-reality) — has been lamented by parliamentarians but not systematically addressed through the oversight mechanisms at their disposal.

Strengthening parliamentary accountability is not a technocratic reform — it is a political economy reform. Effective parliamentary oversight creates a political cost for non-implementation. When a minister knows that the portfolio committee will conduct a rigorous public review of whether beneficiation targets were met, whether infrastructure was delivered on schedule, and whether MSME support programmes produced results, the political incentive to actually implement changes. Without that accountability, strategy documents remain documents — aspirational statements that impose no political cost when they are ignored.

## Why Reforms Fail: The Pattern

If you step back from the specific reform areas and look at the pattern across South Africa's post-1994 economic policy history, a consistent dynamic emerges. It has four stages.

Stage one: crisis recognition. A problem becomes too visible to ignore — load-shedding, unemployment, fiscal deterioration, logistics collapse. Political leaders acknowledge the problem and commit to addressing it.

Stage two: strategy development. Government commissions or produces a strategy document. The document is typically well-researched, technically sound, and aligned with international best practice. It identifies the right problems and proposes sensible solutions. Stakeholders are consulted. The strategy is adopted, often with fanfare.

Stage three: implementation resistance. The specific implementation steps required by the strategy begin to threaten identifiable interests. Mining companies push back against beneficiation requirements. SOE executives resist restructuring. Unions oppose accountability mechanisms. Politically connected firms lobby against competition reforms. Bureaucrats resist regulatory simplification that reduces their discretionary power.

Stage four: quiet abandonment. The strategy is not formally repudiated — it remains official policy. But implementation slows, stalls, and eventually stops. Budget allocations are diverted. Targets are revised downward or quietly dropped. A new crisis emerges, a new strategy is commissioned, and the cycle begins again.

This pattern is not inevitable. It is the product of specific political economy dynamics that can be managed — as the comparator countries demonstrate. Breaking the cycle requires three things that South Africa has consistently lacked.

First, political leadership willing to spend political capital on reform. Reform creates enemies. Leaders who prioritise being liked over being effective will always retreat when the resistance materialises. The leaders who drove successful economic transitions — Lee Kuan Yew in Singapore, Park Chung-hee and later Kim Dae-jung in South Korea, Leszek Balcerowicz in Poland, Lula da Silva in Brazil — all accepted significant political costs in pursuit of economic restructuring. They spent political capital rather than hoarding it.

Second, institutional mechanisms for compensating losers. When you can credibly promise displaced workers retraining and re-employment, resistance diminishes. When you can offer mining companies a phased transition with clear timelines rather than abrupt mandates, compliance increases. When you can show SOE workers that restructuring will produce a more sustainable institution with better long-term employment prospects, cooperation becomes possible. Compensation is not weakness — it is strategic investment in the political viability of reform.

Third, reform coalitions that are broader and more powerful than resistance coalitions. This requires active political work — identifying potential beneficiaries, organising them, giving them voice, and connecting their interests to the reform agenda. It also requires sequencing reforms so that early wins create constituencies that support further reform. The REIPPPP created an independent power producer industry that now advocates for further energy sector reform. Each successful reform creates new allies for the next reform — if you design the sequencing deliberately.

## The Compensation Question

One of the most underappreciated aspects of successful economic reform is the role of explicit compensation for losers. There is a tendency in South African policy discourse to treat reforms as zero-sum: either you support the reform or you oppose it, and if you oppose it, you are an obstacle to progress. This framing makes reform harder, not easier.

Consider how Chile managed the transition from state-dominated copper mining to a mixed public-private model with progressive beneficiation. The state copper company, Codelco, remained in public ownership but was subjected to commercial discipline and performance accountability. Private mining companies were allowed to operate under a clear regulatory framework. A portion of copper revenues was directed to the Economic and Social Stabilisation Fund — Chile's sovereign wealth fund — which provided a visible mechanism for sharing resource wealth broadly. Workers displaced by modernisation received retraining support and severance packages. Communities affected by mining transitions received targeted investment in alternative economic activities.

The cost of this compensation was real. Chile spent significant fiscal resources on transition support. But the return was also real: a reformed mining sector that is more productive, more sustainable, and more broadly beneficial than the pre-reform arrangement. And critically, the existence of compensation mechanisms reduced political resistance to the point where reform could proceed.

South Africa has not, as a rule, accompanied economic reforms with credible compensation mechanisms. When the automotive industry was restructured under the Motor Industry Development Programme and its successors, adjustment support for displaced workers was limited. When trade liberalisation in the 1990s exposed domestic manufacturers to international competition, the transition support was inadequate relative to the scale of displacement. When Eskom's crisis forced load-shedding on the economy, there was no compensation mechanism for businesses that lost production or workers who lost shifts.

The absence of compensation creates a vicious cycle. Reforms impose concentrated costs on identifiable groups. Those groups organise to resist. The resistance blocks or dilutes the reform. The economy stagnates. The next reform attempt faces even greater resistance because previous reforms failed to deliver promised benefits while imposing real costs.

Breaking this cycle requires treating compensation as a core component of reform design, not an afterthought. The fiscal cost of compensating losers is almost always smaller than the economic cost of failed reform. A retraining programme for displaced SOE workers costs millions. A failed SOE that continues to haemorrhage public funds costs billions. The arithmetic is straightforward — but it requires political leaders who are willing to allocate resources to compensation up front, before the political crisis that failed reform inevitably produces.

## From Analysis to Action: What You Can Do

If you have read this far, you might be thinking: this is all very interesting, but what can an ordinary citizen actually do about the political economy of reform? The answer is more than you might expect.

Political economy dynamics are not laws of physics. They are patterns of human behaviour driven by interests and incentives. And those patterns can be shifted by citizens who understand them and act accordingly.

First, you can use the stakeholder map framework in the next section to analyse any proposed economic policy. When a politician announces a new economic initiative, do not ask whether it sounds good in the abstract. Ask who benefits, who loses, whether the losers are being compensated, and whether the reform coalition is strong enough to sustain the policy against resistance. This analysis will tell you more about whether the policy will actually be implemented than any technical assessment of its economic merits.

Second, you can participate in the reform coalition. If you are a small business owner who would benefit from competition reform, join a business association that advocates for it. If you are a consumer who would benefit from lower prices, support consumer organisations that push for market openness. If you are a worker who would benefit from better skills training, engage with organisations that advocate for education reform. Diffuse winners become a political force only when they organise.

Third, you can hold your political representatives accountable for implementation. Attend parliamentary committee meetings — they are open to the public. Read the BRRRs that portfolio committees produce. Write to your representatives (even under a proportional representation system, parties respond to organised constituent pressure). Ask specific questions: has the beneficiation target been met? Has the infrastructure budget been spent? Has the MSME support programme produced results? Accountability requires citizens who demand it.

Fourth, you can support the institutions that produce the evidence base for reform. Organisations like the Public Affairs Research Institute, Trade and Industrial Policy Strategies, the Human Sciences Research Council, the Centre for Development and Enterprise, and university-based research centres produce the analysis that informs reform design and monitors reform implementation. Their work is publicly available. Reading it, sharing it, and citing it in public discourse raises the quality of democratic debate and makes it harder for vested interests to distort the facts.

The transition from economic strategy to economic reality — the subject of the next article in this series, [From Strategy to Reality](/articles/eg-5-2-from-strategy-to-reality) — is fundamentally a political economy challenge. The strategies exist. The technical knowledge exists. What has been missing is the political management of reform — the deliberate, disciplined construction of reform coalitions, the compensation of losers, the strengthening of accountability institutions, and the sustained political commitment to implementation in the face of resistance.

South Africa's economic future depends not on producing better strategy documents. It depends on building the political conditions under which existing strategies can be implemented. That is the work of political economy management, and it is work that every citizen can contribute to.

## Practical Framework: The Stakeholder Map

This is the tool you take away from this article. For any proposed economic policy — whether it comes from a budget speech, a party manifesto, a NEDLAC agreement, or a departmental strategic plan — apply these five questions. Write your answers down. The discipline of writing forces precision that thinking alone does not.

**Question 1: Who are the direct beneficiaries?**

Identify the specific organisations, industries, worker categories, and consumer groups that directly gain from the policy. Be concrete. "The economy" is not an answer. "Workers in downstream platinum processing facilities in Limpopo and North West" is an answer. "Consumers" is too vague. "Households that currently spend more than fifteen per cent of their income on food, who would benefit from lower prices driven by increased retail competition" is specific enough to be useful. The more precisely you can identify the winners, the better you can assess whether they are politically organised enough to sustain the reform.

**Question 2: Who are the direct losers?**

Identify the specific organisations, industries, worker categories, and political actors that directly lose from the policy. Again, be concrete. "Vested interests" tells you nothing. "The five largest mining houses that currently export unprocessed platinum group metals and would face increased processing costs under mandatory beneficiation requirements" tells you exactly who will resist and roughly how much they will spend on resistance. "SOE board members who would lose patronage authority under a competitive procurement mandate" tells you where institutional resistance will be concentrated.

**Question 3: Who are the indirect beneficiaries and losers (second-order effects)?**

Every policy has ripple effects beyond its direct targets. A beneficiation policy that creates downstream processing jobs also benefits the towns and cities where processing plants are located (through multiplier effects), the educational institutions that train processing workers (through increased demand for their graduates), and the logistics companies that transport processed goods (through new freight volumes). It may also create indirect losers: communities that depend on raw material export logistics may see reduced economic activity if export volumes decline. Mapping these second-order effects gives you a more complete picture of the reform coalition and the resistance coalition.

**Question 4: What compensation or transition support is offered to losers?**

This is the question that most policy proposals fail. A reform proposal that identifies winners and losers but offers nothing to the losers is politically naive. Look for specific, funded, time-bound compensation mechanisms. "Retraining programmes" is not enough — how many workers, what skills, what budget, what timeline, what employment outcomes are targeted? "Transition support" is not enough — what form does it take, how is it funded, who administers it, and what happens if it does not deliver? If the answer to this question is "nothing" or "vague promises," the reform is likely to fail regardless of its technical merit, because uncompensated losers will organise to block it.

**Question 5: Is the reform coalition larger and more politically powerful than the resistance coalition?**

This is the ultimate test. Count the winners and the losers. Assess their organisational capacity, their political connections, their financial resources, and their ability to mobilise. A reform that benefits ten million consumers but threatens five politically connected firms will succeed only if those ten million consumers are organised enough to outweigh the lobbying power of the five firms. If they are not — if the winners are diffuse and passive while the losers are concentrated and active — the reform will fail, regardless of its economic merits.

If the answer to Question 5 is "no" and the answer to Question 4 is "nothing," the reform is destined for the shelf. No amount of technical sophistication, no number of strategy documents, no volume of conference presentations will overcome the basic political arithmetic. The reform coalition must be larger, stronger, and better organised than the resistance coalition. And the resistance coalition must be offered enough compensation to reduce its opposition from existential resistance to manageable complaint.

This is the fundamental insight of political economy: good policy is necessary but not sufficient. Good policy plus good political management is what produces change. South Africa has had no shortage of the former. Its future depends on developing the latter.
