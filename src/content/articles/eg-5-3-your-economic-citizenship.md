## Economic Policy Is Not Something That Happens to You

Economic policy is not something that happens to you. It is something you can understand, evaluate, and influence. Over the course of this series, you have built a comprehensive analytical framework: you know where South Africa's economy stands (Part 1), what a credible growth strategy looks like (Part 2), what institutional enablers are required (Part 3), how to measure municipal performance (Part 4), and why implementation is always a political question (Part 5). This final article converts all of that knowledge into practical tools. You will learn to read the Medium Term Budget Policy Statement and understand whether the government is investing in the infrastructure that Stage 1 of the growth strategy requires. You will learn to use the Municipal Performance Index to evaluate whether your municipality is creating the conditions for economic growth. You will learn to engage with your municipality's Local Economic Development process — not as a passive recipient of consultation, but as an informed participant who can evaluate whether the LED strategy aligns with the frameworks you now understand. And you will learn to evaluate the economic promises that politicians make during election campaigns, using a structured framework that separates credible commitments from rhetorical gestures. This article is the capstone of the Economic Growth pillar — the point where knowledge becomes power.

This is not a metaphor. The South African Constitution vests sovereignty in the people, not in the state. Section 195 requires public administration to be accountable, transparent, and responsive to the needs of the public. Section 152 establishes the objects of local government, which include encouraging community participation in governance. These are not aspirational statements; they are binding constitutional obligations. When you walk into an Integrated Development Plan public participation session with a printed copy of your municipality's audit outcomes and a list of questions about the LED strategy's alignment with identified economic opportunities, you are not being a nuisance. You are exercising the rights that the Constitution specifically created for you. The question is whether you have the tools to exercise those rights effectively. After reading this series, you do.

## The Journey So Far

Before we put tools in your hands, it is worth recalling the journey that brought you here, because the tools only make sense in the context of the analytical framework that supports them.

In Part 1, you learned the baseline. South Africa's GDP per capita of approximately $7,000 places it in the upper-middle-income bracket on paper, but the Economic Complexity Index reveals an economy that is structurally simpler than countries with lower incomes. You learned the historical trajectory — how the post-apartheid growth window of 1994 to 2008 closed, and why GDP per capita has stagnated while comparator countries like Malaysia, Poland, and Chile have surged ahead. You learned that the economy remains stuck in the factor-driven stage of Michael Porter's competitive economy framework, dependent on mineral extraction rather than on efficiency or innovation.

In Part 2, you encountered the growth strategy — a sequenced, stage-gated approach that begins with resource optimisation (getting more value from existing assets), moves through infrastructure foundations (building the platforms that efficiency-driven growth requires), transitions to efficiency-driven competition (competing on productivity, quality, and market access rather than on cheap labour and raw materials), and culminates in innovation-driven growth (competing at the technological frontier). You learned that these stages cannot be skipped, that each one builds the institutional capabilities required by the next, and that South Africa's failure to grow is in large part a failure to execute this sequence.

In Part 3, you learned about the enablers — the institutional architecture that turns strategy into reality. The three engines of growth (human capital, infrastructure, and institutions), the execution machinery of the state, and the monitoring and evaluation systems that are supposed to hold implementers accountable. You learned that South Africa has sophisticated M&E frameworks on paper, but that the gap between measurement and action is where implementation goes to die.

In Part 4, you encountered the Municipal Performance Index — a composite tool for evaluating municipal performance across four pillars: Economic Development, Service Delivery, Governance and Finance, and Infrastructure and Sustainability. You saw how the MPI can be applied to South Africa's eight metropolitan municipalities to generate rankings, identify patterns, and surface the specific capabilities that distinguish high-performing metros from low-performing ones. You learned about the infrastructure gap — the chasm between what infrastructure master plans promise and what municipal capital expenditure actually delivers.

In Part 5, you confronted the political economy of reform — why growth is always a political question, why there are always winners and losers, and why even technically sound strategies can fail if they do not navigate the political landscape. You saw the implementation roadmap — KPIs, dashboards, phase gates, and the lessons from Rwanda's EDPRS model about what disciplined implementation actually looks like.

Now it is your turn.

## Reading the Medium Term Budget Policy Statement

Every October, the Minister of Finance tables the Medium Term Budget Policy Statement in Parliament. The MTBPS is not the budget itself — the full budget arrives in February — but it is in many ways the more important document for understanding the government's actual economic priorities. The February budget is constrained by the fiscal framework that the MTBPS establishes. By October, the strategic choices have already been made. The February budget fills in the details; the MTBPS reveals the direction.

You can access the MTBPS on the National Treasury website (treasury.gov.za) on the day it is tabled. The full document is typically between 80 and 120 pages, but you do not need to read all of it. What you need is a structured approach to extracting the information that matters for evaluating economic policy against the framework you have built over this series.

The MTBPS contains several categories of information, and you should approach each one with specific questions in mind. The first category is the macroeconomic forecast. The Treasury provides projections for GDP growth, inflation (CPI), the current account balance, and the exchange rate over the medium term — typically three years forward. These numbers are not predictions in the scientific sense; they are the assumptions on which the fiscal framework is built. When the Treasury projects GDP growth of 1.8 per cent for the coming year, it is saying: this is what we expect, and we have built our revenue and expenditure plans on this assumption. Your job as a citizen reader is not to second-guess the Treasury's econometric models but to ask a simpler question: is this growth rate consistent with the structural transformation that the growth strategy requires? You know from Part 1 that South Africa needs sustained growth of at least 3 to 5 per cent per annum to make meaningful progress on unemployment and poverty. If the Treasury's own projections show growth below 2 per cent for the medium term, that is the government's own admission that its current policy framework is not generating the growth the country needs.

The second category is the fiscal framework itself — revenue and expenditure. Revenue projections tell you how much the government expects to collect in taxes and other income. Expenditure allocations tell you where it plans to spend. The gap between the two is the budget deficit, which the government finances by borrowing. The accumulated borrowing is the national debt, expressed as a percentage of GDP (the debt-to-GDP ratio). As we discussed in [Following the Money](/articles/2-1-following-the-money), the debt-to-GDP ratio is one of the most important fiscal indicators because it determines how much of the budget must be allocated to debt service payments (interest on existing debt) rather than to productive expenditure. When you read the MTBPS, look for the debt-to-GDP trajectory. Is it stabilising, rising, or declining? If it is rising, what is the Treasury's plan to stabilise it? Every rand spent on debt service is a rand not available for infrastructure, education, or healthcare — the very investments that the growth strategy requires.

The third category, and the one most directly relevant to the growth strategy, is the expenditure breakdown by function. The MTBPS allocates expenditure across broad functional areas: education, health, social protection, economic affairs, defence, general public services, and so forth. Within economic affairs, you can find allocations for infrastructure spending, industrial policy programmes, and economic development initiatives. Your question here is specific: are the allocations consistent with the growth stage that South Africa is in? If the country is in the factor-driven stage and needs to transition to efficiency-driven growth, you should expect to see significant and increasing allocations to infrastructure (transport, energy, water, digital connectivity), education and skills development (particularly technical and vocational training), and institutional capacity building. If instead you see real-terms cuts to infrastructure budgets and growing allocations to debt service and the public sector wage bill, that tells you the fiscal framework is not aligned with the growth strategy — regardless of what the political rhetoric says.

The fourth category is the contingent liabilities and risks section. This is where the Treasury discloses the financial risks that could materialise but are not yet reflected in the main budget numbers. State-owned enterprise bailouts are typically disclosed here. Eskom's debt, Transnet's financial position, SAA's ongoing costs — these appear as contingent liabilities. This section tells you whether the government is being honest about the fiscal risks it faces. A government that projects fiscal consolidation while carrying massive contingent liabilities from SOEs that continue to underperform is, at best, being optimistic. At worst, it is deferring costs that will eventually land on the budget and crowd out the very investments the growth strategy requires.

Here is a practical approach. When the MTBPS drops in October, set aside two hours. Download the document and the accompanying statistical tables (these are usually in Excel format and are far easier to work with than the PDF). Focus on four numbers: the GDP growth projection for the coming year, the debt-to-GDP ratio trajectory over the medium term, the real change in infrastructure spending (nominal change minus inflation), and the contingent liabilities from SOEs. Write these four numbers down. Compare them to the previous year's MTBPS projections. Ask yourself: is the trajectory improving, stable, or deteriorating? Then compare the infrastructure spending allocation to the infrastructure gap analysis from Part 4 of this series. Is the government allocating enough capital expenditure to close the infrastructure backlog at a pace that supports the transition from factor-driven to efficiency-driven growth? If the answer is no, you have identified a concrete disconnect between policy rhetoric and fiscal reality — and you have the evidence to raise it in public forums, write to your MP, or submit comments to the Standing Committee on Finance.

:::expert[International Budget Partnership Open Budget Survey]
The International Budget Partnership's Open Budget Survey — the most comprehensive independent assessment of budget transparency worldwide — has consistently ranked South Africa among the top countries globally. South Africa scored 86 out of 100 on the Open Budget Index in 2021 and 83 in 2023, placing it ahead of most OECD countries and far ahead of other African nations. The survey evaluates the availability and comprehensiveness of eight key budget documents, including the pre-budget statement (the MTBPS in South Africa's case), the executive's budget proposal, the enacted budget, and audit reports. However, the IBP's research also reveals a significant gap between transparency and participation. South Africa scores well on whether budget documents are published, but substantially lower on whether meaningful opportunities exist for citizens to engage with budget processes and whether oversight institutions act on audit findings. The documents are available, but the mechanisms for citizens to use them to hold government accountable remain underdeveloped. Publishing the MTBPS is necessary but not sufficient; what matters is whether citizens read it, understand it, and use it to demand accountability for how public resources are allocated and spent.
:::

The Vulekamali budget portal (vulekamali.gov.za) is an additional resource that the National Treasury provides. It presents budget data in more accessible visual formats, broken down by department, programme, and economic classification. For infrastructure spending specifically, you can track allocations over time, compare what was budgeted to what was actually spent (the expenditure performance data), and identify patterns of underspending — which, as we discussed in the infrastructure planning article, is one of the most persistent failures of the South African state. A department that consistently spends less than 80 per cent of its capital budget is not constrained by a lack of resources; it is constrained by a lack of institutional capacity to plan, procure, and execute projects. Vulekamali makes these patterns visible.

## Using the Municipal Performance Index

The Municipal Performance Index, which you encountered in detail in Part 4, is not an abstract academic exercise. It is a tool designed to be used — by citizens, by civil society organisations, by journalists, and by the municipalities themselves. The MPI evaluates municipal performance across four pillars, each with a specific weight for metropolitan municipalities: Economic Development at 35 per cent, Service Delivery at 25 per cent, Governance and Finance at 25 per cent, and Infrastructure and Sustainability at 15 per cent. These weights reflect the centrality of economic development to municipal mandates, while recognising that service delivery, governance, and infrastructure are preconditions for economic growth.

The data that feeds into the MPI is publicly available, and you can access it yourself. Here is where to find it, pillar by pillar.

For Economic Development, the primary data source is Statistics South Africa's Quarterly Labour Force Survey (QLFS), which provides unemployment rates, labour force participation rates, and employment by sector at the metropolitan and provincial level. The QLFS is published quarterly and is freely available at statssa.gov.za. Census data provides additional demographic and economic indicators. The Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC) publishes data on new business registrations, which serves as a proxy for entrepreneurial activity and business formation rates. Between the QLFS and CIPC data, you can construct a reasonable picture of whether your municipality's economy is creating jobs and attracting new business formation, or whether it is stagnating.

For Service Delivery, the data sources include the Department of Water and Sanitation's Blue Drop (drinking water quality) and Green Drop (wastewater treatment) reports, which grade municipal water and sanitation systems on a scale from zero to one hundred. These reports are published periodically and are available on the DWS website. Stats SA's non-financial census of municipalities provides data on service delivery coverage — the proportion of households with access to piped water, electricity, sanitation, and refuse removal. Together, these sources tell you not just whether services are nominally available (coverage) but whether they are being delivered at an acceptable standard (quality).

For Governance and Finance, the single most important data source is the Auditor-General of South Africa's annual Municipal Finance Management Act (MFMA) audit reports. AGSA audits every municipality in South Africa annually and publishes consolidated reports that include audit outcomes (clean audit, unqualified, qualified, disclaimer, or adverse), findings on financial management, compliance with legislation, and material irregularities. These reports are available at agsa.co.za. The National Treasury's Section 71 quarterly financial reports (available at municipaldata.treasury.gov.za) provide in-year financial data — revenue collection, expenditure, cash position, and debt levels. The Municipal Money portal (municipalmoney.gov.za), which is discussed in more detail in [Municipal Councils](/articles/3-3-municipal-councils), presents this data in accessible visual formats that allow you to compare your municipality's financial performance to national benchmarks and to other municipalities.

For Infrastructure and Sustainability, the data sources include the infrastructure-related indicators from the Treasury's municipal data portal (capital expenditure as a percentage of total expenditure, the ratio of repairs and maintenance spending to asset value), the DWS Blue and Green Drop scores (which also reflect infrastructure condition), and municipal Integrated Development Plans (IDPs), which should contain infrastructure investment plans and asset management strategies.

Now, how do you actually use this data? The MPI methodology assigns scores to each indicator within each pillar, normalises them to a common scale, applies the pillar weights, and aggregates to a composite score. You do not need to replicate the full methodology to use the MPI effectively. What you need is a simplified approach that allows you to assess your municipality's performance relative to its peers.

Start with the Governance and Finance pillar, because it is the most straightforward. Look up your municipality's most recent AGSA audit outcome. A clean audit is the gold standard — it means the financial statements are fairly presented, there are no material findings on compliance with legislation, and there are no material findings on performance reporting. An unqualified audit with findings means the financial statements are fair but there are compliance or performance reporting issues. A qualified audit means the auditor found material misstatements in the financial statements. A disclaimer means the auditor could not obtain sufficient evidence to form an opinion. An adverse opinion means the financial statements are materially misstated. If your municipality received a disclaimer or adverse opinion, its governance and financial management are in serious trouble, and this should be your primary concern before anything else — because without functional governance and financial management, no economic development strategy, no service delivery improvement plan, and no infrastructure programme can succeed.

Next, check the Economic Development indicators. Pull the most recent QLFS data for your metro (Stats SA publishes metro-level breakdowns). What is the official unemployment rate? What is the expanded unemployment rate (which includes discouraged work-seekers)? How has it changed over the past four quarters? Is employment growing or shrinking? In which sectors? Then check the CIPC data on new business registrations in your area. Are more businesses being registered than deregistered? What types of businesses? These numbers will not give you a complete picture of economic development, but they will tell you whether the directional trend is positive or negative.

Then look at Service Delivery. Find your municipality's most recent Blue Drop and Green Drop scores. A Blue Drop score below 50 per cent indicates significant drinking water quality risks. A Green Drop score below 50 per cent indicates wastewater management failures that likely include environmental pollution. These are not abstract governance indicators — they are direct measures of whether the water coming out of your tap is safe to drink and whether your municipality is dumping inadequately treated sewage into rivers and the ocean.

Finally, check Infrastructure and Sustainability. On the Municipal Money portal, look at your municipality's capital expenditure as a percentage of total expenditure. Metros should be spending at least 15 to 20 per cent of their budgets on capital (infrastructure investment). If the figure is below 10 per cent, the municipality is not investing enough in infrastructure to maintain existing assets, let alone build new ones. Also look at the repairs and maintenance ratio — spending on repairs and maintenance as a percentage of the value of property, plant, and equipment. The benchmark is 8 per cent; most South African municipalities spend well below this, which means they are allowing their infrastructure to deteriorate.

:::expert[The Implementation Gap in South African Local Government]
Research on municipal governance — including work by the Public Affairs Research Institute (PARI), the South African Cities Network, National Treasury's City Support Programme, and academics at the University of the Western Cape's School of Government — has consistently highlighted what might be called the implementation gap in South African local government: the distance between what municipalities plan (in their IDPs, in their budgets, in their performance agreements) and what they actually deliver. This research reveals that the gap is not primarily a resource problem. Many municipalities that receive substantial transfers from national government and generate significant own revenue nonetheless fail to convert those resources into infrastructure, services, and economic development outcomes. The binding constraint, as the Auditor-General's annual MFMA reports repeatedly confirm, is institutional capability: the ability to plan coherently, procure effectively, manage projects to completion, and maintain assets once they are built. This finding has direct implications for citizen engagement. When you attend an IDP session and the municipality presents a list of planned projects, the question to ask is not "is this a good project?" but rather "does this municipality have the institutional capability to deliver this project on time, on budget, and to specification?" The audit outcomes, the capital expenditure performance, and the maintenance spending ratios are the evidence base for answering that question.
:::

Once you have assembled this data for your municipality, you have a crude but functional MPI scorecard. Take it to the next IDP public participation session. Take it to your ward committee meeting. Take it to your local councillor's constituency office. Ask specific questions: why has the capital expenditure ratio declined for the third year running? Why did the municipality receive a qualified audit? What is the plan to improve the Blue Drop score from 42 per cent to an acceptable level? These are not hostile questions. They are the questions that the Constitution — and the Municipal Finance Management Act, and the Municipal Systems Act — specifically entitle you to ask. And they are questions that can only be answered with data, not with rhetoric.

## Engaging Local Economic Development

Local Economic Development — LED — is one of the most important and least understood functions of local government in South Africa. The Constitution assigns economic development responsibilities across all three spheres of government, but it is at the local level where economic policy meets the lived reality of communities. Your municipality's LED strategy determines whether industrial land is zoned and serviced, whether small business support programmes are available, whether local procurement policies favour local suppliers, whether investment promotion is active, and whether the regulatory environment enables or inhibits economic activity.

The National Framework for Local Economic Development, published by the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA) in collaboration with the Department of Economic Development, provides the policy foundation for LED in South Africa. It defines LED as "an outcome of the actions of local people and communities to build up economic activity and so generate employment within their locality." This is a deliberately broad definition, and the framework recognises that LED strategies must be tailored to local economic conditions — what works in the City of Johannesburg will not work in a rural district municipality in the Eastern Cape.

The primary institutional mechanism for LED at the municipal level is the Integrated Development Plan. Every municipality is required by the Municipal Systems Act (Act 32 of 2000) to adopt an IDP, which serves as the municipality's five-year strategic plan. The IDP must include a spatial development framework (which determines where different types of economic activity are located), an integrated transport plan, a disaster management plan, and — critically — a local economic development strategy. The IDP is reviewed annually and must be developed through a process of public participation. This is your entry point.

The IDP public participation process typically runs between January and May each year, with the final IDP adopted by council before the start of the new financial year on 1 July. The process usually includes ward-level consultations, public hearings, and opportunities for written submissions. The quality of these processes varies enormously — in some municipalities, public participation is a genuine exercise in community engagement; in others, it is a tick-box compliance exercise designed to satisfy the legal requirement without actually incorporating public input. Your job is to engage substantively regardless of the quality of the process, because your submissions become part of the public record and create accountability trails.

Here is how to engage with your municipality's LED strategy effectively. First, obtain a copy of the current IDP. This is a public document that the municipality is legally required to make available. Most municipalities publish their IDPs on their websites; if yours does not, you can request a copy from the municipal manager's office or the IDP office. Find the LED section. It will typically describe the local economic profile (key sectors, employment patterns, major employers), identify economic development priorities, and list specific projects and programmes.

Second, evaluate the LED strategy against the growth framework you have built over this series. Ask yourself: does this strategy reflect an understanding of the municipality's actual competitive position? Is it aligned with the growth stage that the local economy is in? A municipality whose economy depends primarily on agriculture and mining is in a different growth stage than a metro with a diversified services economy, and the LED strategies should reflect this difference. Does the LED strategy identify specific, measurable targets? "Promote economic growth" is not a strategy; "increase the number of registered businesses in the manufacturing sector by 15 per cent over five years by streamlining business licensing turnaround times from 90 days to 30 days" is a strategy. Does the LED strategy identify the institutional capabilities required for implementation? Does it have a realistic budget allocation? Is there evidence that previous LED strategies were actually implemented?

Third, prepare specific questions and submit them in writing during the IDP public participation process. Written submissions carry more weight than verbal comments at public meetings because they become part of the formal record. Frame your questions around the data you have gathered: "The municipality's capital expenditure ratio has declined from 18 per cent to 12 per cent over the past three years. How does the LED strategy propose to attract investment when infrastructure spending is declining?" Or: "The QLFS shows that unemployment in this metro has increased from 28 per cent to 33 per cent over the past two years. What specific interventions in the LED strategy are designed to reverse this trend, and what evidence supports the belief that they will work?"

LED instruments — the specific tools that municipalities use to promote economic development — include several categories that you should be aware of. Spatial planning and land use management, governed by the Spatial Planning and Land Use Management Act (SPLUMA), determines where businesses can operate, what types of economic activity are permitted in different zones, and how land use applications are processed. If your municipality's zoning regulations make it difficult to start a business — if the process for rezoning land for commercial use takes two years, for example — that is a direct impediment to economic development, and it is something you can raise in the IDP process.

Local procurement policies determine whether the municipality prioritises local suppliers when purchasing goods and services. This is a significant lever for local economic development because municipalities are substantial purchasers — the combined procurement spend of South Africa's municipalities runs into hundreds of billions of rands annually. A municipality that actively designs its procurement processes to create opportunities for local suppliers is using one of its most powerful tools for LED. A municipality that awards the bulk of its contracts to firms from other provinces is not.

Infrastructure investment — which we have already discussed at length — is the most fundamental LED instrument. A municipality that provides well-maintained roads, reliable water and electricity, and functional waste management creates the basic platform on which economic activity depends. A municipality that allows its infrastructure to deteriorate is actively undermining its own economic development prospects, regardless of what its LED strategy document says.

Business support services — small business incubators, mentorship programmes, skills development initiatives, access to finance facilitation — represent another category of LED instruments. The quality and effectiveness of these services varies enormously across municipalities, and many municipal business support programmes exist more on paper than in practice. When evaluating your municipality's LED strategy, look for evidence of actual implementation: how many businesses have been supported, what outcomes have been achieved, and how does the municipality measure the effectiveness of its business support programmes?

## Evaluating Economic Promises: A Six-Question Framework

Election season in South Africa generates an avalanche of economic promises. Every political party promises jobs, growth, and development. The rhetoric is often compelling, the ambition is always grand, and the specifics are almost always absent. Having worked through this entire series, you now possess something that most voters do not: a structured framework for separating credible economic commitments from empty rhetoric.

The Economic Promise Evaluation Framework consists of six questions. Apply them to any economic promise made by any political party or candidate, and you will quickly distinguish between proposals that have a chance of working and proposals that are designed to sound good rather than to be good.

**Question 1: Does this promise align with South Africa's current growth stage?**

You know from Part 2 that South Africa is in the factor-driven stage and needs to transition to efficiency-driven growth before it can realistically pursue innovation-driven growth. A political party that promises to turn South Africa into a technology innovation hub within five years without first addressing the infrastructure deficit, the skills crisis, and the institutional weaknesses that keep the economy stuck in the factor-driven stage is making a promise that contradicts the fundamental logic of economic development. It is not that innovation does not matter — it does, enormously — but that innovation-driven growth requires a foundation of efficiency that South Africa has not yet built. When evaluating economic promises, ask: does this proposal address the binding constraints that are keeping the economy in its current stage, or does it leap over those constraints to a politically appealing destination without explaining how to get there?

**Question 2: What institutional architecture does this promise require — and does it exist?**

Every economic policy requires institutional infrastructure to deliver it. An industrial policy requires a functional industrial finance institution, capable sectoral regulators, efficient trade administration, and a bureaucracy that can design and implement incentive programmes without them being captured by rent-seekers. A small business development programme requires business registration systems that work, regulatory environments that do not crush new entrants, municipal services that are reliable, and courts that enforce contracts. When a politician promises to "create a million jobs through small business development," ask: what institutions need to exist for this to work? Do they exist? If not, what is the plan to build them, and what is the realistic timeline? A promise that requires institutional capabilities that do not exist and would take years to build is not a five-year plan; it is a fantasy with a timeline attached.

**Question 3: What is the track record of similar promises?**

South Africa has been through multiple rounds of economic policy frameworks — GEAR in 1996, ASGISA in 2006, the New Growth Path in 2010, the National Development Plan in 2012, the Economic Reconstruction and Recovery Plan in 2020. Each one contained ambitious targets for growth, employment, and investment. None of them achieved their stated targets. This history does not mean that all economic plans are doomed to fail, but it does mean that you should demand evidence that a new proposal has learned from the failures of its predecessors. What specifically will be different this time? What caused previous similar efforts to fail, and how does this proposal address those causes? A party that proposes a new industrial policy without acknowledging or addressing the implementation failures of previous industrial policies is likely to repeat those failures.

**Question 4: Who benefits and who loses?**

Every economic policy creates winners and losers. Trade liberalisation benefits consumers (through lower prices) and export-oriented firms, but it can devastate firms and workers in import-competing sectors. Labour market reform that makes it easier to hire and fire benefits employers and potentially future workers, but it threatens the job security of currently employed workers and the bargaining power of trade unions. Infrastructure investment benefits the communities where infrastructure is built but is financed by taxpayers across the country. A political party that presents an economic proposal as pure gain — everyone benefits, no one loses — is either not being honest or has not thought through the distributional consequences. When evaluating economic promises, ask: who specifically will benefit? Who specifically might lose? And what provisions, if any, does the proposal include to manage the transition for those who are disadvantaged by it?

**Question 5: Does this promise include measurable milestones and mechanisms for course-correction?**

A credible economic programme specifies not just where it wants to end up but how it will know whether it is on track. It includes intermediate milestones — specific, measurable targets with dates — and it specifies what will happen if those milestones are missed. Will the programme be adjusted? Will resources be reallocated? Will there be independent evaluation? Rwanda's EDPRS model, which we discussed in Article 5.2, includes precisely these features: clear targets, regular review cycles, and willingness to adjust strategy based on evidence. A political party that promises "five million new jobs by 2030" without specifying annual milestones, without identifying which sectors will generate those jobs, and without explaining what happens if the economy is off track after two years is not offering a plan; it is offering a slogan.

**Question 6: Can you track delivery using publicly available data?**

This is the accountability question, and it is the most important one. A credible economic promise must be verifiable — you must be able to check whether the government is delivering on it using data that is publicly available and independently produced. Employment targets can be tracked through the QLFS. Infrastructure spending can be tracked through the ENE and Vulekamali. Municipal performance can be tracked through AGSA reports and Treasury municipal data. Business formation can be tracked through CIPC data. GDP growth can be tracked through Stats SA's quarterly GDP releases. If a promise is framed in terms that cannot be independently verified — "we will transform the economy," "we will create a vibrant business environment," "we will unleash economic potential" — it is not a verifiable commitment. It is marketing copy. Demand specifics, demand timelines, and demand data sources. If the politician cannot tell you how you will know whether they have delivered, they do not expect to be held accountable.

:::expert[The Social Compact Gap in South African Economic Policy]
South African economists and political economy researchers — including those at the Presidential Economic Advisory Council, the Economic Research Southern Africa (ERSA) network, and civil society organisations focused on economic justice — have consistently argued that the gap between economic policy announcements and actual implementation reflects a deeper structural problem: the absence of meaningful social compacting around economic strategy. In countries that have successfully implemented economic transformation programmes — South Korea, Singapore, Ireland — the strategy was not simply announced by government and imposed on the economy. It was negotiated, contested, and ultimately agreed upon by the major social partners: government, business, organised labour, and civil society. South Africa has the institutional architecture for this kind of compacting — NEDLAC is specifically designed for it — but the track record of NEDLAC agreements translating into sustained policy implementation is poor, as documented in NEDLAC's own annual reports and in academic assessments of its effectiveness. The implication for citizens is significant: engagement with economic policy cannot be limited to evaluating promises at election time. It must extend to ongoing participation in the institutions where economic strategy is negotiated, contested, and held accountable — from NEDLAC at the national level to IDP processes at the municipal level.
:::

## Election-Season Engagement: Reading Party Manifestos

General elections in South Africa occur every five years, with the next national and provincial elections following the 2024 cycle. In the months before an election, every political party publishes a manifesto — a document outlining its policy positions and commitments across multiple domains, including the economy. These manifestos are the formal, documented basis on which parties ask for your vote. They deserve more serious scrutiny than they typically receive.

When you read a party manifesto's economic section, apply the six-question framework systematically. But also look for several additional features that distinguish serious economic thinking from rhetorical posturing.

First, look for a diagnostic. A serious manifesto does not begin with solutions; it begins with an honest assessment of the current situation. Does the manifesto acknowledge the specific structural challenges facing the economy — the infrastructure deficit, the skills mismatch, the institutional weaknesses, the SOE crisis? Or does it skip straight to promises without demonstrating that the party understands the problem it claims to be solving? A manifesto that promises to "create jobs" without explaining why the economy is not currently creating jobs is offering treatment without diagnosis.

Second, look for sequencing. Does the manifesto acknowledge that economic transformation is a multi-year, multi-stage process? Does it prioritise, or does it promise everything simultaneously? A party that promises to fix infrastructure, reform education, transform industrial policy, revolutionise digital connectivity, and build a green economy all at once within one electoral term is either not being serious or does not understand the capacity constraints of the South African state. The growth framework you have built over this series teaches you that stages must be sequenced — you cannot build an innovation economy on top of crumbling infrastructure and a skills deficit. A manifesto that demonstrates awareness of sequencing is more credible than one that treats every priority as equally urgent.

Third, look for fiscal honesty. Every promise costs money. Does the manifesto explain how its economic proposals will be funded? Are the cost estimates realistic? Do they add up — or does the total spending implied by all the promises exceed any plausible revenue trajectory? You can check this by comparing the manifesto's implicit spending commitments to the most recent MTBPS fiscal framework. If a party promises R500 billion in new infrastructure spending over five years but the MTBPS shows that the government's total capital budget across all departments is R350 billion over the medium term, where is the additional R150 billion coming from? Higher taxes? More borrowing? Reallocation from other priorities? If the manifesto does not say, the promise is not funded, and an unfunded promise is not a plan.

Fourth, look for institutional realism. Does the manifesto acknowledge the capacity constraints of the institutions that would need to implement its proposals? The South African state's track record on implementation is well documented — not just in this series, but in the annual reports of the Auditor-General, the Department of Planning Monitoring and Evaluation, and every parliamentary portfolio committee that oversees government departments. A manifesto that proposes complex new programmes without addressing the capacity of existing institutions to deliver them is assuming away the single biggest constraint on policy effectiveness in South Africa.

Fifth, look for evidence of learning. Does the manifesto reference what has worked and what has not, both in South Africa and internationally? Does it draw on evidence from comparator countries? Does it acknowledge the failures of previous policy frameworks and explain how the proposed approach differs? A party that has engaged seriously with the evidence base will demonstrate it in the manifesto. A party that has not will rely on assertion and aspiration.

You can also compare manifestos across parties. Line up their economic sections side by side and ask: which party has the most credible diagnostic? Which has the most realistic sequencing? Which is most honest about fiscal constraints? Which demonstrates the most awareness of institutional capacity? This kind of comparative analysis is more useful than simply reading each manifesto in isolation, because it reveals the relative quality of economic thinking across the political spectrum.

## Ongoing Monitoring: Tracking Between Elections

Democratic accountability does not begin and end on election day. Between elections, citizens have multiple formal and informal channels for monitoring government economic performance and escalating concerns when performance falls short.

The most powerful ongoing monitoring tool is the public data infrastructure that the South African state maintains. Statistics South Africa publishes GDP data quarterly, the QLFS quarterly, CPI monthly, and a range of other economic indicators on a regular schedule. National Treasury publishes monthly revenue and expenditure data, the quarterly Section 71 municipal financial reports, and the annual budget documentation. The Auditor-General publishes annual audit reports for national departments, provincial departments, and municipalities. The Department of Water and Sanitation publishes Blue Drop and Green Drop reports. The Reserve Bank publishes monthly financial stability data, balance of payments data, and credit extension data. All of this data is publicly available, free of charge, and accessible online.

You do not need to monitor all of these data sources. Choose the indicators that matter most for your context and track them over time. If you live in a metro, the QLFS employment data and your municipality's AGSA audit outcome are probably the highest-value indicators. If infrastructure is a concern, track your municipality's capital expenditure performance on the Municipal Money portal. If water quality is a concern, track the Blue Drop and Green Drop scores. The key is consistency — checking the same indicators at the same intervals (quarterly or annually) so that you can identify trends rather than reacting to individual data points.

When your monitoring reveals problems — deteriorating audit outcomes, rising unemployment, declining infrastructure spending, falling water quality scores — you have several formal escalation channels available. The first is your ward councillor. Ward councillors are elected to represent your community's interests on the municipal council, and you have the right to raise concerns with them and to expect a response. The second is the IDP public participation process, which gives you an annual opportunity to submit formal comments on municipal planning and budgeting. The third is portfolio committee submissions. Both the National Assembly and provincial legislatures have portfolio committees that oversee government departments. These committees hold public hearings on policy and legislation, and members of the public can submit written representations. The Standing Committee on Finance, which oversees National Treasury, holds hearings on the MTBPS and the annual budget. You can submit written comments drawing on your analysis.

The fourth escalation channel is the Public Protector, who has the constitutional mandate to investigate maladministration in government. If your monitoring reveals evidence that a government department or municipality is failing to carry out its legal obligations — not spending allocated infrastructure budgets, not maintaining water treatment works, not implementing its IDP commitments — you can lodge a complaint with the Public Protector. The fifth is the Auditor-General, who accepts information from the public about potential financial misconduct or irregularities in government. If you identify suspicious patterns in municipal financial data — unexplained expenditure spikes, procurement irregularities visible in tender awards data — you can report them to AGSA.

The sixth, often overlooked, channel is petition rights. Section 17 of the Constitution guarantees the right to present petitions. The National Assembly and provincial legislatures have formal petitions processes. A petition signed by community members, supported by data from public sources, and directed at a specific concern can trigger committee inquiries and formal responses from government departments.

The point of all these channels is this: you are not a spectator in South Africa's economic development. You are a participant. The Constitution created multiple mechanisms for citizen engagement precisely because democratic accountability requires active citizens, not passive voters. The tools you have built over this series — the growth stage framework, the institutional analysis, the MPI methodology, the budget literacy skills — transform you from a citizen who can only react to headlines into one who can engage with evidence.

## The Economic Citizen's Checklist

This final section consolidates everything into a practical checklist — five engagement domains that cover the full cycle of economic citizenship. Treat this as your ongoing reference. Return to it at budget time, at IDP season, at election time, and whenever you read a headline about economic policy and want to assess it with rigour rather than reaction.

**Domain 1: Budget Literacy — Reading the MTBPS and ENE**

When the MTBPS is tabled in October, download it and the statistical annexures from treasury.gov.za. Extract four key numbers: the GDP growth projection, the debt-to-GDP trajectory, the real change in infrastructure spending, and the contingent liabilities (SOE exposure). Compare these to the previous year's numbers to identify trends. Use Vulekamali (vulekamali.gov.za) to explore departmental allocations in more accessible formats. When the Estimates of National Expenditure (ENE) arrive with the February budget, check whether the MTBPS commitments have been honoured — do the detailed allocations match the direction signalled in October? Track expenditure performance over the year using Treasury's monthly and quarterly published data: is the government actually spending what it budgeted, particularly on capital and infrastructure? If underspending exceeds 15 per cent in key economic departments, that is a red flag for implementation failure. When the Standing Committee on Finance holds hearings on the MTBPS, consider submitting written comments — you have the right to do so, and committees take public submissions into their deliberations.

**Domain 2: Municipal Accountability — Using the MPI**

At least once a year, assemble a basic MPI scorecard for your municipality. Pull the latest AGSA audit outcome from agsa.co.za. Check the QLFS for your metro's employment data at statssa.gov.za. Review your municipality's Blue Drop and Green Drop scores for water and sanitation quality. Check the Municipal Money portal (municipalmoney.gov.za) for capital expenditure ratios, repairs and maintenance spending, and revenue collection rates. Compare your municipality's performance across these indicators to other metros and to national benchmarks. Identify the areas of greatest concern — declining audit outcomes, falling employment, deteriorating water quality, declining infrastructure investment — and bring these to your ward committee meeting, your IDP public participation submission, or a direct engagement with your ward councillor. Use specific numbers, not general complaints. "The municipality's Blue Drop score has dropped from 72 to 48 over two years" is a statement that demands a specific response. "The water is bad" can be deflected.

**Domain 3: LED Engagement — Participating in the IDP Process**

Obtain your municipality's current IDP (from the municipal website or the IDP office). Read the LED section. Evaluate it against the growth framework: does it reflect a realistic assessment of the local economy's competitive position? Does it identify specific, measurable targets? Does it include budget allocations? Does it address institutional capability constraints? Prepare written submissions for the IDP public participation process (typically January through May). Attend ward-level IDP consultations, not just to listen but to ask questions grounded in data. Follow up after the IDP is adopted: does the final IDP reflect public input? Are the LED projects and programmes actually being implemented? Request quarterly progress reports from the LED unit — this is public information to which you are entitled. If your municipality has a Local Economic Development Forum or similar stakeholder structure, find out how to participate. These forums are often underutilised by ordinary citizens, which means that the voices of organised business and labour dominate LED strategy in ways that may not reflect the interests of the broader community.

**Domain 4: Election Evaluation — Assessing Economic Promises**

When election season arrives, obtain the manifestos of all major parties. Read the economic sections with the six-question framework in hand. Does the economic diagnosis reflect reality? Does the strategy align with the country's growth stage? Does it identify the institutional architecture required for delivery? Does it learn from the track record of previous similar efforts? Does it acknowledge winners and losers? Does it include measurable milestones? Can you track delivery with public data? Compare manifestos side by side on these dimensions. Share your analysis with friends, family, and community groups. Write to parties with specific questions about their economic proposals. Attend manifesto launch events and candidate debates and ask the questions that the six-question framework generates. Remember that your vote is your most direct instrument of democratic accountability, and the economic section of a manifesto tells you more about a party's seriousness than its campaign slogans do.

**Domain 5: Ongoing Monitoring — Tracking KPIs and Escalating**

Between elections and budget cycles, track three to five key economic indicators relevant to your context on a quarterly or annual basis. At the national level, track GDP growth (Stats SA quarterly release), the unemployment rate (QLFS quarterly), and debt-to-GDP (Treasury monthly data). At the municipal level, track your metro's AGSA audit outcome (annual), capital expenditure performance (Municipal Money portal, quarterly updates), and one service delivery indicator that matters to your community (water quality, electricity reliability, or waste management). When indicators deteriorate, use formal channels: ward councillor engagement, IDP submissions, portfolio committee submissions, Public Protector complaints, AGSA tip-offs, or community petitions. Document your engagements — keep copies of submissions, record responses (or non-responses), and build a paper trail. Accountability is cumulative; a single engagement may not produce results, but a documented pattern of evidence-based engagement, sustained over time, shifts the balance of power between citizens and the state.

:::framework
**The Economic Accountability Cycle**

Use this four-step cycle to exercise your economic citizenship systematically throughout the year, rather than engaging only during elections or crises. Each step builds on the previous one to create sustained, evidence-based pressure for accountable governance.

**Step 1: October — Read the MTBPS (2 hours)**

When the Medium Term Budget Policy Statement is tabled, download it and the statistical annexures from treasury.gov.za. Extract four numbers: the GDP growth projection, the debt-to-GDP trajectory, the real change in infrastructure spending (nominal change minus inflation), and contingent liabilities from SOEs. Compare each to last year's MTBPS. Ask: is the fiscal framework consistent with the growth stage transition that South Africa needs? If the Treasury projects growth below 2% and infrastructure spending is declining in real terms, the government's own numbers are telling you that its current policy framework is not generating the transformation the country requires. Submit written comments to the Standing Committee on Finance during its MTBPS hearings.

**Step 2: January to May — Engage the IDP process (3 to 5 hours)**

Obtain your municipality's current IDP from the municipal website. Read the LED section. Evaluate it against three criteria: does it include specific, measurable targets (not vague aspirations)? Does it have a realistic budget allocation? Is there evidence that previous LED commitments were actually implemented? Prepare written submissions with data: your municipality's AGSA audit outcome, capital expenditure ratio from Municipal Money, Blue Drop and Green Drop scores, and QLFS employment trends. Submit these during the IDP public participation process and attend at least one ward-level consultation. Written submissions become part of the public record and create accountability trails that verbal complaints do not.

**Step 3: Quarterly — Track three to five indicators (30 minutes per quarter)**

Choose the indicators most relevant to your context. At the national level: GDP growth (Stats SA quarterly release) and unemployment (QLFS). At the municipal level: your metro's capital expenditure performance (Municipal Money portal) and one service delivery indicator that matters to your community — water quality, electricity reliability, or waste management. Record the numbers each quarter so you can identify trends. A single data point can be dismissed; a documented pattern of deterioration across multiple quarters demands a specific institutional response.

**Step 4: When indicators deteriorate — Escalate through formal channels**

When your monitoring reveals problems, use the channel appropriate to the issue. Service delivery failures: ward councillor, then municipal manager, then IDP submission. Financial mismanagement: Auditor-General tip-off line at agsa.co.za. Constitutional rights violations: South African Human Rights Commission at sahrc.org.za. Maladministration: Public Protector at pprotect.org. Legislative oversight: written submission to the relevant portfolio committee. For maximum impact, document every engagement — keep copies of submissions, record dates, note responses or non-responses. A documented pattern of evidence-based engagement, sustained over time, shifts the balance of power between citizens and the state.

By following this cycle consistently — not once, but every year — you transform yourself from a voter who chooses a party every five years into an economic citizen who participates in governance continuously, using the government's own data to hold it accountable for results rather than promises.

## Closing: From Knowledge to Power

This article — and this entire series — has been built on a simple premise. Economic policy in South Africa is too important to be left to economists and politicians alone. The decisions made in the MTBPS, in IDP chambers, in parliamentary portfolio committees, and at NEDLAC determine whether jobs are created or destroyed, whether infrastructure is built or left to decay, whether schools produce skilled graduates or warehouse children for twelve years without teaching them to read, and whether municipalities create the conditions for economic growth or become obstacles to it.

You now have the knowledge to engage with these decisions. You understand the growth stages and why sequencing matters. You understand the institutional architecture and why capability constraints are the binding limitation on policy effectiveness. You understand the MPI and how to use publicly available data to hold your municipality accountable. You understand the budget process and how to read the documents that translate political commitments into fiscal reality. You understand how to evaluate economic promises and distinguish credible proposals from campaign rhetoric.

What remains is the hardest part: actually doing it. Downloading the MTBPS in October and spending two hours with it. Attending the IDP public participation session in March with a printed scorecard. Writing to the Standing Committee on Finance when the infrastructure allocation does not match the infrastructure gap. Lodging a Public Protector complaint when your municipality's capital expenditure drops below acceptable levels for the third year running. Asking your ward councillor, on the record, what the municipality is doing about its qualified audit.

None of this is glamorous. Economic citizenship is not a revolution; it is a practice. It is the accumulated effect of thousands of informed citizens making the government's own data visible, asking questions that require data-driven answers, and using the formal accountability mechanisms that the Constitution created but that remain underutilised. As we noted in [Watching the Watchers](/articles/eg-3-3-watching-the-watchers), the gap between measurement and action is where accountability goes to die. Your job — your economic citizenship — is to close that gap.

The economy belongs to all of us. It is time we governed it that way.
