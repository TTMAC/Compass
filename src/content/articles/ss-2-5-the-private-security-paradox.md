## The Industry That Should Not Need to Exist

South Africa's private security industry is the largest in the world relative to population. More than 2.7 million people are registered with the Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority (PSIRA) — roughly three times the size of the South African Police Service. The industry generates over R70 billion in annual revenue. It employs more people than mining, more than agriculture, more than several other major economic sectors combined. By any measure, private security is one of South Africa's most significant industries — and its very existence is an indictment of the state's failure to perform its most basic function.

The private security paradox is this: the industry's success is built on the state's failure. Every armed response subscription, every boom gate across a suburban street, every private patrol vehicle circling a neighbourhood at night represents a citizen who has concluded — rationally, based on the evidence — that the South African Police Service cannot keep them safe. The growth of private security is not a market responding to consumer preference. It is a market responding to state failure. And that market response, while individually rational for those who can afford it, creates a collective outcome that deepens inequality, distorts the political economy of policing reform, and turns a constitutional right — the right to freedom and security of the person — into a commodity available only to those who can pay.

This article examines the private security industry in three dimensions: its scale and structure, its role in deepening the inequality documented in [Policing a Divided Country](/articles/ss-2-2-policing-a-divided-country), and its paradoxical effect on the prospect of policing reform. Understanding all three is essential because the private security phenomenon is not just a symptom of the broken system — it is a factor that perpetuates it.

## The Scale: An Industry Larger Than the Police

The numbers are extraordinary by any international comparison. PSIRA's annual reports document the registered private security industry: over 2.7 million registered security officers, over 11,000 registered security businesses, and annual industry revenue exceeding R70 billion. The industry has grown consistently for over two decades, tracking — with almost perfect correlation — the perceived decline in SAPS's ability to provide public safety.

To put this in perspective: SAPS has approximately 190,000 members. The private security industry has roughly fourteen times as many registered personnel. Even accounting for the fact that many PSIRA registrations are inactive, part-time, or in non-operational roles, the active private security workforce substantially outnumbers the active police force. South Africa is the only major country in the world where this ratio is so dramatically skewed toward private rather than public security provision.

The regulatory framework that governs this industry is the Private Security Industry Regulation Act of 2001, administered by PSIRA. PSIRA registers security businesses and officers, sets training standards, handles complaints, and monitors compliance. The regulatory framework is, by the standards of developing countries, relatively robust — South Africa recognised earlier than most that an industry of this scale required formal regulation. But PSIRA's enforcement capacity is limited relative to the industry's size, and compliance varies significantly across the industry's range — from large listed companies with professional standards to small operators at the margins of regulation.

The industry encompasses multiple segments, each serving different market needs. Armed response — the suburban model of alarm monitoring and rapid vehicle dispatch — is the most visible segment and the one that most directly substitutes for public policing in residential areas. Guarding — the largest segment by employment — covers corporate, retail, industrial, and residential security, from the guards at your local shopping centre to the teams securing mining operations and critical infrastructure. Cash-in-transit services manage the physical movement of money and valuables. Close protection provides personal security to individuals. And the technology segment — CCTV monitoring, access control systems, AI-assisted surveillance, and neighbourhood security apps — is the fastest-growing segment, bringing digital infrastructure to safety in ways that the public policing system has not.

:::takeaway
South Africa's private security industry employs roughly 2.7 million registered personnel — approximately fourteen times the size of SAPS — and generates over R70 billion in annual revenue. It is the largest private security industry in the world relative to population, and its growth tracks directly with the perceived decline of public policing. The industry is not a luxury add-on to the public safety system — for millions of South Africans who can afford it, it is the primary safety system.
:::

## The Landscape: From Gated Estates to Neighbourhood Watches

The private security industry's footprint is most visible in South Africa's suburban landscape, where the physical infrastructure of private safety — boom gates, electric fences, CCTV cameras, armed response signage — has become so ubiquitous that it defines the built environment.

The armed response model works like this: a homeowner subscribes to a private security company (Fidelity ADT, G4S, Chubb, or one of thousands of smaller operators) and has an alarm system installed. When the alarm is triggered, the security company's control room is alerted and dispatches an armed response vehicle to the property. Response times are typically measured in minutes — in well-served suburban areas, armed response can arrive in three to eight minutes, which is frequently faster than SAPS can respond to a 10111 call. The armed response officer assesses the situation, secures the property, and — if a crime has occurred or is in progress — contacts SAPS. The armed response company provides the immediate presence and deterrence that a visible police service would provide in a well-functioning system.

Gated communities and boom-gated suburbs represent the next level of privatised safety. By controlling access to entire residential areas — through physical barriers, guard houses, and vehicle registration systems — these communities create defensible spaces that reduce opportunistic crime. The model is effective for the residents inside the gates. It is also, by its nature, exclusionary: the safety created inside the gates is purchased by the residents of the gates and is not available to anyone else. The physical gates are a literal manifestation of the two-tier safety system — inside the gate, safety is managed and reliable; outside the gate, safety depends on SAPS.

Community Improvement Districts (CIDs) — formally constituted areas where property owners levy additional rates to fund enhanced services including security — bring a more structured form of privatised safety to commercial and mixed-use areas. CIDs fund private security patrols, CCTV networks, public space management, and other interventions that enhance safety within the district's boundaries. Several of South Africa's most economically active urban areas — including the Cape Town CBD, Sandton, and sections of Durban — operate under CID arrangements.

At the other end of the spectrum, the private security industry also includes community-based safety initiatives — neighbourhood watches, voluntary patrol groups, and informal arrangements where residents pool resources to hire a security guard — that operate in middle-income and some lower-income communities. These initiatives are smaller in scale and less professionally managed than the corporate security services, but they reflect the same underlying dynamic: communities responding to the perceived absence of effective public policing by organising their own alternatives.

## The Inequality Dimension: Safety as a Commodity

The private security industry's existence creates a two-tier safety system that maps almost perfectly onto South Africa's existing patterns of economic inequality — which themselves are a direct legacy of apartheid spatial planning and economic exclusion.

In the first tier, residents of affluent and middle-class areas access a comprehensive private safety ecosystem: armed response, electric fencing, CCTV, boom gates, private patrols, CID services, and smartphone-based neighbourhood security apps. The combined effect of these layers is a safety environment that approaches, and in some respects exceeds, what well-policed communities in developed countries experience. The private security ecosystem provides rapid response, visible deterrence, technology-enabled surveillance, and — critically — accountability. If your armed response company provides poor service, you can switch providers. If your CID patrol is not performing, the CID board replaces the contractor. The market accountability mechanism that is absent from public policing operates effectively in the private safety market.

In the second tier, residents of townships, informal settlements, and rural areas — the majority of South Africa's population — have no access to private security and depend entirely on SAPS. As [Policing a Divided Country](/articles/ss-2-2-policing-a-divided-country) documented, SAPS provision in these areas is characterised by chronic understaffing, insufficient vehicles, minimal detective capacity, and response times measured in hours rather than minutes. The pipeline failures documented in [The Broken Pipeline](/articles/ss-2-1-the-broken-pipeline) are concentrated in exactly these areas.

The constitutional dimension of this inequality is stark. Section 12 of the Constitution guarantees everyone the right to freedom and security of the person. Section 9 guarantees the right to equality. When the state's delivery of safety — its most basic function — is so inadequate that citizens must purchase their own safety on the private market, and when the ability to do so is determined by wealth, the constitutional guarantee of both security and equality is de facto conditioned on purchasing power. Your Section 12 right to security is real if you can afford R500 per month for armed response, and theoretical if you cannot.

:::expert
The Institute for Security Studies (ISS) has examined the political economy of private security in South Africa, analysing how the industry's growth interacts with public policing provision and policing reform prospects. Their research has identified what they describe as a "safety exit" dynamic: as middle-class and affluent citizens exit the public policing system by purchasing private alternatives, their political demand for public policing reform diminishes. This dynamic operates through multiple channels — reduced engagement with Community Police Forums (because private security replaces the functions that CPFs are supposed to influence), reduced political pressure on elected representatives to prioritise policing (because the constituents most likely to exercise political voice have already solved their safety problem privately), and reduced willingness to fund public policing through taxation (because taxpayers who are already paying for private security perceive public policing expenditure as a cost that benefits others). The ISS has argued that this safety exit dynamic creates a political equilibrium in which the people who could most effectively demand better public policing have the least incentive to do so, while the people who most need better public policing have the least political power to demand it — a structural reinforcement of inequality that makes policing reform politically harder to achieve.
:::

## The Political Economy Paradox: How Private Security Inhibits Reform

The private security industry is not merely a passive symptom of state failure. It is an active participant in the political economy of policing — and its existence creates dynamics that make reform harder to achieve.

The first dynamic is the safety exit described above. When the most politically influential citizens — those with access to media, political representatives, and advocacy resources — can solve their safety problem privately, the political pressure for systemic reform dissipates. Public policing becomes, in political economy terms, a service primarily for the poor — and services primarily for the poor tend to be politically under-funded and under-prioritised.

The second dynamic is the industry's vested interest in the status quo. The private security industry's commercial success depends on a specific condition: that SAPS remains insufficiently effective that citizens feel compelled to purchase private alternatives. An effective, responsive, trustworthy public police service would not eliminate the private security industry — there would still be demand for specialised security services — but it would dramatically reduce the mass-market demand for basic armed response and residential security that drives the bulk of the industry's revenue. The private security industry does not actively sabotage police reform (there is no credible evidence of this), but it has no commercial incentive to advocate for the kind of public policing reform that would reduce demand for its services. In a country where industry lobbying influences policy, this absence of advocacy matters.

The third dynamic is the labour market effect. The private security industry employs 2.7 million people. Any reform that significantly improved public policing and reduced private security demand would threaten a substantial number of jobs. This is not a trivial political consideration. In a country with unemployment rates exceeding 30 per cent, any policy change that could reduce employment in a major industry faces political resistance. The employment dimension adds complexity to the reform calculus — not because reform is wrong, but because the transition must be managed to avoid creating a new crisis while solving an existing one.

:::takeaway
The private security industry's existence creates a paradox for policing reform: the industry is a symptom of SAPS's failure, but its growth creates political and economic dynamics that make reform harder to achieve. The safety exit of affluent citizens reduces political pressure for public policing reform; the industry has no commercial incentive to advocate for effective public policing; and its employment of 2.7 million people creates political sensitivity around any change that might reduce demand for private security. Breaking this paradox requires reform that improves public policing while managing the transition's impact on the private security labour market.
:::

## The Accountability Gap: When Private Security Operates as Quasi-Police

The relationship between private security and SAPS is operationally close but legally ambiguous. In suburban areas, armed response companies are often the first responders to crime — they arrive before SAPS, secure the scene, and sometimes apprehend suspects. In CID areas, private security officers patrol public spaces, interact with members of the public, and manage situations that would normally be police functions. In gated communities, security guards control access, question visitors, and enforce community rules.

This quasi-policing role raises accountability questions that the current regulatory framework does not adequately address. Private security officers are not police officers. They do not have the powers of arrest that SAPS members possess under the Criminal Procedure Act (beyond the limited citizen's arrest powers available to any person). They do not have the power to search, to detain, or to use force beyond what is available to any civilian in self-defence or defence of property. Yet in practice, the operational reality often exceeds these legal boundaries — armed response officers pursuing suspects, CID patrols detaining people suspected of criminal activity, and security guards at gated estates controlling the movement of people in ways that approximate police powers.

When private security officers overstep their legal authority — and incidents of excessive force, wrongful detention, and racial profiling by private security are well documented — the accountability mechanisms are weaker than those that apply to SAPS. IPID investigates complaints against SAPS members; PSIRA handles complaints against private security, but its enforcement capacity is limited. The courts can provide remedies, but access to courts for individuals who have been wronged by private security is constrained by the same barriers — cost, knowledge, power asymmetry — that limit access to justice generally.

The accountability gap is most acute in the areas where private security's quasi-policing role is most extensive. In gated communities and CID areas, private security effectively controls public space — deciding who is welcome and who is not, who is questioned and who is waved through, who is watched with suspicion and who is treated as a legitimate presence. The potential for these decisions to be shaped by racial, class, and social biases — rather than by any legal standard — is significant, and documented incidents of racial profiling by private security in suburban areas and shopping centres illustrate this risk.

## What the Industry Reveals About What Works

For all its problematic dimensions, the private security industry also reveals something important about effective safety provision — something that the proposed policing reform in Part 3 of this series draws on.

Private security companies are responsive because they are accountable to their clients. If an armed response company provides slow response times, the client cancels and switches to a competitor. This market accountability mechanism — absent from public policing — creates a powerful incentive for performance. Private security companies invest in technology (GPS tracking, app-based communication, CCTV integration) because technology improves service delivery and competitive advantage. They recruit locally and maintain neighbourhood-level knowledge because local knowledge improves operational effectiveness. They provide visible presence because presence deters crime and reassures clients.

These features — accountability, technology adoption, local knowledge, and visible presence — are not inherent to private enterprise. They are features of any well-designed service delivery system. A public policing model that incorporated genuine accountability to local communities (through municipal rather than national governance), technology-enabled operations (through digital case management and predictive deployment), local recruitment and deployment (through provincial and municipal police tiers), and visible, community-embedded presence (through grid-based beat policing) would deliver many of the same benefits — but as a public good available to all, rather than a private commodity available only to those who can pay.

This is the insight that connects the private security analysis to the structural reform proposed in [A Three-Tier Policing Architecture](/articles/ss-3-1-three-tier-policing-architecture): the features that make private security effective — local accountability, rapid response, visible presence, technology adoption — are exactly the features that a well-designed public policing system should deliver. The challenge is not to eliminate private security but to build a public policing system that is effective enough to provide universal access to the safety that private security currently provides only to the few.

## The Consumer's Accountability Guide

Whether or not you currently use private security, understanding the industry's regulatory framework and your rights as a consumer or as a member of the public who interacts with private security is practically useful.

:::framework
**The Private Security Consumer's Accountability Guide**

**Check PSIRA Registration:**
Every private security company and every individual security officer must be registered with PSIRA. You can verify registration on the PSIRA website (www.psira.co.za) or by calling the PSIRA contact centre. If a company or officer is not registered, they are operating illegally. Do not use unregistered security providers, and report them to PSIRA.

**Know the Limits of Private Security Authority:**
Private security officers are not police officers. They do not have the power to arrest you (beyond the citizen's arrest powers available to anyone), to search you, or to compel you to identify yourself. If a private security officer demands your identification, searches your person or vehicle, or detains you without your consent and without reasonable grounds for a citizen's arrest, they may be acting unlawfully. Note the officer's name, company, and PSIRA registration number and report the incident to PSIRA and, if appropriate, to SAPS.

**File Complaints Through PSIRA:**
If you experience misconduct by a private security officer — excessive force, unlawful detention, racial profiling, theft, or any other violation — file a complaint with PSIRA. Complaints can be submitted online, by email, or at PSIRA offices. PSIRA is mandated to investigate complaints and can take regulatory action including fines, suspension, or revocation of registration.

**Evaluate Your Provider:**
If you are a private security consumer, evaluate your provider on response times (ask for their average response time to your area), PSIRA compliance history (check whether they have been subject to PSIRA sanctions), insurance coverage (verify that they carry adequate liability insurance), and contractual terms (understand cancellation provisions, liability limitations, and what is and is not covered by their service).

**Advocate for Better Public Policing:**
Using private security does not extinguish your right to effective public policing or your responsibility to advocate for it. Engage with your Community Police Forum, attend station community meetings, and use the accountability tools described in [Policing a Divided Country](/articles/ss-2-2-policing-a-divided-country) to hold your local SAPS station accountable. The long-term solution to South Africa's safety crisis is not better private security — it is public policing that works for everyone.
:::
