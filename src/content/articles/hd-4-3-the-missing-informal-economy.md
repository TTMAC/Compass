## In Most Poor Countries, the Unemployed Sell Something; In South Africa, They Wait

Walk through a working-class district of Lagos, Nairobi, Mumbai, or Lima and you will be surrounded by commerce. People are selling, fixing, cooking, transporting, brokering, manufacturing in doorways and on pavements. A great deal of it is precarious and poorly paid, and none of it shows up cleanly in tax records — but it is _earning_. In those cities, a person who cannot find a formal job does not simply stop participating in the economy. She finds something to sell. The informal economy is the great shock absorber of the developing world: where the formal labour market cannot absorb people, informal self-employment does.

Walk through a South African township and the contrast is immediate and strange. There is informal trade — spaza shops, street vendors, hair salons, taxis — but there is far less of it than the level of unemployment would lead you to expect, and far less of it than you would find in a comparably poor country elsewhere. A person in Khayelitsha or Tembisa who cannot find a formal job is much more likely than her counterpart in Nairobi to do the thing that South African unemployment statistics quietly record: to wait. To be, in the language of the [first article in this Part](/articles/hd-4-1-the-conversion-problem), unable to convert her capabilities into a livelihood through _any_ channel — formal or informal.

This is one of the most distinctive and least understood facts about the South African economy, and it is central to the conversion problem. South Africa's informal sector is anomalously small. Where informal employment accounts for 30, 40, even 50 per cent or more of total employment across much of the developing world, in South Africa it accounts for somewhere around 18 to 20 per cent on Statistics South Africa's narrow informal-sector measure — and even on broader definitions that count informal jobs inside formal firms and in agriculture, it remains far below its peers. The fallback that exists almost everywhere else has, in South Africa, largely been foreclosed. And because it has been foreclosed, the country's enormous pool of unemployed people has nowhere to go. This article asks why the informal economy is missing, what its absence costs, and what it would take to open the self-employment route that so many South Africans are, in effect, locked out of.

## The Anomaly, Measured

Start by being precise about the claim, because it is genuinely counterintuitive. The expectation, well established in development economics, is that informality and poverty go together: poorer countries have larger informal sectors, because formal wage employment is scarce and people fall back on self-employment to survive. By that logic, South Africa — with unemployment among the highest in the world — should have a _large_ informal sector. The opposite is true.

The numbers tell the story. Across low- and middle-income countries, informal employment commonly makes up the majority of all employment. In much of sub-Saharan Africa the informal share exceeds 80 per cent of non-agricultural employment. In India it is of a similar order. In Latin American economies it routinely runs between 40 and 60 per cent. South Africa's informal sector, by contrast, employs under a fifth of those who work — a figure far below not only its income peers but below countries much poorer than itself. South Africa has the unemployment profile of a poor country and the informality profile of a rich one, and the combination is close to unique.

The consequence of that combination is the conversion failure at its starkest. In a typical developing economy, the boundary between "employed" and "unemployed" is blurry, because the informal sector provides a continuum of low-productivity earning options that catch people the formal sector cannot. In South Africa the boundary is sharp. You are in formal employment, or you are very likely doing nothing economic at all — not because you would not work, but because the informal option that would exist elsewhere does not exist here at anything like the same scale. The "discouraged work-seeker," a person who has given up actively looking because there is genuinely nothing to find, is a much larger category in South Africa than in comparable economies, and the missing informal economy is a large part of why.

The same absence shows up in a second fallback that catches the poor elsewhere: subsistence and smallholder agriculture. In much of the developing world, a household that cannot earn cash income can at least grow food, and a large share of the rural population is engaged in small-scale farming that buffers it against destitution. South Africa's smallholder and subsistence agriculture is, like its informal trade, unusually thin — another casualty of a history that dispossessed the black majority of land and confined it to overcrowded former homelands with degraded soil and insecure tenure. So the rural South African, like the urban one, is missing a fallback that her counterpart across the border in Mozambique or Zimbabwe still has. The foreclosure of self-provisioning routes is general, not confined to the cities.

:::takeaway
South Africa is a statistical anomaly: it has the unemployment of a poor country and the informality of a rich one. Informal employment is the great shock absorber of the developing world, commonly accounting for 40–80 per cent of employment elsewhere; in South Africa it is around 18–20 per cent (Statistics South Africa's narrow measure). The fallback that catches the jobless almost everywhere else has, here, largely been foreclosed — which is a major reason the country's unemployed have nowhere to convert their capabilities into earning.

## Why the Fallback Is Missing: A Layered Inheritance

The smallness of South Africa's informal economy is not natural, and it is not a sign of sophistication. It is the product of a specific history and a set of present-day conditions that, layered together, suppress the self-employment that would otherwise flourish. No single cause explains it; the explanation is cumulative.

**The deliberate destruction of black enterprise.** The first and deepest layer is historical, and it is the subject the [Problem That Has No Name](/articles/hd-1-4-the-problem-that-has-no-name) circled. For most of the twentieth century, the state actively suppressed black economic activity. Influx control and the pass laws restricted where black South Africans could live and trade. The Group Areas Act destroyed established black business districts and forced relocation to dormitory townships designed with no commercial heart. Black entrepreneurs were barred from trading in white areas, restricted in the kinds of businesses they could own, and denied the property rights and capital that enterprise requires. Apartheid did not merely neglect black business; it dismantled it, repeatedly, over generations. The entrepreneurial traditions, family firms, accumulated commercial capital, and trading networks that in other societies pass down through generations were, for the black majority, systematically broken. A culture of enterprise is itself a kind of inheritance, and South Africa spent a century destroying the one its majority population was building.

**The dormitory geography.** The second layer is spatial, and it persists in concrete and tar. Townships were engineered as labour reservoirs, not as places to live full economic lives. They were built far from centres of commerce, connected to them by expensive and time-consuming transport, and laid out without the market squares, high streets, and mixed-use density in which informal commerce thrives. This is the _environmental conversion factor_ from the [opening article of this Part](/articles/hd-4-1-the-conversion-problem) in its most physical form. A would-be trader in a dormitory township faces a customer base that is itself poor and largely absent during working hours, in a built environment offering few viable trading locations. The geography that suppresses formal employment suppresses informal enterprise too.

**The dominance of formal retail.** The third layer is contemporary and easy to miss. South Africa has one of the most sophisticated and concentrated formal retail sectors in the developing world. Large supermarket chains and shopping malls have penetrated deep into township and rural markets, offering low prices, wide selection, and convenience that informal traders struggle to match. In many developing countries the informal trader thrives precisely because formal retail has not reached the poor; in South Africa, formal retail reached them, and in reaching them it crowded out a great deal of the small-scale trade that would otherwise provide livelihoods. The mall in the township is a genuine convenience for the consumer and, simultaneously, a competitor that the local informal economy cannot easily withstand.

**The regulatory and credit barriers.** The fourth layer is institutional. Starting and running even a tiny enterprise in South Africa involves licensing, zoning, and compliance requirements that are burdensome for the survivalist trader and effectively exclude many from operating legally. And the capital required to start — even a few thousand rand for stock or equipment — is out of reach for people with no savings and no access to formal credit, a barrier that the [next article](/articles/hd-4-4-from-income-to-assets) examines in detail. The informal trader in many countries can start with almost nothing and scale slowly; in South Africa the combination of regulatory friction and capital scarcity raises the threshold to entry.

These layers reinforce one another. A person with no inherited commercial capital, in a township built without commercial space, facing competition from a supermarket chain, blocked by licensing and unable to raise startup capital, is not choosing idleness over enterprise. She is facing a stacked set of barriers, each of which would be discouraging on its own and which together make self-employment, for most people, not a realistic route at all.

:::expert[Research on South African Informality]
A sustained body of research — associated with institutions such as the Southern Africa Labour and Development Research Unit (SALDRU) at the University of Cape Town, the DPRU, and the international Women in Informal Employment: Globalizing and Organizing (WIEGO) network — has documented and tried to explain South Africa's unusually small informal sector. The findings converge on a multi-causal account rather than a single villain. The apartheid suppression of black enterprise left a deficit in entrepreneurial capital, networks, and traditions that has not been recovered. Apartheid spatial planning located the poor away from markets and trading opportunities. The penetration of large formal retailers into low-income markets crowded out informal trade to a degree not seen in most developing economies. Regulatory barriers and the near-absence of accessible startup finance raised entry costs. Some researchers add a demand-side point: because so many households depend on social grants and remittances rather than locally generated income, the purchasing power circulating within poor communities is limited and leaks quickly to formal retailers, giving local informal enterprise a thin and unstable customer base. The policy implication that recurs across this literature is that South Africa cannot assume the informal sector will absorb the unemployed as it does elsewhere — the conditions for that absorption were deliberately and structurally undermined, and rebuilding them requires active intervention, not merely deregulation.
:::

## The Survivalist and the Entrepreneur: Not the Same Thing

When policy discussions turn to small business, they tend to blur two very different things, and the blur does real damage. There is the high-growth entrepreneur — the founder building a firm with the potential to scale, create jobs, and add complexity to the economy. And there is the survivalist — the person running a spaza shop, a vegetable stall, or a backyard service business not to build an empire but to convert her own labour into enough income to live. Both matter. They need almost entirely different things, and conflating them produces policy that serves neither.

The survivalist enterprise is, in the framework of this series, a _conversion mechanism_ — a way for a person to convert her capability to work into income when no employer will hire her. Its purpose is a livelihood, not growth. Most survivalist enterprises will never employ anyone beyond the owner and perhaps a family member, and judging them by whether they "scale" misses their point. Their value is that they catch people the formal economy drops. A country with a healthy survivalist sector has a floor under unemployment; a country without one, like South Africa, has the void this article describes. The right support for survivalists is not incubators and pitch competitions but the basics: a place to trade, access to a small amount of working capital, freedom from regulatory harassment, and protection from crime and extortion.

The high-growth entrepreneur is a different animal with a different need: access to growth finance, markets, skills, and the ability to navigate the regulatory and competitive environment. This is the entrepreneur the [Economic Growth pillar](/articles/eg-4-1-three-engines-of-growth) is most concerned with, because it is high-growth firms that drive the macro story of job creation and rising complexity. South Africa has too few of these as well, but the reasons and the remedies differ from those for survivalists.

The reason the distinction matters for _this_ Part — the human-development, individual-capability Part — is that the survivalist route is the one that bears directly on the conversion problem for the mass of unemployed people. Most of the twelve million South Africans who want work and cannot find it are not going to be founders of scaling firms. They need, and in another country would have, the survivalist fallback. The tragedy of South Africa's missing informal economy is most acute at exactly this level: the simplest, most accessible form of self-employment, the one that requires the least and catches the most, is the one the country's history and structure have most thoroughly suppressed.

## What the Taxi Industry Teaches

There is one large, black-owned, informal-origin industry that did emerge in South Africa against the odds, and it is worth studying precisely because it is the exception: the minibus-taxi industry. Built from the 1970s and 1980s onward, largely by black entrepreneurs operating outside and often against the apartheid regulatory order, the taxi industry grew into the backbone of the country's commuter transport system, moving the majority of South Africa's workers every day. It did this with negligible state support, in the teeth of official hostility, financed and organised entirely from within black communities. It is the clearest proof available that the entrepreneurial capability was never absent from the population — only suppressed.

The lesson cuts two ways, and both are instructive. On one hand, the taxi industry shows what black enterprise could achieve when it found a market that formal provision had abandoned (the apartheid state never built adequate public transport for the people it had pushed to the urban periphery) and a service that large formal competitors could not easily displace. Where a genuine, defensible market gap existed, enterprise filled it at scale. That is the survivalist-to-substantial trajectory that the missing informal economy is supposed to provide, and here it did.

On the other hand, the conditions of the industry's emergence left a difficult legacy that illustrates the cost of growing an entire sector outside any supportive institutional framework. Because it grew in opposition to the state rather than with it, the industry developed its own systems of route control and dispute resolution, and the resulting contestation over routes has at times been violent. Access to formal finance was historically poor, pushing operators toward expensive vehicle credit. Regulation, when it finally came, sat awkwardly on an industry that had organised itself without it. The taxi industry is thus both a triumph of suppressed enterprise breaking through and a cautionary tale about what happens when a sector is forced to build everything itself, including the institutions that should have been provided. It demonstrates that the capability for enterprise exists in abundance — and that the absence of an enabling environment exacts a price even from the enterprises that succeed.

A second, more contested contemporary case sharpens the point: the spaza-shop sector, where a substantial share of township retail has come to be operated by immigrant entrepreneurs, often running tighter margins and more efficient supply chains than their South African-born counterparts. The dynamic has become a source of real tension, sometimes violent. But beneath the tension is an analytically important observation: the immigrants succeeding in these enterprises are frequently people who arrived with little but who came from economies where informal enterprise is a living tradition — carrying exactly the entrepreneurial capital, networks, and habits that apartheid spent a century destroying in the local population. That outsiders with nothing can build viable informal businesses in the same townships where locals struggle is not evidence of local deficiency; it is further evidence of how thoroughly the local entrepreneurial inheritance was dismantled, and how much of the gap is a deficit of transmitted commercial capability rather than of individual capacity.

## The Support Architecture That Does Not Reach the Ground

South Africa is not without institutions meant to address this. There is a dedicated Department of Small Business Development. There is the Small Enterprise Development Agency, meant to provide non-financial support — training, mentoring, advice. There is the Small Enterprise Finance Agency, meant to provide the capital that the private financial sector will not. There is the National Youth Development Agency, meant to support young entrepreneurs specifically. On paper, the survivalist and the entrepreneur both have somewhere to turn.

In practice, this architecture has struggled to reach the people it is meant to serve, and the reasons echo the SETA failures that the [previous article](/articles/hd-4-2-skills-beyond-schooling) described. The agencies are concentrated in formal offices that the township survivalist rarely encounters. Their processes assume a level of documentation, formality, and literacy that excludes precisely the informal operator most in need. Their finance products are often ill-suited to survivalist scale — too large, too collateral-dependent, too slow. And, as with much of South Africa's developmental state, the agencies have at times been more visible in their administrative and governance difficulties than in their effect on the ground. The result is a support system that serves a thin layer of more formalised small businesses reasonably well and barely touches the survivalist mass below them — the very people whose exclusion from self-employment defines the missing informal economy.

There is also a harder, less comfortable factor that any honest account must name: crime and extortion. In parts of the country, informal and small businesses operate under the threat of protection rackets, theft, and violence that taxes enterprise directly and deters it entirely. A person deciding whether to invest her last few thousand rand in stock for a small shop is weighing not only whether she can sell it but whether it — and she — will be safe. This is where the [Safety and Security pillar](/articles/ss-2-1-the-broken-pipeline) and the human-development story intersect: insecurity is not only a harm in itself but a suppressor of the economic activity that would otherwise lift people out of poverty. An economy cannot grow a robust informal sector in conditions where the informal operator cannot be confident of keeping what she earns.

## What Opening the Route Would Require

If the informal economy is missing because a stack of barriers was deliberately and structurally built, then opening it requires deliberately and structurally dismantling that stack — not waiting for it to correct itself. The conversion framework points to where the dismantling would have to happen.

It would require, first, _space_: the physical and regulatory room to trade. Townships and informal settlements need real commercial infrastructure — trading spaces, markets, serviced sites — and a regulatory posture that treats the survivalist trader as someone to enable rather than to fine, license into oblivion, or move along. Several of the barriers are municipal, which is why this connects directly to the [Reform Agenda's](/articles/ra-4-4-building-municipal-capability) concern with the capability of local government, the sphere that most directly shapes whether a person can legally and safely trade where she lives.

It would require, second, _capital at survivalist scale_: small amounts of accessible working finance, designed for people without collateral or formal credit histories, delivered through channels they actually use. This is the bridge to the [next article](/articles/hd-4-4-from-income-to-assets), which takes up the financial exclusion that keeps even willing entrepreneurs capital-starved.

It would require, third, _safety_: enough security that a person can invest in a small enterprise without expecting to lose it to crime or extortion — which is to say, it requires the safety pillar's work to succeed.

And it would require, fourth, a clear-eyed acceptance of what the informal economy is and is not. It is not a romantic solution to unemployment, and it is not a substitute for the formal job creation that the [Economic Growth pillar](/articles/eg-4-1-three-engines-of-growth) pursues. Informal livelihoods are typically low-productivity, low-income, and insecure, and a development strategy that aimed only to expand them would be settling for too little. But a country that offers its unemployed no informal fallback at all has removed the floor that catches people while the larger work of formal job creation proceeds. Restoring that floor is not the whole answer to the conversion problem. It is the part of the answer that could reach the most people, fastest, at the level where the conversion failure is most absolute.

## What This Means for You

The missing informal economy can feel like a structural fact too large for any individual to act on, and in important respects it is — the barriers are historical, spatial, and institutional. But the framework also locates the points where individual knowledge and collective action make a difference.

If you are unemployed and weighing whether to try a small enterprise, the honest message is twofold. The barriers this article describes are real, and you are not failing if they have blocked you — the suppression of the informal route is a structural condition, not a personal deficiency. At the same time, the specific barriers are worth mapping in your own case, because some are more navigable than they look: a trading space you had not considered, a survivalist-scale finance product through a cooperative or stokvel rather than a bank, a skill from the [previous article](/articles/hd-4-2-skills-beyond-schooling) that is directly self-employable, a gap in the local market that the supermarket does not serve. The route is narrow, but it is not always closed.

If you are a customer in a township or rural community, where you spend is a small but real conversion factor for the enterprises around you. Every rand spent with a local informal trader rather than leaked to a distant formal chain circulates within the community and helps sustain a livelihood. This is not a call to pay more or accept less; it is a recognition that the thin, unstable customer base that researchers identify as a constraint on local enterprise is, in part, the aggregate of individual spending decisions.

If you are a participant in local government or community structures, the barriers most within reach are the municipal ones: trading bylaws, the provision of market space, the posture of officials toward informal traders, and the security of trading areas. These are precisely the levers that the [Participation](/articles/4-2-ward-committees-and-community-engagement) and [Reform](/articles/ra-4-4-building-municipal-capability) work in this series is about, and they are where pressure can change whether the people in your community can legally and safely earn.

If you are a citizen and a voter, the reframing is the contribution. South African political debate treats unemployment almost entirely as a question of formal job creation, and formal job creation matters enormously. But a country that has foreclosed the informal fallback has removed an option that catches people everywhere else, and restoring it is a distinct policy project that rarely gets named. Ask whether the parties and programmes competing for your support understand that South Africa's informal economy is missing, and have anything to say about rebuilding it.

:::takeaway
South Africa's small informal sector is not a sign of development; it is a development failure with a deliberate history. The suppression of black enterprise, a dormitory geography, the dominance of formal retail, regulatory friction, capital scarcity, and crime have together foreclosed the self-employment fallback that catches the jobless almost everywhere else. For most of the unemployed, the survivalist enterprise — not the scaling startup — is the relevant conversion mechanism, and it is precisely the level the support architecture least reaches. Opening the route requires space, survivalist-scale capital, and safety — and an honest recognition that an informal floor is not a substitute for formal jobs, but is the part of the answer that could reach the most people fastest.
:::

:::framework
**The Self-Employment Barrier Map**

Use this framework to diagnose, for a specific person or community, which barriers to informal self-employment are binding — and which are more navigable than they appear. The point is to replace "there is nothing to do" with a list of specific, differently solvable obstacles.

**Barrier 1: Space.** Is there a viable, legal, safe place to trade? Consider not only physical location and foot traffic but the municipal bylaws governing who may trade where. A blocked trading space is often a municipal decision, not a market fact.

**Barrier 2: Capital.** What is the minimum startup amount, and where could it come from? If formal credit is inaccessible, consider survivalist-scale alternatives — stokvels, cooperatives, community finance — designed for people without collateral (see [From Income to Assets](/articles/hd-4-4-from-income-to-assets)).

**Barrier 3: Market.** Is there genuine local demand the formal retailers do not serve — a product, a service, a convenience, a timing? Informal enterprise survives in the gaps formal retail leaves; identifying a real gap is the difference between a livelihood and a loss.

**Barrier 4: Skill.** Is the enterprise built on a capability that is genuinely self-employable — a trade, a service, a craft (see [Skills Beyond Schooling](/articles/hd-4-2-skills-beyond-schooling))? Self-employment built on a real, scarce skill is far more durable than trading built on thin margins alone.

**Barrier 5: Safety.** Can the enterprise and its earnings be kept secure from theft and extortion? Where they cannot, the barrier is a safety problem before it is an economic one, and the response belongs partly to the [safety](/articles/ss-2-1-the-broken-pipeline) domain.

**Barrier 6: Regulation.** What licensing or compliance is genuinely required, and how much of the perceived barrier is actual law versus assumption or official discretion? Some regulatory obstacles are real and binding; others dissolve on inspection or yield to collective pressure on local government.
:::
