## The Core Inheritance You Need to Understand Before Anything Else

South Africa's policing crisis is not a failure of democratic governance. It is the predictable outcome of inheriting a police force that was designed for racial control, not public safety. The South African Police — known simply as the SAP — was built as an instrument of the apartheid state, optimised for surveillance, suppression, and the enforcement of pass laws. When the democratic government renamed it the South African Police Service (SAPS) and placed it under a new Constitution in 1995, the institutional DNA did not change. The uniforms changed colour. The legislation changed language. But the organisational culture, the command structures, the training doctrines, and the deeply ingrained habits of a force that had spent decades treating the majority of the population as the enemy — those persisted. Understanding this inheritance is the prerequisite for understanding why the system fails today and what genuine reform requires.

If you have already read [The Architecture of the State](/articles/1-1-architecture-of-the-state), you know that South Africa's Constitution divides power across three spheres of government — national, provincial, and municipal — each with constitutionally protected functions. You also know, from [How the Spheres Interact](/articles/1-3-how-the-spheres-interact), that policing appears in Schedule 4 of the Constitution as a concurrent function, meaning that both national and provincial government can legislate on it. Yet in practice, policing in South Africa is overwhelmingly a national function, controlled by a single minister in Pretoria. That structural choice — centralised national control over a force of nearly 200,000 members — was itself shaped by the history you are about to read. The ANC government that took power in 1994 had specific reasons for wanting to keep policing centralised, and those reasons were rooted in the very apartheid architecture this article traces.

This article takes you through three eras: the colonial origins of policing in southern Africa, the apartheid transformation of police into a comprehensive system of racial control, and the 1994 transition that attempted to convert this apparatus into a democratic police service. By the end, you will have a mental model — a "History Decoder" — that lets you distinguish between features of today's policing system that are inherited from the colonial and apartheid architecture and features that are products of democratic-era decisions. That distinction matters because it determines whether a given problem can be fixed through better management and training, or whether it requires the kind of fundamental structural redesign that the rest of this series will argue for.

## Colonial Policing: Built to Extract, Not to Protect

The history of policing in South Africa does not begin with apartheid. It begins with colonialism, and understanding the colonial model is essential because it established the foundational logic that apartheid later perfected.

When the British established formal police forces in the Cape Colony in the mid-nineteenth century, they did not import the model that Sir Robert Peel had created for London's Metropolitan Police in 1829. The Peelian model — often summarised as "policing by consent" — was built on the principle that the police are civilians in uniform, that their authority derives from public cooperation, and that their primary function is crime prevention through visible presence and community trust. It was, at least in theory, designed for a population that the police served.

Colonial policing operated on an entirely different logic. Police forces in the Cape Colony, Natal, the Transvaal Republic, and the Orange Free State were paramilitary organisations designed primarily to control indigenous populations and protect settler economic interests. The Cape Mounted Riflemen, established in 1806, were a military unit repurposed for frontier policing — their job was to patrol the boundary between colonial settlement and indigenous territory, suppress resistance, and enforce the labour arrangements that the colonial economy depended on. When the Natal Mounted Police were established in 1874, their primary function was controlling the Zulu population in the aftermath of colonial expansion. The Zuid-Afrikaansche Republiek Politie in the Transvaal, established in 1881, existed to maintain order on the goldfields and ensure that the mining industry had access to a controlled, compliant labour force.

This is not a minor historical footnote. The fundamental design choice — police as agents of control over a subject population rather than servants of a consenting public — established an institutional logic that persisted through every subsequent transformation of South African policing. Colonial police forces were accountable upward to the colonial administration, not outward to the communities they patrolled. They used force as a primary tool rather than a last resort. They operated through intelligence networks designed to identify and suppress political organisation among indigenous populations. And they were intimately connected to the economic system, enforcing labour contracts, pass systems, and vagrancy laws that kept indigenous workers available and compliant.

:::takeaway
Colonial policing in South Africa was never built on the Peelian model of "policing by consent." From the very beginning, police forces in the Cape, Natal, the Transvaal, and the Orange Free State were paramilitary organisations designed to control indigenous populations and protect settler economic interests. This foundational logic — police as instruments of control, not servants of the public — established the institutional DNA that apartheid later perfected and that democracy inherited.
:::

The discovery of diamonds in Kimberley in 1867 and gold on the Witwatersrand in 1886 intensified this model. The mining compounds that housed black mineworkers were policed by private security forces employed by the mining companies, operating under legal frameworks that gave them quasi-police powers. The compound system — in which workers were housed in enclosed, controlled environments, searched on entry and exit, and subject to internal discipline by compound managers — was a prototype for the controlled population management that would become the hallmark of apartheid policing. The line between private mine security and public policing was blurred from the start, and the mining industry's influence over policing policy remained a feature of South African governance well into the twentieth century.

After the South Africa Act of 1909 unified the four colonies into the Union of South Africa, the new government created the South African Police (SAP) in 1913 by merging the colonial police forces. The SAP was, from its inception, a national force under centralised control — a design choice driven by the new Union government's need to maintain control over a vast territory with a restive black majority. The 1913 Natives Land Act, which restricted black land ownership to roughly 7 per cent of the country's territory, created a new enforcement mandate for the SAP: policing the boundaries of the racial land dispensation and managing the movement of black workers between reserves and white-owned farms and cities.

## Apartheid's Perfection of the Control Model

When the National Party came to power in 1948 and began implementing its apartheid programme, it did not need to build a repressive police force from scratch. It inherited one. What apartheid did was systematise, expand, and weaponise the colonial policing model into a comprehensive apparatus of racial control that touched every aspect of black South Africans' daily lives.

The pass laws were the most pervasive interface between black South Africans and the police. Under the Native Laws Amendment Act of 1952 and the subsequent Abolition of Passes and Co-ordination of Documents Act (which, despite its name, replaced multiple passes with a single, mandatory reference book), every black South African over the age of sixteen was required to carry a passbook — the dompas — at all times. The passbook contained the bearer's photograph, employment history, tax payments, and permission to be in a particular area. Failure to produce a valid passbook on demand was a criminal offence, and the SAP enforced this requirement relentlessly.

The scale of pass law enforcement is difficult to comprehend from a contemporary vantage point. Between 1948 and 1986, more than 17 million people were prosecuted under the pass laws — an average of roughly 450,000 prosecutions per year. In some years, pass law arrests accounted for more than a third of all criminal prosecutions in South Africa. This was not a peripheral policing function. It was the central activity of the SAP in black communities. Every encounter between a black South African and a police officer carried the possibility of a pass law arrest — on the street, at a roadblock, at a train station, at a workplace. The pass laws made every black South African a potential criminal, and they made every police officer a potential agent of arbitrary detention.

:::expert
Research by the Institute for Security Studies (ISS) and the Centre for the Study of Violence and Reconciliation (CSVR) has documented how the pass law enforcement system shaped the organisational culture of the SAP in ways that persisted long after the pass laws were abolished in 1986. The ISS's analysis of policing reform in the post-apartheid period has consistently highlighted that the SAP's core competency was population control — the management and surveillance of movement, the processing of identity documents, the enforcement of spatial segregation. When democracy arrived and this core function disappeared overnight, the institution was left with an enormous apparatus and no legitimate primary mission. The detective capacity, the forensic science capability, the community engagement skills, and the crime prevention orientation that a democratic police service requires were never central to the SAP's institutional identity. They were, at best, secondary functions performed by a minority of officers. The ISS has argued that this mismatch between institutional capability and democratic mandate is the single most important structural factor explaining SAPS's continued failure to deliver effective crime prevention and investigation.
:::

Beyond the pass laws, the SAP maintained an extensive political surveillance and repression apparatus. The Security Branch — later known as the Security Police — was responsible for monitoring, infiltrating, and suppressing anti-apartheid political organisations. The Security Branch operated its own intelligence networks, maintained files on hundreds of thousands of political activists, and was responsible for some of the most notorious acts of state violence during the apartheid era — including the torture and killing of detainees such as Steve Biko in 1977, the bombing of the South African Council of Churches headquarters in Johannesburg, and cross-border raids against ANC targets in neighbouring states.

The Security Branch operated with near-total impunity. Its officers were shielded from prosecution, its budgets were opaque, and its activities were authorised at the highest levels of government. The culture of impunity that developed within the Security Branch — the understanding that officers would be protected from consequences for acts of violence committed in service of the state — infected the broader SAP. If Security Branch officers could torture and kill with impunity, the message to ordinary SAP officers was clear: the use of excessive force was not merely tolerated but institutionally sanctioned.

The township uprisings of the 1970s and 1980s turned the SAP into a frontline combat force against its own population. The Soweto uprising of 16 June 1976, in which police opened fire on schoolchildren protesting the mandatory use of Afrikaans as a medium of instruction, killed at least 176 people (some estimates place the figure much higher) and marked a turning point in the relationship between the SAP and black communities. The states of emergency declared in 1985 and 1986 gave the SAP extraordinary powers — detention without trial, the authority to seal off entire townships, the use of military-style operations against civilian populations. During the 1985-1990 period, the SAP worked alongside the South African Defence Force (SADF) in a coordinated campaign of township repression that included mass detentions, curfews, media blackouts, and the deployment of armoured vehicles in residential areas.

## The Homeland Forces: Eleven Police Services Nobody Talks About

One of the least understood aspects of apartheid policing is the proliferation of homeland police forces. The apartheid government's Bantustan policy created nominally independent homelands — Transkei, Bophuthatswana, Venda, and Ciskei (the "TBVC states") — and six self-governing territories, each with its own police force. By the end of apartheid, South Africa had not one but twelve separate police forces: the SAP plus eleven homeland forces.

These homeland police forces were, in many cases, even more brutal and less accountable than the SAP itself. They operated under homeland governments that were authoritarian client states of Pretoria, funded by the South African government, and staffed by officers who had often been rejected by or expelled from the SAP. The Bophuthatswana Police, for example, were notorious for their role in suppressing political dissent in the homeland and for their involvement in the violence that preceded the 1994 elections. The Transkei Police were implicated in political assassinations and widespread corruption under successive homeland governments.

The homeland police forces also served as patronage instruments. In homelands where formal employment was scarce, a position in the police service was a coveted source of income and status. Homeland governments used police appointments as rewards for political loyalty, creating forces that were loyal to the homeland leadership rather than to any professional standard of policing. This patronage dynamic — in which police appointments serve political rather than professional purposes — is a feature that did not disappear with the end of apartheid. It was, as the rest of this series will show, inherited by the democratic dispensation and intensified during the state capture era.

:::takeaway
By the end of apartheid, South Africa had twelve separate police forces: the SAP plus eleven homeland forces. These forces collectively employed over 140,000 members, had deeply embedded cultures of impunity and patronage, virtually no detective or forensic capacity oriented toward ordinary crime, and no tradition of civilian oversight. This is what democracy inherited — not a police service that needed improvement, but a control apparatus that needed fundamental transformation.
:::

The homeland forces also compounded the skills deficit that the democratic government would inherit. The SAP, despite its repressive orientation, at least maintained some detective and forensic capacity — concentrated primarily in units that investigated crimes in white communities. The homeland forces, by contrast, had almost no detective capacity at all. Their function was patrol, suppression, and the enforcement of homeland-specific regulations. When all twelve forces were merged into SAPS after 1994, the new service absorbed tens of thousands of officers whose training, skills, and institutional culture were oriented entirely toward population control and political loyalty rather than crime investigation and community policing.

## The 1994 Transition: Democracy's Impossible Inheritance

The negotiated transition to democracy required merging all twelve police forces — the SAP, the four TBVC homeland forces, the six self-governing territory forces, and elements of the liberation movement's self-defence units — into a single South African Police Service. This was one of the most ambitious institutional integration projects in modern democratic transitions, and it was undertaken under extraordinary pressure. Crime was surging. Communities that had been subject to decades of police repression now expected the same police service to protect them. The international community was watching. And the political negotiations that produced the new Constitution required compromises on policing structure that would shape the institution for decades.

The most consequential structural decision was to keep policing as a primarily national function. The Constitution lists "police services" in Schedule 4 Part A as a concurrent competency between national and provincial government, and Section 206 gives provincial executives some oversight role through provincial secretariats and community police forums. But in practice, the 1995 South African Police Service Act established SAPS as a single, national police service under a National Commissioner appointed by the President, with provincial commissioners who reported upward to national headquarters rather than outward to provincial governments.

Why did the ANC prefer centralised national control? The reasons were rooted in the apartheid experience itself. The homeland police forces had demonstrated what could happen when policing was devolved to sub-national governments: each homeland had created its own repressive apparatus, answerable only to the homeland leader. The ANC — which had fought a liberation struggle against a decentralised system of racial oppression — was deeply sceptical of devolving policing power to provinces, some of which (like KwaZulu-Natal under the IFP) had their own political dynamics and potential for creating autonomous security forces. There was also a practical concern: the new government needed to integrate twelve disparate forces into a single institution with uniform standards, and centralisation seemed the most direct path to achieving this.

The irony, which the rest of this series will explore in detail, is that the centralisation decision — made for understandable historical reasons — created exactly the structural vulnerability that the ANC sought to avoid. A single national police service under a single minister, with a single national commissioner appointed by the president, proved to be extraordinarily easy to capture politically. The homeland forces had been captured by homeland leaders; SAPS would later be captured by the national executive. The mechanism was different, but the outcome was structurally similar.

:::expert
Analysis by the African Policing Civilian Oversight Forum (APCOF) and the Civilian Secretariat for Police Service has documented the structural contradictions embedded in the 1994 policing settlement. Their research has shown that the decision to centralise policing under national government, while comprehensible given the apartheid inheritance of fragmented and captured homeland forces, created a governance architecture in which accountability was concentrated at the top of a single hierarchy. When that hierarchy functioned well — as it did, to some degree, during the first decade of democracy — it could drive reform from the centre. But when political leadership prioritised loyalty over competence, the same centralised architecture meant that dysfunction flowed from the top down through the entire service. APCOF's comparative analysis of policing governance in other post-transition democracies — including Northern Ireland, Colombia, and post-conflict states in West Africa — has found that distributed policing governance, with multiple accountability centres, tends to be more resilient to political capture than single-hierarchy models, even when the initial transition favours centralisation for coordination purposes.
:::

The integration process itself was enormously complex. The new SAPS had to absorb officers from forces with vastly different training standards, organisational cultures, and operational practices. SAP officers who had spent their careers enforcing pass laws and suppressing township resistance were now expected to serve the same communities they had previously policed as an occupying force. Homeland police officers who had been appointed through patronage networks were expected to meet professional standards that many had never been trained to achieve. And members of liberation movement self-defence units — who had been on the other side of the conflict — were expected to integrate into a force alongside former adversaries.

The Civilian Secretariat for Police Service was created as an oversight mechanism, tasked with providing civilian policy guidance to SAPS. The Independent Complaints Directorate (ICD) — later reconstituted as the Independent Police Investigative Directorate (IPID) — was established to investigate police misconduct and deaths in police custody. Community Police Forums (CPFs) were created at station level to provide a mechanism for community participation in policing priorities. These were genuine innovations — attempts to build civilian oversight into a policing system that had never had any.

But the reality was that these oversight mechanisms were grafted onto an institution whose fundamental character had not changed. The organisational structure, the rank hierarchy, the training curriculum, the promotion criteria, the deployment patterns, and the internal culture of SAPS remained substantially those of the SAP. The institution was renamed, not rebuilt. Officers were retrained in human rights awareness, but the operational systems in which they worked — the case management processes, the intelligence apparatus, the command-and-control structure — continued to reflect their origins.

## What Democracy Actually Received

When you step back from the political negotiations and the legislative frameworks, what the democratic government actually received was this: a force of over 140,000 members, drawn from twelve separate organisations with incompatible training standards and organisational cultures, carrying deeply embedded habits of impunity and political subservience, with a severe skills deficit in exactly the functions that a democratic police service needs most — detective work, forensic science, community policing, and crime prevention.

The detective capacity was concentrated in the SAP's Criminal Investigation Department, which had historically focused on crimes in white communities. The forensic science capability was rudimentary and would become catastrophically inadequate as DNA evidence and digital forensics became central to modern policing. The community policing skills — the ability to build trust, gather intelligence through cooperation rather than coercion, and prevent crime through engagement rather than suppression — were virtually non-existent, because the SAP had never needed them. Its relationship with the majority of the population had been adversarial by design.

The intelligence apparatus was perhaps the most toxic inheritance. The Security Branch had been disbanded, but its personnel were absorbed into SAPS's Crime Intelligence division. The skills these officers brought — surveillance, infiltration, informant management — were potentially valuable for a democratic police service combating organised crime. But the culture they brought — secrecy, impunity, the instrumental use of intelligence for political purposes — would prove catastrophically damaging. As [From Rainbow Nation to State Capture](/articles/ss-1-2-from-rainbow-nation-to-state-capture) will document, Crime Intelligence became one of the most politically captured components of SAPS during the state capture era, with its secret service account looted and its capabilities redirected toward factional surveillance rather than crime prevention.

And then there was the population itself. Three decades of apartheid policing had left black communities with a deep and justified distrust of the police. The SAP had been the daily face of racial oppression — the officers who demanded your passbook, who raided your home at night, who shot your children in the streets of Soweto, who detained your neighbours without trial. The expectation that communities would suddenly cooperate with the same institution, under a new name and new legislation but with many of the same officers, was ambitious to the point of naivety. Building police legitimacy — the willingness of communities to cooperate with, report to, and trust the police — is the work of generations, not legislative sessions.

:::expert
The Centre for the Study of Violence and Reconciliation (CSVR) has conducted extensive research into the persistence of apartheid-era policing culture within SAPS, particularly through their analyses of police use of force, torture in custody, and the organisational dynamics that sustain these practices. Their work has demonstrated that the problem is not simply the presence of individual officers who served in the apartheid-era SAP — most of whom have by now retired. The problem is institutional: the organisational systems, the informal reward structures, the training methodologies, and the command culture that the SAP established were transmitted to subsequent generations of SAPS officers through institutional socialisation. New recruits entering SAPS after 1994 were trained and socialised within an institution that carried the SAP's DNA, even when the formal training curriculum had been updated. The CSVR has documented, for instance, how the use of violence as a primary policing tool — a direct inheritance of the SAP's approach — persisted in SAPS well into the 2010s and 2020s, particularly in township policing, where the historical dynamic of police-as-occupying-force proved resistant to reform. The Marikana massacre of 2012, in which SAPS officers killed 34 striking miners, was not an aberration — it was an expression of institutional continuity.
:::

## The Structural Mismatch That Defines Everything

Here is the structural problem that this entire series addresses, and it flows directly from the history you have just read. South Africa has a single, centralised, national police service — designed during the colonial era for territorial control, perfected during the apartheid era for racial control, and restructured after 1994 with a new mandate (crime prevention and public safety) but without a new architecture. SAPS is simultaneously too big and too incapable. It has nearly 200,000 members, making it one of the largest police forces in the world relative to population, yet it cannot effectively police the vastly different environments across nine provinces and 257 municipalities — from the gang-controlled Cape Flats to the stock-theft-plagued farms of the Free State to the financial crime corridors of Sandton to the informal settlements of Ekurhuleni where police response times can exceed an hour.

The centralised model means that a single minister and a single national commissioner are responsible for policing outcomes across this entire spectrum. When that minister and commissioner are competent and committed, the centralised model allows for top-down reform. But when they are incompetent, corrupt, or politically captured — as multiple ministers and commissioners have been — the same centralisation transmits dysfunction through the entire system. There is no redundancy, no fallback, no alternative policing tier that can compensate for failure at the centre.

This is the mismatch that the colonial and apartheid inheritance created: a police architecture designed for centralised control, operating in a constitutional framework that envisions three distinctive, interdependent, and interrelated spheres of government. The Constitution's designers created a governance model built on distributed power, checks and balances, and multiple accountability centres. But they left policing — one of the most consequential state functions — in a single-hierarchy, single-point-of-failure architecture that is structurally incompatible with the cooperative governance model.

Understanding this mismatch is the key to everything that follows in this series. The broken pipeline documented in Part 2, the geographic inequality in policing, the remand detention crisis, the failure on gender-based violence, the private security paradox — all of these are downstream consequences of a structural architecture that was designed for a purpose fundamentally different from the one it is now expected to serve.

## The History Decoder: A Framework for the Rest of This Series

Now that you have the historical arc — from colonial policing through apartheid's perfection of the control model to the compromised 1994 transition — you can use it as a decoder ring for every policing problem you encounter in this series and in the news. The framework is simple: when you encounter a feature of today's policing system that is failing, ask whether it is an inherited structural feature or a democratic-era decision.

**Inherited structural features** are those that trace directly to the colonial and apartheid architecture. These include the centralised national command structure (inherited from the Union-era decision to create a single national force, reinforced by the ANC's post-1994 preference for centralisation); the intelligence apparatus and its vulnerability to political capture (inherited from the Security Branch); the absence of meaningful provincial and municipal policing capacity (a consequence of the decision to concentrate policing power nationally); the weak detective and forensic capacity (a legacy of a force that was optimised for pass law enforcement and political surveillance rather than criminal investigation); and the deep distrust between police and communities in formerly oppressed areas (a direct inheritance of decades of adversarial policing).

**Democratic-era decisions** are those that were made after 1994 and that could, in principle, be reversed or reformed through democratic processes. These include the specific appointment decisions that placed politically loyal rather than competent individuals in command positions; the resource allocation choices that determine how many detectives, vehicles, and forensic technicians each station receives; the legislative framework that defines SAPS's powers and accountability mechanisms; and the policy choices that determine training curricula, promotion criteria, and disciplinary procedures.

The distinction matters because inherited structural features generally cannot be fixed through better management, more training, or incremental reform. They require structural redesign — changing the architecture of the system itself. Democratic-era decisions, by contrast, can be changed through the normal mechanisms of democratic governance: new appointments, new legislation, new policies, and sustained political will.

:::framework
**The History Decoder**

When you encounter a policing failure in South Africa, apply this three-step framework:

**Step 1 — Identify the feature.** What specific aspect of the policing system is failing? Is it detective capacity? Community trust? Response times? Intelligence quality? Accountability?

**Step 2 — Trace the origin.** Is this feature inherited from the colonial/apartheid architecture (centralised control, intelligence culture, absence of community policing tradition, weak detective capacity) or is it a product of democratic-era decisions (specific appointments, resource allocation, legislative choices, policy failures)?

**Step 3 — Determine the fix.** Inherited structural features require structural redesign — new institutions, new tiers of policing, new accountability architectures. Democratic-era decisions require political will — better appointments, better legislation, better resource allocation. Confusing the two leads to frustration: you cannot fix a structural problem with a management solution, and you do not need to redesign the system to fix a bad appointment.

This decoder is your essential tool for the rest of the Citizen Safety & Security series. Every article that follows — from the analysis of the broken criminal justice pipeline to the proposal for a three-tier policing architecture — builds on this distinction between structural inheritance and democratic-era choice.
:::

The next article in this series, [From Rainbow Nation to State Capture](/articles/ss-1-2-from-rainbow-nation-to-state-capture), picks up the story from 1994 and carries it through to the present — showing how the post-apartheid crime explosion overwhelmed a police service already struggling with its inheritance, and how the state capture era then deliberately degraded the institutions that were supposed to deliver justice. The inherited structural weaknesses you now understand made SAPS vulnerable to both crises. The democratic-era decisions made during those crises either compounded or, in rare cases, partially addressed the vulnerabilities. Understanding which is which — using the History Decoder — is how you move from frustration to informed engagement with the system that is supposed to keep you safe.
