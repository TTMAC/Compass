## Earning Money and Keeping It Are Two Different Capabilities

Suppose the conversion problem this Part has been tracing were solved. Suppose the person at the centre of these articles — healthy, schooled, and until now unable to find work — gets a job. The skills gap is bridged, the formal labour market absorbs her, or the informal route opens and she builds a small enterprise. She is earning. Is the development story complete?

It is not, and the reason is the subject of this final article in the Part. Earning an income is a flow — money arriving and, for most households, leaving again almost as fast as it comes. Wealth is a stock — assets that persist, that generate security, that can be drawn on in a crisis, borrowed against to start an enterprise, and passed to the next generation. Deprivation does not end at the moment income begins. It ends when income converts into assets — when a household crosses from living paycheck to paycheck into owning something that outlasts the paycheck. And that conversion, like every other conversion in this Part, can succeed or fail. In South Africa, for the majority, it fails.

This is why the capability to earn, which the [opening article](/articles/hd-4-1-the-conversion-problem) established as the missing third leg of human development, has a second storey that is easy to overlook. The full capability is not merely to earn but to _accumulate_ — to convert a flow of income into a stock of security. A country can raise incomes and still leave the deep structure of deprivation intact if those incomes never become assets. And South Africa has a particular, historically manufactured reason why they do not, which connects this article back to the [problem this series named but could not solve in a single Part](/articles/hd-1-4-the-problem-that-has-no-name): the country's wealth is even more unequally distributed than its income, and that wealth gap reproduces itself across generations with a persistence that income statistics conceal.

## Income Is a Flow; Wealth Is a Stock — and the Gap Between Them Is Racial

Begin with the distinction that organises everything that follows, because South African public debate persistently blurs it. When we discuss inequality, we almost always mean income inequality — the gap between what people earn. Income inequality in South Africa is severe; by most measures the country has among the highest income Gini coefficients in the world. But income inequality, severe as it is, understates the real structure of advantage, because _wealth_ inequality is dramatically worse.

The research on South African wealth — most rigorously assembled by economists working with tax data and the World Inequality Database — finds that the wealthiest tenth of the population owns somewhere around 85 per cent of all wealth, and the wealthiest fraction of that tenth owns a large share again. The wealth Gini coefficient sits above 0.9, close to the theoretical maximum of total concentration. By comparison, the income Gini, around 0.63, looks almost moderate. The implication is stark: the bottom half of South Africans, taken together, own essentially nothing — no meaningful financial assets, no property equity, no retirement savings, often a net negative position once debt is counted. They may earn; they do not own.

This gap is not race-neutral, and there is no honest way to discuss it as though it were. The concentration of wealth maps onto the racial structure that a century of dispossession produced and that thirty years of democracy has shifted at the margins but not at the core. The [Problem That Has No Name](/articles/hd-1-4-the-problem-that-has-no-name) traced how the apartheid state built and protected white asset accumulation while systematically preventing black asset accumulation — through land dispossession, the denial of property rights, the destruction of black business, and exclusion from the financial system. Income can change within a generation; a person who gets a good job earns a good income immediately. Wealth changes across generations, because it is accumulated, inherited, and compounded over decades. This is why the income gap has narrowed somewhat since 1994 while the wealth gap has barely moved: the mechanisms that build wealth are slow, and the head start that apartheid engineered is, by the nature of compounding, self-perpetuating.

For the conversion problem, this means the finish line is further away than income figures suggest. Closing the income gap — getting people into earning work — is necessary but not sufficient. Without the further conversion of income into assets, a generation can earn more than its parents and still own as little, leaving the deep structure of advantage and disadvantage intact. The capability to accumulate is the capability that actually dismantles inherited deprivation, and it is the one South Africa has done least to build.

:::takeaway
South Africa's wealth inequality is far worse than its income inequality: the top tenth owns roughly 85 per cent of all wealth, the wealth Gini exceeds 0.9, and the bottom half owns essentially nothing. Income can change within a generation; wealth accumulates and compounds across generations, which is why the racial wealth gap has barely moved since 1994 even as the income gap narrowed. The capability to earn is incomplete without the capability to accumulate — and accumulation is precisely what apartheid engineered the majority out of.
:::

## The Debt Trap: When the Flow Runs Backwards

For many South African households, the problem is not merely that income fails to become assets. It is that income is captured, before it can accumulate, by debt — so that the flow runs backwards, and a household with a wage is left with less than nothing. To understand the wealth gap, you have to understand the debt trap, because for the indebted, every rand earned is a rand already owed.

South African households are, in aggregate, heavily indebted: household debt has long run at well over 60 per cent of disposable income, and for lower-income borrowers the burden is far heavier. The credit regulator tracks tens of millions of credit-active consumers, a large share of whom have impaired records — meaning they are behind on payments, in default, or subject to legal action. The texture of this indebtedness matters. Much of it is _unsecured_ credit — personal loans, store cards, and short-term lending not backed by an asset — carrying high interest rates and taken on not to build wealth but to bridge the gap between an income that does not stretch and a cost of living that does not bend. Debt taken to buy a home or start a business can build wealth; debt taken to survive the month destroys it.

At the harshest end sit two mechanisms that deserve naming. The first is the abuse of the emolument attachment order — the "garnishee" order — by which a lender obtains a court instruction to deduct repayments directly from a borrower's wages before she ever sees them. Investigations and litigation over the past decade exposed how these orders were, in many cases, obtained improperly and stacked so heavily that workers were left with a fraction of their earnings, the rest captured at source by creditors. A landmark court ruling tightened the rules, but the underlying vulnerability — a low-wage worker whose pay can be intercepted before it reaches her — remains. The second is the informal moneylender, the _mashonisa_, who extends credit in cash-strapped communities at interest rates that compound punishingly and is enforced not through courts but through the retention of bank cards, identity documents, and social pressure. For a household with no savings buffer and no access to affordable formal credit, the mashonisa is often the only lender available, and the most expensive.

The point, in the language of this series, is that financial capability is not primarily a matter of personal virtue, and treating it as one is both unkind and analytically wrong. A worker whose wages are garnisheed at source, or who must borrow from a mashonisa to eat before payday, is not failing a test of self-discipline. She is operating inside a financial system whose design, in its harsher corners, extracts from the poor rather than building them up. The conversion of income into assets cannot begin while the flow is being captured by debt — and for millions of earning South Africans, it is.

:::expert[The Garnishee Order Investigations and the National Credit Regulator]
The scale of South Africa's debt problem became visible through a sequence of investigations and legal actions in the 2010s. Research drawing on the events at Marikana documented that many mineworkers were severely over-indebted, with multiple unsecured loans and emolument attachment orders deducting large portions of their wages — a factor in the desperation underlying that tragedy. The University of Stellenbosch Legal Aid Clinic, acting for over-indebted workers, brought a constitutional challenge that culminated in a 2016 Constitutional Court judgment requiring judicial oversight of emolument attachment orders and curbing the worst abuses by which orders had been obtained in distant jurisdictions without proper scrutiny. The National Credit Regulator, established under the National Credit Act of 2005, was meant to prevent reckless lending and over-indebtedness, and the Act did introduce affordability assessments and debt-review mechanisms. But the persistence of high unsecured lending, impaired credit records across a large share of credit-active consumers, and the continued reach of informal lenders demonstrate the limits of regulation against a structural demand for credit driven by incomes that do not cover the cost of living. The lesson is that the debt trap is downstream of the conversion problem: people borrow to survive because earning is insufficient or absent, and the borrowing then forecloses the accumulation that would otherwise build security.
:::

## Banked but Not Built: The Hollow Version of Financial Inclusion

South Africa appears, on one common measure, to be a financial-inclusion success. A large majority of adults — well over three-quarters — have a bank account, a far higher share than in most of sub-Saharan Africa. The expansion was driven substantially by the payment of social grants into bank accounts and by basic transactional account products designed for the mass market. By the headline metric, most South Africans are inside the financial system.

But inclusion measured by account ownership is a hollow version of the thing that matters. An account into which a grant or a wage is deposited and from which it is immediately withdrawn or spent is a _transactional_ relationship with the financial system, not a _wealth-building_ one. The capability that converts income into assets requires more than an account: it requires access to safe, affordable savings vehicles that earn a real return; to credit priced for building rather than extracting; to insurance that protects accumulated assets against shocks; and to investment products that let small savings compound over time. On these deeper measures of inclusion, the majority remains effectively outside. They can receive and spend money through the formal system. They cannot easily use it to build.

It is worth honouring, here, the indigenous institution that millions of South Africans built to do what the formal system did not: the _stokvel_. These rotating savings and credit associations — collective savings clubs in which members contribute regularly and draw out in turn or at agreed times — hold tens of billions of rands and count millions of members. They are a genuine, self-organised wealth-building mechanism, rooted in trust and community, that emerged precisely because the formal financial system excluded or underserved the people who created them. The stokvel demonstrates the same thing the [taxi industry demonstrated in the previous article](/articles/hd-4-3-the-missing-informal-economy): the capability and the will to accumulate are present in abundance; what has been missing is an enabling formal environment that meets people where they are. A financial system serious about closing the wealth gap would build on the stokvel logic rather than around it.

## Dead Capital: The Asset That Cannot Be Used

Even where the post-1994 state did transfer assets to the poor, the conversion into usable wealth has often stalled — and the largest example is also the most instructive. Since 1994, the state has delivered millions of subsidised houses, one of the more substantial asset transfers attempted anywhere in the developing world. In principle, a house is the foundational asset of a middle-class balance sheet: a store of value, a hedge against rising rents, and collateral against which to borrow to start a business or educate a child. The housing programme should have been a wealth-building engine of historic scale.

In practice, much of that housing became what the economist Hernando de Soto called "dead capital" — assets that cannot be fully used as assets because the legal and market machinery to activate them is missing. Large numbers of subsidised houses were occupied for years without their owners receiving registered title deeds, leaving a backlog of homes whose occupants could not prove ownership, sell legally, or borrow against the property. Many such houses sit in areas with thin or non-existent property markets, so that even with title, the asset cannot easily be converted to cash or leveraged. A house you cannot sell, cannot mortgage, and cannot prove you own delivers shelter — a real and important benefit — but it does not deliver the wealth-building function that home ownership performs for those higher up the ladder. The asset exists; the capability to use it as an asset does not.

The same pattern of incomplete conversion appears in retirement savings. Formal pension and provident fund coverage reaches only a portion of the workforce, and among those it reaches, the preservation problem is severe: when workers change jobs or are retrenched, many cash out their accumulated retirement savings to meet immediate needs, dissolving decades of compounding in a single withdrawal. The reforms intended to encourage preservation are recent and partial. The result is that even South Africans who earn formal incomes and contribute to retirement funds frequently arrive at old age with little, dependent on the state old-age grant — the same grant that the [first article in this Part](/articles/hd-4-1-the-conversion-problem) identified as the state substituting a transfer for a conversion the economy and the financial system failed to perform.

## Black Tax and the Compounding of Disadvantage

There is a further mechanism, specific to South Africa's structure of advantage, that drains accumulation from precisely the households trying hardest to build it — and it deserves naming because it is so often experienced privately as a personal burden rather than recognised as a structural feature. It is widely known as "black tax": the obligation that falls on a newly earning member of a poor extended family to support parents, siblings, and others who have no income of their own.

The dynamic is a direct consequence of the conversion problem operating across a whole family network. When one person in an extended family finally converts capability into a formal income — the first graduate, the first to land a stable job — that income does not accrue to her alone. It is, in effect, the family's only conversion success, and it must stretch to cover the consumption needs of relatives whom the broken labour market has left without livelihoods. The earner supports the unemployed siblings, the parents without adequate pensions, the school fees of nieces and nephews. This is an act of solidarity and often of love, and it is also, in cold balance-sheet terms, a transfer that prevents the earner from accumulating. The white peer earning the same salary, more likely to come from a family with its own assets and incomes, keeps her income to build her own position; the black peer redistributes hers across a network of dependents. Two people with identical incomes thus accumulate at entirely different rates, and the wealth gap reproduces itself one salary at a time, through a mechanism that looks like private generosity but is in fact the privatised cost of a public failure to create enough livelihoods.

This is how the racial wealth gap perpetuates itself even as the income gap narrows. It is not mysterious, and it is not primarily about financial literacy or spending habits. It is about the head start that apartheid built into white balance sheets, the absence of inherited assets on the other side, and the way the broken conversion machinery forces successful earners to fund their families' survival rather than their own accumulation. Income mobility without wealth mobility leaves the deep structure intact, which is exactly what South Africa has experienced.

## What Building the Capability Would Look Like

It would be easy to read this article as a counsel of despair — the wealth gap is historical, compounding, and self-perpetuating, and individual thrift cannot close it. But other societies have deliberately built asset-holding among populations that started with nothing, and their methods are instructive precisely because they treat accumulation as something policy can engineer rather than something that must wait generations to occur naturally.

The most relevant comparator is Singapore, which made mass asset ownership a deliberate instrument of nation-building. Through the Central Provident Fund — a mandatory, preserved savings system — and a public housing programme that channelled those savings into home ownership, Singapore took a population that in the 1960s was largely propertyless and, within two generations, turned the overwhelming majority into homeowners with accumulated retirement balances. The design lessons are pointed for South Africa: savings were _preserved_ rather than easily withdrawn, the transferred housing came with _real, tradable title_ rather than dead capital, and the system was _universal_ rather than a thin safety net. Singapore engineered the conversion from income to assets that South Africa has left to chance.

A different family of approaches, tried in various forms in the United States and the United Kingdom, builds assets from birth. "Child development accounts" or "baby bonds" endow every child — or every poor child — with a small, growing, locked savings account that matures into a stake at adulthood: seed capital for education, a home deposit, or an enterprise. The logic is to interrupt the intergenerational transmission of the wealth gap at its source, by ensuring that no child arrives at adulthood with a balance sheet of exactly zero. Matched-savings schemes, which add public funds to every rand a poor household saves, similarly use policy to accelerate accumulation rather than waiting for it. None of these is a silver bullet, and each has limits and costs. But together they make a point this Part insists on: the failure of South Africans to accumulate wealth is not a fixed fact of poverty. It is the result of a system that was designed, over a century, to prevent black accumulation and has not yet been deliberately redesigned to enable it. What was engineered can be re-engineered.

## Completing the Capability: From This Article to the Cycle

This article closes the Part, and it is worth stepping back to see the whole arc it completes. The [first article](/articles/hd-4-1-the-conversion-problem) established that health and education are capabilities that must convert into the functioning of a livelihood, and that South Africa's conversion machinery is broken across personal, social, and environmental factors. The [second](/articles/hd-4-2-skills-beyond-schooling) examined the personal layer — the gap between a qualification and a usable skill. The [third](/articles/hd-4-3-the-missing-informal-economy) examined the social layer — the foreclosed informal economy that leaves the unemployed without a fallback. This article has examined the deepest layer: the conversion that should happen _after_ income is earned, from flow to stock, from earning to owning, and the historical and structural reasons it fails.

Together these four articles supply the third leg of human development that the [opening of this series](/articles/hd-1-1-beyond-gdp) promised but the Health and Education Parts could not provide on their own: the capability to earn and to accumulate. With that leg in place, the [reinforcing cycle](/articles/hd-1-3-the-reinforcing-cycle) can finally be reasoned about in full. The Part that follows this one, [The Reinforcing System](/articles/hd-5-1-when-the-cycle-works), asks how safety, growth, and development lock together into virtuous or vicious cycles — and it can only do that now that all three human-development capabilities are on the table. A population that is healthy, educated, _and_ able to convert those endowments into livelihoods and assets is a population that reinforces the cycle: it pays tax, sustains demand, accumulates the security that underwrites stability, and passes advantage rather than deprivation to the next generation. A population stuck at the conversion stage — healthy, educated, and unable to earn or to own — feeds the vicious version instead. The capability to accumulate is, in the end, what allows the gains of one generation to become the foundation of the next, which is the very definition of development succeeding rather than merely cycling in place.

## What This Means for You

The wealth gap is a structural inheritance, and no individual can close it alone. But the conversion from income to assets is also, more than any other stage in this Part, a place where knowledge and deliberate practice change individual outcomes — and where collective demand can change the system.

If you are earning an income, however modest, the single most consequential idea in this article is that earning and accumulating are different capabilities, and the second one is partly learnable. Protecting even a small, regular flow from the debt trap and directing it toward a genuine store of value — a stokvel, a low-cost savings or investment vehicle, a retirement contribution you do not cash out when you change jobs — is the mechanism by which a flow becomes a stock. The amounts can be small; the discipline of preservation and the patience to let it compound are what matter. This is not a moral lecture about thrift to people with too little; it is a technical point about which actions actually build security and which merely move money through.

If you are carrying debt, understand the difference between debt that builds and debt that extracts. Debt to buy an appreciating asset or fund a real enterprise can build wealth; unsecured debt to bridge the month destroys it. Know your rights against the abuses described here — improper garnishee orders, reckless lending, the documents a mashonisa may not lawfully hold — because the financial system's harsher corners rely on borrowers not knowing them. Debt counselling and the protections of the National Credit Act exist precisely for the over-indebted.

If you carry black tax, the reframing offered here is meant to be a relief rather than a reproach. The strain you feel is not a personal failure of budgeting; it is the privatised cost of a public failure to create enough livelihoods, landing on you because you are the family's conversion success. Naming it accurately will not lighten the obligation, but it locates the problem where it belongs — in the broken machinery, not in you — and it clarifies that the long-term answer is more earners in the family, which loops back to everything this Part has argued.

If you are a citizen and a voter, the demand that follows from this Part is more specific than the usual call to "fix the economy." It is a demand that policy treat the capability to _accumulate_, not only to earn, as a development goal: title deeds delivered so that transferred assets become usable wealth; a financial system that builds the poor rather than extracting from them; protection against predatory debt; and, above all, enough livelihoods that the burden of supporting the jobless stops falling on the few who earn. Ask of any economic programme not only "will it create jobs?" but "will it let ordinary people keep and build what they earn?" That question is the one this Part was written to help you ask — and the answer to it is what will determine whether a generation that earns more than its parents finally owns more as well.

:::takeaway
Development does not end when income begins; it ends when income becomes assets. South Africa's wealth gap, far deeper than its income gap and barely moved since 1994, persists because the conversion from earning to owning is broken — by a debt trap that runs the flow backwards, a financial system that includes the poor transactionally but not as builders, transferred housing left as dead capital, dissolved retirement savings, and the black tax that drains accumulation from the family's few earners. Completing the capability to earn means building the capability to accumulate — and that is the third leg of human development on which the reinforcing cycle finally stands.
:::

:::framework
**The Income-to-Assets Checklist**

Use this framework to assess whether an income — your own, a household's, or a community programme's beneficiaries' — is actually converting into wealth, or merely passing through. Each item marks a point where the conversion commonly breaks.

**1. Is the flow protected from extractive debt?**
Before income can accumulate, it must survive the month without being captured. Audit debt for the build-versus-extract distinction: secured, asset-building debt is one thing; high-interest unsecured debt and informal lending are another. Stopping the backward flow comes before building the forward one.

**2. Is there a genuine store of value, not just an account?**
Account ownership is not accumulation. Is some portion of income reaching a vehicle that actually preserves and grows value — a stokvel, a low-cost savings or investment product, a preserved retirement contribution? A transactional account that empties each month builds nothing.

**3. Are owned assets legally usable?**
For any asset the household holds — especially a house — is ownership registered and provable, and is there a market in which it could be sold or borrowed against? An asset that cannot be proven, sold, or leveraged is shelter or use, not wealth (the dead-capital problem).

**4. Is long-term, compounding saving preserved?**
The enemy of accumulation is premature withdrawal. Is retirement or long-term saving being preserved across job changes and shocks, or cashed out? Compounding only works if the stock is left to grow.

**5. Is accumulation surviving the family network?**
Where black tax applies, recognise it explicitly in the household plan rather than letting it silently consume every surplus. The structural answer is more earners; the interim one is honest accounting of what solidarity costs and deliberate protection of some accumulation alongside it.
:::
