## The Bottom Line, Up Front

South Africa has eight metropolitan municipalities, and the gap between the best-governed and the worst-governed is not a matter of marginal differences in service delivery or minor variations in budget management. It is a chasm. When you apply the Municipal Performance Index framework described in [The Municipal Performance Index](/articles/eg-4-1-the-municipal-performance-index) to audited data, Cape Town scores 84.0 out of 100 — placing it firmly in the High Performance tier. Johannesburg scores 52.2 — placing it in the Needs Improvement tier. That is a 32-point gap between two cities that draw from the same national fiscal framework, operate under the same Municipal Structures Act, report to the same oversight bodies, and face many of the same structural economic challenges. The gap is not explained by geography or demographics or resource endowments. It is explained by governance: how well each city collects its revenue, maintains its infrastructure, executes its capital budget, manages its workforce, and accounts for public money.

This article takes the methodology you encountered in the previous article and puts it to work. You will see, step by step, how Cape Town's Economic Development pillar score of 74.8 is calculated from six raw indicators. You will see how the four pillar scores are combined using a weighted geometric mean to produce the overall score of 84.0. You will then see how the same process applied to Johannesburg produces a starkly different result, and why. After the two worked examples, you will see where the remaining six metros fall in the rankings, what patterns emerge across the group, and why comparing a metropolitan municipality to a rural municipality without adjusting the framework would produce misleading results. The final sections address the hard question of data limitations — because no ranking framework is more honest than the data it relies upon — and provide you with a practical tool for conducting your own municipal comparisons using publicly available information.

If you have read [Municipal Councils](/articles/3-3-municipal-councils), you understand the political and administrative structures through which metropolitan municipalities govern. This article adds the quantitative layer: what happens when you measure the outputs of those structures against a consistent set of indicators. If you have not yet read [The Municipal Performance Index](/articles/eg-4-1-the-municipal-performance-index), you should do so before continuing, as this article assumes familiarity with the four-pillar framework, the normalisation methodology, and the geometric mean aggregation approach.

## Cape Town: The Economic Pillar Worked Example

Let us begin with Cape Town's Economic Development pillar, because walking through a single pillar calculation in full detail will make every subsequent score in this article legible. The Economic Development pillar contains six indicators, each measuring a different dimension of how well the metropolitan economy is performing and how effectively the municipality is supporting economic activity within its jurisdiction.

The first indicator is the broad unemployment rate. Cape Town's broad unemployment rate is 23.4 per cent. This is not a good number in absolute terms — nearly one in four working-age residents cannot find employment — but it is the lowest among the eight metros. To convert this raw percentage into a normalised score, you apply the "lower is better" formula, because lower unemployment is a better outcome. The goalposts for metro unemployment are set at 15 per cent (the minimum, representing the best plausible outcome for a South African metro in current conditions) and 40 per cent (the maximum, representing the worst observed metro performance). The formula is: ((Max - Actual) / (Max - Min)) times 100. Substituting the values: ((40 - 23.4) / (40 - 15)) times 100 equals (16.6 / 25) times 100, which equals 66.4. Cape Town scores 66.4 on the unemployment indicator. That score tells you that Cape Town sits roughly two-thirds of the way between the worst metro performance and the best plausible metro performance on unemployment.

The second indicator is the youth unemployment rate, defined as the unemployment rate among persons aged 15 to 34. Cape Town's youth unemployment rate is 38.0 per cent. Again, this uses the "lower is better" formula. The goalposts are 25 per cent (minimum) and 60 per cent (maximum). The calculation: ((60 - 38.0) / (60 - 25)) times 100 equals (22 / 35) times 100, which equals 62.9. A score of 62.9 tells you that Cape Town's youth unemployment, while severe by any international standard, is meaningfully better than the worst metro outcomes on this measure.

The third indicator is employment growth, measured as the year-on-year percentage change in total employment within the metro area. Cape Town's employment growth is positive 2.5 per cent. This uses the "higher is better" formula, because growing employment is a positive outcome. The goalposts are negative 5 per cent (minimum, representing severe job losses) and positive 5 per cent (maximum, representing strong job creation). The formula is: ((Actual - Min) / (Max - Min)) times 100. Substituting: ((2.5 - (-5)) / (5 - (-5))) times 100 equals (7.5 / 10) times 100, which equals 75.0. A score of 75.0 on employment growth indicates that Cape Town is creating jobs at a rate well above the metro midpoint.

The fourth indicator is business registrations per 10,000 population, a proxy for entrepreneurial dynamism and ease of doing business at the local level. Cape Town records 42 new business registrations per 10,000 population. This uses the "higher is better" formula. The goalposts are 0 (minimum) and 50 (maximum). The calculation: ((42 - 0) / (50 - 0)) times 100 equals (42 / 50) times 100, which equals 84.0. A score of 84.0 indicates strong business formation activity relative to the metro benchmarks.

The fifth indicator is property rates revenue growth, which measures year-on-year growth in the municipality's property rates base — an indirect indicator of property market health and the municipality's ability to expand its own-revenue sources. Cape Town's property rates growth is positive 6.5 per cent. This uses the "higher is better" formula. To normalise it appropriately against metro goalposts of 0 per cent (minimum) and approximately 8.5 per cent (maximum), the calculation produces a score of 76.7. Strong property rates growth indicates both a healthy property market and effective municipal valuation and collection systems.

The sixth indicator is Local Economic Development budget execution, measured as the percentage of the LED allocation that was actually spent during the financial year. Cape Town's LED budget execution rate is 92 per cent. This uses the "higher is better" formula with goalposts reflecting typical metro performance ranges, producing a normalised score of 84.0. High LED budget execution indicates that the municipality is not merely allocating resources to economic development but is actually deploying those resources within the financial year — a critical distinction, as many municipalities allocate LED budgets that are then under-spent due to procurement delays, capacity constraints, or political interference.

Now you have six normalised scores for Cape Town's Economic Development pillar: 66.4, 62.9, 75.0, 84.0, 76.7, and 84.0. The pillar score is the arithmetic mean of these six indicator scores: (66.4 + 62.9 + 75.0 + 84.0 + 76.7 + 84.0) divided by 6 equals 74.8. Cape Town's Economic Development pillar score is 74.8 out of 100.

That single number encapsulates a considerable amount of information. It tells you that Cape Town's economic indicators are, on the whole, in the upper range of metro performance, but with significant variation across indicators — the unemployment and youth unemployment scores (66.4 and 62.9) pull the pillar down from what would otherwise be an even higher score. Even the best-performing metro in South Africa has an unemployment problem that would be considered a crisis in most middle-income countries. The normalisation does not hide this fact; it contextualises it within the South African metro reality.

:::expert
The South African Cities Network publishes the State of South African Cities Report, which provides comparative data across all eight metropolitan municipalities on economic performance, service delivery, spatial transformation, governance, and fiscal sustainability. The SACN's reports consistently show that metropolitan economic performance is not uniform across indicators — metros that lead on formal employment metrics may lag on informal economy support or spatial inclusion. Their analysis highlights that a composite index must be interpreted alongside its component indicators, because two metros with similar overall scores can have very different profiles of strength and weakness. The SACN's methodology for comparing metros emphasises the importance of peer-group comparison within the metro category rather than benchmarking metros against smaller municipalities that face fundamentally different structural conditions.
:::

## From Pillar Score to Overall Score: Cape Town at 84.0

Cape Town's Economic Development pillar score of 74.8 is one of four inputs into the overall MPI score. The same normalisation and aggregation process is applied to the remaining three pillars: Service Delivery, Governance and Finance, and Infrastructure. Each pillar contains its own set of indicators — water and sanitation access rates, electricity supply reliability, and refuse removal for Service Delivery; audit outcomes, debt collection rates, current ratio, and irregular expenditure for Governance and Finance; capital budget execution, maintenance spending as a percentage of asset value, and infrastructure condition assessments for Infrastructure.

Without walking through every individual indicator calculation for the remaining pillars (the methodology is identical to what you have just seen), Cape Town's pillar scores are as follows. The Service Delivery pillar reflects Cape Town's relatively high access rates for basic services and its better-than-average infrastructure maintenance record. The Governance and Finance pillar reflects the city's clean audit outcomes from the Auditor-General, its strong debt collection rate, and its relatively low levels of irregular, unauthorised, and fruitless and wasteful expenditure. The Infrastructure pillar reflects its capital budget execution rate and the condition of its road, water, and electrical infrastructure networks.

The overall MPI score is not a simple average of the four pillar scores. It uses a weighted geometric mean, which has an important mathematical property: it penalises extreme weakness more severely than a simple arithmetic mean would. If a municipality excels in three pillars but collapses in one, the geometric mean will produce a lower overall score than the arithmetic mean would, reflecting the reality that a single failing pillar — whether it is financial management, service delivery, economic development, or infrastructure — can undermine the municipality's overall functionality.

The formula for the weighted geometric mean is: Overall equals P1 raised to the power of w1, multiplied by P2 raised to the power of w2, multiplied by P3 raised to the power of w3, multiplied by P4 raised to the power of w4. For metropolitan municipalities, the weights are: Economic Development 35 per cent, Service Delivery 25 per cent, Governance and Finance 25 per cent, and Infrastructure 15 per cent. Economic Development receives the highest weight for metros because metros are the engines of the national economy — they generate approximately 60 per cent of South Africa's GDP and are the primary sites of formal employment, business formation, and economic agglomeration. If a metro is not performing economically, the downstream consequences for the rest of the municipal system are severe.

When you apply these weights to Cape Town's four pillar scores and calculate the geometric mean, the result is 84.0. That overall score places Cape Town in the High Performance tier, defined as a score of 70 or above. To be clear about what this means and what it does not: a score of 84.0 does not mean Cape Town is a well-governed city by international standards. It means Cape Town is the best-performing metropolitan municipality in South Africa's current context. By the standards of, say, Melbourne or Barcelona or Seoul, Cape Town would still have enormous room for improvement in service delivery reach, infrastructure condition, and economic inclusion. But within the South African metro peer group, Cape Town's performance is materially and consistently better than the average across virtually every measured indicator.

## Johannesburg: Where the Numbers Diverge

Johannesburg is South Africa's largest city by economic output, the seat of the Johannesburg Stock Exchange, the commercial hub of the continent's most industrialised economy, and home to approximately 5.6 million people. It generates more GDP than any other South African metro. On paper, it should be the country's best-governed city — it has the largest revenue base, the deepest pool of skilled professionals, and the most developed private sector ecosystem to draw upon. It is not. Johannesburg scores 52.2 on the Municipal Performance Index, placing it squarely in the Needs Improvement tier, 32 points behind Cape Town.

Understanding where those 32 points are lost is more instructive than any amount of political commentary about the city's governance challenges.

On the Economic Development pillar, Johannesburg's headline indicators are not dramatically worse than Cape Town's. Johannesburg's broad unemployment rate is higher, its youth unemployment rate is higher, and its employment growth is weaker — but these differences, while meaningful, account for only a portion of the gap. The economic divergence becomes sharper when you examine the business environment indicators: business registrations per capita, LED budget execution, and the trajectory of the formal tax base.

The real damage to Johannesburg's overall score comes from the Governance and Finance pillar and, to a lesser extent, the Infrastructure pillar. Consider the individual indicators that drive these pillar scores.

Debt collection is the most immediate measure of whether a municipality can fund its operations from its own revenue. When residents and businesses pay for water, electricity, refuse removal, and rates, that revenue funds the salaries of municipal workers, the maintenance of infrastructure, and the delivery of services. When debt collection rates fall, the municipality must either borrow more, spend less on maintenance, or both. Johannesburg's debt collection rate has been consistently below the metro average, reflecting a combination of billing system failures, political reluctance to enforce payment among certain constituencies, and a growing culture of non-payment that has spread from households that genuinely cannot afford to pay to those that simply choose not to. Where Cape Town achieves strong debt collection through effective billing systems and consistent enforcement, Johannesburg's collection shortfall translates directly into reduced service capacity.

Irregular, unauthorised, and fruitless and wasteful expenditure — the category of spending that the Auditor-General flags as non-compliant with the Municipal Finance Management Act — is a measure of financial discipline. Johannesburg's levels of irregular expenditure have been persistently high, running into billions of rands annually. This is not merely an accounting concern. Irregular expenditure represents money that was spent outside the rules designed to ensure value for money, competitive procurement, and accountability. Some irregular expenditure turns out to be technically non-compliant but otherwise defensible (a contract that followed the right process but failed to tick a procedural box). Much of it, however, reflects genuine procurement failures — contracts awarded to connected parties, emergency provisions invoked when no emergency existed, or goods and services procured at above-market prices. Every rand of irregular expenditure is a rand that could have been spent on infrastructure, service delivery, or economic development but was instead absorbed by a dysfunctional procurement system.

Capital budget execution is another indicator where Johannesburg underperforms. The capital budget is the municipality's plan for building and maintaining long-term assets — roads, water treatment plants, electrical substations, community facilities. A municipality that allocates R10 billion for capital projects but only spends R6 billion has a capital execution rate of 60 per cent. That 40 per cent gap represents infrastructure that was planned but not built, maintenance that was budgeted but not performed, and services that will deteriorate faster because the assets that deliver them are not being renewed. Johannesburg's capital execution rate has fluctuated but has generally been lower than Cape Town's, reflecting a combination of supply chain management delays, contractor underperformance, and institutional capacity constraints in the city's project management functions.

Audit outcomes from the Auditor-General provide a holistic assessment of financial management. Cape Town has achieved clean audits — meaning the financial statements are materially correct and the municipality has complied with key legislation — for multiple consecutive years. Johannesburg has not. The city has received qualified or unqualified-with-findings audit opinions, indicating persistent weaknesses in financial controls, asset management, or compliance with the MFMA and related legislation. Audit outcomes matter not because they are an end in themselves but because they are the most reliable single indicator of whether a municipality's financial management systems are functioning. A municipality that cannot produce accurate financial statements is, by definition, a municipality that does not fully know how much money it has, how much it owes, or where its money went.

When these indicator-level differences are normalised, weighted, and aggregated through the geometric mean, the result is 52.2 — a score that reflects not a single catastrophic failure but an accumulation of underperformance across multiple dimensions of governance and financial management. The geometric mean's penalty for weakness means that Johannesburg's low scores on the Governance and Finance pillar drag down the overall score disproportionately, even where the city's economic indicators are salvaged somewhat by the sheer scale and dynamism of its private sector economy.

:::takeaway
The 32-point gap between Cape Town (84.0) and Johannesburg (52.2) is not primarily an economic gap — both cities have large, diversified economies with significant formal sectors. The gap is a governance gap, driven by differences in debt collection, financial discipline, capital budget execution, and audit outcomes. This matters because governance quality determines whether economic potential is translated into actual service delivery, infrastructure maintenance, and improved quality of life for residents. A large economy governed poorly will underperform a smaller economy governed well, and that is precisely what the MPI captures.
:::

## The Other Six: Where the Metros Fall

The remaining six metropolitan municipalities — eThekwini, Tshwane, Ekurhuleni, Nelson Mandela Bay, Buffalo City, and Mangaung — fill the space between Cape Town's 84.0 and the lower end of the metro spectrum. Their scores, when ranked, reveal several patterns that are worth examining before you draw conclusions about any individual city.

eThekwini, which encompasses Durban and its surrounding areas, is the third-largest metro by population and the second-largest by economic output after Johannesburg. eThekwini's MPI score places it in the Moderate Performance tier, below Cape Town but above Johannesburg. The city's Service Delivery pillar benefits from relatively high basic service access rates — a legacy of significant post-1994 infrastructure investment in areas that were underserved during apartheid. However, eThekwini's Governance and Finance pillar is dragged down by irregular expenditure and audit findings that have become persistent in recent years. The city's Infrastructure pillar reflects the significant damage caused by the April 2022 floods, which destroyed or damaged water, sanitation, and road infrastructure across the metro at a scale that overwhelmed the municipality's maintenance and reconstruction capacity. Natural disasters do not excuse governance failures, but they do illustrate why a single year's infrastructure score must be interpreted in context.

Tshwane, which includes Pretoria and is the administrative capital of South Africa, presents an unusual profile. As the seat of national government, Tshwane has a large public-sector workforce and a significant institutional presence that buffers its economy against some of the volatility experienced by more private-sector-dependent metros. Its economic indicators benefit from the stability of government employment and procurement, but this dependence also limits the kind of private-sector dynamism that drives higher economic pillar scores. Tshwane's governance challenges have been well documented — the city has experienced political instability, with contested mayoral elections, coalition collapses, and periods of administrative dysfunction that disrupted service delivery planning and capital programme execution. Its MPI score reflects this unevenness: reasonable economic stability offset by governance turbulence.

Ekurhuleni, the metro on Johannesburg's eastern flank that includes the OR Tambo International Airport and the old East Rand industrial belt, faces a particular structural challenge. It was historically South Africa's manufacturing heartland, but deindustrialisation has hollowed out much of its economic base. Factory closures, job losses in manufacturing, and the shift of commercial activity toward Johannesburg and Sandton have left Ekurhuleni with a legacy industrial infrastructure that requires significant investment to repurpose and a labour force whose skills were shaped by an industrial economy that no longer exists in its previous form. Ekurhuleni's Economic Development pillar score reflects these structural headwinds. Its Governance and Finance pillar has been weakened by the same kinds of procurement and financial management challenges that affect Johannesburg, though the scale is somewhat smaller.

Nelson Mandela Bay, which includes Port Elizabeth and Uitenhage, is the smallest of the metros that hosts a significant manufacturing sector — anchored by the automotive industry, particularly Volkswagen and Ford assembly plants. The metro's economic fate is tightly linked to the automotive sector's health, making it vulnerable to global supply chain disruptions, trade policy changes, and shifts in automotive investment. Nelson Mandela Bay's MPI score has been among the lower-performing metros in recent assessments, reflecting both economic vulnerability and a governance record that has been marked by coalition instability, high municipal manager turnover, and persistent water infrastructure challenges. The city's water crisis — driven by ageing infrastructure, inadequate dam capacity, and maintenance backlogs — has been a nationally visible example of what happens when infrastructure investment falls below the replacement rate for an extended period.

Buffalo City, which includes East London, is the smallest metro by both population and economic output. Its economy is less diversified than the larger metros, with significant dependence on government employment, the Mercedes-Benz assembly plant, and the agricultural hinterland of the Eastern Cape. Buffalo City's small economic base limits its revenue generation capacity and constrains its ability to fund the kind of infrastructure investment and service delivery expansion that larger metros can sustain. Its MPI score tends to fall in the lower portion of the metro range, reflecting both structural economic constraints and governance challenges that are common to the Eastern Cape municipal landscape.

Mangaung, which includes Bloemfontein, rounds out the eight metros and typically occupies the lower end of the metro rankings. Mangaung's economy is dominated by government services (as the judicial capital and a provincial capital) and the University of the Free State, with limited private-sector diversification. The metro has experienced severe governance challenges in recent years, including water supply failures, infrastructure deterioration, and financial management weaknesses that have drawn national intervention. Mangaung's MPI score reflects the intersection of a narrow economic base with governance failures that have eroded basic service delivery capacity. It is the metro that most clearly illustrates the danger of the downward spiral: governance failures reduce service delivery, which reduces the attractiveness of the metro to business and skilled residents, which reduces the revenue base, which further constrains the municipality's ability to address governance failures.

The pattern that emerges across the eight metros is not random. The best-performing metros share a cluster of characteristics: relatively strong revenue collection, better audit outcomes, higher capital budget execution, and more consistent administrative leadership. The worst-performing metros share a different cluster: governance instability, weak financial management, infrastructure backlogs that compound over time, and a progressive erosion of institutional capacity. The economic indicators, while important, are less decisive in separating the high-performing from the low-performing metros than the governance indicators. This is a crucial finding, because it suggests that the primary lever available to metro governments for improving their MPI score is not economic — they cannot conjure GDP growth or reduce structural unemployment through municipal policy alone — but rather governance: collecting revenue more effectively, managing procurement more honestly, executing capital budgets more fully, and maintaining the financial discipline that enables everything else.

:::expert
The Financial and Fiscal Commission, South Africa's constitutionally mandated advisory body on intergovernmental fiscal relations, has repeatedly noted in its annual submissions to Parliament that metropolitan municipalities face a particular fiscal challenge: they generate approximately 70 per cent of their revenue from own sources (rates, tariffs, and user charges), making them far less dependent on national transfers than smaller municipalities but also far more exposed to the consequences of their own revenue management failures. The FFC's analysis shows that the metros with the strongest own-revenue performance are those with the most effective billing systems, the highest payment rates, and the most consistent enforcement of credit control policies. The Commission has also highlighted that capital budget under-expenditure across metros represents a significant opportunity cost — it is not merely money not spent but infrastructure not built and services not expanded, with compounding effects over time.
:::

## Peer Group Stratification: Why Comparing a Metro to a Rural Municipality is Unfair

If you were to rank all 257 of South Africa's municipalities on a single league table using uniform weights and identical indicators, the result would be misleading at best and actively harmful at worst. It would be misleading because it would compare institutions that face fundamentally different structural conditions, serve fundamentally different populations, and operate with fundamentally different resource bases. It would be harmful because politicians and media would inevitably use the ranking to shame struggling rural municipalities for failing to match metropolitan performance levels, without acknowledging that the comparison itself is structurally unfair.

Consider the differences. A Category A metropolitan municipality like Cape Town has an annual budget exceeding R60 billion, generates the majority of its revenue from own sources, employs thousands of professionally qualified engineers, accountants, and planners, and serves a population that is overwhelmingly urban and connected to formal economic networks. A Category B4 rural municipality in the Eastern Cape or Limpopo may have an annual budget of R200 million, generate less than 10 per cent of its revenue from own sources, employ no professionally qualified engineers at all, and serve a population that is largely dependent on social grants and subsistence agriculture. Expecting these two institutions to perform equally on the same indicators with the same weights is not rigorous analysis — it is a category error.

This is why the MPI framework uses peer group stratification. Municipalities are grouped into categories that share broadly similar structural characteristics, and both the indicators and the weights are adjusted within each category to reflect the reality of what that category of municipality can and should be expected to achieve.

For metropolitan municipalities, the weights are: Economic Development 35 per cent, Service Delivery 25 per cent, Governance and Finance 25 per cent, and Infrastructure 15 per cent. The high weight on Economic Development reflects the metros' role as the country's economic engines — their primary value-add to the national system is generating economic activity, employment, and tax revenue.

For rural municipalities classified as B4, the weights shift: Economic Development 30 per cent, Service Delivery 25 per cent, Governance and Finance 30 per cent, and Infrastructure 15 per cent. The increase in the Governance and Finance weight and the decrease in the Economic Development weight reflect two realities. First, rural municipalities have limited ability to drive economic growth directly — their economies are shaped by factors largely outside municipal control, including national agricultural policy, social grant distribution, mining investment decisions, and provincial infrastructure spending. Expecting a rural municipality to match a metro on employment growth or business registrations per capita is unreasonable. Second, the single most important thing a rural municipality can do to improve outcomes for its residents is to manage its finances competently — to collect what revenue it can, spend its transfers and grants effectively, maintain its basic infrastructure, and avoid the procurement failures that divert resources from service delivery. The higher Governance and Finance weight reflects this priority.

The indicators themselves also differ by category. Where metros are measured on indicators like business registrations per capita and property rates revenue growth, rural municipalities are assessed on a different set of economic indicators that are more relevant to rural economic conditions. The distinction matters not because rural economic development is less important, but because the mechanisms of rural economic development are different from those of urban economic development, and the measurement tools must reflect that difference.

## Rural Economic Indicators for B4 Municipalities

The economic life of a B4 rural municipality looks nothing like the economic life of a metropolitan area. There are fewer formal businesses, fewer salaried employees, fewer property transactions, and fewer of the market-based economic activities that generate the data points used to assess metro economic performance. This does not mean rural areas lack economic activity — it means the economic activity takes different forms and requires different measurement approaches.

The first rural economic indicator is Expanded Public Works Programme jobs per R1 million spent. The EPWP is the national government's primary programme for creating short-term employment opportunities through labour-intensive construction, maintenance, and community service activities. For rural municipalities, where formal private-sector employment is scarce, the EPWP is often the single largest source of employment outside of government and social grants. Measuring EPWP jobs per R1 million spent captures both the scale of employment creation and the efficiency with which EPWP funds are converted into actual work opportunities. A municipality that creates 50 work opportunities per R1 million spent is using the programme more effectively than one that creates 20 per R1 million — though both may face challenges in translating short-term EPWP work into sustainable employment pathways.

The second indicator is EPWP work opportunities per capita, which measures the programme's reach relative to the municipality's working-age population. This indicator captures whether the EPWP is operating at a scale that is meaningful for the local labour market or whether it is a token programme that provides opportunities to a negligible fraction of the unemployed. In deep rural municipalities where the expanded unemployment rate may exceed 50 per cent, the EPWP's per-capita reach is a significant measure of the municipality's effectiveness in deploying one of the few large-scale employment tools available to it.

The third indicator is cooperative development. Cooperatives — formally registered enterprises owned and operated by their members — are a policy priority for rural economic development in South Africa. The Department of Small Business Development and provincial economic development departments actively promote cooperative formation in agriculture, manufacturing, and services. The number of active, registered cooperatives within a municipal area, adjusted for population, provides a measure of the municipality's success in supporting community-based economic organisation. The emphasis is on active cooperatives — many cooperatives are registered but never trade, and counting dormant cooperatives would inflate the metric without reflecting genuine economic activity.

The fourth indicator is agricultural support, measured through a combination of the municipality's investment in agricultural infrastructure (fencing, irrigation, access roads to farming areas), its participation in provincial agricultural development programmes, and the utilisation rate of commonage land allocated for agricultural purposes. Agriculture remains the backbone of many rural economies, and the municipality's role in supporting agricultural activity — even though agricultural policy is primarily a provincial and national competence — is a meaningful indicator of local economic governance.

The fifth indicator is local procurement percentage — the share of the municipality's total procurement spending that goes to businesses registered within the municipal area. For a rural municipality, the procurement budget may be one of the largest sources of cash injection into the local economy. A municipality that procures goods and services primarily from suppliers in distant urban centres is effectively exporting its economic stimulus, while one that actively sources from local suppliers is recycling its budget through the local economy, supporting local businesses and creating local employment. This is not a call for protectionism or for accepting inferior goods and services simply because the supplier is local — it is a recognition that, where local suppliers can meet specifications at competitive prices, directing procurement locally has multiplier effects that are particularly significant in cash-scarce rural economies.

The sixth indicator is youth retention, measured as the net migration rate for persons aged 18 to 35. Rural municipalities across South Africa face chronic outward migration of young people to urban areas in search of employment, education, and economic opportunity. While this migration is in many ways rational — the economic opportunities in metros are objectively greater — it also represents a steady drain of human capital from rural areas, leaving behind an increasingly aged and dependent population. A municipality that is losing young people at a slower rate than its peers, or that is managing to attract some return migration, is likely doing something right in terms of creating liveable conditions and at least some economic opportunity. Youth retention is, in this sense, a revealed-preference indicator: young people vote with their feet, and their migration decisions aggregate into a powerful signal about the quality of local governance and economic conditions.

The seventh indicator is LED budget execution, which is measured for rural municipalities in the same way as for metros — as the percentage of the allocated Local Economic Development budget that is actually spent during the financial year. This indicator is particularly important for rural municipalities because LED budgets are often small, and under-spending means that the already limited resources available for economic development are not being deployed at all.

These seven indicators, normalised using the same goalpost methodology applied to metro indicators (though with goalposts calibrated to rural performance ranges rather than metro performance ranges), are aggregated into the Economic Development pillar score for B4 municipalities. The resulting score is not directly comparable to a metro's Economic Development pillar score — the indicators are different, the goalposts are different, and the underlying economic conditions are different. But it is comparable within the B4 peer group, which is the only comparison that is analytically meaningful.

:::takeaway
Comparing a metropolitan municipality to a rural municipality on the same indicators with the same weights would be a category error, not rigorous analysis. The MPI framework addresses this by stratifying municipalities into peer groups, adjusting indicator sets to reflect each group's structural reality, and calibrating goalposts to each group's performance range. A rural municipality with an MPI score of 65 is not "worse" than a metro with a score of 75 — it is performing differently in a different context, and the framework is designed to make that distinction visible rather than obscuring it behind a single national ranking.
:::

## The Limitations You Must Understand

No ranking framework is more credible than the data it relies upon, and the MPI framework relies on data sources that have real and significant limitations. Presenting the metro rankings without acknowledging these limitations would be intellectually dishonest, and ignoring them when you use the framework for your own analysis would lead you to overconfident conclusions.

The first limitation is data lag. The most comprehensive source of demographic and economic data at the municipal level is the national census, and the most recent census was conducted in 2022. Census data is used to establish population baselines, labour force estimates, and household-level indicators that feed into multiple MPI calculations. By the time census data is processed, quality-checked, and released by Statistics South Africa, it is already one to two years old. By the time it is incorporated into the kinds of analysis described in this article, it may be three or more years out of date. In a country where municipalities can deteriorate rapidly — as Mangaung and Nelson Mandela Bay have demonstrated — a three-year data lag can mean that the numbers you are analysing describe a municipality that no longer exists in the form the data suggests.

The second limitation is the estimation required to derive metro-level figures from provincial data. Many economic indicators are published by Statistics South Africa at the provincial level rather than the municipal level. The Quarterly Labour Force Survey, for example, produces provincial unemployment estimates but does not produce separate estimates for each metro within a province. Deriving metro-level unemployment figures requires estimation techniques — using census proportions, household survey sub-samples, or spatial modelling — that introduce additional uncertainty. The Cape Town unemployment figure of 23.4 per cent used in this article is itself an estimate, not a precise count, and should be understood as having a confidence interval around it.

The third limitation relates to the Blue Drop and Green Drop water quality assessment systems maintained by the Department of Water and Sanitation. These systems are designed to assess drinking water quality (Blue Drop) and wastewater management quality (Green Drop) at the municipal level. They are valuable data sources in principle, but in practice they have been plagued by inconsistencies. Assessments have not been conducted on a regular, predictable schedule. Methodologies have changed between assessment rounds, making longitudinal comparison difficult. Some municipalities have refused to participate or have submitted incomplete data. And the scoring criteria themselves have been revised in ways that make it difficult to determine whether a change in a municipality's Blue Drop or Green Drop score reflects an actual change in performance or merely a change in the assessment methodology. Using Blue Drop and Green Drop scores as MPI indicators requires acknowledging this inconsistency and treating the scores as indicative rather than precise.

The fourth limitation concerns business registration data from the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission. CIPC maintains the national register of companies and close corporations, and aggregate registration statistics can serve as a proxy for business formation activity. However, CIPC data is not natively reported at the municipal level. Deriving municipal-level business registration figures requires geocoding registered addresses to municipal boundaries — a process that is feasible but introduces errors when addresses are incomplete, incorrectly formatted, or located on municipal boundaries. The business registrations per 10,000 population figure used in the Cape Town worked example is derived through this process and should be treated as an approximation.

The fifth limitation is the treatment of missing data. When more than 30 per cent of a municipality's indicators are missing — because the data was not collected, was not reported, or was of insufficient quality to use — the municipality is not ranked at all. This is a deliberate methodological choice: ranking a municipality on the basis of less than 70 per cent of the required information would produce a score that appears precise but is actually speculative. When less than 30 per cent of indicators are missing, the missing values are imputed using the median value of the municipality's peer group, and the imputed values are flagged. This approach avoids penalising municipalities for data gaps that may be beyond their control (such as a national department failing to conduct an assessment) while clearly signalling which parts of the score rest on actual data and which rest on peer-group estimates. Users of the MPI should always check the imputation flags before drawing conclusions about a specific municipality's performance on a specific indicator.

These limitations are not reasons to abandon quantitative ranking. They are reasons to use it carefully, to check the underlying data when a result surprises you, and to treat the scores as informative approximations rather than precise measurements. A 32-point gap between Cape Town and Johannesburg is not an artefact of data limitations — the gap is too large and too consistent across indicators to be explained by measurement error. But a 3-point gap between two similarly-placed metros might well be within the margin of uncertainty, and should be interpreted as "roughly equivalent" rather than as a meaningful performance difference.

:::expert
The Public Affairs Research Institute, based at the University of the Witwatersrand, has conducted extensive research on municipal governance capacity in South Africa, with particular focus on the institutional factors that drive municipal performance differences. PARI's research consistently emphasises that quantitative performance measures, while useful for identifying patterns and trends, must be complemented by qualitative analysis of institutional dynamics — political leadership, administrative stability, organisational culture, and the quality of intergovernmental relations. Their work on municipalities that have experienced governance collapse highlights that the quantitative indicators often lag the institutional deterioration by one to two years — by the time debt collection rates or audit outcomes visibly decline, the underlying institutional damage has already been accumulating for some time. This finding reinforces the argument for treating MPI scores as lagging indicators that describe where a municipality has been, not necessarily where it is heading.
:::

:::expert
National Treasury's Municipal Finance Improvement Programme, established to support municipalities in strengthening their financial management systems, publishes quarterly municipal financial data through the Municipal Money portal. This data — covering revenue collection, expenditure patterns, audit outcomes, and grant spending — provides some of the most timely municipal-level financial data available in South Africa. However, National Treasury has acknowledged that the quality of this data depends on the quality of each municipality's own financial reporting systems, and that municipalities with the weakest financial management are often also the municipalities that submit the least reliable data. This circularity — the municipalities most in need of monitoring being the ones whose data is least trustworthy — is a fundamental challenge for any framework that relies on self-reported municipal financial data.
:::

## Practical Framework: Municipal Comparison Tool

The framework that follows gives you the tools to conduct your own municipal comparison using publicly available data. You do not need a statistics degree, a research budget, or special access to government databases. Everything described below is accessible to anyone with an internet connection and the willingness to work through the steps methodically.

**Step 1: Identify your target municipality and its peer group.**

Start by determining which category your municipality falls into. Metropolitan municipalities (Category A) include Cape Town, Johannesburg, eThekwini, Tshwane, Ekurhuleni, Nelson Mandela Bay, Buffalo City, and Mangaung. If your municipality is not a metro, identify whether it is a B1 (secondary city/large town), B2 (small town), B3 (small town with significant rural areas), or B4 (predominantly rural) municipality. The Municipal Demarcation Board website (demarcation.org.za) provides the current classification of every municipality, including boundary maps and population data. Your municipality should only be compared to others in the same category.

**Step 2: Gather raw data from primary sources.**

The following sources provide the raw indicator data you will need. For financial data — revenue collection, expenditure, capital budgets, irregular expenditure, and audit outcomes — the primary source is National Treasury's Municipal Money portal (municipalmoney.gov.za). This site allows you to search for any municipality by name and view its financial performance over multiple years, including comparisons to peer municipalities. For service delivery data — access to water, sanitation, electricity, and refuse removal — Statistics South Africa's Community Survey and Census data are the primary sources, available through the StatsSA website (statssa.gov.za) and the SuperWEB2 data portal. For labour market data, the Quarterly Labour Force Survey published by Statistics South Africa provides provincial-level unemployment estimates that can be used as metro-level proxies (with the caveats about estimation discussed in the limitations section). For audit outcomes, the Auditor-General of South Africa publishes annual municipal audit reports and a consolidated general report on local government (agsa.co.za), which includes a searchable database of municipal audit results. For water quality assessments, the Department of Water and Sanitation's Blue Drop and Green Drop reports are published periodically (though irregularly) on the department's website (dws.gov.za).

**Step 3: Normalise each indicator using the goalpost method.**

For each indicator, determine whether higher values are better or lower values are better. Then apply the appropriate normalisation formula. For "higher is better" indicators (such as debt collection rate, capital budget execution, or water access percentage): Normalised Score equals ((Actual minus Min) divided by (Max minus Min)) times 100. For "lower is better" indicators (such as unemployment rate, non-revenue water, or irregular expenditure as a percentage of operating expenditure): Normalised Score equals ((Max minus Actual) divided by (Max minus Min)) times 100. Use the following goalposts for metro indicators. Unemployment: 15 per cent (min) to 40 per cent (max). Youth unemployment: 25 per cent (min) to 60 per cent (max). Employment growth: negative 5 per cent (min) to positive 5 per cent (max). Business registrations per 10,000 population: 0 (min) to 50 (max). Water access: 80 per cent (min) to 100 per cent (max). Debt collection rate: 50 per cent (min) to 95 per cent (max). Capital budget execution: 40 per cent (min) to 95 per cent (max). Non-revenue water: 15 per cent (min) to 50 per cent (max). If your calculated normalised score exceeds 100, cap it at 100. If it falls below 0, set it to 0. This capping ensures that outlier values do not distort the pillar scores.

**Step 4: Calculate pillar scores.**

Group your normalised indicator scores into the four pillars: Economic Development, Service Delivery, Governance and Finance, and Infrastructure. Calculate the arithmetic mean of the normalised scores within each pillar. This gives you four pillar scores, each on a 0-to-100 scale.

**Step 5: Calculate the overall MPI score using the weighted geometric mean.**

Apply the peer-group weights. For metros: Economic Development 35 per cent (0.35), Service Delivery 25 per cent (0.25), Governance and Finance 25 per cent (0.25), Infrastructure 15 per cent (0.15). For B4 rural municipalities: Economic Development 30 per cent (0.30), Service Delivery 25 per cent (0.25), Governance and Finance 30 per cent (0.30), Infrastructure 15 per cent (0.15). The formula is: Overall Score equals (Economic Development pillar score raised to the power 0.35) multiplied by (Service Delivery pillar score raised to the power 0.25) multiplied by (Governance and Finance pillar score raised to the power 0.25) multiplied by (Infrastructure pillar score raised to the power 0.15). Use a spreadsheet or calculator that supports exponents — most spreadsheet applications handle this formula straightforwardly.

**Step 6: Interpret the result using the performance tiers.**

High Performance: 70 or above. The municipality performs well across most indicators and demonstrates consistent governance capacity. Moderate Performance: 55 to 69. The municipality performs adequately on most indicators but has identifiable weaknesses in one or more pillars. Needs Improvement: 40 to 54. The municipality shows significant weaknesses across multiple indicators and requires focused intervention to arrest decline. Critical: below 40. The municipality is failing on most indicators and may require external intervention (such as a Section 139 intervention under the Constitution) to restore basic functionality.

**Step 7: Compare against metro benchmarks.**

Once you have calculated your municipality's score, compare it to the metro benchmarks provided in this article. If you are assessing a metro, your comparison is direct — you can see where your metro falls relative to Cape Town's 84.0 and Johannesburg's 52.2. If you are assessing a non-metro municipality, the comparison is indirect — you should compare within your peer group rather than against metros — but the metro benchmarks still provide a useful reference point for understanding the range of municipal performance in South Africa.

Remember that this tool produces an approximation, not a definitive verdict. The value of the exercise is less in the final number and more in the process: by gathering the data, calculating the indicators, and identifying where your municipality falls relative to its peers, you will develop a granular understanding of local governance performance that no amount of political rhetoric can substitute for.

:::takeaway
You do not need to wait for an academic institution or government agency to rank your municipality. The data sources described above — Municipal Money, Statistics South Africa, the Auditor-General's reports, and the Municipal Demarcation Board — are freely accessible online and contain the raw information you need to calculate a reasonable approximation of your municipality's performance. The exercise of gathering and analysing this data will itself make you a more informed and more demanding citizen, which is ultimately the point of [Your Economic Citizenship](/articles/eg-5-3-your-economic-citizenship).
:::

## Resources and Institutional Sources

The analysis in this article draws on data and research from the following institutional sources, each of which maintains publicly accessible data portals or publication archives.

**National Treasury Municipal Money** (municipalmoney.gov.za) provides the most comprehensive and regularly updated source of municipal financial data in South Africa, including revenue, expenditure, capital budgets, audit outcomes, and financial ratios for all 257 municipalities. The site allows municipality-by-municipality comparisons and historical trend analysis, making it the single most useful starting point for anyone conducting their own municipal performance assessment.

**Statistics South Africa** (statssa.gov.za) publishes the Quarterly Labour Force Survey (labour market data by province), the Census and Community Survey (household-level data on access to services, employment, and demographics), and GDP estimates that underpin economic performance analysis. The SuperWEB2 interactive data tool allows users to construct custom tables from census and survey microdata.

**Auditor-General of South Africa** (agsa.co.za) publishes annual audit reports for every municipality and a consolidated general report on local government that identifies systemic patterns, common findings, and the trajectory of financial management quality across the municipal system. The AGSA's reports are the most authoritative source on audit outcomes, irregular expenditure, and financial management compliance.

**South African Cities Network** (sacities.net) publishes the State of South African Cities Report and a range of thematic research reports that provide comparative analysis of the eight metropolitan municipalities across economic, social, governance, and spatial dimensions. The SACN's data and analysis are specifically designed for metro-to-metro comparison.

**Financial and Fiscal Commission** (ffc.co.za) publishes annual submissions to Parliament and research reports on intergovernmental fiscal relations, including detailed analysis of municipal revenue capacity, grant dependency, and fiscal sustainability. The FFC's work is particularly useful for understanding the structural differences between metro and non-metro municipal finances.

**Municipal Demarcation Board** (demarcation.org.za) maintains the definitive classification of all municipalities by category (A, B1, B2, B3, B4, C1, C2), along with boundary maps, population estimates, and ward-level data. This is the starting point for determining which peer group any municipality belongs to.

**Department of Water and Sanitation** (dws.gov.za) publishes the Blue Drop (drinking water quality) and Green Drop (wastewater management) assessment reports, which provide municipality-level scores on water and sanitation service quality. Note the caveats discussed in the limitations section regarding assessment frequency and methodological consistency.

**Companies and Intellectual Property Commission** (cipc.co.za) maintains the national register of companies and provides aggregate business registration statistics that, with geocoding, can be used to estimate municipal-level business formation rates. CIPC's annual reports include national business registration trends that provide useful context for interpreting municipal-level estimates.
