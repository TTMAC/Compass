## High Development on Paper, Low Development in Reality

South Africa looks like a "high human development" country on paper. Its HDI of 0.741 places it in the same category as Brazil, Colombia, and Peru — countries with significant challenges but recognisable middle-income living standards for substantial portions of their populations. Apply the inequality adjustment — ask not what the average South African theoretically has access to, but how development outcomes are actually distributed across the population — and South Africa drops to 0.462. That score does not place it among its upper-middle-income peers. It places it among countries like Myanmar, Papua New Guinea, and the Solomon Islands. The 37.6% penalty that the inequality adjustment imposes is the largest among all upper-middle-income countries and one of the largest in the world. No other country at South Africa's income level wastes as much human potential through unequal distribution.

This article conducts a forensic examination of each component of South Africa's human development scores. It takes the headline HDI apart, dimension by dimension, and then reassembles the picture using the inequality adjustment, the Gender Development Index, the Gender Inequality Index, and the Multidimensional Poverty Index. The goal is not to produce a catalogue of misery — South Africa has genuine achievements in its post-apartheid development record that deserve acknowledgement. The goal is to produce an honest, data-grounded portrait of where the country stands, so that citizens can evaluate policy proposals against reality rather than against political rhetoric.

If you have read [Beyond GDP](/articles/hd-1-1-beyond-gdp), you understand the conceptual framework: Amartya Sen's capabilities approach, the structure of the HDI, and why moving beyond GDP matters. This article applies that framework to South Africa's specific data. If you have read [The R7,000 Economy](/articles/eg-1-1-the-seven-thousand-rand-economy), you understand the economic baseline — the GNI component of the HDI. This article adds the health and education dimensions, and then subjects all three to the inequality test.

## Dimension One: A Nation's Health

Life expectancy at birth is the HDI's indicator for the health dimension, and South Africa's story in this dimension is unlike that of almost any other upper-middle-income country. It is a story of catastrophe, denial, belated response, and partial — only partial — recovery.

South Africa's current life expectancy at birth is approximately 63 years. That number, by itself, tells you that something is seriously wrong. For a country with South Africa's income level, the expected life expectancy — based on the cross-country relationship between income and health outcomes — would be approximately 73 to 75 years. South Africa's citizens die, on average, more than a decade earlier than citizens of countries at comparable income levels. The life expectancy deficit is the single most damning statistic in South Africa's development profile.

To understand how the country arrived at this point, you must understand the HIV/AIDS catastrophe — not just as a health crisis, but as a governance failure with consequences that continue to shape every dimension of South African development.

**The arc of the epidemic.** HIV prevalence in South Africa began rising rapidly in the early 1990s. By 2000, antenatal clinic surveys showed prevalence rates among pregnant women exceeding 24%. By 2005, an estimated 5.5 million South Africans were living with HIV — the largest number of any country in the world. The epidemic was not a natural disaster that struck without warning. Epidemiologists, public health officials, and civil society organisations warned repeatedly through the 1990s that South Africa was facing a catastrophic epidemic that required immediate, large-scale intervention.

The response of the government under President Thabo Mbeki was, by any objective assessment, a policy failure of historic proportions. Mbeki publicly questioned the causal link between HIV and AIDS, gave credence to dissident scientists whose views were rejected by the overwhelming consensus of the global medical community, and delayed the rollout of antiretroviral treatment (ART) for years while the epidemic raged. His Minister of Health, Manto Tshabalala-Msimang, promoted garlic, beetroot, and African potatoes as alternatives to antiretroviral drugs. This was not merely eccentric — it was lethal. A landmark study published in the *Journal of Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndromes* by researchers at the Harvard School of Public Health estimated that the delay in rolling out antiretroviral treatment between 2000 and 2005 resulted in approximately 330,000 preventable deaths.

The effect on life expectancy was devastating. From approximately 62 years in 1990, life expectancy in South Africa dropped to approximately 52 years by 2005 — a decline of ten years in fifteen years, something virtually unprecedented outside of wartime. To put this in perspective: South Africa's life expectancy decline during the HIV/AIDS epidemic was comparable in magnitude to the life expectancy decline experienced by Russia during the post-Soviet collapse, which was itself considered one of the great peacetime demographic catastrophes of the twentieth century.

**The recovery.** The turning point came with the change in government policy under President Kgalema Motlanthe and then President Jacob Zuma (who, whatever his other failings, reversed AIDS denialism). Starting in 2008-2009, South Africa dramatically scaled up its antiretroviral treatment programme, eventually building the largest ART programme in the world. By 2023, an estimated 5.7 million South Africans were receiving antiretroviral treatment. The programme, funded primarily through the domestic budget with significant support from the US President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR) and the Global Fund, is a genuine achievement — one of the largest public health interventions in human history.

Life expectancy has recovered to approximately 63 years — a gain of more than ten years from the nadir. But this recovery has merely restored life expectancy to approximately where it was in 1990. Three decades after the democratic transition, South Africans live no longer, on average, than they did under apartheid. This is not a health system that is delivering progress. It is a health system that has managed, at enormous cost, to recover from a self-inflicted wound.

**The remaining burdens.** HIV/AIDS, while now manageable with treatment, remains the leading cause of death in South Africa. The country also bears one of the highest tuberculosis (TB) burdens in the world — with an incidence rate of approximately 468 per 100,000 population, driven in large part by HIV co-infection (people living with HIV are 15 to 22 times more likely to develop active TB than HIV-negative individuals). TB kills approximately 56,000 South Africans annually. The convergence of HIV and TB — each disease amplifying the other in a deadly synergy — is a distinctly South African catastrophe that has no parallel in any other upper-middle-income country.

Maternal mortality, while declining, remains high for an upper-middle-income country at approximately 127 deaths per 100,000 live births — compared to 44 in Brazil, 55 in Mexico, and 17 in Turkey. Non-communicable diseases — diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular disease — are rising rapidly, creating a "double burden of disease" in which the health system must simultaneously manage infectious diseases associated with poverty and chronic diseases associated with urbanisation and dietary change. Violence is itself a major health burden: South Africa's homicide rate of approximately 42 per 100,000 population makes interpersonal violence a leading cause of premature death, particularly among young men.

The cumulative effect is a health dimension index of approximately 0.662 — the weakest of South Africa's three HDI components and the primary driver of the country's lower-than-expected overall HDI. A country at South Africa's income level should have a health index above 0.800. The deficit is enormous, and it reflects not insufficient spending — South Africa spends approximately 8.3% of GDP on health, above the global average — but insufficient returns on that spending, driven by institutional weaknesses, corruption, inequality in access, and the lingering consequences of the HIV/AIDS policy failure.

:::expert-perspective
**Olive Shisana**, former CEO of the Human Sciences Research Council and lead investigator of the South African National HIV Prevalence, Incidence, Behaviour and Communication Surveys: "The HIV/AIDS epidemic did not just kill individuals. It destroyed households, overwhelmed health systems, orphaned a generation of children, and set back human development by decades. The ART programme has saved millions of lives, but we must be honest that we are still recovering from a crisis that better governance could have prevented or dramatically mitigated." The life expectancy cost of that governance failure — ten years lost and only partially recovered — is embedded in South Africa's HDI to this day.
:::

## Dimension Two: What South Africans Know

South Africa's education numbers present a paradox that has confounded development economists, education researchers, and policy-makers for two decades. On the quantity measures used by the HDI, South Africa performs surprisingly well. Mean years of schooling for adults aged 25 and older is 11.3 years — higher than Brazil (8.1), Mexico (9.2), Turkey (8.7), and the global average (8.7). Expected years of schooling for children entering the system today is 14.0 years. These numbers suggest a country where education is widely accessible and where citizens accumulate substantial years of formal learning.

Then you look at what learners actually know, and the picture inverts completely.

**The quantity achievement.** South Africa's post-apartheid expansion of educational access is a genuine accomplishment. Under apartheid, the education system was fragmented into nineteen racially and ethnically defined departments, with per-pupil spending in white schools exceeding that in black schools by ratios of up to ten to one. The 1996 South African Schools Act unified the system, and subsequent policy expanded access dramatically. Net primary enrolment is now effectively universal — above 98%. Secondary school completion rates have risen substantially, with the National Senior Certificate (matric) pass rate reaching above 80% in recent years (though this figure is contested, as we shall discuss). The expansion of Early Childhood Development (ECD) programmes, the introduction of the no-fee school policy covering approximately 80% of schools, and the National School Nutrition Programme feeding approximately nine million learners daily have all contributed to keeping children in school.

These are real achievements. In terms of years of schooling — which is what the HDI measures — South Africa has made substantial progress since 1994. The 11.3 mean years figure reflects both the younger generation's expanded access and the educational attainments of older cohorts.

**The quality catastrophe.** The Progress in International Reading Literacy Study (PIRLS) 2021 found that 81% of South African Grade 4 learners could not read for meaning — the highest rate of reading failure among the fifty countries that participated in the study. This is not a misprint. More than four out of five South African nine-year-olds — children who have been in school for four years — cannot extract basic meaning from a simple text in any language. The Trends in International Mathematics and Science Study (TIMSS) tells a similarly devastating story: South African Grade 5 learners ranked last among the participating countries in mathematics. Grade 9 learners performed only marginally better.

These results are not primarily about resources. South Africa spends approximately 6.2% of GDP on education — above the global average and above most of its peer countries. Per-pupil spending, while unequally distributed, is not negligible even in the least-resourced schools. The problem is what happens inside classrooms. Research by the National Income Dynamics Study (NIDS) and the Stellenbosch Economic Working Papers has documented that many South African teachers, particularly in historically disadvantaged schools, lack adequate content knowledge in the subjects they teach. A study testing mathematics teachers on the content they were expected to teach to Grade 6 learners found that a significant proportion could not solve the problems themselves.

The consequences cascade through the system. Learners who cannot read by Grade 4 cannot access the curriculum in subsequent years — they sit in classrooms, accumulate years of schooling (which the HDI faithfully counts), but learn almost nothing. By the time they reach Grade 12, many have twelve years of attendance but five or six years of actual learning. The matric pass rate, which the government celebrates annually as evidence of educational success, is inflated by several mechanisms: learners who have been counselled to drop out or switch to easier subjects before Grade 12 are not counted in the denominator; the pass mark for several subjects is 30%, a threshold that would not constitute a pass in any other education system of comparable ambition; and the percentage of learners achieving a "bachelor's pass" — the qualification required for university entrance — is substantially lower than the headline pass rate.

**The quality-quantity disconnect and the HDI.** This disconnect between years of schooling and actual learning reveals a limitation of the HDI's education dimension. The HDI counts years. It does not — it cannot, given the available cross-country data — measure what learners actually know. For South Africa, this means the education index of approximately 0.720 significantly overstates the country's educational achievement. If the HDI could incorporate learning outcomes — PIRLS and TIMSS scores, for example — South Africa's education index would be substantially lower, and the gap between South Africa and its peers would widen dramatically.

The Human Capital Index developed by the World Bank attempts to address this limitation. It adjusts years of schooling for learning quality using harmonised test scores, producing a measure of "learning-adjusted years of schooling." South Africa's 11.3 mean years of schooling, once adjusted for learning quality, drops to approximately 5.8 learning-adjusted years — a figure that more accurately reflects what South African adults actually know and can do. By this measure, South Africa's educational achievement is comparable not to Brazil or Turkey but to countries with far lower income levels and far fewer years of formal schooling.

:::takeaway
South Africa's education paradox: 11.3 mean years of schooling (higher than Brazil, Mexico, or Turkey) coexists with learning outcomes among the worst in the world. PIRLS 2021 found 81% of Grade 4 learners cannot read for meaning. The World Bank's learning-adjusted years of schooling reduces South Africa's 11.3 years to approximately 5.8 — exposing a system that keeps children in school without teaching them. The HDI's education index of 0.720 substantially overstates actual educational capability.
:::

## Dimension Three: What South Africans Earn

The income dimension of South Africa's HDI is both the strongest component and, paradoxically, the one most distorted by inequality. South Africa's gross national income per capita of approximately $12,948 in PPP terms places it firmly in the upper-middle-income category. The logarithmic transformation used in the HDI — which reflects the diminishing marginal utility of income — yields an income index of approximately 0.741. By this measure, South Africa's material resources are comparable to those of Brazil ($16,390 GNI per capita PPP), not far behind Mexico ($21,030), and substantially ahead of many countries with higher HDI scores.

**The PPP adjustment and what it reveals.** The purchasing power parity adjustment is designed to make income comparable across countries by accounting for differences in the cost of goods and services. South Africa's nominal GNI per capita — approximately $7,000 in current US dollar terms — is less impressive than the PPP figure. The PPP adjustment reflects the fact that many goods and services in South Africa are cheaper than in high-income countries: a loaf of bread, a taxi ride, a haircut cost less in Johannesburg than in London. But the PPP adjustment assumes that these goods are equally accessible to all citizens, which in South Africa they emphatically are not. A loaf of bread may be cheaper in South Africa than in Switzerland, but that is irrelevant to a household in rural Limpopo where the nearest shop is twenty kilometres away and the household has no income at all.

**The distribution behind the average.** South Africa's GNI per capita of $12,948 PPP is, as discussed in [Beyond GDP](/articles/hd-1-1-beyond-gdp), an average that describes almost nobody's lived reality. According to data from the World Inequality Database, the top 10% of South African income earners capture approximately 65% of all national income. The top 1% captures approximately 20%. The bottom 50% — thirty million people — share less than 6% of national income. This means that for the bottom half of the population, GNI per capita is not $12,948 but approximately $1,500 PPP — a figure that places their lived economic reality in the "low income" category, comparable to countries like Senegal or Tanzania.

The HDI income index of 0.741 captures the national average. It does not capture the fact that this average is produced by a tiny minority with incomes comparable to those in Southern Europe and a vast majority with incomes comparable to those in sub-Saharan Africa's poorest countries. The logarithmic transformation partially mitigates this problem — because it gives more weight to income gains at lower levels, it somewhat reduces the upward pull of the very rich. But "somewhat" is not "sufficiently." In a country with a Gini coefficient of 0.63, the log transformation is a Band-Aid on a haemorrhage.

**The labour market context.** South Africa's income distribution cannot be understood without reference to the labour market — or, more precisely, to the labour market's failure to employ the population. The expanded unemployment rate — which includes discouraged work-seekers who have stopped actively looking for work — exceeds 42%. Youth unemployment (ages 15-34) on the expanded definition exceeds 60%. These are not recession statistics. They are structural features of an economy that has never generated sufficient employment for its working-age population. For the millions of South Africans who are unemployed, GNI per capita is an abstraction. Their income comes from social grants (approximately 28 million South Africans receive some form of grant), from informal economic activity, from remittances from employed household members, or from nothing at all.

The social grant system — particularly the Child Support Grant (approximately R510 per month per child), the Old Age Pension (approximately R2,080 per month), and the Social Relief of Distress Grant (R370 per month) introduced during the COVID-19 pandemic and repeatedly extended — represents one of the most significant anti-poverty interventions in the developing world. Research by the Southern Africa Labour and Development Research Unit (SALDRU) at the University of Cape Town has documented that social grants reduce the poverty headcount by approximately 8 percentage points and the poverty gap by even more. Without the grant system, South Africa's income distribution — and its HDI — would be substantially worse.

But grants are a mitigation mechanism, not a solution. They prevent the worst deprivation without enabling the capabilities that sustained income from productive employment would provide. A household surviving on a Child Support Grant and a Social Relief of Distress Grant has income — it is not destitute. But it does not have the income to invest in children's nutrition, educational materials, healthcare beyond what the public system provides, or any of the other capabilities that income is meant to enable. The gap between "surviving" and "thriving" is the gap that South Africa's income dimension does not capture.

## The Headline Number: 0.741

South Africa's HDI of 0.741 — calculated as the geometric mean of the health index (0.662), the education index (0.720), and the income index (0.741) — places the country in the "high human development" band (0.700-0.799). It ranks approximately 110th out of 193 countries, in the lower half of the "high development" group.

This number is both mathematically accurate and deeply misleading.

It is accurate in the sense that the arithmetic is correct. The underlying data — life expectancy, years of schooling, GNI per capita — are collected by reputable institutions using established methodologies. South Africa's HDI of 0.741 is a true statement about the averages of these three indicators across the national population.

It is misleading in the sense that averages in the most unequal country in the world tell you almost nothing about actual human experience. The HDI of 0.741 describes a country where people live to 63, attend school for 11.3 years, and earn $12,948 per year in PPP terms. That describes the experience of, perhaps, a small civil servant in a secondary city — someone with a stable job, access to decent healthcare, and children in reasonably functional schools. It does not describe the experience of the unemployed mother in an informal settlement, the farmworker in the Northern Cape, the domestic worker in Sandton, or the retrenched mineworker in the Free State. It does not describe the experience of the majority.

The geometric mean — the method used to combine the three dimensions — does impose some penalty for imbalance. South Africa's weak health dimension (0.662) pulls the overall HDI below what a simple average of the three indices would produce. This is intentional: the geometric mean reflects the philosophical position that the three dimensions are not fully substitutable. But the penalty for imbalance is modest. What the HDI does not do — what it cannot do by design — is account for the distribution of outcomes within each dimension. Two countries can have the same average life expectancy of 63 years, but in one country most people live to between 60 and 66, while in the other some people live to 85 and others die at 35. The HDI treats these countries identically. The IHDI does not.

:::expert-perspective
**Sabina Alkire**, Director of the Oxford Poverty and Human Development Initiative: "The HDI was designed to be a summary measure — a way of focusing attention on human outcomes rather than economic aggregates. It was never intended to be the final word on human development. In countries with extreme inequality, the headline HDI can be actively misleading, because it assigns the same score to a country where development is broadly shared and a country where it is concentrated among elites." South Africa is the paradigm case of the second category.
:::

## The Inequality Adjustment: From 0.741 to 0.462

The Inequality-adjusted Human Development Index was introduced in the 2010 Human Development Report to address precisely the limitation that South Africa illustrates so starkly. It adjusts each of the three HDI dimensions for inequality using the Atkinson inequality measure — a method that penalises not just the level of inequality but its impact on overall welfare. In a perfectly equal society, the IHDI equals the HDI. In any society with inequality, the IHDI is lower. The greater the inequality, the greater the gap.

The Atkinson measure works as follows for each dimension. Instead of using the arithmetic mean of the distribution (which is what the standard HDI effectively does), the IHDI uses a generalised mean that gives greater weight to people at the lower end of the distribution. The parameter epsilon (set at 1 in the IHDI) determines how much weight is given to the bottom — at epsilon = 1, the generalised mean equals the geometric mean of individual outcomes, which means that inequality always reduces the adjusted score. A single person with zero — zero income, zero years of schooling, death at birth — pulls the geometric mean toward zero regardless of how well everyone else is doing.

For South Africa, the IHDI calculation is devastating.

**Income inequality penalty: approximately 57%.** South Africa's income distribution, with a Gini coefficient exceeding 0.63, imposes the largest penalty of the three dimensions. The income index drops from 0.741 to approximately 0.319. This collapse reflects the mathematical reality that when you apply the geometric mean to a distribution where 50% of the population shares 6% of income, the bottom half drags the adjusted score far below the arithmetic mean. The 57% income inequality penalty is among the largest in the world — comparable to those of Namibia and the Central African Republic, countries with far lower income levels.

**Health inequality penalty: approximately 25%.** Inequality in life expectancy within South Africa is substantial, driven by differential access to healthcare (private versus public), geographic disparities (urban versus rural), the socioeconomic gradient of HIV/AIDS and TB, and the high burden of violence that falls disproportionately on young men in low-income communities. The health index drops from 0.662 to approximately 0.497. A 25% penalty on health is high by international standards — it reflects not just inequality in healthcare access but inequality in the social determinants of health: nutrition, sanitation, housing quality, exposure to violence, and environmental risks.

**Education inequality penalty: approximately 22%.** Despite the expansion of educational access, inequality in years of schooling persists — older cohorts, particularly older black South Africans who were educated under Bantu Education, have substantially fewer years of schooling than younger cohorts. The education index drops from 0.720 to approximately 0.562. If learning quality rather than years of schooling were used, this penalty would almost certainly be larger, because inequality in learning outcomes (measured by test scores) is even greater than inequality in years of attendance.

**The composite IHDI: 0.462.** Combining the three inequality-adjusted dimension indices yields an IHDI of approximately 0.462. The loss from the standard HDI of 0.741 is 37.6% — meaning that more than a third of South Africa's apparent human development achievement is lost to inequality.

To grasp what this means concretely: an IHDI of 0.462 places South Africa not among its upper-middle-income peers but among countries classified as "low human development." Brazil's IHDI is 0.576 (a 24% loss — substantial, but far less than South Africa's). Mexico's is 0.607 (a 22% loss). Turkey's is 0.700 (a 16% loss). Thailand's is 0.639 (a 20% loss). South Africa's inequality penalty is not just the largest among upper-middle-income countries — it is so large that it effectively erases the country's claim to be a "high development" nation.

The 37.6% penalty is not an abstract statistic. It is the quantitative expression of what it means to live in a country where some children attend schools with swimming pools, science laboratories, and one-to-one computing, while others attend schools without running water. It is the quantitative expression of a health system in which some citizens access world-class private care and others wait eight hours for treatment at understaffed clinics. It is the quantitative expression of an economy in which a hedge fund manager in Sandton earns more in a day than a domestic worker earns in a year. The IHDI does not merely measure inequality. It measures the cost of inequality in human development terms — the capabilities that are destroyed when a society that produces enough for everyone distributes it as though most people do not matter.

:::takeaway
The IHDI collapse from 0.741 to 0.462 is the single most important number in South Africa's development profile. The 37.6% penalty — driven by income inequality (57% loss), health inequality (25% loss), and education inequality (22% loss) — is the largest among upper-middle-income countries. It means that more than a third of South Africa's apparent human development is lost to unequal distribution. The IHDI places South Africa not among its peers but among "low human development" countries.
:::

## The Gender Development Index

The Gender Development Index compares female and male HDI scores, providing a measure of gender-based disparities in human development. South Africa's GDI reveals a picture more complex than simple narratives of gender disadvantage would suggest.

**Female advantages in health and education.** South African women have higher life expectancy than men — approximately 67 years versus 60 years. The gender gap in life expectancy is large by international standards, driven primarily by three factors: men's higher HIV/AIDS mortality (men are less likely to test and less likely to adhere to treatment), men's dramatically higher exposure to interpersonal violence (the homicide rate for men is approximately seven times that for women), and men's higher rates of occupational hazard, substance abuse, and risk-taking behaviour. South African women also have slightly more years of schooling than men on average, reflecting both the higher dropout rates among boys and the expansion of girls' educational access since 1994.

**Male advantage in income.** The income dimension inverts the pattern. South African men earn significantly more than women — the gender pay gap, measured as female-to-male earned income ratio, is approximately 0.65, meaning women earn approximately 65 cents for every rand earned by men. This gap reflects multiple overlapping factors: occupational segregation (women are concentrated in lower-paying sectors), the "motherhood penalty" (women's labour force participation drops during childbearing years and often does not fully recover), and the unequal burden of unpaid care work that limits women's availability for paid employment.

**The composite GDI.** South Africa's GDI places it in "Group 3" of the UNDP's five-group classification — countries with medium equality in HDI achievements between women and men. The female HDI is marginally lower than the male HDI overall, with the income disadvantage outweighing the health and education advantages. South Africa's GDI is in line with its regional peers but below countries like Rwanda and Namibia, which have made more deliberate policy investments in gender equality.

The GDI, however, does not capture what may be the most significant gender dimension of South Africa's development challenge: gender-based violence. South Africa has one of the highest rates of intimate partner violence, sexual assault, and femicide in the world. The South Africa Demographic and Health Survey reports that approximately one in five women has experienced physical violence from a partner. Femicide rates are approximately five times the global average. This violence does not appear directly in the GDI or the HDI, but its impact on women's capabilities is pervasive — it constrains mobility, undermines mental health, disrupts education and employment, and creates a climate of fear that limits women's freedom in ways that no index fully captures. For a detailed analysis, see [The System's Most Catastrophic Failure](/articles/ss-2-4-the-systems-most-catastrophic-failure) in the Citizen Safety and Security series.

## The Gender Inequality Index

The Gender Inequality Index provides a different and complementary perspective to the GDI. While the GDI compares female and male HDI, the GII measures gender-based disadvantage across three dimensions: reproductive health, empowerment, and labour market participation.

**Reproductive health.** This dimension uses two indicators: the maternal mortality ratio and the adolescent birth rate. South Africa's maternal mortality ratio of approximately 127 per 100,000 live births is high for an upper-middle-income country — reflecting both the burden of HIV in pregnancy and weaknesses in obstetric care. The adolescent birth rate (births per 1,000 women aged 15-19) is approximately 44 — again, high for the country's income level, reflecting inadequate access to reproductive health services and comprehensive sexuality education, particularly in rural areas.

**Empowerment.** This dimension uses the share of parliamentary seats held by women and the share of the population with at least some secondary education. South Africa performs relatively well on parliamentary representation — women hold approximately 45% of seats in the National Assembly, one of the highest proportions globally, reflecting the ANC's voluntary gender quotas and the proportional representation electoral system. Secondary education attainment, as noted, is also relatively high for both sexes.

**Labour market participation.** Women's labour force participation in South Africa is approximately 49%, compared to approximately 62% for men. This gap, while not the largest globally, reflects the structural barriers to women's employment discussed above.

South Africa's overall GII score of approximately 0.405 places it in the middle of the global distribution — better than many sub-Saharan African countries but worse than most upper-middle-income peers. The relatively strong empowerment indicators (parliamentary representation, educational access) are offset by the weak reproductive health indicators. And, again, the GII does not directly capture the impact of gender-based violence, which may be the most significant constraint on women's development in South Africa.

:::expert
"Gender-based violence in South Africa is not a marginal problem affecting a few unlucky individuals. It is a structural feature of the society — a system of social control that limits what women can do, where they can go, and who they can become. Any measure of gender equality that does not account for this violence fundamentally understates the depth of gender inequality in this country." The GDI and GII capture economic and institutional gender disparities. They do not capture the capability-destroying effect of living in a country where violence against women is endemic.
:::

## The Multidimensional Poverty Index

The Multidimensional Poverty Index represents the most granular attempt to measure deprivation beyond income poverty. Developed by the Oxford Poverty and Human Development Initiative (OPHI) in partnership with the UNDP, the MPI identifies people who are simultaneously deprived across multiple dimensions using ten indicators grouped into three dimensions that mirror the HDI: health (nutrition and child mortality), education (years of schooling and school attendance), and living standards (cooking fuel, sanitation, drinking water, electricity, housing, and assets).

A person is identified as MPI-poor if they are deprived in at least one-third of the weighted indicators simultaneously. The MPI captures forms of deprivation that income measures miss — a person can be above the income poverty line but MPI-poor if their household lacks access to clean water, adequate sanitation, and electricity simultaneously.

**South Africa's MPI profile.** South Africa's MPI headcount — the proportion of the population identified as multidimensionally poor — is approximately 6.3%, with an intensity of deprivation (the average share of deprivations experienced by poor people) of approximately 41%. The MPI value (headcount multiplied by intensity) is approximately 0.025. These figures place South Africa substantially better than most sub-Saharan African countries — a reflection of the significant post-apartheid investment in basic services delivery.

The progress is real. Between 1994 and the present, the proportion of households with access to piped water increased from approximately 60% to over 88%. Electrification expanded from approximately 50% to over 90%. The social grant system, as noted, reaches approximately 28 million beneficiaries. The expansion of basic services — whatever its limitations and backlogs — has reduced multidimensional poverty more effectively than the stagnant economy has reduced income poverty.

**Where multidimensional poverty persists.** The national MPI figure, like the HDI, masks significant geographic variation. Multidimensional poverty is concentrated in three types of areas. First, the former apartheid homelands — the Transkei and Ciskei regions of the Eastern Cape, the former KwaZulu homeland, northern Limpopo, and parts of Mpumalanga — where colonial and apartheid spatial planning deliberately concentrated black South Africans in areas with minimal economic opportunity and inadequate infrastructure. These areas remain trapped in multidimensional deprivation despite two decades of infrastructure investment, because the underlying economic structures that would generate employment and income have not developed. Second, informal settlements on the periphery of major cities, where rapid urbanisation has outpaced service delivery. Third, deep rural areas far from service delivery points, where the cost of providing infrastructure is highest and where institutional capacity is weakest.

**Which deprivations are most prevalent.** The most common MPI deprivations in South Africa are in the living standards dimension — specifically, inadequate sanitation, reliance on polluting cooking fuels (wood, paraffin, coal), and poor housing quality. These are deprivations that reflect infrastructure backlogs rather than income constraints — a household can have grant income but lack access to flush sanitation because the municipality has not extended sewerage to their area. This pattern confirms that multidimensional poverty in South Africa is at least as much a governance and service delivery problem as it is an income problem.

**The MPI and the HDI.** The MPI and the HDI measure different things. The HDI is a national average of development outcomes. The MPI identifies individuals who fall below a multidimensional threshold. A country can have a relatively high HDI and a relatively low MPI (as South Africa does) if average development levels are reasonable but concentrated among a subset of the population, while the remaining multidimensionally poor population is relatively small. The danger is in treating the low MPI headcount as evidence that deprivation is a residual problem. In South Africa, the MPI threshold is set relatively high — people must be deprived on a third of indicators to be counted. A large proportion of the population experiences one or two deprivations without meeting the MPI threshold but is nevertheless far from the "high human development" standard that the HDI implies.

## What the Gap Reveals

The distance between South Africa's HDI of 0.741 and its IHDI of 0.462 is not merely a statistical artefact. It is the quantitative signature of a society that has failed to convert its considerable resources — economic, institutional, and human — into broadly shared human development. The gap reveals several things simultaneously.

**It reveals the continuing legacy of apartheid.** Apartheid was, in human development terms, a system designed to concentrate capabilities among a racial minority and to systematically deprive the majority. The spatial geography of inequality (former homelands versus former white South Africa), the racial distribution of income and wealth, the quality differential in education, and the patterns of health inequality all trace back to apartheid-era policies. Thirty years of democracy have not erased these patterns — they have mitigated them at the margins while leaving the underlying structure largely intact.

**It reveals governance failures since 1994.** The apartheid legacy explains the starting point but not the trajectory. Countries with comparable colonial legacies and initial inequality — Malaysia, for instance, which inherited deep ethnic economic inequality from British colonial rule — have made substantially more progress in reducing inequality and expanding human development. South Africa's governance failures — the AIDS denialism of the Mbeki era, the state capture of the Zuma era, the chronic underperformance of basic education, the hollowing out of municipal capacity — have squandered opportunities that a more competent state would have seized.

**It reveals the limits of social spending without structural reform.** South Africa spends a substantial share of GDP on social services — approximately 6.2% on education, 8.3% on health, and significant amounts on social grants and housing. This spending has prevented the worst deprivation and has reduced multidimensional poverty. But it has not reduced inequality in outcomes, because spending alone — without institutional reform, management improvement, and accountability mechanisms — does not translate into capability expansion for the poorest.

**It reveals the human cost of unemployment.** The single largest driver of the IHDI collapse is income inequality, and the single largest driver of income inequality is the structural unemployment crisis. In a country where more than 40% of the labour force is unemployed on the expanded definition, no amount of redistributive spending can close the gap between those with employment income and those without it. The IHDI collapse is, in significant part, a labour market failure expressed in human development terms.

**It reveals the urgency of integrated policy.** You cannot fix the IHDI gap by addressing health alone, education alone, or income alone. The three dimensions are interconnected. Improving education quality would increase employment prospects, which would increase income, which would improve health outcomes (through better nutrition, housing, and access to care), which would improve educational performance (healthy children learn better). Conversely, failing in any one dimension undermines the others. This interconnection — the reinforcing cycle — is the subject of the next article.

The 0.741-to-0.462 gap is not just a number. It is sixty million people's lived experience of inequality. It is the quantitative distance between what South Africa produces and what its citizens experience. It is, in the language of the capabilities approach, the measure of unfreedom — the capabilities that exist in aggregate but are denied to the majority. Closing that gap is not a technical challenge. It is the central moral and political challenge of South African governance, and it will remain so until the reinforcing cycle of safety, growth, and development is turned from a vicious spiral into a virtuous one.

:::takeaway
The gap between South Africa's HDI (0.741) and its IHDI (0.462) reveals the legacy of apartheid, post-1994 governance failures, the limits of social spending without structural reform, the devastating impact of mass unemployment, and the urgent need for integrated policy that addresses health, education, and income simultaneously. This gap is not a technical problem — it is the central challenge of South African governance, quantifying the distance between what the economy produces and what citizens actually experience.
:::

**The Development Dashboard**

Use this five-indicator dashboard to assess South Africa's human development trajectory. Each indicator corresponds to a dimension of the HDI family and can be tracked using publicly available data.

**Indicator 1: Life expectancy at birth.**
Source: Stats SA mid-year population estimates; UNDP Human Development Report.
What to watch: Is life expectancy increasing? Is it approaching the Preston Curve prediction for South Africa's income level (approximately 73 years)? Is the gap between provinces narrowing or widening?

**Indicator 2: Learning-adjusted years of schooling.**
Source: World Bank Human Capital Index; PIRLS and TIMSS assessment results (published every four to five years).
What to watch: Are international assessment scores improving? Is the gap between Quintile 1 and Quintile 5 schools narrowing? Mean years of schooling is a misleading indicator — demand learning-adjusted figures.

**Indicator 3: The IHDI penalty.**
Source: UNDP Human Development Report (published annually).
What to watch: Is the percentage gap between HDI and IHDI shrinking? A shrinking penalty means development gains are being distributed more broadly. South Africa's current penalty of approximately 37 per cent is among the highest in the world. Any government serious about development must demonstrate progress on this metric.

**Indicator 4: Expanded unemployment rate.**
Source: Stats SA Quarterly Labour Force Survey.
What to watch: Is the expanded unemployment rate (which includes discouraged work-seekers) declining? This is a better measure than the narrow rate because it captures the full scale of joblessness. The income dimension of the HDI cannot improve meaningfully while 40 per cent of the labour force is unemployed.

**Indicator 5: MPI headcount and intensity.**
Source: Stats SA (using General Household Survey data) and UNDP MPI reports.
What to watch: Is the share of people experiencing multidimensional poverty declining? Is the intensity of deprivation (average number of deprivations among the poor) falling? Are the geographic concentrations of poverty — former homelands, informal settlements, deep rural areas — receiving targeted attention?
