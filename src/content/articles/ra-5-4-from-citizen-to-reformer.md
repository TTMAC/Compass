## You Now Have a Diagnostic Framework — Use It

You have made it through fifteen articles. That is not a small thing. Most commentary on South African governance gives you outrage — a catalogue of failures, a parade of incompetent or corrupt officials, and the implicit message that nothing can be done because the problems are too deep, too widespread, too entrenched. You now have something different. You have a diagnostic framework.

## You Now Have a Diagnostic Framework

In [The Eight Tests Every Government Institution Must Pass](/articles/ra-1-1-eight-tests), you learned the RAARICLE™ framework — eight elements that every government institution needs in order to function. Responsibility: one entity must own the task end-to-end. Accountability: one name must be on the performance agreement. Authority: the accountable entity must control the levers — hiring, firing, procurement, regulation. Resources: the money must follow the function. Information: performance data must be timely, accurate, and public. Capability: the institution must have qualified people in the right posts. Legitimacy: there must be a clear constitutional or legislative mandate. Enforcement: there must be automatic consequences for failure. Eight tests. When a government function passes all eight, services tend to work. When it fails several simultaneously — which is the norm — the result is the systemic collapse that millions of South Africans experience as daily life.

In [Why South Africa's Institutions Keep Failing](/articles/ra-1-2-why-institutions-fail), you learned to classify what you find into five failure patterns. Fragmentation, where too many institutions share a function and nobody owns the outcome. Misalignment, where the entity held accountable does not control the inputs that determine the outcome. Absence, where a critical RAARICLE™ element — usually enforcement — simply does not exist. Conflation, where functions that should be separated are bundled into one entity, creating conflicts of interest. And capture, where the institutional architecture has been deliberately distorted to serve private interests rather than public ones.

Then you watched these tools applied to the functions that matter most in South African life. In [Fixing Basic Education](/articles/ra-2-1-fixing-basic-education), you saw how the education value chain fragments responsibility across the Department of Basic Education, nine provincial departments, seventy-five districts, the Education Labour Relations Council, and twenty-three thousand School Governing Bodies — with the result that provincial MECs are held accountable for outcomes they cannot control. In [Fixing Primary Healthcare](/articles/ra-2-2-fixing-primary-healthcare), you traced how the concurrent listing of health in Schedule 4 creates a misalignment between national norms and provincial delivery that shows up as unequal access to clinic-level care across the country. In [Fixing Human Settlements](/articles/ra-2-3-fixing-human-settlements), you mapped a value chain so fragmented that a housing beneficiary's journey from application to title deed crosses at least five entities across three spheres — and frequently takes a decade or more.

You then turned to the municipal coalface, where service delivery failure is most acutely felt. In [Fixing Water and Sanitation](/articles/ra-4-1-fixing-water-and-sanitation), you examined how the conflation of the Water Services Authority and Water Services Provider functions in a single municipality creates a regulator-operator paradox. In [Fixing Electricity](/articles/ra-4-2-fixing-electricity), you traced the structural consequences of Eskom's vertically integrated monopoly and the constitutional ambiguity around municipal electricity reticulation. In [Fixing Waste and Roads](/articles/ra-4-3-fixing-waste-and-roads), you saw how unglamorous but essential municipal functions suffer from capability collapse and enforcement absence. And in [Building Municipal Capability](/articles/ra-4-4-building-municipal-capability), you confronted the foundational problem: many municipalities lack the qualified personnel to perform even basic administrative functions, let alone deliver complex infrastructure services.

Along the way, you examined the provincial system — its performance crisis, the challenge of making concurrent functions work, and what happens when provinces fail so badly that national intervention under Section 100 becomes necessary. You explored the legal pathways for reform — what needs constitutional amendment, what needs legislation, and what can be achieved through regulation alone. You learned how institutional design can either resist or enable capture. And in [The Integrated Reform Programme](/articles/ra-5-3-the-integrated-reform-programme), you saw why these function-specific reforms must be pursued through a single legislated programme — a Governance Reform Programme Act that establishes common principles, coordinates implementation, and connects measurement to enforcement through existing institutions.

A natural question arises from the reform blueprints across Parts 3 and 4: how do the provincial and municipal models relate to each other? The answer is that they apply the same design principle — structured autonomy — at different spheres, to different functions, through different institutional vehicles. For concurrent functions like education and healthcare (Part 3), the delivery entity is typically a provincial department or a delegated authority within the provincial sphere, receiving operational autonomy from the national standard-setter. For municipal functions like water, electricity, and waste (Part 4), the delivery entity is a municipal department, a regional utility, or a district shared services entity, receiving operational autonomy within nationally or provincially defined standards. The two models do not compete — they complement each other. A district municipality that provides shared technical services to local municipalities for water and sanitation (the Part 4 model) is performing a different function from a provincial department that delivers education under structured autonomy from the national department (the Part 3 model). The structured autonomy principle is the same in both cases: the entity closest to delivery holds operational authority, within outcome standards set by the entity with the broader mandate, with automatic enforcement when outcomes fall short.

:::takeaway
Across fifteen articles, you have built the ability to do something most South Africans — including most commentators, journalists, and politicians — cannot do: diagnose governance failure structurally. You can name the specific RAARICLE™ elements that are misaligned, classify the failure pattern, assess severity, and identify the legal pathway for a fix. That is not theoretical knowledge. That is a practical analytical toolkit.
:::

The question now is what you do with it. The remaining distance — from diagnosis to action — is what this article covers. It is not a short distance, and it is not without obstacles. But it is navigable, and the tools you now possess make you a fundamentally more effective advocate than someone armed only with frustration and a social media account. The six steps that follow take you from citizen to reformer.

## Step 1: Pick Your Function

The first instinct, when you understand the scale of South Africa's governance design problems, is to try to fix everything at once. Resist that instinct. Reform movements that try to address every failure simultaneously achieve nothing, because their energy is diffused across too many fronts, their message becomes incoherent, and decision-makers learn to ignore them. The most effective citizen advocacy in South Africa's history has been function-specific. The Treatment Action Campaign focused on antiretroviral access. Equal Education focused on school infrastructure norms. Section27 focused on the right to basic education and health care. **Their power came precisely from their narrow focus — they could not be dismissed as generalised complainers, because they knew their function intimately and could articulate exactly what was broken and how to fix it.**

So start with one function. Choose the government function that affects your life most directly. If your tap runs dry, if the water is brown, if your street has been without water for three days this month — your function is water. If your child's school lacks textbooks, if the toilets are pit latrines, if the maths teacher has not been replaced since February — your function is basic education. If the clinic turns you away because there is no medication, if the queue starts at four in the morning and the doctor arrives at ten — your function is primary healthcare. If the refuse has not been collected in two weeks and the illegal dump site is growing at the end of your street — your function is waste management. If the potholes on your road are deep enough to damage a vehicle — your function is roads. If the electricity goes off unpredictably and for hours at a time, beyond the national load-shedding schedule — your function is electricity reticulation.

Once you have chosen your function, you need to identify the right institution. This is where the sphere assignment rules from [The Architecture of the State](/articles/1-1-architecture-of-the-state) become essential. South Africa's Constitution assigns functions to spheres in Schedules 4 and 5. Some functions are exclusively national, some are exclusively provincial, some are exclusively municipal, and some — the most problematic ones — are concurrent, meaning two or more spheres share responsibility.

For water supply, the institution that bears primary responsibility is your municipality in its capacity as a Water Services Authority under the Water Services Act. The WSA is responsible for ensuring access to water services within its area of jurisdiction. This is a municipal function, even though national government — through the Department of Water and Sanitation — sets the regulatory framework, manages bulk water resources, and provides conditional grant funding. If your water is not flowing, the first institution to target is your municipality's water services division.

For basic education, the picture is more complex because education is a concurrent function listed in Schedule 4. The provincial Department of Education is the employer of teachers and the manager of schools. But critical inputs — curriculum, teacher salary scales, post provisioning norms, school funding norms — are set nationally by the Department of Basic Education. If your child's school lacks a teacher, the provincial department is the institution that fills the post. If the curriculum itself is the problem, the national department sets it. Your diagnostic will need to identify which institution controls the specific input that is failing.

For electricity reticulation — the distribution of electricity within a municipal area — the responsible institution is your municipality, which purchases bulk electricity from Eskom (or, increasingly, from independent power producers) and distributes it to households and businesses. For healthcare at the primary level, the responsible institution is the provincial Department of Health, which manages clinics and community health centres. For refuse collection and local roads, the responsible institution is your municipality.

:::expert
The South African Local Government Association has published ward-level data linking specific municipal functions to the responsible departments and budget allocations. This data, available through SALGA's municipal barometer and through your municipality's annual report, can help you confirm which institution is responsible for the function you care about — and how much money has been allocated to it.
:::

*The key principle is precision.* Do not say "the government must fix the water." **Say "the Emfuleni Local Municipality, as the Water Services Authority for my area, is responsible under Section 11 of the Water Services Act for ensuring access to a basic water supply."** That level of specificity changes the nature of the conversation. It signals that you understand the institutional architecture and that you are holding a specific entity to a specific legal obligation.

## Step 2: Run the Eight Tests

You have chosen your function and identified the responsible institution. Now you need to diagnose the problem structurally. This is where the RAARICLE™ framework moves from theory to practice. You are going to apply all eight tests to your chosen function, one by one, and record what you find. Do this on paper, in a spreadsheet, in a notebook — the medium does not matter. What matters is that you work through each test systematically rather than jumping to the one that seems most obvious.

Let us walk through an example. Suppose you live in a mid-sized municipality and your function is water.

### Responsibility

The first test: can you name one entity that owns the water delivery task end-to-end? In most municipalities, the answer should be the municipality itself, acting as both Water Services Authority and Water Services Provider. But in practice, the picture is often murkier. Some municipalities have separated the WSA and WSP functions — the municipality retains the authority role while contracting out the provider role to a municipal entity, a water board, or a private operator. Others have nominally separated these functions but in practice conflate them. To answer this test, look at your municipality's organisational structure, available in its Integrated Development Plan or annual report. Can you identify a single division or entity that is responsible for water from source to tap? Or is responsibility fragmented across multiple divisions, with bulk water managed separately from reticulation, billing managed by the finance department, and capital infrastructure managed by a separate infrastructure division?

### Accountability

Second test: who answers when the water fails? In a municipality, the accounting officer — the municipal manager — is legally accountable for all municipal functions under the Municipal Finance Management Act. The portfolio councillor for water services (if your municipality has such a portfolio) is politically accountable. But effective accountability requires more than a name on an organogram. It requires that the accountable person faces real consequences for failure. Has your municipal manager ever been disciplined for water service failures? Has the portfolio councillor faced any repercussions? If the answer is no — and in most municipalities, it will be no — then you have identified an enforcement gap, which is itself a critical RAARICLE™ failure.

### Authority

Third test: does the entity responsible for water actually control the levers that determine whether water flows? This means authority over hiring (can they appoint qualified water engineers and process controllers?), authority over procurement (can they contract for chemicals, pipes, and maintenance without unreasonable delays?), authority over operations (can they make real-time decisions about treatment processes, pressure management, and network maintenance?), and regulatory authority (can they enforce water quality standards?). In many municipalities, hiring authority is constrained by political deployment practices, procurement authority is hobbled by supply chain management regulations that prioritise procedural compliance over operational outcomes, and operational authority is fragmented across multiple departments.

### Resources

Fourth test: does the institution have the money? For water, resources come from three main sources: the equitable share allocation from national government, conditional grants (primarily the Municipal Infrastructure Grant and the Water Services Infrastructure Grant), and revenue from water tariffs. The question is not just whether money arrives but whether it is adequate, whether it is ring-fenced for water, and whether it is actually spent on water. National Treasury's municipal finance data, available through the Municipal Money portal, will tell you how much your municipality receives for water-related grants, how much it collects in water revenue, and — critically — how much it actually spends. If your municipality consistently underspends its water infrastructure grants while the infrastructure deteriorates, you have found a resource management problem even if the nominal allocation is adequate.

### Information

Fifth test: is performance data available, timely, and accurate? For water, the key information sources are the Blue Drop and Green Drop reports published by the Department of Water and Sanitation, which assess drinking water quality and wastewater management respectively. If your municipality has not been assessed — or if its scores are declining — that is a finding. Your municipality should also publish water quality results, service delivery statistics, and infrastructure condition assessments in its annual report. If this information is absent, incomplete, or obviously unreliable, the information test fails. Without reliable data, neither the municipality nor its residents can assess whether the system is working.

### Capability

Sixth test: does the institution have qualified people in the right posts? For water, this means process controllers with the qualifications required by the National Water Act to operate water and wastewater treatment works, engineers to design and maintain infrastructure, artisans to repair pumps and pipes, and managers with the technical knowledge to oversee operations. The Water Services Act requires that water services institutions employ or have access to appropriately qualified personnel. If your municipality's water treatment works is operated by someone without the requisite process controller qualification — and this is common, particularly in smaller municipalities — you have a capability gap that no amount of money can compensate for.

### Legitimacy

Seventh test: is there a clear constitutional or legal mandate? For water, the mandate is unambiguous. Section 27(1)(b) of the Constitution guarantees the right of access to sufficient water. The Water Services Act assigns responsibility for water services to Water Services Authorities, which are municipalities or district municipalities. Schedule 4 Part B of the Constitution lists water and sanitation services as a local government matter. There is no ambiguity about the mandate. But legitimacy in the RAARICLE™ sense also encompasses democratic legitimacy — does the institution have the democratic mandate to make binding decisions about water services in its area? If your municipality is dysfunctional to the point where it cannot convene a council meeting, its democratic legitimacy as a decision-making body is compromised even if its legal mandate is clear.

### Enforcement

Eighth test: what happens when the institution fails? This is, across South African governance, the test that fails most consistently. If your municipality delivers water that does not meet drinking water quality standards, what are the automatic consequences? If the municipality fails to maintain its water infrastructure until it collapses, who intervenes and when? If the municipal manager presides over years of water service failure, what mechanism triggers removal? In most cases, the answer is: nothing automatic happens. The Department of Water and Sanitation can issue directives, but compliance is voluntary in practice. Provincial government can intervene under Section 139 of the Constitution, but the political threshold for intervention is high and the track record of successful interventions is poor. The only enforcement mechanism that consistently works is litigation — citizens going to court under Section 27 to enforce their right to water. But litigation is expensive, slow, and inaccessible to most South Africans. If you find that there is no automatic, institutional consequence for failure in your chosen function, you have identified what is arguably the most critical structural flaw in South African governance.

:::takeaway
Running all eight RAARICLE™ tests on a single function in a single institution will typically reveal not one problem but several interconnected ones. The power of the framework is that it prevents you from fixating on the most visible symptom — usually a capability or resource gap — and forces you to examine the full institutional architecture. Most service delivery failures involve at least three or four RAARICLE™ elements failing simultaneously.
:::

Write down your findings for each test. Be specific. Do not write "accountability is weak." **Write "the municipal manager has been in post for four years during which the Blue Drop score fell from 72 to 38, with no disciplinary consequences."** The more specific your findings, the more powerful your diagnosis.

## Step 3: Find the Data

A diagnosis is only as strong as its evidence. The good news is that South Africa, for all its governance failures, produces a remarkable quantity of public data about government performance. The challenge is knowing where to find it, how to read it, and how to use it. What follows is a guide to the most important sources, organised by the RAARICLE™ elements they illuminate.

The single most valuable source for any governance diagnostic is the Auditor-General of South Africa. The AG audits every national department, every provincial department, and every municipality annually, and the audit reports are published on the AGSA website at agsa.co.za. For municipalities, the AG's annual Municipal Finance Management Act audit outcomes provide a wealth of information: whether the municipality received a clean audit, a qualified opinion, an adverse opinion, or a disclaimer; the nature and extent of irregular expenditure, fruitless and wasteful expenditure, and unauthorised expenditure; the effectiveness of internal controls; the quality of financial management; and — increasingly — the quality of service delivery performance reporting. The AG's general reports, published alongside the individual audit reports, identify systemic trends across the local government sector. If you are diagnosing a municipal function, start with the AG's most recent MFMA report for your municipality. It will tell you more about the institution's financial health, governance practices, and accountability mechanisms than any other single document.

For financial data — the Resources element of your RAARICLE™ diagnostic — National Treasury is the primary source. The Municipal Money portal at municipalmoney.gov.za provides detailed financial data for every municipality in South Africa, including revenue sources, expenditure by function, conditional grant allocations and spending, and financial ratios that indicate fiscal health. Treasury also publishes the annual Division of Revenue, which shows how much money flows to each sphere and through which grant mechanisms. For understanding whether your municipality has the resources to perform a function, and whether it is spending those resources effectively, Treasury's data is indispensable.

Statistics South Africa publishes data that helps you assess outcomes — the end result of governance. The Community Survey, the General Household Survey, and the Census provide data on access to water, sanitation, electricity, refuse removal, and other basic services at municipal and even ward level. Stats SA data will tell you what percentage of households in your municipality have access to piped water in their dwelling or yard, what percentage rely on communal taps, and what percentage have no access at all. This is the demand-side data — what citizens actually experience — that complements the supply-side data from the AG and Treasury.

For sector-specific diagnostics, you need sector-specific sources. The Department of Water and Sanitation publishes the Blue Drop report, which assesses drinking water quality management at municipal level, and the Green Drop report, which assesses wastewater management. The No Drop report assesses water losses — the percentage of treated water that is lost through leaks, theft, and meter inaccuracies before it reaches a paying customer. If your function is water, these three reports together provide a comprehensive picture of your municipality's water management capability. For health, the Health Systems Trust publishes the District Health Barometer, an annual assessment of primary healthcare performance at district level that tracks indicators from immunisation coverage to maternal mortality. For infrastructure more broadly, the South African Institution of Civil Engineering publishes a periodic Infrastructure Report Card that assesses the condition of water, sanitation, roads, electricity, and other infrastructure nationally and, where data permits, provincially.

Operation Vulindlela — the joint initiative of the Presidency and National Treasury to accelerate structural reforms — publishes a reform tracker that monitors progress on priority reforms across energy, water, transport, telecommunications, and the visa regime. The tracker, available through the Presidency's website, tells you which reforms are on track, which are stalled, and what the bottlenecks are. If your proposed fix aligns with a Vulindlela priority, you have a powerful ally — and if it does not, you may need to advocate for its inclusion.

For understanding what Parliament knows about your function, the Parliamentary Monitoring Group at pmg.org.za is an extraordinary resource. PMG — which has tracked and documented parliamentary committee proceedings since 1998, and whose published analyses of parliamentary oversight capacity include its 2022 *Monitoring Parliament* reviews — records and publishes the proceedings of parliamentary portfolio committee meetings, where government departments report on their performance and are questioned by Members of Parliament. The committee minutes for Water and Sanitation, for Basic Education, for Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs, and for other portfolios contain detailed information about departmental performance, challenges, and planned reforms. If a department has told Parliament that it plans to address a problem you have identified, that creates an accountability trail you can use.

At the local level, your municipality is required by law to produce an Integrated Development Plan and an annual report. The IDP sets out the municipality's development priorities, service delivery targets, and budget allocations for a five-year cycle, with annual reviews. The annual report assesses performance against those targets. Both documents are supposed to be available on your municipality's website and at municipal offices. They are often poorly written, excessively long, and difficult to navigate — but they contain the municipality's own account of its performance, which you can compare against the AG's assessment and Stats SA's household-level data.

:::expert
The Constitution's Section 32 guarantees the right of access to any information held by the state, or held by a private body when that information is required for the exercise or protection of any right. This right is given effect by the Promotion of Access to Information Act (PAIA). If you cannot find the data you need through published sources, you can submit a PAIA request to the relevant institution. The institution is legally required to respond within 30 days. PAIA requests are free (or nearly free — a small reproduction fee may apply). The South African Human Rights Commission, which oversees PAIA compliance, has published guides to making PAIA requests on its website at sahrc.org.za.
:::

The point of gathering data is not to produce a comprehensive research report. It is to support your RAARICLE™ diagnosis with evidence that cannot be dismissed as anecdotal. When you say "the municipality's water treatment works lacks qualified process controllers," and you can point to the Blue Drop report showing a declining score and the municipal annual report showing vacant funded posts in the water division, your diagnosis carries weight. When you say "there are no consequences for failure," and you can show that the AG has issued qualified opinions for five consecutive years with no change in municipal leadership, your argument is difficult to refute.

## Step 4: Name the Structural Problem

You have run the eight tests. You have gathered evidence. Now comes the step that separates effective advocacy from mere complaint: you need to name the structural problem. Not the person. Not the party. The structure.

This is a difficult discipline. When your water has been off for three days, when your child's school has no functioning toilets, when the clinic has run out of chronic medication — it is natural and entirely reasonable to be angry at the officials who are supposed to be delivering these services. But **anger directed at individuals, while emotionally satisfying, is strategically weak.** Officials change. Politicians rotate. Municipal managers get suspended and then reinstated and then suspended again. **If the problem is structural — if the institutional design would produce failure regardless of who occupies the posts — then replacing the individuals achieves nothing.** The next set of officials will fail in exactly the same ways, for exactly the same structural reasons.

In [Why South Africa's Institutions Keep Failing](/articles/ra-1-2-why-institutions-fail), you learned the five failure patterns. Now you need to apply them to what you found in Steps 2 and 3. Here is how to recognise each one.

Fragmentation is present when your RAARICLE™ diagnostic reveals that multiple entities share responsibility for the function, and no single entity owns the outcome end-to-end. The signature symptom is that every entity can point to its piece of the chain and say "we did our part," while the citizen at the end of the chain receives no service. If you found in the Responsibility test that three or more entities are involved in delivering your function, and in the Accountability test that no single person bears unambiguous consequences for the outcome, you are looking at fragmentation. Human settlements is the classic case, but water services in district municipalities — where the district is the WSA but the local municipality controls the reticulation infrastructure — often exhibit the same pattern.

Misalignment is present when the entity that is held accountable for outcomes does not control the inputs that determine those outcomes. The signature symptom is that the accountable entity has excuses that are structurally valid — the provincial MEC for Education genuinely cannot control teacher salary scales, because those are negotiated at the national ELRC. If you found in the Accountability test that one entity is answerable for the function, but in the Authority and Resources tests that a different entity controls the budget, hiring, or regulatory framework, you are looking at misalignment. This is the dominant pattern in concurrent functions listed in Schedule 4 — education, health, housing — where national government sets the framework but provinces bear accountability for delivery.

Absence is present when a critical RAARICLE™ element simply does not exist. The most common form is enforcement absence — there are no automatic, institutional consequences for failure. If you found in the Enforcement test that nothing happens when the institution fails its mandate, you are looking at absence. But absence can also apply to other elements: there may be no information system to track performance, no capability framework to ensure qualified personnel, or no clear assignment of responsibility at all. The distinguishing feature of absence is that the element is not just weak — it is missing entirely, and no amount of improvement within the existing architecture can create it. It needs to be built from scratch.

Conflation is present when functions that should be separated are bundled into one entity, creating internal conflicts of interest. The signature symptom is that the entity is asked to both perform a function and regulate its own performance. If you found that your municipality is simultaneously the Water Services Authority (the regulator that sets standards and monitors compliance) and the Water Services Provider (the operator that runs the infrastructure), and that there is no independent entity assessing whether the municipality's water meets the standards it is supposed to enforce, you are looking at conflation. The fix for conflation is always separation — creating independent entities for functions that create conflicts of interest when combined.

Capture is present when the institutional architecture has been deliberately distorted to serve private interests. Capture is different from corruption. Corruption is an individual act — an official takes a bribe. Capture is a structural condition — the procurement system, the appointment process, or the regulatory framework has been redesigned to channel public resources to connected private interests on an ongoing basis. If you found in the Capability test that key posts are filled by unqualified political appointees, in the Information test that performance data is suppressed or falsified, and in the Enforcement test that oversight mechanisms have been disabled, you may be looking at capture. The Zondo Commission documented capture at the national level, but the same patterns operate at municipal level — where they are less visible but no less damaging.

Most real-world cases involve multiple patterns operating simultaneously. A municipality's water system might be conflated (WSA and WSP in one entity), suffer from absence (no enforcement mechanism for water quality failures), and exhibit capture (the water engineering post filled by a politically connected appointee without the requisite qualifications). Your task is to identify the primary pattern — the structural flaw that, if fixed, would create the conditions for addressing the others. In the water example, if you could establish an independent regulator to separate the WSA and WSP functions (fixing conflation) and create automatic enforcement triggers for water quality failures (fixing absence), you would create the institutional conditions that make capture harder to sustain — because an independent regulator with enforcement powers would detect and act on the appointment of unqualified personnel.

The critical discipline is this: **frame your finding as a structural diagnosis, not a personal accusation.** Do not say "the mayor is corrupt." Say "procurement data for water services contracts is not published, the WSA and WSP functions are conflated in a single entity with no independent oversight, and there are no automatic consequences for appointing unqualified personnel to technical posts. This institutional architecture creates structural vulnerability to commercial capture." *That framing is harder to dismiss. It does not depend on proving an individual's guilt. It identifies the design flaw that makes failure — and capture — predictable.* And it survives changes in political leadership, because it describes the system, not the person.

## Step 5: Propose the Structural Fix

The difference between a complainant and a reformer is a proposal. Anyone can say "the water is broken." A reformer can say "the water is broken because the WSA and WSP functions are conflated, enforcement is absent, and the capability framework does not mandate minimum qualifications for process controllers. The fix requires three things: first, the establishment of an independent economic regulator for water services, which needs legislation amending the Water Services Act; second, automatic enforcement triggers that suspend a WSA's operational authority when Blue Drop scores fall below a threshold, which can be achieved through regulation under the existing Act; and third, mandatory minimum qualifications for all personnel operating water and wastewater treatment works, which requires amendment to the regulations under the National Water Act."

That level of specificity transforms the conversation. A politician or official can deflect a general complaint with a general promise — "we are working on it," "we have a plan," "the situation is being addressed." It is much harder to deflect a specific structural proposal. The proposal creates a concrete standard against which future action can be measured. Did the Water Services Act amendment bill get introduced? Did the regulator get established? Were the qualification regulations published? These are verifiable, binary questions that do not depend on subjective assessments of political will or administrative competence.

To move from diagnosis to proposal, you need to match each structural problem to a structural remedy and then identify the legal pathway for implementation. The legal pathways were mapped in detail in [What Needs Constitutional Change and What Doesn't](/articles/ra-5-1-constitutional-vs-legislative), and you should refer to that article for the full analysis. But the essential framework is straightforward.

If your fix can be achieved within existing legislation — by changing regulations, issuing directives, or adjusting policy — it sits on the regulatory pathway. This is the fastest route and the one with the fewest political obstacles. Regulations can be changed by the relevant minister, usually after a public comment period. Examples include adjusting the qualification requirements for municipal technical staff under the Municipal Systems Act regulations, or tightening the conditions attached to conditional grants under the Division of Revenue Act.

If your fix requires a new law or the amendment of an existing law, it sits on the legislative pathway. This requires a bill to be introduced in Parliament, debated, and passed. For matters affecting provinces — which includes most service delivery functions — the bill must also be approved by the National Council of Provinces under the Section 76 procedure. This is a slower process, typically taking one to three years from introduction to promulgation. Examples include amending the Water Services Act to establish an independent regulator, or amending the Municipal Structures Act to change the allocation of functions between district and local municipalities.

If your fix requires changing the Constitution itself — moving a function from one schedule to another, altering the powers of a sphere, or creating a new constitutional institution — it sits on the constitutional amendment pathway. This requires a two-thirds majority in the National Assembly and, for amendments affecting provincial powers, a two-thirds majority in the NCOP. This is the hardest pathway, and you should only advocate for constitutional amendment when the structural problem genuinely cannot be solved within the existing constitutional text. The threshold is high, the political consensus required is broad, and the process is lengthy. But some problems — such as the concurrent listing of functions in Schedule 4 that produces the misalignment pattern — may genuinely require constitutional change.

**The more specific your proposal, the harder it is for decision-makers to ignore it.** "Fix the water" can be deflected. "Amend Section 30 of the Water Services Act to establish an independent Water Services Regulator with the power to issue binding compliance orders and impose administrative penalties for non-compliance with drinking water quality standards, as recommended by the National Development Plan and supported by Operation Vulindlela's water reform programme" cannot be deflected in the same way. It demands a substantive response.

:::expert
The National Planning Commission's Diagnostic Report and the National Development Plan both identified institutional reform as critical to improving service delivery. Operation Vulindlela, the structural reform programme housed in the Presidency and National Treasury, has prioritised reforms in water, energy, transport, and telecommunications. Where your proposal aligns with existing reform commitments — particularly Vulindlela priorities — you can leverage institutional momentum. Reference these commitments in your advocacy to demonstrate that your proposal is not radical but consistent with the government's own reform agenda.
:::

## Step 6: Use the Channels That Exist

South Africa's constitutional democracy provides multiple formal channels through which citizens can participate in governance, submit proposals, access information, and — when necessary — enforce their rights through the courts. These channels are imperfect, often under-resourced, and sometimes deliberately obstructed. But they exist, they have legal backing, and they have been used effectively by citizen movements throughout South Africa's democratic history. The task is not to invent new channels but to use the existing ones more effectively, armed with the structural analysis you have now developed.

### Ward Committees and Municipal Participation

At the municipal level, the most direct channel is the ward committee system. Every ward in South Africa is supposed to have a ward committee — a body of up to ten community members, chaired by the ward councillor, that serves as a formal link between residents and the municipal council. Ward committees are established under Section 73 of the Municipal Structures Act and are meant to provide a structured forum for community input on municipal matters. In practice, ward committees vary enormously in their effectiveness. In well-functioning municipalities, they are genuine spaces for community deliberation and input into the Integrated Development Plan process. In dysfunctional municipalities, they are either non-existent, controlled by the ruling party as patronage structures, or convened so rarely as to be meaningless.

Regardless of their current quality in your municipality, ward committees are a formal channel with legal standing. You have the right to attend ward committee meetings, to raise issues, and to submit written proposals. If the ward committee in your area is not functioning, that itself is a governance failure worth documenting — the municipality has a legal obligation to establish and support ward committees, and failure to do so can be reported to the provincial Department of Cooperative Governance.

Beyond ward committees, municipalities are legally required to conduct public participation processes as part of adopting their Integrated Development Plan, their annual budget, and their service delivery and budget implementation plan. These are not optional consultations — Section 16 of the Municipal Systems Act requires municipalities to develop a culture of community participation and to establish mechanisms for community involvement in municipal affairs. The IDP public participation process is a particularly important channel, because the IDP sets the municipality's development priorities and budget allocations for the next five years. If you can get a RAARICLE™-based structural proposal into the IDP process, you are embedding it in the municipality's own planning framework.

### Accessing Information Through PAIA

The Promotion of Access to Information Act gives effect to Section 32 of the Constitution — the right of access to information held by the state. A PAIA request is a formal, written request to a government institution for specific information. The institution must respond within 30 days, either providing the information or giving reasons for refusal (and even refusals can be appealed to the courts). PAIA requests are a powerful tool for filling gaps in your RAARICLE™ diagnostic. If you cannot find your municipality's water quality testing results, Blue Drop assessment data, or the employment records showing whether technical posts are filled by qualified personnel, a PAIA request compels the institution to provide it or explain why it cannot.

The practical reality is that many institutions delay, obstruct, or simply ignore PAIA requests. The South African Human Rights Commission, which is the oversight body for PAIA, has documented persistent non-compliance across all spheres of government. But non-compliance with PAIA is itself evidence of an information failure in your RAARICLE™ diagnostic — an institution that refuses to disclose performance data is an institution that fails the Information test. And PAIA non-compliance can be challenged in court, with costs awarded against the non-compliant institution.

### Parliamentary and Provincial Submissions

South Africa's Parliament and nine provincial legislatures operate through portfolio committees that oversee specific government departments. These committees regularly call for public comment on proposed legislation, conduct oversight visits, and hold hearings on matters of public concern. The Parliamentary Monitoring Group at pmg.org.za publishes the schedule of committee meetings, calls for public submissions, and committee reports. When a committee calls for public comment on a bill that affects your function — an amendment to the Water Services Act, a new Municipal Systems Amendment Bill, the annual Division of Revenue Bill — you have the right to submit a written comment. You do not need to be an organisation, a lawyer, or a subject-matter expert. Any citizen can submit.

A submission grounded in RAARICLE™ analysis stands out from the typical public comment, which tends to be either emotional ("I am angry about the water") or vaguely aspirational ("the government should do better"). **A submission that identifies specific RAARICLE™ failures, supports the diagnosis with evidence from the AG, Treasury, and sector-specific data, names the structural failure pattern, and proposes a specific legislative fix is the kind of submission that committee researchers flag for members' attention.** Parliamentary committees are staffed by researchers who are looking for substantive, evidence-based input. Give them what they need.

Provincial legislatures have similar committee structures and similar public participation rights. In addition, most provincial legislatures have a petitions process — a formal mechanism through which citizens or groups can petition the legislature to investigate a matter of public concern. A petition supported by a RAARICLE™ diagnostic and documentary evidence is a powerful instrument, because it creates a formal record that the legislature must acknowledge and respond to.

### Public Interest Litigation

When institutional channels fail — when the municipality ignores your submissions, when the provincial government does not respond to your petitions, when the ward committee is a hollow shell — the Constitution provides a final recourse: the courts. Section 38 of the Constitution grants broad standing to approach the courts for constitutional relief. You can approach a court in your own interest, on behalf of another person who cannot act in their own name, as a member of a group or class of persons, in the public interest, or as an association acting in the interest of its members. This is among the most generous standing provisions in any constitution in the world, and South African courts have consistently upheld it.

Public interest litigation has been one of the most effective tools for governance reform in South Africa's democratic history. The Treatment Action Campaign's successful litigation against the government's refusal to provide antiretrovirals changed national health policy. Equal Education's litigation on school infrastructure norms led to binding regulations requiring minimum standards for school buildings. Section27's litigation on textbook delivery in Limpopo resulted in court orders compelling the provincial department to deliver textbooks **and established the principle that the right to basic education includes the right to timely access to learning materials.**

Litigation is not the first option — it is expensive, slow, and uncertain. But *the possibility of litigation gives weight to every other form of advocacy.* When a municipal official knows that a PAIA request, if ignored, can result in a court order with costs, the request is harder to ignore. When a provincial department knows that failure to meet infrastructure norms can result in a structural interdict, the norms acquire teeth. **The courts are the enforcement mechanism of last resort in a system where institutional enforcement mechanisms are often absent.**

### Civil Society and Community Organisations

You do not have to do this alone. South Africa has a rich ecosystem of civil society organisations that do governance work, and many of them welcome — indeed, depend on — citizen engagement grounded in the kind of structural analysis you now possess. The Public Affairs Research Institute conducts research on state capability and governance design. The Institute for Security Studies analyses governance and safety at municipal level. Section27 litigates for the rights to education and healthcare. Equal Education advocates for school infrastructure and quality. The Legal Resources Centre provides free legal assistance for public interest matters, including PAIA enforcement and constitutional litigation. The Centre for Environmental Rights works on environmental governance, including water and waste management. At the local level, community advice offices — spread across the country and often supported by the Black Sash — provide free advice on accessing government services and exercising constitutional rights.

These organisations have institutional knowledge, legal expertise, and established relationships with government departments, parliamentary committees, and the media. A RAARICLE™ diagnostic produced by a citizen and supported by evidence is exactly the kind of input that strengthens their advocacy. Approach them not as a supplicant asking for help, but as a fellow analyst contributing evidence and proposals to a shared reform agenda.

### Keep Records

Whatever channels you use, keep meticulous records of every submission, every request, and every response — or lack thereof. Date your PAIA requests and note when the 30-day deadline expires. Record the date and content of your ward committee submission. Save the confirmation email from your parliamentary submission. Document the municipality's response to your IDP input — or document the absence of a response. These records serve two purposes. First, they create an accountability trail — evidence that you engaged through legitimate channels and that the institution did or did not respond as legally required. Second, they build the evidentiary foundation for escalation. If you eventually need to approach a civil society organisation, a parliamentary committee, or a court, your records demonstrate that you exhausted less adversarial options first and that the institution had every opportunity to respond.

:::framework
**The Citizen Reform Action Framework**

You have the diagnostic tools. Here is how to turn analysis into action, step by step:

**Step 1: Pick one function and one institution.**

Do not try to reform everything at once. Choose the government function that affects your daily life most — water, electricity, waste, roads, education, healthcare — and identify the specific institution responsible. Use the sphere assignment rules from earlier in this series to confirm you are targeting the right entity.

**Step 2: Run the RAARICLE™ diagnostic.**

For your chosen function and institution, assess all eight elements: Responsibility (is it clearly assigned?), Accountability (can someone be held answerable?), Authority (do they have the legal power?), Resources (do they have the money?), Information (is performance data available?), Capability (do they have qualified people?), Legitimacy (is there a constitutional or legal mandate?), Enforcement (are there consequences for failure?). Write down your findings for each element.

**Step 3: Gather evidence from public sources.**

Support your diagnosis with data. The key sources are: Auditor-General reports (www.agsa.co.za), National Treasury municipal finance data (municipaldata.treasury.gov.za), departmental annual reports (available on department websites), your municipality's Integrated Development Plan and annual report, and Blue/Green Drop reports for water. You have a constitutional right to this information under Section 32 of the Constitution, enforceable through PAIA requests.

**Step 4: Name the structural problem — not the person.**

Frame your finding as a structural diagnosis, not a personal complaint. Instead of "the mayor is corrupt," say "procurement data is not published, creating structural vulnerability to commercial capture." Instead of "the water department is useless," say "the WSA and WSP functions are conflated, and there are no qualified process controllers." Structural framing is harder to dismiss and survives changes in political leadership.

**Step 5: Propose a specific structural fix.**

Match your diagnosis to a reform proposal. If the problem is conflation, propose separation of functions. If it is a capability gap, propose mandatory professional qualifications. If it is an enforcement failure, propose automatic triggers. Reference the legal pathway — does your fix need a constitutional amendment, legislation, or just regulation? The more specific your proposal, the harder it is to ignore.

**Step 6: Use the channels that exist.**

Submit your analysis through formal channels: ward committee meetings, municipal public participation processes, PAIA requests for information you cannot find, written submissions to parliamentary portfolio committees, or engagement with civil society organisations that do governance work (such as the Public Affairs Research Institute, the Institute for Security Studies, or your local community advice office). Keep records of every submission and every response.

With these six steps, you move from being a frustrated citizen to being an informed reformer — someone who can diagnose structural problems, gather evidence, and propose specific fixes through legitimate channels. That is how governance actually changes.
:::

## The Long Game

*Structural reform is not a sprint.* If you go into this expecting quick results — expecting that a well-crafted submission will change policy within months, that a single court case will fix a broken institution, that naming the problem will be sufficient to solve it — you will burn out and give up. **The honest truth is that structural governance reform in any country takes years, and in South Africa's complex constitutional democracy, it takes longer still.**

Consider the scale of what South Africa has already achieved. The constitutional transition — from the unbanning of political organisations in February 1990 to the adoption of the final Constitution in December 1996 — took nearly seven years of negotiation, drafting, public participation, and Constitutional Court certification. The Municipal Demarcation Act and the Municipal Structures Act, which created the current local government architecture, were passed in 1998 and fully implemented only in the 2000 municipal elections. The Municipal Finance Management Act, which established the financial governance framework for local government, was enacted in 2003 and took several more years before municipalities were expected to comply fully. Each of these reforms was the product of sustained advocacy by citizens, civil society organisations, academics, and reform-minded officials working within the system. None of them happened because someone wrote a single submission.

Operation Vulindlela's reform programme, launched in 2020, illustrates both the pace and the possibility of structural reform within the existing constitutional framework. The energy reforms — including the raising of the licensing threshold for embedded generation, which allowed businesses and households to install their own solar power without a generation licence — took nearly two years from conception to implementation. The water reforms, including the establishment of a National Water Resources Infrastructure Agency and progress toward an independent economic regulator for water services, are multi-year undertakings that remain in progress. These are not failures of will. They are the natural pace of reforms that must navigate legislative processes, stakeholder consultation, institutional design, and implementation planning.

The implication for you as a citizen reformer is that you need to think in terms of sustained engagement, not single interventions. A useful frame is the campaign model: choose your function, develop your RAARICLE™ diagnostic, propose your fix, and then pursue it through multiple channels over months and years. Submit to the ward committee this quarter. Follow up with a PAIA request next quarter. Submit to the parliamentary portfolio committee when the relevant legislation comes up for review. Engage with civil society organisations working on the same function. Update your diagnostic as new AG reports and sector data become available. Build a record of engagement that demonstrates both the persistence of the structural problem and the seriousness of your advocacy.

There is a deeper reason why this matters — one that goes beyond the specific function you have chosen and the specific reform you are pursuing. South Africa's democracy is thirty years old. It was born out of the most successful negotiated transition in modern history, and it produced one of the most progressive constitutions in the world. But the institutional architecture that gives effect to that constitution — the allocation of functions to spheres, the coordination mechanisms between institutions, the accountability and enforcement frameworks — was designed in the late 1990s and has not been comprehensively updated since. The governance failures you experience daily are, in large part, the accumulated consequences of institutional designs that were never revised to match the realities of implementation.

The generation that designed this architecture faced different challenges — principally, the challenge of building a democratic state from the wreckage of apartheid, integrating homelands and independent states into a unified system, and establishing democratic governance in communities that had never had it. They did remarkable work under extraordinary constraints. But the architecture they built was always intended to evolve. Section 40 of the Constitution describes national, provincial, and local government as "distinctive, interdependent and interrelated" — a formulation that invites ongoing institutional design work, not a fixed settlement. The Constitutional Review Committee was established precisely to assess the functioning of the Constitution and recommend amendments where necessary.

That ongoing design work has largely stalled. The political system has been consumed by other priorities — economic policy, energy crisis, fiscal pressures, coalition politics — and the technical, unglamorous work of institutional redesign has fallen off the agenda. Citizens who can articulate structural problems in RAARICLE™ terms, who can propose specific fixes, who can identify the legal pathway for implementation, and who can sustain advocacy through the formal channels — those citizens are the ones who can put institutional reform back on the agenda.

:::takeaway
Every major governance reform in South Africa's democratic history — from the constitutional transition itself to the restructuring of local government to the energy reforms under Operation Vulindlela — was driven by citizens and organisations who could name the structural problem, propose a specific fix, and sustain advocacy over years. RAARICLE™ gives you the diagnostic language. The evidence is publicly available. The channels exist. The question is whether you will use them.
:::

You may not see the results of your advocacy in your lifetime. The citizens who petitioned for universal suffrage in the 1950s did not live to vote in 1994. The activists who fought for the right to water in the early 2000s are still waiting for universal access. But the arc of South African governance, for all its failures, bends toward the institutional designs that citizens demand. The Constitution exists because citizens demanded it. The right to water exists because citizens litigated for it. School infrastructure norms exist because learners and parents and civil society organisations refused to accept pit latrines as acceptable.

You are not starting from nothing. You are joining a tradition of citizen-driven institutional reform that is as old as South Africa's democracy — older, in fact, because it predates the Constitution itself. What is different now is that you have a framework. You can name the problem precisely. You can propose the fix specifically. You can trace the legal pathway accurately. And you can pursue it through channels that exist, with evidence that is publicly available, in a constitutional democracy that — whatever its failures — still recognises your right to do exactly this.

That is the transition from citizen to reformer. Not a change in your formal status or your qualifications or your political connections, but a change in your analytical capability — the ability to see the structure beneath the failure, to name it, and to propose what a working design would look like. Fourteen articles have given you that capability. Now use it.
