## Build the Institutional Scaffolding Before You Need It

When South Korea's per-capita income crossed $15,000 in the late 1990s, the country had already spent two decades building the institutional scaffolding that would carry it to $30,000. Malaysia hit the same threshold in the 2010s after methodical investment in education, manufacturing clusters, and regulatory modernisation. Poland crossed it after EU accession forced a decade of institutional reform that made its factories competitive with German supply chains. In every case, the pattern is the same: the transition from resource-dependent growth to efficiency-driven growth took longer, cost more political capital, and required more institutional quality than any government initially expected.

**South Africa has not yet reached that $15,000 threshold in real terms** — but the work of building the institutional infrastructure for Stage 2 cannot wait until Stage 1 is complete. The interventions described in [Resource Optimisation](/articles/eg-2-1-resource-optimisation) and [Infrastructure Foundations](/articles/eg-2-2-infrastructure-foundations) are necessary but not sufficient. They stabilise the economy and build the physical platform. Stage 2 is where South Africa must learn to compete not on what it digs out of the ground, but on how efficiently it produces, educates, regulates, and trades. That is a fundamentally different kind of economic challenge, and it requires a fundamentally different kind of state capacity.

This article maps the four intervention areas that define the efficiency transition: manufacturing scale-up in strategic sectors, services export development, the education-to-employment pipeline, and regulatory and institutional quality. These four areas are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive — they cover the full surface area of what must change for South Africa to move from a commodity-dependent middle-income economy to an efficiency-driven upper-middle-income economy. Each area has clear comparators, measurable benchmarks, and identifiable failure modes. None of them can be skipped. And all of them require the kind of sustained, cross-administration institutional commitment that South Africa's political system has historically struggled to deliver.

The honest assessment is sobering. South Africa's manufacturing sector has been shrinking as a share of GDP for two decades. The education system produces graduates that employers routinely describe as unemployable. Regulatory compliance costs price small businesses out of formal markets. And regional trade integration through the Southern African Development Community remains well below its potential. Stage 2 is not about optimism — it is about understanding exactly what must change, in what sequence, and at what cost.

## What Efficiency-Driven Growth Actually Means

To understand the efficiency transition, you need a framework that distinguishes between different kinds of competitive advantage. Michael Porter's diamond model, developed at Harvard Business School in the 1990s, remains the most useful lens for this purpose. Porter argued that national competitive advantage depends on four interconnected factors: factor conditions (the inputs available to firms — labour, capital, infrastructure, natural resources), demand conditions (the sophistication and scale of domestic demand), related and supporting industries (the depth of local supply chains and service providers), and firm strategy, structure, and rivalry (the competitive environment that shapes how firms are created, organised, and managed).

In Stage 1, a country competes primarily on factor conditions — cheap labour, abundant natural resources, basic infrastructure. South Africa's mining sector, its agricultural base, and its relatively low-cost labour force are classic factor-condition advantages. The interventions in [Resource Optimisation](/articles/eg-2-1-resource-optimisation) are designed to maximise the returns from these existing endowments. But factor-condition advantages are, by definition, advantages that other countries also have. Vietnam has cheaper labour. Australia has more mining infrastructure per capita. Brazil has more arable land. Competing on factor conditions alone means competing in a race where the finish line keeps moving.

Stage 2 shifts the basis of competition from factor conditions to efficiency — doing more with the same inputs. This means factories that produce higher-quality goods at lower unit costs, not because wages are low but because production processes are sophisticated. It means an education system that produces workers whose skills match what employers need, not just workers who have certificates. It means a regulatory environment where starting a business, connecting to utilities, and enforcing contracts can be done quickly and cheaply, not because rules are absent but because institutions work well. And it means demand conditions that are large enough and sophisticated enough to support firms that can then compete internationally.

Applied to South Africa specifically, Porter's diamond reveals both opportunities and structural gaps. On factor conditions, South Africa has significant advantages: the deepest capital markets on the continent, a sophisticated financial sector, world-class mining and engineering expertise, and geographic proximity to major shipping routes. On demand conditions, the picture is mixed: a domestic market of 62 million people with significant purchasing power at the top end, but severe inequality that constrains mass-market demand — and a regional market through the Southern African Development Community (SADC) of 350 million people that remains poorly integrated. On related and supporting industries, South Africa has genuine depth in mining services, financial services, and logistics, but thin clusters in manufacturing and technology. And on firm strategy and rivalry, the economy is dominated by large conglomerates with limited competitive pressure in many sectors, which suppresses the kind of innovation that efficiency-driven growth requires.

The efficiency transition, then, is not about picking winners or launching grand industrial plans. It is about systematically improving all four corners of the diamond simultaneously. Manufacturing scale-up strengthens related industries and firm rivalry. Education reform improves factor conditions. Regulatory quality shapes firm strategy. Regional integration expands demand conditions. None of these interventions works in isolation. The reason Stage 2 is the hardest transition is precisely because it requires everything to improve at roughly the same time.

## Manufacturing Scale-Up: Where South Africa Can Realistically Compete

The romantic version of manufacturing strategy involves building the next Shenzhen — a gleaming export zone churning out electronics for the global market. The realistic version is more modest and more achievable. South Africa will not compete with Vietnam on labour-intensive light manufacturing, where Vietnamese factory workers earn a fraction of South African wages and Vietnamese infrastructure investment has been relentless for two decades. South Africa will not compete with Taiwan or South Korea on semiconductor fabrication, where decades of accumulated expertise and hundreds of billions of dollars in capital investment create insurmountable barriers to entry. What South Africa can do is identify sectors where its existing capabilities, geographic position, and resource endowments create genuine competitive advantages — and then build the institutional infrastructure to exploit those advantages at scale.

**Automotive components** represent the most developed manufacturing opportunity. The Automotive Production and Development Programme (APDP), which succeeded the Motor Industry Development Programme in 2013, has maintained South Africa's position as a significant vehicle assembler — roughly 600,000 vehicles per year (NAAMSA *Quarterly Review*, 2023), with major plants operated by BMW, Mercedes-Benz, Toyota, Volkswagen, and Ford. But assembly is the low-value segment of the automotive value chain. The efficiency transition requires moving up into component manufacturing, where margins are higher and employment multipliers are larger. South Africa currently imports a disproportionate share of the components that go into locally assembled vehicles. Closing that gap — increasing local content from roughly 40 per cent to 60 per cent or more — would create tens of thousands of manufacturing jobs and deepen the domestic supply chain. The obstacle is not demand (global automakers want supply chain diversification away from China) but productivity: South African component factories must match the quality and cost benchmarks set by factories in Poland, Turkey, and Thailand.

**Agro-processing** is the second strategic sector, and arguably the one with the largest employment potential. South Africa produces significant volumes of fruit, wine, maize, sugar, and meat — but exports a disproportionate share of these as raw or minimally processed commodities. The value-added segment — canned goods, packaged foods, beverages, processed meat — is where employment multipliers are highest. South Africa's citrus industry provides a useful illustration: the country is the world's second-largest exporter of fresh citrus, but captures only a fraction of the value that would come from processing that citrus into juice, concentrates, and flavourings domestically. Agro-processing also has strong backward linkages to smallholder agriculture, which means it can generate employment and income in rural areas where formal jobs are scarcest.

**Green hydrogen and renewable energy components** represent the most forward-looking manufacturing opportunity. South Africa has among the world's best solar and wind resources, and the green hydrogen strategy announced in 2022 positions the country as a potential major producer and exporter of green hydrogen and its derivatives (green ammonia, green steel). But the manufacturing opportunity is not just in hydrogen production itself — it is in the components and equipment required for renewable energy generation. South Africa currently imports most of its solar panels, wind turbine components, and battery storage systems. Localising even a portion of this supply chain would create a manufacturing sector with guaranteed domestic demand (from the ongoing energy transition) and export potential (to the rest of Africa and beyond).

**Pharmaceuticals**, specifically generic drug manufacturing, is the fourth strategic sector. South Africa has existing pharmaceutical manufacturing capacity — Aspen Pharmacare is one of the largest generic drug manufacturers in the southern hemisphere — and the COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated both the strategic importance and the commercial opportunity of local pharmaceutical production. The African market for pharmaceuticals is growing rapidly, and the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) creates a framework for continental-scale distribution. The competitive gap with India, which dominates global generic manufacturing, is significant but not insurmountable in specific product categories, particularly biologics and vaccines where South Africa's scientific research base provides a genuine advantage.

:::expert[Trade and Industrial Policy Strategies (TIPS)]
Research from the Trade and Industrial Policy Strategies centre highlights that South Africa's manufacturing decline has been driven not by inevitable deindustrialisation but by specific policy failures: energy unreliability that raises production costs, port inefficiency that delays exports, and administered prices (particularly for steel and chemicals) that make downstream manufacturing uncompetitive. Addressing these structural costs could unlock manufacturing growth even without new industrial policy instruments, because the binding constraint is not incentive design but input cost competitiveness.
:::

The competitive gap between South Africa and current manufacturing leaders is real but varies significantly by sector. In light manufacturing — textiles, footwear, consumer electronics assembly — the gap with Vietnam is likely unbridgeable. Vietnamese manufacturing wages are roughly one-third of South African wages, and Vietnam has invested heavily in export-oriented infrastructure. But in medium-technology manufacturing — automotive components, renewable energy equipment, processed foods, generic pharmaceuticals — the gap with comparators like Poland, Turkey, and Malaysia is narrower and closeable through targeted intervention. Poland's manufacturing renaissance after EU accession was built on institutional reform (regulatory simplification, skills development, infrastructure investment) rather than on wage suppression. South Africa could follow a similar path, using institutional quality rather than low wages as the basis for manufacturing competitiveness.

## Services Export Development: The Underexploited Advantage

South Africa's services sector accounts for roughly 65 per cent of GDP, yet services exports remain a small fraction of total exports. This represents one of the largest underexploited opportunities in the economy. The country has a set of latent advantages for services exports that few other middle-income countries can match: a time zone that overlaps with European, Middle Eastern, and East African business hours; widespread English proficiency (English is the dominant language of business and higher education); a sophisticated financial sector with deep expertise in banking, insurance, and asset management; an established business process outsourcing (BPO) industry; and a legal system based on Roman-Dutch law that is broadly compatible with European commercial law.

The BPO sector provides the most immediate illustration of both the potential and the constraints. South Africa's BPO industry employs roughly 270,000 people and has been growing steadily, with major international firms including Amazon, Discovery, and various UK financial institutions operating contact centres and back-office processing facilities in Cape Town, Johannesburg, and Durban. The industry benefits from government incentives, English-language capability, and a cultural affinity with European and North American client markets that competing destinations (the Philippines, India) cannot easily replicate. But the industry operates well below its potential. **Cape Town alone could plausibly support three to four times its current BPO employment** if broadband infrastructure were more reliable, if skills pipelines were better aligned to industry needs, and if regulatory barriers to data transfer were addressed.

Beyond BPO, the services export opportunity extends into higher-value segments. South Africa's financial services sector has deep expertise in areas — risk management, fintech, mobile banking, insurance product design — that are in high demand across the rest of Africa. Johannesburg is already the financial hub of the continent, but this position is held more by historical accident than by deliberate strategy, and competitors (Nairobi, Lagos, Kigali) are investing aggressively to capture financial services business. Professional services — engineering consulting, legal services, accounting, management consulting — represent another export opportunity, particularly for projects across the African continent where South African firms have established reputations. And the creative industries — film, television, advertising, design, gaming — are a nascent but growing export sector, with South Africa's production infrastructure (studios, post-production facilities, creative talent) attracting international projects.

The constraints on services export growth are well-documented and specific. Broadband quality and cost remain significant barriers: South Africa's fixed broadband speeds rank in the bottom half globally, and data costs, though falling, remain high by international standards. The skills mismatch between what the education system produces and what services employers need is acute — a point explored in detail in the next section. And regulatory barriers, including data protection rules that create uncertainty about cross-border data flows, labour regulations that make flexible staffing difficult, and immigration rules that make it hard to bring in specialised foreign talent, collectively raise the cost of doing business in ways that erode South Africa's natural advantages.

The strategic question for services exports is whether South Africa will deliberately build the institutional infrastructure to exploit its latent advantages, or whether it will continue to watch those advantages erode as competing destinations invest more aggressively. Rwanda, with a population one-twentieth the size of South Africa's, has positioned itself as a technology and services hub through deliberate government strategy — investing in broadband, simplifying regulations, and actively courting international firms. South Africa does not need to copy Rwanda's model, but it does need to match Rwanda's intentionality.

## The Education-to-Employment Pipeline: Where Stage 2 Will Be Won or Lost

If you had to identify the single most important determinant of whether South Africa's efficiency transition succeeds or fails, it would be the education-to-employment pipeline. Every successful efficiency transition in modern economic history — South Korea in the 1980s, Poland in the 2000s, Malaysia in the 2010s — was preceded by a dramatic improvement in the quality and relevance of education. And every country that has remained stuck in the middle-income trap — Brazil, Turkey, Thailand — has an education system that produces graduates whose skills do not match what the productive economy needs.

South Africa's education system is, by most measurable outcomes, failing to produce the human capital that an efficiency-driven economy requires. The problems are well-documented but worth restating because they are so severe. At the basic education level, **South Africa's performance on international assessments (PIRLS for reading, TIMSS for mathematics and science) places the country near the bottom of participating nations, despite spending a higher share of GDP on education than most comparator countries.** The problem is not funding — it is the efficiency with which that funding is converted into learning outcomes. Teacher subject knowledge, particularly in mathematics and science, is weak. School management capacity is uneven. And the curriculum-assessment-teaching cycle does not consistently produce the foundational literacy and numeracy that all subsequent education depends on.

At the Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) level, the crisis is structural. South Africa has 50 public TVET colleges enrolling roughly 700,000 students, but throughput rates (the percentage of enrolled students who complete their qualifications) are dismally low — in many programmes below 30 per cent. More importantly, the qualifications that TVET colleges offer are poorly aligned with employer needs. The artisan development pipeline, which should be producing electricians, plumbers, welders, and mechanics for the manufacturing and construction sectors, produces a fraction of the artisans that the economy requires. **Germany, with a population roughly 30 per cent larger than South Africa's, produces over 300,000 new apprentices per year through its dual education system; South Africa produces fewer than 30,000.** The dual education model — where students split their time between classroom instruction and workplace training — has been studied, piloted, and praised in South African policy documents for decades, but never implemented at the scale required to make a difference.

At the university level, the numbers are somewhat better but still concerning. South Africa's 26 public universities enrol roughly 1.1 million students, but six-year completion rates for undergraduate degrees hover around 60 per cent. Science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM) graduates are in particularly short supply: **South Africa produces roughly 30,000 STEM graduates per year, compared to roughly 600,000 in South Korea** (which has a similar-sized population) and roughly 200,000 in Poland (which has a smaller population). The disconnect between what universities teach and what employers need is a persistent complaint from the private sector, though the blame for this disconnect is shared: universities have been slow to update curricula, but employers have also underinvested in workplace training and graduate development.

:::expert[Development Policy Research Unit (DPRU), University of Cape Town]
Analysis from the Development Policy Research Unit shows that South Africa's unemployment crisis is not simply a demand-side problem (too few jobs) but also a supply-side problem (mismatched skills). Their research demonstrates that sectors with the highest vacancy rates — ICT, engineering, artisanal trades, healthcare — are precisely the sectors where the education system produces too few graduates. This suggests that even significant economic growth will not resolve unemployment without simultaneous reform of the education-to-employment pipeline, because growth will create jobs that the existing workforce is not equipped to fill.
:::

The comparator solutions are well-known. Germany's dual education system integrates workplace training into vocational education from the start, with employers co-designing curricula and co-funding training. The result is youth unemployment rates consistently below 10 per cent and a manufacturing sector staffed by workers with world-class practical skills. South Korea's STEM investment strategy, launched in the 1970s and sustained across multiple administrations, prioritised mathematics and science education at every level — and backed that prioritisation with funding, teacher training, and institutional reform. Singapore's polytechnic model created a tier of post-secondary institutions focused explicitly on applied, industry-relevant training, with close links to employers and regular curriculum updates driven by industry advisory boards.

South Africa does not need to copy any of these models wholesale. Each was designed for a specific institutional context, and direct transplantation rarely works. But the common principles are clear: vocational education must be co-designed with employers, not designed by education bureaucrats in isolation; workplace training must be integrated into qualifications from the start, not bolted on as an afterthought; STEM education requires sustained investment in teacher quality, not just curriculum design; and the education-to-employment pipeline must be managed as a system, with feedback loops between employer needs, student choices, and institutional capacity.

:::takeaway
The efficiency transition cannot happen without an education system that produces graduates employers want to hire. South Africa spends more on education as a share of GDP than most comparator countries but gets worse outcomes. The problem is not funding — it is the institutional quality of how that funding is converted into learning and employment.
:::

## Regulatory and Institutional Quality: The Invisible Tax on Productive Investment

When a South African entrepreneur decides to start a formal business, she faces a series of regulatory interactions that collectively determine whether her investment is productive or punished. She must register the company (a process that takes days to weeks, depending on the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission's workload). She must register for tax (which requires navigating the South African Revenue Service's systems). She must comply with labour regulations (the Basic Conditions of Employment Act, the Labour Relations Act, sectoral determinations, skills development levies, unemployment insurance contributions). She must obtain whatever sector-specific licences and permits apply to her business. She must connect to electricity, water, and telecommunications. And if anything goes wrong — a contract dispute, a regulatory challenge, a labour complaint — she must navigate a court system where commercial disputes routinely take years to resolve.

Each of these regulatory interactions has a cost — in time, in money, and in uncertainty. And those costs are not distributed equally. Large firms with legal departments and compliance teams absorb regulatory costs as overhead. Small firms and new entrants, which are the primary source of employment growth in every economy, experience regulatory costs as existential barriers. When it takes weeks to register a business, months to get a building permit, and years to resolve a commercial dispute, the rational response for many potential entrepreneurs is to stay informal — to operate outside the regulatory system entirely. This is not a failure of individual motivation; it is a rational response to an institutional environment that punishes formality.

The conceptual framework that the World Bank's former Doing Business indicators used — measuring the time, cost, and number of procedures required to start a business, get construction permits, connect to electricity, register property, obtain credit, protect minority investors, pay taxes, trade across borders, enforce contracts, and resolve insolvency — remains useful even though the specific Doing Business index was discontinued in 2021 amid methodological controversies. The underlying insight is sound: regulatory quality is measurable, comparable across countries, and directly related to economic outcomes.

South Africa's performance on these dimensions is mixed. Company registration has been simplified and can now be done electronically in some cases, but the full process of becoming a compliant formal business — tax registration, labour compliance, sector licences — remains cumbersome. Construction permits are notoriously slow, particularly in major metros, with approval timelines stretching to months in Johannesburg and Cape Town. Electricity connection times have been affected by Eskom's capacity constraints and municipal infrastructure backlogs. And contract enforcement through the courts remains South Africa's most significant regulatory weakness: **the average time to resolve a commercial dispute through the South African court system exceeds three years, and in some jurisdictions approaches five years.** For comparison, Singapore resolves equivalent disputes in under 200 days. South Korea in under 300 days. Even Brazil, which is not typically cited as a model of institutional efficiency, resolves commercial disputes faster than South Africa.

The regulatory burden falls disproportionately on sectors with the highest employment potential. The hospitality sector — hotels, restaurants, tourism services — faces a thicket of health, safety, liquor licensing, and zoning regulations that vary by municipality and are often administered inconsistently. The construction sector faces building permit processes that add months and significant costs to projects. The agricultural sector faces phytosanitary, environmental, and land-use regulations administered by multiple national and provincial departments with overlapping mandates. In each case, the regulations themselves may be individually justifiable — food safety matters, building standards save lives, environmental protection is essential. But the cumulative effect of well-intentioned regulations administered by under-capacitated institutions is a regulatory environment that functions as an invisible tax on productive investment.

The institutional reforms required are neither mysterious nor politically controversial. They include digitising government-to-business interactions to reduce processing times and corruption opportunities. They include reducing the number of separate registrations and approvals required to start and operate a business, ideally through a single-window system where one application triggers all necessary registrations. They include investing in the capacity of the courts to resolve commercial disputes faster, through additional judges, case management systems, and alternative dispute resolution mechanisms. And they include systematic regulatory impact assessment — requiring that every new regulation be accompanied by an analysis of its costs and benefits, and that existing regulations be periodically reviewed for continued relevance.

:::expert[National Planning Commission]
The National Development Plan's diagnostic assessment identified regulatory inefficiency as one of the primary constraints on job creation and inclusive growth. The NPC's analysis noted that South Africa's regulatory framework was designed for a different economic era — one dominated by large firms in established sectors — and is poorly suited to the small-firm, new-sector dynamism that efficiency-driven growth requires. The Commission recommended a comprehensive programme of regulatory simplification, with particular attention to the cumulative burden on small and medium enterprises, but implementation of these recommendations has been uneven.
:::

The relationship between regulatory quality and the broader efficiency transition is direct. Manufacturing firms considering South Africa as a production base compare regulatory costs across potential locations. A firm choosing between South Africa and Poland for an automotive component plant will factor in not just wages and infrastructure, but also how long it takes to get building permits, how reliable electricity is, how quickly commercial disputes are resolved, and how burdensome ongoing compliance requirements are. A BPO firm comparing Cape Town to Manila will assess not just labour costs and language skills, but also data protection regulations, labour law flexibility, and the ease of scaling operations up and down. Regulatory quality is not a secondary consideration — for many investment decisions, it is the deciding factor.

:::takeaway
Regulatory friction does not show up in any government's growth strategy documents, but it is the single most common reason cited by investors — both domestic and foreign — for choosing to invest elsewhere. Every day that a permit takes longer than it should, every month that a court case drags on, every hour that an entrepreneur spends on compliance rather than production, is a cost that competitors in Poland, Vietnam, and Malaysia do not pay.
:::

## The SADC Integration Opportunity: Market Scale as Manufacturing Prerequisite

One of the least-discussed constraints on South Africa's manufacturing development is market scale. Manufacturing requires fixed investment in plant and equipment that can only be justified by sufficient demand. A factory producing automotive components needs customers for those components — and a domestic market of 62 million people, of whom only a fraction have the purchasing power to buy new vehicles, may not provide sufficient scale for many manufacturing investments. This is why every successful manufacturing economy either has a large domestic market (China, the United States, India) or has deep integration into a regional or global market that provides the scale domestic demand cannot.

For South Africa, the relevant regional market is the Southern African Development Community, a 16-member grouping with a combined population exceeding 350 million people and a combined GDP of roughly $700 billion. Add the broader African Continental Free Trade Area, which encompasses 1.3 billion people across 54 countries, and the potential market scale is enormous. But potential and reality remain far apart. **Intra-SADC trade as a share of total SADC trade remains below 25 per cent** — compared to over 60 per cent for intra-European Union trade and over 50 per cent for intra-ASEAN trade. The barriers are well-known: poor transport infrastructure between SADC countries (the Beitbridge border post between South Africa and Zimbabwe is a notorious bottleneck), non-tariff barriers (customs procedures, product standards, phytosanitary requirements) that are often more restrictive than tariffs, and macroeconomic instability in several SADC member states that creates exchange rate risk and payment uncertainty.

South Africa is, by far, the largest economy in SADC — accounting for roughly 60 per cent of the grouping's total GDP. This gives South Africa both an opportunity and a responsibility. The opportunity is that deeper SADC integration would disproportionately benefit South African manufacturers, who would gain access to a market six times the size of the domestic market. The responsibility is that South Africa, as the dominant economy, must be willing to invest in regional infrastructure, accept some asymmetry in trade arrangements that benefits smaller member states, and resist the temptation to use its market power to extract concessions that undermine regional solidarity.

The AfCFTA, which entered its operational phase in 2021, provides the institutional framework for continental trade integration. But institutional frameworks mean little without implementation. The practical questions are whether customs procedures can be harmonised and digitised, whether transport corridors can be upgraded to move goods efficiently, whether product standards can be mutually recognised to reduce non-tariff barriers, and whether payment systems can be integrated to reduce the cost and risk of cross-border transactions. South Africa's role in answering these questions — through the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition, through the South African Reserve Bank, and through South African firms that already operate across the continent — will significantly shape whether the AfCFTA becomes a real engine of manufacturing-led growth or remains a paper agreement.

For the efficiency transition specifically, SADC and AfCFTA integration matters because it changes the investment calculus for manufacturing. A pharmaceutical factory serving only the South African market may not justify the capital investment required. A pharmaceutical factory serving the entire SADC region — with guaranteed market access through harmonised regulations and reduced tariffs — becomes a much more attractive proposition. The same logic applies to agro-processing, automotive components, and renewable energy equipment. Market scale is not a nice-to-have; for manufacturing investment, it is a prerequisite.

## The Sequencing Problem: Why Stage 2 Is the Hardest Transition

You may have noticed that the four intervention areas described above are deeply interconnected. Manufacturing scale-up requires regulatory quality (to attract investment), education reform (to staff factories), infrastructure reliability (to produce competitively), and market scale (to justify investment). Services exports require broadband infrastructure, skilled workers, sensible data regulations, and trade agreements. Education reform requires institutional capacity in government, cooperation from employers, and fiscal resources generated by economic growth. And regulatory reform requires political will, institutional capacity, and the kind of evidence-based policymaking that a well-functioning education system produces.

This interconnection is precisely what makes Stage 2 the hardest transition. In Stage 1, you can make progress through sector-specific interventions — fix the electricity system, upgrade the ports, reform mining regulation. These interventions have identifiable champions, measurable outcomes, and relatively contained implementation requirements. Stage 2 is different. The binding constraint is not any single sector or institution — it is the systemic quality of institutions across the board. You cannot manufacture competitively with unreliable electricity and an unskilled workforce. You cannot export services with bad broadband and onerous data regulations. You cannot attract FDI with slow courts and opaque permit processes.

The countries that have successfully navigated Stage 2 all found ways to manage this sequencing problem. South Korea created a powerful Economic Planning Board that coordinated across ministries and held line departments accountable for specific economic outcomes. Singapore vested extraordinary authority in the Economic Development Board to cut through bureaucratic obstacles. Poland used EU accession conditionality as an external forcing mechanism that compelled reform across multiple domains simultaneously. Malaysia's Economic Transformation Programme created a delivery unit in the Prime Minister's office that tracked implementation of specific reform initiatives.

South Africa's version of this coordinating mechanism is described in [The Execution Machine](/articles/eg-3-2-the-execution-machine), but the principle is worth stating here: Stage 2 requires a level of institutional coordination that South Africa's current governance architecture — with its division of powers across three spheres of government, its complex web of regulatory agencies, and its tendency toward policy proliferation without implementation follow-through — does not naturally produce. The National Planning Commission's 2012 National Development Plan identified many of the right interventions but was never given the institutional authority to drive implementation. Operation Vulindlela, the reform unit established in the Presidency and National Treasury, has shown that targeted reform delivery is possible but has operated with a relatively narrow mandate.

The implication for citizens — for you, reading this — is that the efficiency transition is not something that happens in a government strategy document. It happens (or fails to happen) in the lived experience of entrepreneurs trying to register businesses, graduates trying to find jobs, manufacturers trying to export products, and investors trying to make sense of the regulatory environment. Monitoring whether Stage 2 is succeeding requires watching not just GDP growth rates but the specific institutional indicators that determine whether growth is becoming more efficient or whether South Africa is simply running faster on the same treadmill.

:::takeaway
The efficiency transition is the hardest economic pivot any country makes because it requires simultaneous improvement across multiple institutional domains. You cannot fix manufacturing without fixing education, infrastructure, regulation, and market access at the same time. Countries that succeed at Stage 2 — South Korea, Poland, Singapore — all created powerful coordinating mechanisms that could drive reform across the full institutional surface area. South Africa has not yet built this mechanism at the required scale.
:::

## Practical Framework: Efficiency Readiness Checklist

The following framework provides five measurable dimensions for tracking South Africa's readiness for and progress through the efficiency transition. Each dimension includes observable indicators with public data sources that you can check yourself.

**Dimension 1: Infrastructure Reliability**

The foundation of manufacturing competitiveness is reliable, affordable infrastructure. Three indicators capture the essential picture. Energy uptime measures the percentage of hours in a year during which grid electricity is available without interruption; during the load-shedding crisis, this figure dropped below 85 per cent in some areas, whereas manufacturing competitiveness requires 99 per cent or above. You can track this through Eskom's published generation data and the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR) energy statistics. Port turnaround time measures the average number of days a container ship spends in port from arrival to departure; Durban and Cape Town ports have averaged four to six days in recent years, compared to under two days in competing ports like Tanjung Pelepas (Malaysia) and Laem Chabang (Thailand). Transnet publishes quarterly port performance statistics. Broadband availability and cost measures both the percentage of the population with access to fixed or mobile broadband at speeds sufficient for productive economic activity (at least 25 Mbps) and the cost of that access as a percentage of average income. The Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA) publishes annual reports on telecommunications access and pricing.

**Dimension 2: Education Pipeline Quality**

The efficiency transition requires human capital that matches employer needs. TVET throughput rate measures the percentage of enrolled TVET students who complete their qualifications within the intended timeframe; a rate above 65 per cent would indicate a functional system, whereas current rates in many programmes fall below 30 per cent. The Department of Higher Education and Training publishes annual TVET statistics. University STEM graduation rate measures the number of graduates in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics fields per 100,000 population; South Africa currently produces roughly 50 per 100,000, compared to over 100 in Malaysia and over 1,000 in South Korea. The Council on Higher Education publishes annual higher education data. Skills match index measures the alignment between graduate output by field and employer demand by sector; the Human Resource Development Council publishes periodic analyses of skills supply and demand, and the Quarterly Labour Force Survey from Statistics South Africa provides data on unemployment by qualification level.

**Dimension 3: Regulatory Environment**

Regulatory quality determines whether productive investment is rewarded or punished. Business registration time measures the number of days from application to full regulatory compliance (company registration, tax registration, labour compliance, sector licences); a target of under 10 days would match upper-middle-income country benchmarks. The Companies and Intellectual Property Commission publishes registration statistics. Building permit timeline measures the number of days from application to approval for a standard commercial construction project; targets below 60 days would match regional benchmarks, whereas current timelines in major metros often exceed 120 days. Municipal planning departments publish approval statistics, though inconsistently. Commercial dispute resolution time measures the average number of days from filing to resolution for a standard commercial contract dispute; South Africa's current average exceeds 600 days, whereas upper-middle-income benchmarks are below 400 days. The Department of Justice publishes court performance statistics, and the Superior Courts annual reports contain case management data.

**Dimension 4: Trade Integration**

Market scale determines whether manufacturing investment can be justified. Intra-SADC trade share measures intra-regional trade as a percentage of total SADC trade; a target above 30 per cent (from the current sub-25 per cent) would indicate meaningful integration progress. The SADC Secretariat publishes annual trade statistics, and the South African Revenue Service publishes detailed trade data by destination. AfCFTA implementation progress can be tracked through the African Union's AfCFTA dashboard, which monitors tariff liberalisation schedules, rules of origin negotiations, and trade facilitation measures. Border crossing times — specifically the average time for a commercial vehicle to clear the Beitbridge, Lebombo, and Maseru Bridge border posts — provide a ground-level indicator of trade facilitation effectiveness; the Cross-Border Road Transport Agency publishes border post performance data.

**Dimension 5: Institutional Coordination**

The systemic quality of institutional coordination determines whether individual reforms translate into aggregate improvement. This dimension is harder to measure directly, but three proxy indicators are useful. Policy implementation rate measures the percentage of National Development Plan and Medium Term Strategic Framework targets that are achieved within their stated timelines; the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation publishes quarterly and annual reports on government performance. Cross-departmental project completion rate measures the percentage of projects requiring coordination across multiple departments or spheres of government that are completed on time and within budget; the Auditor-General's annual report provides data on government project delivery. And reform delivery unit output — currently Operation Vulindlela — measures the number of specific regulatory reforms completed per year and their measured impact on the targeted regulatory dimension. The Presidency publishes periodic updates on Operation Vulindlela's progress.

You do not need to track all fifteen indicators to get a useful picture of efficiency readiness. Tracking even one indicator from each dimension will give you a better basis for assessing South Africa's economic trajectory than any politician's speech or any newspaper headline. The data is public. The benchmarks are clear. The question is whether the trajectory is improving, stagnating, or deteriorating — and that question has an empirical answer.

## Where This Leads: From Efficiency to Innovation

The efficiency transition described in this article is not the destination — it is the necessary precursor to the next stage. [Innovation-Driven Growth](/articles/eg-2-4-innovation-driven-growth) describes Stage 3, where an economy stops competing on doing things more efficiently than others and starts competing on doing things that others cannot do at all. South Korea's transition from efficient manufacturer to innovation leader — from Hyundai assembling cars designed by Mitsubishi to Hyundai designing cars that compete with BMW — took roughly fifteen years and was built entirely on the institutional foundation laid during Stage 2.

South Africa is not yet at Stage 2. The interventions described in this article are aspirational in the sense that they describe what must happen, not what is currently happening. But they are also grounded in the sense that every intervention has clear comparator evidence, measurable benchmarks, and identifiable institutional owners. The efficiency transition is not a mystery. It is a known challenge with known solutions. The question is whether South Africa's institutions — the state capacity described in [The Architecture of the State](/articles/1-1-architecture-of-the-state), the infrastructure described in [Infrastructure Foundations](/articles/eg-2-2-infrastructure-foundations), and the execution machinery that must be built — can deliver those solutions at the scale and speed the economy requires.

The honest answer, as of today, is that the track record is mixed. South Africa has pockets of institutional excellence — the South African Revenue Service at its best, the Reserve Bank, elements of the National Treasury — and large expanses of institutional mediocrity. The efficiency transition requires institutional quality to become the norm rather than the exception. That is the work of a decade, not a single election cycle. And it is work that will determine whether South Africa's children grow up in a middle-income country that finally breaks through to prosperity, or in a middle-income country that remains permanently stuck between what it was and what it could have been.

:::framework
**The "Efficiency Readiness" Assessment Framework**

When evaluating whether South Africa is making progress on the efficiency transition — or when assessing a policy proposal that claims to advance it — use this five-dimension checklist. Each dimension has specific, publicly available indicators you can track yourself.

**Dimension 1: Can firms produce reliably?**

Check three infrastructure indicators. Is grid electricity available at least 99% of the time in industrial areas? (Track via Eskom generation data and CSIR energy statistics.) Is port turnaround time below two days at Durban and Cape Town? (Track via Transnet quarterly port performance statistics.) Is broadband at speeds above 25 Mbps available and affordable for businesses outside major metros? (Track via ICASA annual reports.) If the answer to any of these is no, the infrastructure foundation for efficiency-driven manufacturing is not yet in place — and any policy that assumes it is will underperform.

**Dimension 2: Can the education system supply the workers the economy needs?**

Check three education pipeline indicators. Are TVET throughput rates above 65%? (Current rates in many programmes fall below 30% — track via Department of Higher Education and Training annual statistics.) Is South Africa producing at least 100 STEM graduates per 100,000 population? (Currently approximately 50 — track via Council on Higher Education data.) Do employer surveys show improving satisfaction with graduate quality? (Track via the Human Resource Development Council and the Quarterly Labour Force Survey's data on unemployment by qualification level.) If throughput is low, STEM output is weak, and employers report a skills mismatch, the human capital precondition for the efficiency transition is not being met.

**Dimension 3: Does the regulatory environment reward or punish productive investment?**

Check three regulatory quality indicators. Can a business achieve full regulatory compliance — company registration, tax registration, labour compliance, sector licences — in under 10 days? (Track via Companies and Intellectual Property Commission statistics.) Can a standard commercial building permit be approved in under 60 days? (Track via municipal planning departments — current timelines often exceed 120 days.) Can a commercial contract dispute be resolved in under 400 days? (South Africa's current average exceeds 600 days — track via Department of Justice court performance statistics.) Every day beyond these benchmarks is a cost that competitors in Poland, Vietnam, and Malaysia do not pay.

**Dimension 4: Is South Africa integrated into regional markets at sufficient scale?**

Check two trade integration indicators. Is intra-SADC trade above 30% of total SADC trade? (Currently below 25% — track via SADC Secretariat trade statistics and SARS trade data.) Are border crossing times at Beitbridge, Lebombo, and Maseru Bridge falling? (Track via Cross-Border Road Transport Agency data.) Manufacturing investment requires market scale. If regional integration is not deepening, the investment case for many manufacturing sectors remains weaker than it needs to be.

**Dimension 5: Is institutional coordination improving across government?**

Check two coordination indicators. What percentage of National Development Plan and Medium Term Strategic Framework targets are achieved within their stated timelines? (Track via Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation quarterly reports.) How many specific regulatory reforms has Operation Vulindlela completed in the past year, and what is their measured impact? (Track via Presidency updates on Vulindlela progress.) If implementation rates are low and reform delivery is slow, individual sector improvements will not translate into the systemic institutional quality that the efficiency transition demands.

You do not need to track all fifteen indicators to get a useful picture. Tracking even one indicator from each dimension — and checking it every six months — will give you a better basis for assessing South Africa's economic trajectory than any politician's growth promise. The data is public. The benchmarks are clear. The only question is whether the numbers are moving in the right direction.
