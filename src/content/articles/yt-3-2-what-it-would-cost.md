## A Serious Proposal Shows Its Arithmetic

Everything to this point has described an institution and a way to build it. None of it means anything until it meets arithmetic, because in public policy the difference between a vision and a fantasy is whether the numbers have been done honestly and survive contact with a tight budget. This article does the numbers.

A warning about how to read it, stated plainly up front. The figures in this article are **illustrative**, not a costed estimate. Their purpose is to demonstrate a _method_ — a transparent, bottom-up way of building the cost so that anyone can interrogate it — and to establish the rough order of magnitude and the shape of where the money goes. They are deliberately built from explicit assumptions, each of which can be challenged, adjusted, and replaced with better data. A real costing would require detailed work by the National Treasury and the relevant departments, with actual unit costs and verified cohort numbers. What this article offers is not that costing but the scaffolding for it: the structure of the calculation, the levers that move it, and an honest sense of the scale involved. Read every rand figure below as "approximately, on these stated assumptions, subject to detailed costing" — because that is exactly what it is.

That caveat is not a hedge; it is the method. The whole argument of this article is that costs should be built from the bottom up, transparently, so they can be checked — and the figures here are offered in exactly that spirit, as a worked example to be pressure-tested, not a number to be trusted on authority.

## Why Bottom-Up, Not Top-Down

There are two ways to cost a programme like this, and one of them is useless.

The top-down way is to reach for a headline: "national youth service would cost tens of billions." Numbers like that are easy to produce, impossible to interrogate, and worthless for decision-making, because they hide every assumption inside a single intimidating figure. They invite the lazy dismissal — "we can't afford it" — without anyone being able to see what, exactly, is being afforded or not.

The bottom-up way builds the cost from its smallest unit: what does it cost to put _one_ young person through the service for one year? Get that number right, with every component visible, and you can multiply it by the cohort sizes of each phase to get phase costs, test how the total moves when you change any assumption, and locate precisely where the money goes and therefore where the savings and trade-offs lie. A bottom-up cost is a cost you can argue with — which is exactly what a democracy deciding whether to spend public money on this should be able to do. So that is how this article proceeds: per participant first, then per phase, then the sensitivities.

There is a deeper reason to insist on this, beyond analytical tidiness. The bottom-up method is itself a form of accountability, and South Africa's recent history makes that accountability precious. Too many large programmes have been launched on top-down headline figures that nobody could interrogate, only for the real costs to balloon, the money to leak, and the public to be left with a bill it was never shown the components of. A cost built openly, from named unit costs that anyone — a journalist, a parliamentary committee, an ordinary citizen — can check against reality, is far harder to inflate quietly or capture invisibly. The same transparency that lets a democracy decide whether to fund the service also lets it watch, afterward, whether the money is being spent as promised. Building the cost from the bottom up is therefore not only the honest way to estimate it; it is the first line of defence against the cost overruns and capture that the implementation article named as central risks. A number you cannot see inside is a number you cannot govern.

## The Cost of One Participant, One Year

Start with the unit. The cost of putting a single young person through a year of national service is the sum of a handful of components. Here they are, with illustrative figures and the reasoning behind each — and, again, every number is a stated assumption to be tested, not a claim.

- **Stipend.** The largest single component. A young person serving full-time for a year must be paid enough to live and to make the service worth choosing — but not so much that it competes with entry-level wages or breaks the budget. Take an illustrative stipend of around **R3,500 a month**, which over twelve months is about **R42,000**. This single figure will turn out to dominate everything, which is why it is the most important assumption in the whole model.
- **Basic training.** The universal crucible — instructors, facilities, food and accommodation during training, and the training-phase share of kit. Apportioned per participant, take an illustrative **R15,000–25,000**, depending on the length of the training phase. Call it **R20,000**.
- **Deployment, placement, and supervision.** The cost of placing and supervising the participant in their track for the rest of the year — the coordinating staff, the host-site support, the management of the civilian streams or continued military service. Take an illustrative **R12,000**.
- **Uniforms and equipment.** Issued kit, amortised. An illustrative **R5,000**.
- **Administration and systems.** The central machinery — the data backbone, the institution's running costs, monitoring and evaluation — apportioned per participant. An illustrative **R10,000**.

Add these and the illustrative cost of one participant for one year lands in the region of **R85,000–95,000** — call it, for round numbers and ease of testing, roughly **R90,000 per participant-year**. Hold that number loosely; its precision is fictional, but its order of magnitude — tens of thousands of rand per young person per year, dominated by the stipend — is the durable finding. Everything else in this article follows from multiplying and stress-testing that one figure.

The stipend assumption deserves a closer look, because it is doing most of the work and because South Africa already has real-world reference points for it. The country's existing public-employment and youth schemes pay stipends, and they cluster in a range that has itself been the subject of debate — some critics argue the stipends paid by programmes like the school-assistant initiative are too low to be meaningful, others that raising them would blow the budget. That debate is exactly the trade-off this model has to navigate, and it cuts both ways. Set the stipend too low and the service fails on two fronts at once: it provides too little real relief to the young person and their household to matter, and it signals that the service is cheap and therefore low-status — feeding the very stigma the prestige design is trying to defeat. Set it too high and two different problems appear: the budget balloons, since the stipend is half the unit cost; and the stipend may begin to rival or exceed entry-level wages, creating the perverse effect of making a year of service more attractive than a permanent job — the opposite of the bridge-into-work the service is meant to be.

The illustrative R3,500 a month sits deliberately in the awkward middle: enough to provide genuine relief and to be taken seriously, low enough to remain below typical entry-level full-time wages and to keep the budget within reach. Whether that exact figure is right is precisely the kind of question a real costing and a real political process would have to settle, weighing relief, dignity, labour-market effects, and affordability against one another. The point here is not that R3,500 is correct, but that the stipend is a _choice_ with visible consequences on every side — and that a bottom-up model makes those consequences explicit instead of burying them. There is no costless stipend; there is only a stipend chosen with eyes open to what each rand up or down does to relief, to prestige, to the labour market, and to the total.

## The Cost of Each Phase

Now apply the unit cost to the phased rollout of the previous article. The phases differ enormously in size, and therefore in cost, which is the whole point of phasing: the early, uncertain phases are cheap, and the large commitment comes only after the model is proven.

- **Phase One (Prove It).** An illustrative pilot cohort of, say, **20,000** participants. At ~R90,000 each, that is about **R1.8 billion a year** in direct cost — but Phase One is deliberately over-measured, so load it with extra evaluation cost and call it on the order of **R2–2.5 billion a year**. In the context of the national budget, this is a modest, contained sum — the price of buying hard evidence about whether the model works before betting more on it.
- **Phase Two (Scale It).** An illustrative national cohort of, say, **150,000**. At ~R90,000 each, about **R13.5 billion a year**, building up over several years as the cohort and the capacity grow. A serious figure, but one reached gradually and only after Phase One has proven the model and refined the unit cost.
- **Phase Three (Universalise It).** Here the numbers become large, and there is no use pretending otherwise. A near-universal cohort might be on the order of **600,000–800,000** young people a year, depending on how the cohort is defined and how exemptions work. At ~R90,000 each, that is in the region of **R55–70 billion a year** at maturity.

That Phase Three figure is the one that makes people stop, and it should. It is a large annual commitment — comparable in scale to substantial existing areas of public spending. Two things must be said about it immediately, and the rest of this article and the next are devoted to them. First, it is the _mature, universal_ figure, reached only after a decade or more of phased build-up, not a cost incurred on day one. Second, a large part of it is not new money at all but the consolidation of fragmented spending South Africa already does — the subject of the funding article that follows. The headline number is real, but it is neither immediate nor wholly additional, and treating it as if it were both is the fastest way to dismiss the proposal dishonestly.

## Is This a Big Number? Putting It in Context

A figure means nothing in isolation; it means something only against the scale of what the state already spends. So set the illustrative mature cost beside the public finances it would sit within — not to make it sound small, because it is not, but to size it honestly.

South Africa's national budget runs to well over two trillion rand a year. The state already spends very large sums on the broad areas the youth service touches — basic education, higher education and training, health, and the social grant system that supports millions of South Africans, including many young people, run to hundreds of billions of rand each. Against that backdrop, an illustrative mature youth-service cost in the region of R55–70 billion a year is significant but not outlandish: it is a meaningful share of public spending, of the same order as major individual programmes, and far from the largest line in the budget. It is the kind of number that represents a real national choice — something the country would have to decide to prioritise and fund — rather than a fantasy figure outside the realm of what a state of South Africa's size spends.

Two comparisons sharpen the point. The state already spends substantial sums on the fragmented youth and public-employment landscape of Part 1 — the NYDA, the EPWP and Community Work Programme, the Presidential Youth Employment Intervention, learnership and skills funding, and more. That existing spending, consolidated, is a meaningful fraction of the youth service's cost, which is precisely why the next article treats consolidation as central. And the social grant system demonstrates that South Africa already chooses to transfer very large sums directly to its citizens in need; the youth service's stipend is, in part, a transfer of the same kind — but one made to young people in exchange for a year of service and skills-building rather than as unconditional support. The question the cost poses is therefore not "can a country like South Africa spend at this scale?" — it manifestly already does, in adjacent areas — but "is _this_ the thing worth prioritising at this scale, and can the money be assembled?" That is a legitimate, answerable question, and a far more honest one than the reflexive "we can't afford it."

## Where the Money Goes: The Stipend Dominates

The single most useful thing a bottom-up costing reveals is _where the money goes_, because that is where the real design choices and trade-offs live. And the answer here is unambiguous: at scale, the **stipend dominates everything**.

Return to the unit cost. Of the ~R90,000 per participant-year, the stipend alone is ~R42,000 — close to half. Every other component combined — training, placement, kit, administration — is roughly the other half. Multiply across a universal cohort and the implication is stark: the majority of the cost of a national youth service is not bureaucracy, facilities, or overhead. It is the money paid directly into the hands of young people. At a cohort of 700,000, the stipend bill alone is on the order of **R29 billion a year** — more than half the total.

This matters for two reasons. First, it means the cost is, in an important sense, _progressive and circulating_: most of the spending goes directly to young people, disproportionately poor, who spend it immediately in their local economies, generating demand and supporting livelihoods rather than disappearing into administration. The stipend is a cost, but it is also an income transfer to exactly the population that most needs one, with a service performed in return. Second, it means the **stipend level is the master lever** of the whole budget. Move it, and the total moves more than for any other choice. That makes setting the stipend the single most consequential cost decision in the design — high enough to make the service worth choosing and to provide real relief, low enough to be affordable and not to distort the entry-level labour market. The next section shows just how powerful that lever is.

It is worth being clear about what the _other_ half of the unit cost buys, because the temptation, when budgets are tight, is to attack it as overhead and cut it toward zero — and that temptation would wreck the service. The non-stipend half pays for the things that make the year worth more than the stipend alone: the basic-training crucible that builds the cohesion and discipline; the supervision and placement that turn the civilian streams into real, skill-building work rather than idle time; the kit that lets participants actually do the job; and the administration, data systems, and monitoring that make the whole thing accountable and the benefits automatic. Cut these and you do not get a cheaper version of the service — you get a stipend with nothing attached, which is to say you get exactly the relief-without-transformation that Part 1 identified as the failure mode of past programmes. The non-stipend spending is what distinguishes a national _service_ from a cash transfer. It can be made efficient, and it must be protected from capture, but it cannot be hollowed out without turning the service back into the thing it was designed to replace. A government that funds the stipends but starves the training, supervision, and measurement will have bought the costs of the programme without its benefits — the worst of both worlds. Efficiency in this half is worth pursuing; abolition of it is not a saving but a self-inflicted defeat.

## The Sensitivities: Which Levers Move the Total

Because the cost is built from explicit assumptions, you can test how it responds when you pull each lever — and this is where a bottom-up model earns its keep, because it converts "we can't afford it" into "here is exactly what we can afford, and here are the trade-offs."

Four levers move the total, in roughly descending order of power:

- **The stipend.** The master lever. Because the stipend is about half the unit cost, a change in it moves the total almost as much proportionally. An illustrative R3,500 monthly stipend versus, say, R2,500 is the difference, at a 700,000 cohort, of roughly R8 billion a year. The stipend can be set to fit the available budget — but not without consequence, because too low a stipend undermines the relief and the attractiveness the service depends on. This is a genuine trade-off, not a free saving.
- **The cohort size.** How many young people serve. This is the lever the phasing pulls deliberately, and it is why Phase One costs billions while Phase Three costs tens of billions. It is also the lever behind the Nordic "selective" alternative of Part 2: a service that takes a fairly-selected portion of each cohort rather than all of it costs proportionally less, at the price of the universality that gives the crucible its cohesion power. Cost and cohesion trade against each other here, directly and unavoidably.
- **The duration split.** How long the expensive basic-training crucible runs versus the deployment phase, and how long the whole service lasts. A longer service costs more per participant; a shorter one costs less but may deliver less. The training phase is relatively costly per week, so its length is a real cost driver as well as a design choice about how much crucible is enough.
- **The track mix.** How participants divide between the military and civilian tracks, and among the civilian streams, since the streams differ in cost and in the value of the services they produce. This lever is smaller than the others but real, and it interacts with the offsetting value the next article discusses.

A worked illustration shows how powerfully these levers interact. Suppose a future government decided it could allocate a fixed envelope — say, on these illustrative unit costs, enough to fund a service in the region of R25–30 billion a year once mature. That single budget buys very different services depending on the levers. It could fund a near-universal-basic-training model for a large cohort with a short, cheaper deployment tail; or a fully-featured, longer service for a fairly-selected portion of each cohort on the Nordic pattern; or a universal service at a leaner stipend. Each is a coherent design; each makes a different trade between coverage, depth, and the size of the direct transfer to young people. The budget does not dictate the service; it dictates the _space of possible services_, within which the country chooses according to what it values most — maximum cohesion through universality, maximum depth through a longer richer service, or maximum relief through a higher stipend. The levers are how that choice is made visible and deliberate rather than accidental.

The point of laying out the levers is not to pick values — that is for a real costing exercise — but to show that the total is not a fixed, take-it-or-leave-it number. It is a function of choices, every one of which can be set against what the country can afford and what it wants the service to achieve. "We can't afford it" is not an analysis; it is the refusal to do one. The honest question is "at what stipend, cohort size, and duration can we afford what, and what do we get for it?" — and a bottom-up model is what lets a democracy actually answer that.

## The Cost of Doing Nothing

There is one more figure that belongs in any honest costing, and it is the one almost always left out: the cost of the status quo. A national youth service is expensive. So is the post-matric cliff — it is simply a cost the country already pays, quietly, and has stopped noticing.

Part 1 traced those costs, and they are not small. Millions of young people outside employment, education, or training represent an enormous standing loss: the output they do not produce, the taxes they do not pay, the demographic dividend expiring unclaimed. They represent direct fiscal costs in the social support that sustains them and their households. They represent the costs that flow through the Safety pillar — the well-documented relationship between mass youth idleness and crime, with its own vast price in policing, courts, prisons, private security, and lost investment. And they represent the slow, uncounted erosion of the social contract that Part 1 described, as a generation concludes the country has no use for it. None of these costs appears as a line item labelled "youth unemployment," which is precisely why they are so easily ignored — but they are paid, in full, every year, by everyone.

The honest framing of the youth service's cost, then, is not "R55–70 billion versus zero." It is "R55–70 billion, much of it consolidated from existing spending and offset by services delivered, versus the unmeasured but very large sum the country already loses to the cliff every year." This does not prove the service is worth it — that depends on whether it actually delivers, which is why the measurement scorecard of the next article matters so much. But it corrects the asymmetry in how these decisions are usually framed, where the visible cost of action is weighed against an imagined zero cost of inaction. Inaction is not free. It is one of the most expensive things South Africa does, and the expense simply does not carry the programme's name. A serious comparison weighs the cost of the service against the cost of continuing to pay for the cliff — and only then can the affordability question be answered honestly.

There is also a compounding dimension that a single year's comparison misses. The cost of the cliff is not paid once; it is paid every year, and it accumulates. Each cohort left to fall does not simply cost the country for one year and then resolve itself — the scarring effect described in Part 1 means a young person stranded in their twenties tends to remain less employable, less productive, and more dependent for decades afterward. The bill for one wasted cohort is paid across that cohort's whole working life, and a new bill is added each year a new cohort is wasted. Spending to bring a cohort into productive life, by contrast, compounds in the opposite direction: a young person launched into work or enterprise pays taxes, supports a household, and stays off the social-support rolls for decades. The relevant comparison, properly understood, is therefore not between two annual figures but between two compounding trajectories — one of accumulating loss, one of accumulating return. That framing does not settle the decision, but it changes its character entirely from the way "we can't afford it" imagines it.

## The Costing Worksheet

The whole method reduces to a worksheet anyone can fill in, challenge, and improve. To estimate the cost of any version of the service, you need only:

1. **Set the unit cost.** Add up the per-participant-year components — stipend, basic training, deployment and supervision, kit, administration. Change any assumption you disagree with; the structure holds.
2. **Set the cohort size** for the phase you are costing — the pilot's tens of thousands, the scaled phase's hundreds of thousands, or the universal cohort.
3. **Multiply** unit cost by cohort size for the annual direct cost.
4. **Adjust for phase specifics** — extra evaluation loading in the pilot, ramp-up in the scaling phase.
5. **Test the levers.** Re-run with a different stipend, a smaller cohort, a shorter service, and watch the total move — so you can see the trade-offs rather than guess at them.

Run that worksheet honestly and you arrive where this article does: a contained, affordable cost for the proving and scaling phases, and a large but not unthinkable cost for the mature universal phase — dominated by stipends paid directly to young people, reached only after years of phased build-up, and substantially offset by consolidation of existing spending and by the value the service produces.

It is worth being clear about what this exercise has, and has not, shown, because honesty about the limits of a costing is part of doing it properly. It has _not_ shown that the service is affordable — no costing can show that on its own, because affordability is a political choice about priorities as much as an arithmetic fact, and because the figures here are illustrative scaffolding awaiting real data. What it has shown is more modest and more useful: that the cost can be built transparently from the bottom up; that its order of magnitude, while large at maturity, is within the range of things a state of South Africa's size already spends on adjacent priorities; that it is dominated by a stipend that is itself a progressive transfer with a service attached; that it is reached gradually through phases that each cost far less than the destination; and that it is a function of choices — stipend, cohort, duration — rather than a single fixed number to accept or reject. That is what a costing is for: not to end the argument, but to make an honest argument possible. It replaces "we can't afford it" and "it would pay for itself" — two slogans that substitute for thought — with a structured set of numbers and trade-offs a democracy can actually debate.

The cost, built honestly from the bottom up, is real and significant. Whether it is _affordable_ is not a question about the gross number alone. It is a question about how it is funded — where the money is found, what it is weighed against, and what it returns — and that is where this Part ends. The final article takes the gross cost established here and asks the two questions that decide everything: how would South Africa actually pay for it, and how would the country know it was getting its money's worth? Answer those well, and the frightening gross number becomes a manageable national choice; answer them badly, or not at all, and no costing, however careful, can save the proposal.
