## Optimising What We Have

South Africa's natural resource endowment is not the problem. The failure to systematically convert those resources into industrial capabilities is. The country exports platinum group metals worth tens of billions of rands annually but imports catalytic converters manufactured from those very metals. It exports raw citrus by the shipload but imports processed juice concentrates. **It sits on the world's largest manganese reserves — roughly eighty per cent of known global deposits — yet captures a fraction of the downstream processing value.** Stage 1 of the growth strategy outlined in this series is not about abandoning resources or pretending that a mining-dependent economy can leapfrog straight into software engineering and biotechnology. It is about applying Justin Yifu Lin's comparative advantage test, which you encountered in [The Comparator Countries](/articles/eg-1-3-the-comparator-countries), to every major resource sector and then systematically moving up the value chain where South Africa has a genuine, demonstrable edge — while using resource revenues to fund the education, infrastructure, and institutional capacity that Stage 2 requires.

If you have followed this series from [The R7,000 Economy](/articles/eg-1-1-the-seven-thousand-rand-economy) through [How We Got Here](/articles/eg-1-2-how-we-got-here) and [The Comparator Countries](/articles/eg-1-3-the-comparator-countries), you understand three things. First, South Africa is stuck in the factor-driven stage of development at approximately $7,000 GDP per capita. Second, the economy was historically structured for extraction — minerals out, manufactured goods in — and that structure persists. Third, comparator countries like Chile, Malaysia, and Botswana demonstrate that resource-rich nations can use their endowments as launching pads rather than traps. This article is where the series shifts from diagnosis to prescription. Part 1 told you where South Africa stands. Part 2 tells you what to do about it, starting with the resources already in the ground and on the land.

## Reframing the Beneficiation Debate

"Beneficiation" has been a political slogan in South Africa for at least two decades. Every iteration of industrial policy since the mid-2000s has invoked it. The Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act of 2002 enshrines the principle. The Mining Charter references it. Successive ministers of mineral resources have promised it. And yet, despite all the rhetoric, South Africa's share of downstream mineral processing has not meaningfully increased. In some subsectors, it has declined.

The reason is not a lack of political will or even a lack of policy. The reason is that "beneficiate everything" is an economically incoherent demand. It treats beneficiation as a moral imperative — we have the minerals, therefore we must process them here — rather than as an economic calculation that must be evaluated on a case-by-case basis. And when you evaluate it case by case, using Lin's comparative advantage framework, the picture becomes far more nuanced and far more useful than the blanket sloganeering suggests.

Lin's test, as applied to resource beneficiation, asks a simple set of questions. Does South Africa have or can it realistically build the factor endowments — energy supply, skilled labour, logistics infrastructure, scale economies — required to process this particular mineral competitively? Is there a plausible path to capturing enough of the global market to justify the investment? And critically, what are the opportunity costs — what else could the capital, energy, and policy attention be spent on instead?

Apply this test honestly, and you get a differentiated picture. Some beneficiation opportunities pass with flying colours. Others fail decisively. And the distinction between the two is where a serious resource optimisation strategy begins.

### Platinum Group Metals: The Strongest Case

**South Africa produces roughly seventy per cent of the world's platinum** and a significant share of palladium, rhodium, and other platinum group metals (PGMs). These metals are essential inputs for catalytic converters (which reduce vehicle emissions), fuel cells (a growing market as hydrogen energy scales), jewellery, and various industrial applications including electronics and medical devices. The global PGM market is worth tens of billions of dollars annually.

Here is the absurdity: **South Africa mines the ore, smelts and refines a substantial portion of it domestically, and then exports the refined metal — only for manufacturers in Germany, Japan, and increasingly China to fabricate it into catalytic converters and fuel cell components** that are sold back to South Africa and the rest of the world. The value added in the fabrication step — transforming refined platinum into a functional catalytic converter — is substantial. A kilogram of refined platinum might sell for $30,000 to $35,000 depending on market conditions. The catalytic converters manufactured from that platinum sell at a significant markup reflecting engineering, intellectual property, and assembly costs.

Does South Africa have the factor endowments to capture this fabrication step? The answer is a qualified yes. The country already has the upstream refining capability (through companies like Sibanye-Stillwater and Anglo American Platinum's refineries). It has engineering graduates, albeit not enough of them. It has proximity to the raw material, which reduces logistics costs for inbound supply. What it lacks — and this is where policy must focus — is reliable baseload energy supply for manufacturing operations, a sufficiently deep pool of specialised metallurgical engineers, and the trade agreements that would give South African-manufactured catalytic converters preferential access to major automotive markets.

The fuel cell opportunity is even more compelling. As the global hydrogen economy develops — and projections from the International Energy Agency's *Global Hydrogen Review 2023* suggest that hydrogen could supply up to twelve per cent of global energy by 2050 in its Net Zero Emissions scenario — demand for PGMs in fuel cell manufacture is expected to grow substantially. South Africa has a narrow window to position itself as a manufacturer of fuel cell components, not merely a supplier of the metal that goes into them. The Hydrogen Society Roadmap, published by the Department of Science and Innovation in 2021, acknowledges this opportunity. But acknowledgement and execution are different things, and the execution requirements — energy stability, skills development, manufacturing incentives, trade facilitation — are the substance of resource optimisation.

:::expert
The Minerals Council South Africa, in its 2023 *Facts and Figures Pocketbook* and the accompanying *State of the Mining Industry* report, noted that South Africa's share of global PGM beneficiation has remained stagnant despite two decades of policy pronouncements. The Council's analysis identified three binding constraints: unreliable electricity supply (which makes continuous manufacturing processes unviable), regulatory uncertainty around mining charter obligations, and the absence of targeted manufacturing incentives comparable to those offered by competing jurisdictions. The Council emphasised that beneficiation is not a question of willingness but of investment conditions — firms will locate downstream processing where the combination of input costs, energy reliability, skills availability, and regulatory predictability is most favourable. On current trends, that location is increasingly China and India rather than South Africa.
:::

### Chrome and Ferrochrome: Conditional Success

South Africa holds approximately seventy-two per cent of the world's known chromite reserves and has historically been a major producer of ferrochrome — the alloy of chrome and iron that is the essential input for stainless steel production. Ferrochrome production is a classic beneficiation success story: rather than exporting raw chromite ore, South African smelters convert it into ferrochrome, capturing significant processing value domestically.

But the ferrochrome sector also illustrates the limits of beneficiation without the right preconditions. Ferrochrome smelting is extraordinarily energy-intensive. A single large ferrochrome smelter can consume as much electricity as a small city. During the period when Eskom supplied reliable, relatively cheap baseload power, South Africa's ferrochrome smelters were globally competitive. **As electricity costs escalated and load-shedding disrupted production schedules, that competitiveness eroded.** Several smelters mothballed capacity or shifted production to countries with more reliable energy supply. The lesson is sharp: beneficiation that depends on cheap, reliable electricity is only viable when cheap, reliable electricity actually exists. The energy question is not a separate issue from beneficiation — it is the foundational precondition.

If South Africa stabilises its baseload energy supply through the combination of renewable energy procurement, battery storage, gas peaking plants, and reformed Eskom operations discussed in [Infrastructure Foundations](/articles/eg-2-2-infrastructure-foundations), the ferrochrome sector can recover and expand. If it does not, ferrochrome production will continue migrating to jurisdictions — Turkey, India, Kazakhstan — that offer the energy reliability South Africa cannot.

### Steel: Where Beneficiation Fails Lin's Test

Now consider a case where the comparative advantage test yields a different answer. South Africa has iron ore and coal — the basic inputs for steelmaking. It has a domestic steel industry, anchored by ArcelorMittal South Africa. The beneficiation impulse says: we have the inputs, therefore we should make steel, therefore we should protect the domestic industry.

But apply Lin's framework honestly and the picture darkens. China produces roughly fifty-five per cent of the world's steel. Chinese steelmakers operate at a scale that South African producers cannot remotely match. A single large Chinese steel complex can produce more steel in a year than South Africa's entire industry combined. That scale translates into cost advantages — in procurement, in energy efficiency, in logistics — that tariff protection cannot sustainably overcome. ArcelorMittal South Africa has struggled financially for years, requiring tariff protection, special pricing agreements, and periodic government intervention to remain operational.

This does not mean South Africa should have no steel industry. Steel is strategically important, and some domestic capacity is justified on security-of-supply grounds. But it does mean that pouring policy energy and public resources into trying to make South African steel globally competitive against Chinese scale advantages is likely to produce poor returns. Those resources would generate higher value if directed toward sectors where South Africa has a genuine edge — PGM beneficiation, ferrochrome (with energy stability), and the agricultural value chains discussed below. Resource optimisation is as much about choosing where not to invest as where to invest, and the political courage to make that distinction is one of the scarcest resources of all.

### Manganese: The Unrealised Opportunity

South Africa's manganese story is perhaps the most frustrating of all the mineral value chain cases. The country holds approximately eighty per cent of the world's known manganese reserves, concentrated in the Kalahari Manganese Field in the Northern Cape. Manganese is essential for steel production (it strengthens steel and prevents corrosion), for battery manufacturing (manganese-rich cathode chemistries are increasingly important in electric vehicle batteries), and for various chemical applications.

Despite this extraordinary endowment, South Africa exports the overwhelming majority of its manganese as raw ore or at best as manganese alloy. The higher-value products — electrolytic manganese dioxide for batteries, manganese sulphate for electric vehicle battery cathodes, high-purity manganese chemicals — are manufactured elsewhere, predominantly in China. South Africa ships the rock; China adds the value.

The battery opportunity is time-sensitive. As the global electric vehicle market expands, demand for manganese in lithium-ion battery cathodes is projected to grow significantly through the 2030s and beyond. **South Africa has a window — perhaps a decade — to establish itself as a processor of battery-grade manganese products rather than merely a supplier of ore.** This requires targeted investment in processing facilities, reliable energy supply, water infrastructure in the Northern Cape (where water scarcity is a genuine constraint), and trade agreements with battery manufacturers and electric vehicle producers in Europe, the United States, and Asia.

:::takeaway
The beneficiation debate in South Africa has been distorted by treating all downstream processing as equally desirable. Lin's comparative advantage test reveals a more useful picture: platinum group metals and fuel cell components (strong case), ferrochrome (strong case if energy stabilised), battery-grade manganese products (strong case but time-sensitive), processed food and agriculture (strong case, detailed below), and primary steel production (weak case against Chinese scale). Resource optimisation means directing policy energy and public investment toward the sectors where South Africa can genuinely compete, not spreading resources across every conceivable value chain simultaneously.
:::

## Agriculture Value Chain Upgrading

Mining dominates the resource conversation in South Africa, but the agricultural sector offers beneficiation opportunities that are in some ways more accessible, more employment-intensive, and more immediately realisable. The reason is straightforward: agricultural value chain upgrading does not require the massive energy inputs that mineral processing demands. A pack house, a cold chain facility, or a juice processing plant runs on a fraction of the electricity that a ferrochrome smelter consumes. And agricultural value chain jobs tend to be more labour-absorbing, creating employment at skill levels that match the current South African workforce better than heavy industrial processing does.

### The Chile Benchmark

Chile's agricultural transformation is the benchmark that South Africa should study most carefully, and it was introduced in [The Comparator Countries](/articles/eg-1-3-the-comparator-countries) precisely because the parallels are so direct. In the 1970s and 1980s, Chile exported raw agricultural products — unprocessed fruit, bulk wine, raw timber. By the 2000s, Chile had become one of the world's leading exporters of branded, processed, high-value agricultural products: fresh counter-seasonal fruit sold in European and North American supermarkets, premium bottled wine recognised globally, farmed salmon, processed forestry products, and increasingly sophisticated food ingredients.

The transformation did not happen because Chilean farmers spontaneously decided to add value. It happened because a set of institutional interventions systematically addressed the binding constraints that prevented value chain upgrading. CORFO (the Chilean Economic Development Agency) funded technology transfer — bringing international processing techniques, quality management systems, and packaging technology to Chilean producers. ProChile (the export promotion agency) opened market access, negotiated phytosanitary standards with importing countries, and promoted Chilean brands in target markets. Public investment built the cold chain infrastructure — refrigerated storage at farms, refrigerated transport to ports, and cold storage at port facilities — without which fresh produce export is impossible. And the Chilean government pursued bilateral trade agreements with a relentlessness that gave Chilean agricultural products preferential tariff access to major consumer markets including the United States, the European Union, China, Japan, and South Korea.

The scale of the results speaks for itself. Chile's fresh fruit exports grew from approximately $800 million in 1990 to over $6 billion by the early 2020s. Wine exports grew from a niche product to over $1.5 billion annually. Salmon exports — built from scratch starting in the 1980s, as detailed in the Comparator Countries article — reached over $5 billion. The agricultural sector became one of Chile's largest employers and a significant source of foreign exchange, diversifying the economy away from copper dependence.

### What This Looks Like for South Africa

South Africa has the agricultural base to replicate a version of the Chilean model, adapted to local conditions and endowments. The country is already a significant exporter of raw agricultural products. Citrus (oranges, lemons, soft citrus) is one of the largest agricultural export categories — South Africa is the world's second-largest exporter of fresh citrus after Spain. Wine exports have grown substantially over the past two decades, though from a smaller base. Macadamia nuts are a fast-growing export crop in which South Africa is now the world's largest producer. Rooibos tea is a uniquely South African product with growing international demand. Dried fruit, avocados, table grapes, and stone fruit round out a substantial fresh and processed agricultural export portfolio.

The value chain gap is that South Africa exports most of these products in raw or minimally processed form. Fresh citrus is packed and shipped, but very little is processed domestically into juice concentrates, essential oils, or pectin (a high-value ingredient extracted from citrus peel that is used in food manufacturing globally). Wine is increasingly exported in bulk — in flexitanks shipped to bottling facilities in Europe and the United Kingdom, where it is bottled, labelled, and sold under European brands at a significant markup over the bulk price South African producers receive. Macadamia nuts are exported in-shell or as raw kernel, with the roasting, flavouring, and branding — the steps that capture the majority of the retail value — happening in destination markets.

The binding constraints are specific and addressable. First, cold chain infrastructure. Moving perishable agricultural products from farm to port in export condition requires unbroken refrigeration — from the pack house to the transport vehicle to the port cold store to the vessel's reefer containers. South Africa's cold chain has significant gaps, particularly in the inland transport segment and in port cold storage capacity. Investment in cold chain infrastructure is not glamorous, but it is the single most important enabling investment for agricultural value chain upgrading.

Second, quality certification and food safety standards. Accessing premium markets in the European Union, the United Kingdom, Japan, and the United States requires compliance with stringent food safety and quality standards — GlobalGAP, HACCP, British Retail Consortium standards, and various market-specific phytosanitary requirements. Many South African producers, particularly smaller-scale farmers and processors, lack the technical capacity and resources to achieve and maintain these certifications. A systematic programme of certification support — modelled on Chile's approach through CORFO and the Chilean food safety agency SAG — would expand the number of producers capable of accessing premium markets.

Third, trade agreements and market access. South Africa has trade agreements, but they are not optimised for agricultural value chain exports. The Economic Partnership Agreement with the European Union provides tariff preferences for some agricultural products, but tariff rate quotas limit volumes, and rules of origin requirements can disadvantage processed products. The African Growth and Opportunity Act (AGOA) provides preferential access to the United States market, but AGOA's renewal is periodic and uncertain, which discourages long-term investment in processing capacity aimed at the American market. The African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA), if fully implemented, offers enormous potential for processed food exports into the African market — a market of 1.4 billion consumers with rapidly growing demand for processed and packaged food products. Negotiating market access is slow, unglamorous work. It is also among the highest-return activities a government can undertake for agricultural value chain development.

:::expert
The Department of Trade, Industry and Competition (the dtic), through its various Sector Masterplans, has identified agro-processing as one of the priority sectors for industrial development. The Poultry Sector Master Plan and the Sugar Value Chain Master Plan are the most developed of these initiatives. However, research by the Trade and Industrial Policy Strategies (TIPS) institute has noted that the Masterplan approach has produced uneven results. TIPS analysis found that the masterplans which achieved measurable outcomes — increased investment, expanded production, or improved export performance — were those with specific, time-bound commitments from both government and industry, transparent monitoring mechanisms, and direct linkages to trade policy. Masterplans that remained at the level of general aspirations without binding commitments and clear accountability structures produced limited impact. The TIPS research suggests that the institutional design of the masterplan process — how commitments are negotiated, monitored, and enforced — matters more than the sectoral strategy itself.
:::

Fourth, and perhaps most fundamental, is the question of who participates. South Africa's agricultural sector remains deeply dualistic: a commercial farming sector that is globally competitive and export-oriented alongside a smallholder sector that is largely subsistence-oriented and disconnected from formal value chains. Resource optimisation in agriculture must address this duality, not by pretending that every smallholder can become an exporter overnight, but by building systematic pathways — through aggregation schemes, contract farming arrangements, and cooperative processing facilities — that connect smaller producers to the value chains where the returns are highest. This is not primarily an economic efficiency argument, though it is that. It is a structural transformation argument: the agricultural value chains will not generate the employment numbers South Africa needs if they remain confined to the existing commercial farming sector.

## Energy Independence: The Foundational Prerequisite

Every resource optimisation strategy discussed in this article — PGM beneficiation, ferrochrome smelting, manganese processing, agro-processing, cold chain infrastructure — depends on one thing that South Africa currently cannot reliably provide: electricity. This section is necessarily brief because the energy question is treated comprehensively in [Infrastructure Foundations](/articles/eg-2-2-infrastructure-foundations). But it must be addressed here because energy is not merely one input among many. It is the foundational prerequisite without which the entire resource optimisation stage collapses.

The facts are well known. Eskom, the state-owned utility that generates approximately ninety per cent of South Africa's electricity, has been in operational and financial crisis for over a decade. Load-shedding — the deliberate, scheduled interruption of electricity supply to prevent grid collapse — became a recurring feature of South African life from 2008, escalated severely from 2019, and reached unprecedented levels in 2022 and 2023. The impact on manufacturing and processing industries has been devastating. Every load-shedding event forces smelters, processing plants, and cold storage facilities to shut down. For continuous-process industries like ferrochrome smelting, an unplanned shutdown does not merely pause production — it can damage equipment, waste partially processed material, and require hours or days of restart procedures. For cold chain logistics, a power interruption can spoil an entire consignment of perishable goods.

The path to energy stability runs through three parallel tracks. The first is the Renewable Energy Independent Power Producer Procurement Programme (REIPPPP), which has already procured over 6,400 megawatts of renewable energy capacity from private producers since its inception in 2011. The programme has demonstrated that private investment in energy generation can be attracted at competitive prices — the most recent bid windows have produced solar and wind tariffs that are substantially below Eskom's average cost of generation. Expanding and accelerating the REIPPPP is the fastest route to adding new generation capacity.

The second track is battery storage and flexibility. Renewable energy — solar and wind — is intermittent. It generates electricity when the sun shines and the wind blows, not necessarily when demand peaks. Battery energy storage systems bridge this gap, storing excess renewable generation for dispatch during peak demand or low-generation periods. The cost of lithium-ion battery storage has fallen dramatically over the past decade, and South Africa's own mineral endowments (manganese, vanadium for redox flow batteries) create an opportunity to manufacture storage components domestically.

The third track is reforming Eskom itself — separating its generation, transmission, and distribution functions to create a competitive electricity market where multiple generators (including Eskom's own plants, independent power producers, and embedded generation by commercial and industrial users) compete to supply the grid. The establishment of the National Transmission Company of South Africa (NTCSA) as a separate entity is a step in this direction, though implementation remains incomplete.

The timeline matters enormously for resource optimisation. Most of the beneficiation and processing investments discussed in this article require reliable power supply as a precondition for investment decisions. If energy stability is achieved within the next three to five years, the window for resource optimisation remains open. If it takes a decade or more, competing jurisdictions will capture the processing investments that South Africa's resource endowments should naturally attract.

:::takeaway
Energy independence is not a separate policy goal from resource optimisation — it is the foundational precondition. Every processing investment, every cold chain facility, every smelter expansion depends on reliable, competitively priced electricity. The three-track approach — accelerated renewable procurement, battery storage deployment, and Eskom reform — must deliver measurable improvements within three to five years to keep the resource optimisation window open. The detailed treatment of how to achieve this is in [Infrastructure Foundations](/articles/eg-2-2-infrastructure-foundations).
:::

## Logistics Corridor Modernisation

If energy is the first foundational prerequisite for resource optimisation, logistics is the second. You can beneficiate every mineral and process every agricultural product in the country, but if you cannot move those goods to port efficiently and ship them to international markets at competitive cost, the value chain advantage evaporates.

South Africa's logistics infrastructure was, at its peak, among the best in the developing world. The port of Durban is the largest container port in sub-Saharan Africa. The port of Cape Town serves the Western Cape's agricultural export corridor. The port of Ngqura (Coega) in the Eastern Cape was purpose-built as a deepwater facility for bulk and container exports. The national rail network, operated by Transnet Freight Rail, connects mines, farms, and factories to these ports across thousands of kilometres of track.

The problem is that this infrastructure has been deteriorating for years. Transnet's operational performance has declined sharply, with rail volumes falling as maintenance backlogs have grown, equipment has aged, and — as emerged through various investigations — corruption and mismanagement under state capture eroded both investment and operational capacity. The consequences are measurable. Rail freight volumes on key corridors — the coal export line from Mpumalanga to Richards Bay, the iron ore line from the Northern Cape to Saldanha Bay, the manganese line to Port Elizabeth/Gqeberha and Ngqura — have all declined from their peaks. Cargo that should move by rail has shifted to road, increasing costs, damaging road infrastructure, and raising carbon emissions.

Port efficiency tells a similar story. **The World Bank's Container Port Performance Index has consistently ranked South African ports among the worst-performing globally.** Durban, which should be the logistics gateway to southern Africa, has suffered from equipment breakdowns, labour disputes, inefficient terminal operations, and inadequate landside connections. Ships queue for days to berth. Containers sit in port for far longer than international benchmarks suggest is acceptable. Every day of delay adds cost — and for perishable agricultural exports, delay can mean spoilage.

### The Vietnam Benchmark

The contrast with Vietnam's logistics transformation is instructive. In the early 2000s, Vietnam's logistics infrastructure was, by most measures, worse than South Africa's. Roads were poor, ports were congested and inefficient, and the rail network was antiquated. Vietnam's score on the World Bank's Logistics Performance Index was well below South Africa's.

Over the following two decades, Vietnam systematically rebuilt its logistics infrastructure. The government invested heavily in port expansion — deepening channels, building new container terminals, installing modern cranes and equipment. Hai Phong port and the cluster of ports around Ho Chi Minh City were upgraded to handle the surge in manufactured exports that the government's FDI strategy was designed to attract. Highway construction connected industrial zones to port facilities, reducing trucking times and costs. Customs procedures were streamlined, with electronic processing replacing manual paperwork.

The results were dramatic. By the 2020s, Vietnam's Logistics Performance Index score had risen significantly, overtaking South Africa's on several component measures. Vietnamese ports were handling vastly greater volumes than two decades earlier, at lower cost per container and with shorter dwell times. The logistics transformation was not a standalone achievement — it was a precondition for the manufacturing FDI boom that made Vietnam one of the world's fastest-growing exporters.

### What South Africa Needs

The logistics reform agenda for South Africa has three components. The first is rail recovery. Transnet Freight Rail must restore volumes on the key export corridors. This requires investment in rolling stock (locomotives and wagons), track maintenance, signalling systems, and — critically — opening parts of the rail network to private operators through third-party access arrangements. The private sector has indicated willingness to operate trains on Transnet's network if given access on reasonable commercial terms. The Transnet National Ports Authority (TNPA) has begun a process of structural separation from Transnet's port operating company, Transnet Port Terminals (TPT), which should enable private terminal operators to compete for port handling contracts. These reforms are underway but incomplete, and their pace will directly determine whether South Africa's logistics costs fall fast enough to support the resource optimisation strategy.

The second component is port efficiency. South Africa does not need to build many new ports — it needs to operate its existing ports competently. That means investment in modern handling equipment, reforms to labour scheduling and productivity, improved landside connections (the road and rail links that move cargo into and out of port precincts), and streamlined customs and documentation processes. The Durban port in particular needs transformative intervention. If Durban operated at the efficiency levels of comparable ports — Colombo, Tanjung Pelepas, or even Mombasa after its recent reforms — the cost savings for South African exporters would be measured in billions of rands annually.

The third component is trade facilitation — the regulatory and administrative processes that determine how quickly and cheaply goods cross borders. South Africa's performance on the World Bank's Trading Across Borders indicators has been mediocre. Documentary compliance, border compliance, and the time and cost of both importing and exporting are all worse than developing-country best practice. For a resource optimisation strategy that depends on exporting processed goods into global markets, every unnecessary day of delay and every unnecessary rand of compliance cost directly undermines competitiveness.

## Resource Revenue Capture and Deployment

Resource optimisation is not only about moving up value chains — it is also about capturing a fair share of the revenues from resource extraction and deploying those revenues strategically for long-term development. This is where the sovereign wealth fund argument becomes relevant, and where the experiences of Norway and Botswana offer direct lessons for South Africa.

### The Norwegian Model

Norway's Government Pension Fund Global — commonly known as the Oil Fund — is the world's largest sovereign wealth fund, with assets exceeding $1.4 trillion. It was established in 1990 to invest surplus revenues from Norwegian petroleum production. The fund's design reflects a simple but powerful insight: petroleum is a finite, non-renewable asset. When you extract and sell oil, you are converting natural wealth into financial wealth. If you spend that financial wealth on current consumption, you are getting poorer — using up an asset without replacing it with anything of equivalent value. If you invest it, you are converting one form of wealth (underground petroleum) into another form of wealth (financial assets that generate returns indefinitely).

Norway's fiscal rule limits the government's annual withdrawal from the fund to the expected long-term real return on the fund's investments — approximately three per cent. This means the fund's principal is preserved, and Norway effectively lives off the investment returns rather than drawing down the capital. The result is that Norway has transformed a finite resource — North Sea oil and gas that will eventually be depleted — into a permanent income stream for future generations.

### The Botswana Precedent

Norway is often dismissed as irrelevant to developing countries on the grounds that it was already wealthy and institutionally sophisticated before the oil money arrived. Botswana demolishes that argument. As described in [The Comparator Countries](/articles/eg-1-3-the-comparator-countries), Botswana established the Pula Fund to manage surplus diamond revenues — and it did so from a starting point of extreme poverty and minimal institutional capacity. The Pula Fund is smaller than Norway's, but the principle is identical: convert finite mineral wealth into financial assets that generate returns beyond the life of the mine.

Botswana's Sustainable Budget Index added a critical fiscal discipline: mineral revenues should fund investment expenditure (infrastructure, education, productive assets), not recurrent expenditure (salaries, consumption transfers). This distinction — spend mineral wealth on things that generate future returns, not on things that are consumed immediately — is the core fiscal discipline that resource-rich developing countries need.

### What This Means for South Africa

**South Africa has no sovereign wealth fund.** Despite decades of mining revenues — gold, platinum, coal, iron ore, manganese, chrome, diamonds — **the country has accumulated no permanent financial asset from its mineral endowment.** Mining revenues have flowed through the fiscus as royalties and corporate taxes, been spent on current expenditure, and left no lasting financial reserve.

The argument for establishing a South African sovereign wealth fund is not that it would solve the country's fiscal challenges overnight. At current mining revenue levels, the initial contributions would be modest. The argument is structural and disciplinary. A sovereign wealth fund with clear rules — contributions linked to mineral revenues, withdrawals limited to investment returns or capped at a percentage of fund value, governance by an independent board with transparent investment mandates — would do three things.

First, it would impose fiscal discipline on the management of resource revenues. By requiring that a portion of mineral royalties and taxes flow into the fund rather than the consolidated revenue fund, it would create a mechanism for saving during commodity boom years rather than spending every rand of windfall revenue on current consumption. **South Africa's fiscal history is littered with commodity booms — gold in the 1970s, commodities broadly in the 2000s — whose revenues were spent in full, leaving no buffer for the inevitable downturns.**

Second, it would create a counter-cyclical fiscal tool. During commodity price downturns or economic recessions, a sovereign wealth fund provides a source of fiscal spending that does not require borrowing. Chile's Economic and Social Stabilisation Fund, discussed in the Comparator Countries article, allowed Chile to implement a substantial fiscal stimulus during the 2008 financial crisis without increasing debt. South Africa entered the 2008 crisis without any such buffer and has been accumulating debt at an unsustainable rate ever since.

Third, it would embody intergenerational equity. South Africa's mineral deposits are finite. Every ton of platinum, manganese, or iron ore extracted today is a ton that future generations will not have. A sovereign wealth fund converts those finite physical assets into financial assets that can benefit future generations — through investment returns that fund education, infrastructure, or direct transfers — long after the mines have closed.

:::expert
National Treasury's 2011 discussion document on a South African sovereign wealth fund explored the feasibility and design options for such an institution. The document acknowledged the potential benefits — fiscal stabilisation, intergenerational saving, strategic investment — but noted significant design challenges: the level of initial capitalisation, the governance structure, the investment mandate, and the fiscal rules governing contributions and withdrawals. A decade later, the fund has not been established. Research by the Southern Africa Labour and Development Research Unit (SALDRU) at the University of Cape Town has argued that the primary obstacle is not technical but political — that the short-term fiscal pressures of funding service delivery, social grants, and debt service make it politically difficult to divert any revenue into a long-term savings vehicle, even when the long-term case for doing so is compelling. SALDRU's analysis suggests that the most feasible approach would be to ring-fence a specific revenue stream — such as mineral royalties, which are relatively small and identifiable — rather than attempting to divert general tax revenue, and to start with a modest fund that grows over time as the principle of resource revenue saving becomes institutionalised.
:::

## The Sequencing Challenge

If you have read this far, you may be feeling the tension between all of these priorities. Beneficiate PGMs. Process manganese. Upgrade agricultural value chains. Stabilise energy. Modernise logistics. Establish a sovereign wealth fund. Each is individually compelling. Together, they risk becoming yet another wish list — the kind of comprehensive strategy document that South African governments have excelled at producing and failed at implementing.

The sequencing challenge is real, and it is the distinguishing feature of countries that have managed resource-based transitions successfully versus those that have not. Chile did not attempt everything simultaneously. It started with copper revenue management and agricultural export facilitation, built those capabilities over a decade, and then expanded into more complex industrial activities. Botswana started with diamond revenue governance and basic infrastructure, then moved to broader economic diversification. Vietnam started with basic logistics infrastructure and low-complexity manufacturing, then progressively added complexity.

For South Africa, the sequencing logic of the resource optimisation stage should follow the comparative advantage test and the binding constraint analysis. Start with the interventions that have the highest return and the fewest preconditions, then sequence the more demanding interventions as the preconditions are met.

The highest-return, lowest-precondition interventions are agricultural value chain upgrading (cold chain investment, certification support, trade facilitation) and resource revenue governance reform (establishing the fiscal framework for a sovereign wealth fund, even if initial contributions are modest). These do not require solving the energy crisis first. They do not require massive capital investment. They require institutional reform, targeted public investment, and sustained policy attention.

The medium-term interventions — PGM beneficiation into catalytic converters and fuel cell components, ferrochrome capacity recovery, manganese processing for battery materials — require energy stability as a precondition. Their sequencing is therefore directly tied to the energy reform timeline. As baseload stability improves, the business case for these investments strengthens, and policy can shift to actively facilitating them through investment incentives, skills programmes, and trade agreements.

The longest-term intervention — building a sovereign wealth fund of meaningful size — requires sustained fiscal discipline across commodity cycles. It is a multi-decade project whose benefits accrue primarily to future generations. Starting now, even modestly, establishes the institutional architecture and the political norm of resource revenue saving that can compound over time.

:::takeaway
Resource optimisation is not a single policy but a sequenced programme. Start with agricultural value chains and resource revenue governance (low preconditions, high returns). Move to mineral beneficiation as energy stability improves (medium preconditions, high returns). Build the sovereign wealth fund steadily across commodity cycles (long-term, intergenerational). The sequence matters because attempting everything simultaneously without the preconditions in place is how South Africa has repeatedly produced ambitious strategies that fail at implementation.
:::

## Measuring Progress: The Resource Value Chain Tracker

This series is committed to giving you practical tools for evaluating whether the government's stated strategies are producing results. For the resource optimisation stage, the tool is the Resource Value Chain Tracker — a framework that maps South Africa's major resources against five dimensions that reveal whether the country is actually moving up value chains or merely talking about it.

**Dimension one: share of value chain captured domestically.** For each major resource, what percentage of the total value chain — from extraction through processing to final manufactured product — is captured within South Africa? For platinum, the share captured through smelting and refining is significant, but the share captured through downstream fabrication (catalytic converters, fuel cells, jewellery) is small. For citrus, the share captured through growing and packing is high, but the share captured through processing (juicing, essential oils, pectin extraction) is low. For manganese, the share captured beyond raw ore and basic alloy is minimal. Tracking this percentage over time tells you whether beneficiation is actually happening or merely being promised.

**Dimension two: trend direction.** Is the domestic share of the value chain increasing, stable, or declining? A declining share — even in a sector that performs some domestic processing — indicates that value is migrating to other jurisdictions faster than South Africa is capturing it. The ferrochrome sector's experience during the load-shedding crisis illustrates how quickly a positive trend can reverse when preconditions deteriorate.

**Dimension three: comparator country benchmark.** What share of the value chain do comparator countries capture in the same resource? Chile's share of the copper value chain (including mining services exports and downstream copper products), Botswana's share of the diamond value chain (including cutting and polishing through the Okavango Diamond Company), and Malaysia's share of the palm oil value chain (from plantation through refining to oleochemicals) all provide benchmarks against which South Africa's performance can be measured. If a comparator country with similar endowments captures significantly more of the value chain, the gap represents an achievable target.

**Dimension four: binding constraint.** For each resource value chain, what is the single most important constraint preventing South Africa from capturing a greater share? Is it energy reliability? Skills availability? Logistics costs? Trade agreements? Regulatory uncertainty? Identifying the binding constraint for each value chain focuses policy attention on the intervention that would have the greatest impact, rather than spreading effort across every conceivable bottleneck.

**Dimension five: institutional accountability.** Which government department, state-owned enterprise, or public institution is responsible for addressing the binding constraint? And what measurable target have they committed to, with what deadline? This dimension transforms the tracker from an analytical tool into an accountability tool. If the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy is responsible for the energy precondition that ferrochrome smelting requires, and they have committed to a specific megawatt capacity target by a specific date, you can track whether that commitment is being met. If no institution has taken clear responsibility and no measurable target has been set, that itself is the binding constraint — an accountability vacuum that no amount of strategy documents can fill.

Applying this tracker to South Africa's five most significant resource value chains produces a working assessment that you can update as new data becomes available.

For platinum group metals, the domestic share of the value chain is moderate (extraction through refining), the trend is flat, the comparator benchmark is the growing PGM fabrication industries in Japan and Germany, the binding constraint is energy reliability and the absence of targeted manufacturing incentives, and institutional accountability sits primarily with the dtic and the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy.

For manganese, the domestic share is low (mostly raw ore and basic alloy), the trend is flat to declining as Chinese processing capacity has expanded, the comparator benchmark is China's near-total dominance of downstream manganese products, the binding constraint is the combination of energy supply and water infrastructure in the Northern Cape, and institutional accountability spans the dtic, the Department of Water and Sanitation, and provincial government.

For citrus and fresh produce, the domestic share is moderate (growing, packing, and some processing), the trend is slowly positive (driven by private sector investment in pack houses and some processing), the comparator benchmark is Chile's agricultural value chain capture, the binding constraint is cold chain infrastructure and trade agreement optimisation, and institutional accountability sits with the dtic, the Department of Agriculture, Land Reform and Rural Development, and Transnet.

For chrome and ferrochrome, the domestic share was historically high but has declined, the trend is negative (smelter closures and mothballing), the comparator benchmark is Turkey's and India's growing ferrochrome industries, the binding constraint is unambiguously energy cost and reliability, and institutional accountability lies squarely with the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy and Eskom.

For wine, the domestic share is being eroded by the shift to bulk exports (wine shipped in flexitanks for bottling abroad), the trend is negative in terms of value capture, the comparator benchmark is Chile's and Australia's branded wine export models, the binding constraint is a combination of branding investment and trade agreement terms that favour bulk over bottled exports, and institutional accountability sits with the dtic and the wine industry's own collective marketing structures.

You do not need to be an economist to use this framework. You need to ask five questions about each resource: how much of the value chain do we capture, is that share growing, what do comparable countries achieve, what is blocking us, and who is accountable for fixing it? If you can answer those questions for the five or six most important resource value chains, you have a better analytical grip on South Africa's resource optimisation performance than most policy documents provide.

## From Resources to Capabilities

Resource optimisation is Stage 1 of the growth strategy for a reason. It is not the destination. It is the launchpad. The purpose of moving up resource value chains is not merely to capture a larger share of commodity revenues — though that matters. The deeper purpose is to build the industrial capabilities, the institutional competencies, and the fiscal resources that South Africa needs for Stage 2: the efficiency transition explored in [The Efficiency Transition](/articles/eg-2-3-the-efficiency-transition) and the broader infrastructure programme detailed in [Infrastructure Foundations](/articles/eg-2-2-infrastructure-foundations).

Chile's trajectory illustrates this perfectly. Chile did not stop at processed copper and fresh fruit exports. The capabilities built through resource value chain upgrading — engineering expertise, quality management systems, export logistics, trade negotiation capacity — became the foundation for diversification into entirely new sectors. Chilean mining services companies, originally built to serve the domestic copper industry, now export their expertise to mining operations on four continents. Chilean food safety and cold chain expertise, originally built for fresh fruit exports, enabled the development of the salmon farming industry and the expansion into processed food manufacturing.

The same logic applies to South Africa. The engineering and metallurgical capabilities required for PGM beneficiation are transferable to other advanced materials processing. The cold chain and quality management capabilities required for agricultural exports are transferable to pharmaceutical logistics and other temperature-sensitive supply chains. The trade negotiation and market access capabilities required for processed food exports are transferable to manufactured goods exports. And the fiscal discipline required for a sovereign wealth fund is transferable to the broader fiscal management challenges that the South African state faces.

The cross-cutting growth engines that power every stage of this transition — new ventures, new exports, and new financing mechanisms — are the subject of [Three Engines of Growth](/articles/eg-3-1-three-engines-of-growth). The institutional architecture required to coordinate and sustain the strategy across political cycles — the lesson South Africa should learn from Malaysia's EPU — is the subject of [The Execution Machine](/articles/eg-3-2-the-execution-machine).

Resource optimisation is where the growth strategy begins because it starts with what South Africa already has. You do not need to build a semiconductor industry from scratch or attract billions in greenfield manufacturing FDI before results become visible. You need to apply the comparative advantage test honestly, invest in the preconditions that binding constraint analysis reveals, sequence the interventions according to their prerequisites, and hold institutions accountable for measurable progress. None of this is easy. All of it is achievable. And the evidence from comparable countries demonstrates that it works.

:::framework
**The "Resource Value Chain" Evaluation Framework**

When you hear a claim about beneficiation, resource processing, or value chain upgrading in South Africa, use this five-question process to assess whether the proposal is economically sound or politically motivated sloganeering.

**Question 1: Does this pass Lin's comparative advantage test?**

For the specific resource and processing step being proposed, ask: does South Africa have or can it realistically build the factor endowments required to compete? The key endowments are energy supply (is reliable baseload power available?), skilled labour (are the metallurgical engineers, technicians, or food scientists available?), logistics infrastructure (can the processed product reach export markets at competitive cost?), and scale economies (is the investment large enough to compete with existing global producers?). If the answer is no on two or more of these factors, the beneficiation case is weak regardless of how appealing it sounds politically.

**Question 2: What share of the value chain does South Africa currently capture?**

For the resource in question, map the full value chain from extraction to final manufactured product. Then identify where South Africa's participation ends. For platinum: extraction through refining (yes), but catalytic converter and fuel cell fabrication (mostly no). For citrus: growing and packing (yes), but juice concentrates, essential oils, and pectin (mostly no). For manganese: raw ore and basic alloy (yes), but battery-grade chemicals (no). The gap between where participation ends and where the highest value is captured tells you where the opportunity lies.

**Question 3: What is the binding constraint preventing South Africa from capturing more?**

For each value chain, identify the single most important obstacle. Is it energy reliability (ferrochrome, PGM fabrication)? Logistics costs (agricultural exports)? Skills availability (advanced materials processing)? Trade agreements (processed food market access)? Water infrastructure (Northern Cape manganese processing)? Regulatory uncertainty (mining charter obligations)? Identifying the binding constraint focuses policy attention on the one intervention that would have the greatest impact, rather than spreading effort across every conceivable bottleneck.

**Question 4: What do comparator countries achieve in this value chain?**

Check what Chile captures in copper (mining plus mining services exports plus downstream copper products), what Botswana captures in diamonds (including cutting and polishing through Okavango Diamond Company), or what Malaysia captures in palm oil (from plantation through refining to oleochemicals). If a comparator country with similar endowments captures significantly more of the value chain, the gap represents an achievable target and proves the transition is possible.

**Question 5: Who is accountable, and what is the measurable target?**

Identify which government department, state-owned enterprise, or public institution is responsible for addressing the binding constraint. Has that institution committed to a specific, time-bound, measurable target? If the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy is responsible for energy stability that ferrochrome requires, have they committed to a specific megawatt target by a specific date? If no institution has taken clear responsibility and no measurable target exists, that accountability vacuum is itself the binding constraint — and no strategy document will fill it.

This framework transforms the beneficiation debate from a political slogan into an analytical exercise. South Africa's resource endowments are extraordinary. The question is not whether to add value — it is where to add value, in what sequence, and with what institutional accountability for results.
:::
