## Violence Subtracts Three to Four Years From National Life Expectancy

Violence is not just a criminal justice problem. It is a public health emergency that directly shortens lives, overwhelms the health system, and deepens the development deficit measured by every indicator that matters. South Africa's homicide rate — approximately forty-five per hundred thousand population, roughly seven times the global average — makes interpersonal violence the leading cause of death for men aged fifteen to forty-four and one of the most significant contributors to the country's life expectancy deficit. Add gender-based violence, which imposes an enormous physical, psychological, and reproductive health burden on women and children, and road traffic fatalities that kill more than twelve thousand people per year, and you arrive at a composite "violence tax" on life expectancy of approximately three to four years. That is three to four years that South Africa should be living but is not — years subtracted from the national average not by disease or poverty alone but by the direct application of force to human bodies.

This article makes the connection that policy discourse typically severs: the connection between safety and health, between the criminal justice system and the health system, between the policing budget and the life expectancy number. If you have read [The Life Expectancy Deficit](/articles/hd-2-1-the-life-expectancy-deficit), you understand how HIV and TB account for the bulk of South Africa's ten-year gap below the Preston Curve. This article accounts for the next largest portion of that gap. And if you have read the safety and security series — particularly [The System's Most Catastrophic Failure](/articles/ss-2-4-the-systems-most-catastrophic-failure) on gender-based violence and [Policing a Divided Country](/articles/ss-2-2-policing-a-divided-country) — you understand the structural drivers of violence. This article shows you the health consequences: the bodies in the trauma units, the overwhelmed mental health system, the children growing up in toxic stress environments, and the fiscal drain that violence imposes on a health system that cannot afford it.

The core analytical point is this: you cannot achieve the life expectancy improvements described in the previous article — closing the treatment cascade, managing the TB burden, addressing non-communicable diseases — while simultaneously absorbing a violence burden that would be classified as a low-intensity conflict by international humanitarian standards. Violence and health are not separate policy domains. They are the same domain, and until South Africa treats them as such, the life expectancy deficit will persist.

## The Numbers That Should Shock

Let us begin with the headline number and its context. In the 2022/23 reporting period, the South African Police Service recorded 27,494 murders. That translates to a homicide rate of approximately forty-five per hundred thousand population. The global average homicide rate, according to the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime, is approximately six per hundred thousand. South Africa's rate is therefore roughly seven and a half times the global average. To find countries with comparable homicide rates, you must look at nations experiencing narco-violence (Honduras, El Salvador before its recent crackdown, Mexico's worst-affected states), post-conflict fragility (parts of Central America, some Caribbean island states), or outright state failure. If you live in one of the country's hyper-endemic violence zones, these are not statistics — they are the sound of sirens at night and funerals on weekends.

But South Africa is not a narco-state, a post-conflict society, or a failed state. It is a constitutional democracy with functional institutions, a sophisticated financial system, a diversified economy, and a per capita income that places it in the upper-middle-income category. The juxtaposition — upper-middle-income economy, conflict-zone homicide rate — is one of the defining anomalies of contemporary South Africa, and it demands explanation.

The murder statistics alone understate the violence burden. For every murder, there are multiple non-fatal violent assaults — the SAPS recorded over 160,000 cases of assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm in the same period. Aggravated robbery — robbery with the use or threat of a weapon — exceeded 140,000 cases. Common assault exceeded 170,000 cases. These are reported cases only; surveys including the Victims of Crime Survey conducted by Statistics South Africa consistently indicate that a large proportion of violent crimes — particularly those occurring within households and communities — go unreported.

The demographic profile of violence mortality is heavily skewed. Interpersonal violence is the leading cause of death for South African men aged fifteen to forty-four — the most economically productive demographic cohort. This is not the case in any comparable upper-middle-income country. In Brazil, despite its own severe violence problem, motor vehicle accidents and cardiovascular disease rival interpersonal violence as leading causes of death in this age group. In South Africa, homicide dominates. The South African Medical Research Council's National Burden of Disease Study identifies interpersonal violence as the second leading cause of years of life lost in the country, behind HIV/AIDS and ahead of tuberculosis, lower respiratory infections, and diarrhoeal diseases.

The spatial distribution of violence is also revealing. Homicide is heavily concentrated in specific geographic areas — predominantly urban informal settlements, township peripheries, and certain rural hotspots — creating what researchers have described as "hyper-endemic violence zones" where the homicide rate may be three to five times the already-elevated national average. In parts of the Cape Flats (Nyanga, Khayelitsha, Philippi), parts of Gauteng's East Rand and West Rand, and parts of KwaZulu-Natal's eThekwini municipality, homicide rates approach or exceed one hundred per hundred thousand — comparable to the most violent cities in the Western Hemisphere.

:::expert
The measurement of violence in South Africa is itself a contested terrain. The SAPS crime statistics, released annually, are the primary official data source but have been criticised by criminologists and civil society organisations for undercounting — particularly of sexual offences, domestic violence, and community-level violence. The South African Medical Research Council's injury mortality surveillance system, which captures data from mortuaries and forensic pathology services, provides an independent check on the SAPS data and has at times suggested higher homicide counts than the police statistics indicate. The discrepancy arises partly from definitional differences (the police count cases opened, the MRC counts bodies processed) and partly from institutional incentives that may discourage the recording of cases at station level. The Victims of Crime Survey adds a further layer, showing that for some categories of violence — particularly assault and sexual offences — reporting rates to the police may be as low as forty to fifty percent. Taken together, the available data sources suggest that the official statistics, alarming as they are, represent a lower bound on the actual violence burden. The true homicide count is likely between twenty-seven thousand and thirty thousand per year, and the true violent assault count is almost certainly significantly higher than the reported figures.
:::

## Gender-Based Violence: The Health Dimension

South Africa's gender-based violence crisis — documented extensively in the safety and security series — has a health dimension that is staggering in its scope and devastating in its consequences. If you are a woman in South Africa, or if you have a daughter, a sister, or a partner, the statistics in this section are not abstract: they describe the medical consequences of violence that one in three South African women will experience. This section does not duplicate the analysis of GBV's causes and structural drivers, which is covered in [The System's Most Catastrophic Failure](/articles/ss-2-4-the-systems-most-catastrophic-failure). Instead, it focuses specifically on the health burden: the injuries, the infections, the pregnancies, the psychological trauma, and the health system costs.

South Africa's femicide rate — the rate at which women are killed by intimate partners or in gender-motivated violence — is approximately five times the global average. The South African Medical Research Council's research, drawing on mortuary-based surveillance, estimates that a woman is killed by an intimate partner every four to five hours in South Africa. These deaths represent the extreme end of a continuum of violence that includes physical assault, sexual assault, psychological abuse, and economic coercion.

The health consequences of non-fatal GBV are extensive and cumulative. Physical injuries from intimate partner violence range from bruising and lacerations to fractures, traumatic brain injury, and permanent disability. Sexual violence carries the risk of HIV transmission — South Africa's generalised HIV epidemic means that sexual assault in this context carries a particularly high risk of infection — as well as other sexually transmitted infections, unwanted pregnancy, and the physical injuries associated with forced penetration. The post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) protocols that should be administered to sexual assault survivors within seventy-two hours of exposure are available in principle at all health facilities, but access is uneven and the quality of implementation varies.

The reproductive health consequences deserve specific attention. Unwanted pregnancies resulting from sexual violence place women and girls in impossible situations in a country where abortion, while legally available on demand up to twelve weeks of gestation under the Choice on Termination of Pregnancy Act, is in practice inaccessible for many women due to the shortage of providers willing to perform the procedure. Approximately fifty-two percent of designated abortion facilities were non-functional as of the most recent audit, and women in rural areas may need to travel hundreds of kilometres to access legal termination. The result is that many sexual violence survivors carry unwanted pregnancies to term or resort to unsafe termination methods, with consequent health risks.

The psychological health burden of GBV is immense and inadequately addressed. Post-traumatic stress disorder, major depression, anxiety disorders, substance use disorders, and suicidal ideation are all significantly more prevalent among GBV survivors than in the general population. South African studies, including longitudinal research conducted by the Gender and Health Research Unit of the South African Medical Research Council, have documented PTSD prevalence rates of forty to fifty percent among women who have experienced intimate partner violence — rates comparable to those found among combat veterans. Depression prevalence among GBV survivors is approximately three times higher than in the general female population.

These mental health consequences are almost entirely untreated. South Africa has approximately 0.28 psychiatrists per hundred thousand population and approximately 0.32 psychologists per hundred thousand population — both far below WHO-recommended minimums. The vast majority of primary healthcare facilities do not have dedicated mental health professionals. Mental healthcare is nominally integrated into primary healthcare through the task-shifting model — in which primary care nurses are trained to screen for and manage common mental disorders — but implementation has been limited, and the quality of mental health screening and management at primary care level remains poor.

The health system cost of GBV is substantial but poorly quantified. Emergency department visits, hospitalisation for injuries, post-exposure prophylaxis, pregnancy-related care, mental health services (to the extent they are provided), and the long-term management of chronic conditions exacerbated by GBV (including the metabolic and cardiovascular consequences of chronic stress) all impose costs on an already strained system. A KPMG study commissioned by the South African government estimated the direct health cost of GBV at approximately R28 billion to R42 billion per year — a figure that, while necessarily imprecise, indicates the scale of the fiscal burden.

:::takeaway
Gender-based violence imposes a health burden that encompasses physical injury, HIV transmission, unwanted pregnancy, and psychological trauma at epidemic levels. With femicide rates five times the global average and PTSD prevalence of forty to fifty percent among survivors, GBV represents one of the largest health burdens in the country that is almost entirely inadequately addressed. The estimated R28 billion to R42 billion annual health cost of GBV represents resources that are absorbed by the consequences of violence rather than invested in prevention and development.
:::

## Road Fatalities: The Overlooked Epidemic

Road traffic injuries and fatalities constitute a major contributor to South Africa's violence-related health burden that is typically discussed as a transport problem rather than a health problem. This framing error has significant consequences for policy: road safety sits in the transport and law enforcement portfolios, distant from the health decision-makers who are grappling with the life expectancy deficit it exacerbates. If you commute by taxi or walk along a national road to reach public transport, you navigate this risk every day — and the state largely does not account for it as a health burden it has a duty to address.

South Africa records approximately twelve thousand to fourteen thousand road fatalities per year, depending on the data source and methodology. The Road Traffic Management Corporation's annual reports place the figure at approximately twelve thousand, while the World Health Organization's modelled estimates — which adjust for underreporting — suggest the true figure may be closer to fourteen thousand. The road traffic fatality rate of approximately twenty-two to twenty-five per hundred thousand population is among the highest in the world and approximately three to four times the rate in high-income countries.

The demographic profile is concentrated in the most economically productive age groups. Pedestrians account for approximately thirty-eight percent of road fatalities — a strikingly high proportion by international standards and a reflection of the apartheid-era spatial planning that placed townships and informal settlements far from economic centres, connected by highways without adequate pedestrian infrastructure. Drivers and passengers account for the remainder, with young men aged eighteen to thirty-four dramatically overrepresented. Weekend nights, when alcohol consumption is highest, account for a disproportionate share of fatal crashes — the South African road fatality data shows a pronounced weekend peak, particularly on Friday and Saturday nights between 6pm and midnight.

Alcohol is implicated in approximately fifty-eight percent of road fatalities, according to the most recent forensic data. This is an extraordinarily high proportion — in high-income countries, the figure is typically twenty to thirty percent — and reflects both the prevalence of hazardous drinking in South Africa and the weak enforcement of drink-driving legislation. The Road Traffic Act sets the legal blood alcohol limit at 0.05 grams per hundred millilitres, but enforcement through roadblocks and breath testing is inconsistent and concentrated during holiday periods rather than sustained year-round.

The health system absorbs the consequences. Trauma units across the country — and particularly the major trauma centres at Level 1 hospitals including Chris Hani Baragwanath Academic Hospital in Soweto, Groote Schuur Hospital in Cape Town, Inkosi Albert Luthuli Central Hospital in Durban, and Steve Biko Academic Hospital in Pretoria — treat road traffic injury volumes that are, by international standards, extraordinary. Orthopaedic wards are filled with fracture patients. Neurosurgical units manage traumatic brain injuries. Intensive care beds are occupied by ventilated polytrauma patients. The opportunity cost — the care that is not being provided because the resources are absorbed by road trauma — is enormous but largely invisible in policy discourse.

The economic cost compounds the human cost. The Road Traffic Management Corporation has estimated the total economic cost of road crashes in South Africa at approximately R176 billion per year — equivalent to approximately three percent of GDP. This includes direct costs (medical care, vehicle damage, emergency response), indirect costs (lost productivity, foregone earnings of victims), and intangible costs (pain, suffering, reduced quality of life). For a country struggling to grow its GDP at all, a three percent annual drain from road violence is a macroeconomic factor, not merely a transport inconvenience.

:::expert
The concept of "years of life lost" (YLL) provides a particularly stark measure of the road traffic mortality burden. Because road fatalities disproportionately affect young people — the median age of road fatality victims in South Africa is approximately thirty-two years — each death represents a large number of potential years of life lost. A thirty-two-year-old who dies in a road crash in South Africa has, based on current life expectancy, lost approximately thirty-one years of potential life. Aggregated across twelve thousand to fourteen thousand annual deaths, road traffic fatalities account for approximately 350,000 to 450,000 years of life lost per year — a burden comparable to that of some major disease categories. The Global Burden of Disease study, which computes disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) for all health conditions, ranks road injuries among the top ten contributors to DALYs in South Africa. The DALY framework captures not only deaths but also disability — and for every road death, there are an estimated twenty to thirty serious non-fatal injuries, many resulting in permanent disability including paraplegia, traumatic brain injury, and limb amputation. The disability burden imposes long-term costs on the health system, the social protection system, and the affected families, extending the impact of each crash far beyond the immediate event.
:::

## The Trauma System Under Siege

South Africa's public hospital trauma system is among the busiest and most clinically experienced in the world — and this is not a compliment. What this means for you practically is that when you or a family member presents at a public emergency department for any reason — a difficult labour, a suspected heart attack, a child's illness — you are entering a system whose capacity is already partially consumed by the consequences of violence. The volume of penetrating trauma (gunshot wounds, stab wounds) and blunt trauma (assaults, motor vehicle crashes) that flows through South African emergency departments on a daily basis is comparable to what trauma systems in other countries experience during mass casualty events or armed conflicts.

Chris Hani Baragwanath Academic Hospital in Soweto — the largest hospital in the Southern Hemisphere, with approximately three thousand beds — sees trauma volumes that few civilian hospitals anywhere in the world can match. Its emergency department manages an estimated four hundred to five hundred patients per day, a significant proportion of whom present with violence-related injuries. On weekend nights, the trauma bay resembles a field hospital: gunshot wounds to the abdomen, stab wounds to the chest, head injuries from assaults, and road traffic polytrauma arriving in rapid succession. The surgical teams — trauma surgeons, orthopaedic surgeons, neurosurgeons, anaesthetists — are among the most experienced in the world at managing penetrating trauma precisely because they see it in volumes that surgeons in safer countries encounter only in military deployments.

Groote Schuur Hospital in Cape Town, Red Cross War Memorial Children's Hospital (which manages paediatric trauma, including injuries from crossfire in gang-affected communities), and Inkosi Albert Luthuli Central Hospital in Durban face comparable pressures. The Western Cape, despite being among the better-resourced provinces, sees some of the highest trauma volumes in the country, driven by gang violence on the Cape Flats. A 2019 study published in the South African Medical Journal documented that trauma surgeons at Groote Schuur spent approximately eighty percent of their operating time on violence-related injuries — eighty percent — leaving only twenty percent for elective surgery, cancer operations, and other planned procedures.

This resource diversion — from planned care to emergency violence management — is perhaps the most insidious health system impact of South Africa's violence crisis. Every hour that a surgeon spends repairing a stab wound to the liver is an hour not spent removing a cancerous tumour. Every intensive care bed occupied by a gunshot victim is a bed unavailable for a post-operative cardiac patient. Every unit of blood transfused into a trauma patient is a unit unavailable for an obstetric haemorrhage. The violence burden does not only kill the people who are directly injured. It degrades the quality and availability of care for everyone else who depends on the same overburdened system.

The nursing burden is equally severe. Trauma nursing requires specialised skills — triage assessment, wound management, resuscitation support, post-operative monitoring — and the emotional toll of managing a continuous stream of severely injured patients, many of them young, many of whom die despite intervention, contributes to burnout, compassion fatigue, and staff turnover. The high rate of workplace violence in emergency departments — healthcare workers being assaulted by patients, patients' companions, or perpetrators who follow victims into the hospital — adds another dimension of stress. Surveys of emergency department nurses in South African public hospitals have documented that a majority have experienced physical or verbal assault at work.

The ambulance and pre-hospital emergency medical services are similarly stretched. Response times in urban areas, while improving, remain inconsistent, and in rural areas, response times of an hour or more are common. The availability of advanced life support (ALS) paramedics — paramedics with the training and equipment to perform advanced airway management, administer drugs, and manage chest injuries in the field — is limited, particularly outside major metropolitan areas. Many trauma patients arrive at hospitals via private transport or police vehicles rather than ambulance, which means they receive no pre-hospital stabilisation and present to emergency departments in a more critical condition.

:::takeaway
South Africa's trauma system treats violence volumes comparable to those in conflict zones. At Groote Schuur Hospital, trauma surgeons spend eighty percent of their operating time on violence-related injuries, diverting surgical capacity from cancer operations, elective procedures, and other planned care. The resource diversion — surgical time, ICU beds, blood products, nursing hours — degrades healthcare quality for the entire population, not just violence victims. The trauma burden is a hidden mechanism through which violence worsens health outcomes across every disease category.
:::

## Mental Health: The Invisible Burden

If physical trauma is the visible face of violence's health impact, the mental health burden is its invisible counterpart — vast in scale, devastating in its consequences, and almost entirely unaddressed by the health system. You may recognise this burden in yourself or in people around you: the hypervigilance, the difficulty sleeping, the flattened affect that characterises communities where violence has become background noise. These are not personality traits — they are documented clinical responses to chronic exposure to threat. South Africa's violence epidemic does not only break bones and puncture organs. It breaks minds, at a population scale, with cascading effects on productivity, parenting, community cohesion, and the capacity of individuals and communities to function.

The South African Stress and Health (SASH) study — the most comprehensive psychiatric epidemiological survey conducted in South Africa, funded by the National Institute of Mental Health and published in leading international journals — found that approximately thirty percent of South Africans will experience a mental disorder in their lifetime, with anxiety disorders, substance use disorders, and mood disorders the most prevalent categories. Exposure to violence was among the strongest predictors of mental disorder onset. Given that a large proportion of the population has direct or indirect exposure to violence — as victims, witnesses, or family members of victims — the population-level mental health burden attributable to violence is enormous.

Post-traumatic stress disorder is the most directly violence-linked mental health condition. PTSD prevalence in the general South African population is estimated at approximately three to four percent — high by global standards — but in violence-affected communities, the prevalence is dramatically higher. Studies in communities on the Cape Flats, in parts of Gauteng, and in conflict-affected areas of KwaZulu-Natal have documented PTSD prevalence rates of fifteen to twenty-five percent among adults and even higher among children and adolescents. These are prevalence rates that, in the international literature, are associated with populations that have experienced war or major natural disasters.

The consequences of untreated PTSD and other trauma-related mental health conditions extend far beyond individual suffering. Depression and PTSD impair cognitive function, reduce the ability to sustain employment, undermine parenting capacity, and increase the risk of substance abuse. Adults with untreated PTSD are more likely to use alcohol and drugs as coping mechanisms, which in turn increases the risk of violence perpetration, creating a self-reinforcing cycle. Parents with untreated mental health conditions provide less responsive and stimulating parenting, which affects child development — the "intergenerational transmission of trauma" is not a metaphor but a documented developmental pathway through which violence in one generation produces developmental harm in the next.

Children are particularly vulnerable. The concept of "adverse childhood experiences" (ACEs) — developed initially in a landmark United States study by Felitti and colleagues and since replicated internationally — demonstrates that exposure to violence, abuse, and household dysfunction in childhood has dose-dependent effects on adult health outcomes. Each additional ACE increases the risk of depression, substance abuse, suicide, cardiovascular disease, cancer, and premature death. South African children, growing up in one of the most violent societies in the world, accumulate ACEs at rates that place a significant proportion of the next generation at elevated risk for the full spectrum of physical and mental health conditions.

The mental health treatment gap — the proportion of people with diagnosable mental health conditions who do not receive any treatment — is estimated at approximately seventy-five percent in South Africa. Three out of four South Africans with a mental health condition receive no treatment of any kind. The reasons are structural: insufficient mental health professionals (0.28 psychiatrists and 0.32 psychologists per hundred thousand population, compared to WHO recommendations of at least one psychiatrist and one psychologist per ten thousand), inadequate integration of mental health into primary healthcare, stigma surrounding mental illness, and the historical prioritisation of infectious disease management over mental health in resource allocation.

The economic cost of untreated mental illness — measured in lost productivity, absenteeism, presenteeism (being at work but functioning poorly), and disability — has been estimated by the WHO at approximately four percent of GDP for low- and middle-income countries. Applied to South Africa, this suggests a mental health-related productivity loss of approximately R280 billion per year — a figure that dwarfs the mental health treatment budget by a factor of more than fifty. The return on investment from mental health treatment is correspondingly high: the WHO's estimates suggest that every dollar invested in scaling up treatment for depression and anxiety yields a return of approximately four dollars in improved health and productivity.

:::expert
The neuroscience of violence exposure has advanced significantly in recent decades, providing biological mechanisms for the epidemiological associations between violence, mental health, and physical health. Chronic exposure to violence activates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis — the body's stress response system — on a sustained basis. Chronic HPA activation leads to elevated cortisol levels, which in turn produce a cascade of physiological effects: inflammation, immune suppression, metabolic dysregulation, and cardiovascular strain. These effects are measurable, dose-dependent, and cumulative. Research from the Drakenstein Child Health Study — a South African birth cohort study based in Paarl, Western Cape, conducted by researchers at the University of Cape Town — has documented measurable neurobiological effects of violence exposure on infant brain development, including altered cortisol reactivity, reduced hippocampal volume, and changes in white matter integrity. These findings provide a biological pathway from violence exposure in early life to cognitive impairment, mental health conditions, and cardiometabolic disease in adulthood. They also provide the scientific basis for understanding why violence is not merely a criminal justice problem but a public health problem with biological mechanisms that connect safety to every dimension of human development.
:::

## The "Violence Tax" on Life Expectancy

Having documented the specific channels through which violence shortens lives — homicide, GBV-related mortality, road fatalities, the degradation of the health system through trauma burden, and the mental health consequences that increase morbidity and mortality across conditions — we can now attempt to quantify the total "violence tax" on South Africa's life expectancy. This is the portion of the life expectancy gap that sits directly in the space between where you live and where the Preston Curve says you should be living — years subtracted not by disease or destiny but by the structural failure to keep people safe.

The direct mortality contribution is the most straightforward to estimate. If South Africa had a homicide rate equal to the global average (approximately six per hundred thousand rather than forty-five per hundred thousand), approximately twenty-three thousand fewer people would be murdered each year. Given the young age profile of homicide victims — the median age is approximately twenty-eight to thirty years — each prevented homicide would save approximately thirty to thirty-five years of potential life. The aggregate effect on life expectancy at birth has been estimated by demographers at the South African Medical Research Council and the Burden of Disease Research Unit at approximately 1.5 to 2 years. That is, if South Africa's homicide rate were at the global average, life expectancy at birth would be approximately 1.5 to 2 years higher than it currently is.

Road traffic fatalities add a further component. If South Africa achieved road traffic fatality rates comparable to those in upper-middle-income countries with effective road safety programmes (approximately eight to ten per hundred thousand rather than twenty-two to twenty-five per hundred thousand), approximately five thousand to seven thousand fewer people would die on the roads each year. Given the similarly young age profile of road fatality victims, the life expectancy contribution would be approximately 0.5 to 1 year.

The indirect mortality contribution — deaths attributable to the health system degradation caused by violence burden, and deaths attributable to untreated mental health conditions that are violence-related — is harder to quantify precisely but is almost certainly positive and may be substantial. If the trauma burden were halved, freeing surgical, ICU, blood bank, and nursing capacity for other conditions, cancer survival rates would improve, elective surgical waiting times would decrease, and obstetric emergency management would be more effective. If the mental health burden of violence were addressed, substance abuse rates would decrease (reducing violence-related trauma further, in a virtuous cycle), chronic disease management would improve (through better adherence to treatment regimens), and economic productivity would increase (through reduced disability and absenteeism).

Summing the direct and indirect contributions, the total "violence tax" on South Africa's life expectancy is plausibly in the range of three to four years. Combined with the HIV/TB-driven deficit documented in the previous article, this accounts for the vast majority of South Africa's ten-year gap below the Preston Curve.

The fiscal dimension is equally significant. The direct costs of violence to the health system — emergency department care, trauma surgery, intensive care, rehabilitation, mental health services (to the limited extent they are provided), and medico-legal services (forensic pathology, district surgeon examinations of assault and sexual violence survivors) — have been estimated at approximately R30 billion to R50 billion per year. The total economic cost of violence — including lost productivity, property damage, and the costs borne by the criminal justice system — has been estimated by various researchers at between three and five percent of GDP, or approximately R200 billion to R350 billion per year. These are resources that, in a less violent society, would be available for investment in health, education, and economic infrastructure — the three pillars of human development.

:::takeaway
The "violence tax" on South Africa's life expectancy is approximately three to four years, comprising approximately 1.5 to 2 years from excess homicide, 0.5 to 1 year from road fatalities, and an indirect component from health system degradation and untreated mental health consequences. Combined with the HIV/TB deficit, violence accounts for a substantial portion of the ten-year gap below the Preston Curve. The fiscal cost of violence — R200 billion to R350 billion annually — represents resources diverted from development investment, creating a reinforcing cycle in which violence begets underdevelopment and underdevelopment begets violence.
:::

## Violence and the Reinforcing Cycle

The connection between violence and development is not merely additive — violence reduces life expectancy, and life expectancy is an HDI component. The connection is cyclical, and the cycle is vicious. You can trace this cycle in any high-violence community you know: the businesses that closed, the school absences, the nurse who transferred out, the clinic that now shuts early because staff don't feel safe walking to their cars after dark. Understanding this cycle is essential to understanding why South Africa's violence problem is so persistent and why treating it as "just a policing problem" has been so comprehensively inadequate.

The cycle operates through multiple interconnected channels.

Violence overwhelms the health system, reducing the capacity for preventive care. When trauma surgeons are spending eighty percent of their time on violence injuries, they have twenty percent of their time for everything else. When emergency departments are filled with assault and road traffic patients, chronic disease patients wait longer, screening programmes are understaffed, and community health outreach is neglected. The health system triage that violence imposes — emergency care crowds out preventive and chronic care — means that the very conditions that determine long-term life expectancy (HIV treatment retention, TB case-finding, NCD management) receive less attention and fewer resources than they should.

Violence reduces economic productivity, which reduces fiscal space for health investment. The three to five percent of GDP lost to violence annually is three to five percent that is not generating tax revenue, not funding health and education expenditure, and not creating the economic growth that is the prerequisite for everything else in the human development portfolio. Young men who are killed or disabled by violence are not contributing to the economy. Women whose productivity is impaired by GBV and its mental health consequences are not reaching their economic potential. Communities where businesses close or fail to open because of crime and violence are not generating the employment and income that drive both economic growth and health improvement.

Violence degrades human capital formation, which reduces future economic growth and health outcomes. Children growing up in violent environments accumulate adverse childhood experiences that impair cognitive development, reduce educational achievement, and increase the risk of mental and physical health conditions in adulthood. The intergenerational transmission of violence — whereby children who witness or experience violence are more likely to become perpetrators or victims in adulthood — means that today's violence crisis produces tomorrow's. The human capital damage is not limited to direct victims; the pervasive anxiety and hypervigilance that characterise life in violence-affected communities affect the cognitive and emotional development of all children in those communities, not just those who are directly exposed.

Violence erodes social cohesion and institutional trust, which reduces the effectiveness of public health interventions. Public health programmes — HIV testing, TB case-finding, vaccination campaigns, mental health outreach — depend on community engagement, trust in institutions, and the willingness of individuals to interact with government services. In communities where violence has eroded trust — trust in the police, trust in the health system, trust in neighbours — public health programmes are less effective. People are less likely to disclose HIV status, less likely to bring children for immunisation, less likely to seek care for mental health conditions, and less likely to participate in community health initiatives.

The fiscal mechanism tightens the cycle. Less economic growth means less tax revenue. Less tax revenue means constrained budgets. Constrained budgets mean less funding for both policing (which might prevent violence) and health (which treats the consequences of violence). The result is a system trapped in a low-growth, high-violence, poor-health equilibrium — what development economists would recognise as a "poverty trap" but is more precisely described as a "violence trap."

:::expert
The concept of violence as a development trap has been formalised in the economic development literature, most influentially by the World Bank's 2011 World Development Report on Conflict, Security, and Development. That report documented that countries trapped in cycles of violence — even sub-national violence that does not reach the level of civil war — experienced systematically worse development outcomes than peaceful countries at similar income levels. The report identified institutional capacity, legitimate and effective security forces, and inclusive political settlements as the primary mechanisms for escaping violence traps. Applied to South Africa, this framework suggests that the violence problem cannot be solved by the criminal justice system alone. It requires a comprehensive approach that addresses the structural drivers of violence — inequality, unemployment, substance abuse, gender norms, and spatial inequality — while simultaneously strengthening the capacity of the state to protect its citizens and the capacity of the health system to manage the consequences. The concept of "violence as a cause of poverty" (rather than merely a consequence of it) has been further developed by researchers including Gary Haugen and Victor Boutros, whose work at the International Justice Mission has documented the mechanisms through which violence against the poor traps them in poverty by destroying assets, deterring investment, undermining health, and preventing the accumulation of human capital.
:::

## What the Evidence Shows Works

If violence is a public health crisis — and the evidence presented in this article should leave no reasonable doubt that it is — then the response should include public health approaches, not just criminal justice approaches. When you next read a political party's manifesto, or attend a ward committee meeting, you can use the evidence base below to ask whether your representatives are talking about prevention or just reaction. The two are not mutually exclusive. Effective policing is essential for violence prevention, and the safety and security series addresses this in detail. But the public health evidence base offers additional interventions that target the upstream determinants of violence and that complement law enforcement with prevention.

The World Health Organization's INSPIRE framework — developed by WHO, UNICEF, and partner agencies — provides an evidence-based package of seven strategies for reducing violence against children and youth, each supported by rigorous evaluation evidence. The strategies are: Implementation and enforcement of laws (including firearms legislation, alcohol regulation, and domestic violence legislation); Norms and values change (programmes that shift social norms around violence, gender, and masculinity); Safe environments (modifications to the physical environment that reduce opportunities for violence, including improved street lighting, urban design interventions, and alcohol outlet density regulation); Parent and caregiver support (parenting programmes that reduce harsh discipline and improve child-caregiver relationships); Income and economic strengthening (programmes that reduce economic stress, which is a driver of both violence perpetration and victimisation); Response and support services (including health and social services for victims); and Education and life skills (school-based programmes that teach conflict resolution, emotional regulation, and healthy relationship skills).

Several of these strategies have been tested and found effective in South African contexts. The Stepping Stones programme — a participatory group-based intervention that addresses gender norms, communication, and relationship skills — was evaluated in a randomised controlled trial in the Eastern Cape and found to reduce men's perpetration of intimate partner violence by approximately thirty-eight percent over two years. The Isibindi programme — a community-based child and youth care worker intervention — has demonstrated improvements in child wellbeing indicators in violence-affected communities. Alcohol regulation interventions — including restrictions on trading hours and alcohol outlet density — have been associated with reductions in violence in multiple South African studies, including natural experiments during the COVID-19 alcohol bans, which, whatever their other merits and demerits, produced measurable reductions in trauma unit admissions.

The "violence interruption" model — which deploys trained community members (often former offenders) to identify and mediate conflicts before they escalate to physical violence — has shown promising results in high-violence urban settings internationally (the Cure Violence model in Chicago, the Ceasefire model in various US cities) and is being piloted in South African settings including parts of the Cape Flats and Gauteng townships. Early results suggest significant reductions in violence in targeted areas, though the evidence base in South African contexts is still developing.

Road traffic violence — the third major component of the violence tax — is addressed by a different set of evidence-based interventions: enforcement of speed limits and drink-driving legislation, investment in pedestrian infrastructure (sidewalks, pedestrian crossings, speed bumps in residential areas), vehicle safety standards, and post-crash emergency care improvement. South Africa's Road to Safety Strategy 2030, which targets a fifty percent reduction in road fatalities, identifies many of the correct interventions but has struggled with implementation, as road safety involves multiple government departments (transport, health, police, public works) and requires coordination across spheres of government.

The common thread across all of these interventions is that they are preventive rather than reactive. They aim to reduce the incidence of violence rather than merely treating its consequences. This is the fundamental shift that the "violence as public health crisis" framing demands: from a model in which the criminal justice system responds after violence has occurred and the health system treats the resulting injuries, to a model in which both systems invest upstream in preventing violence from occurring in the first place.

## The Connection to the Life Expectancy Deficit

This article has demonstrated that violence is not a side issue for the human development agenda. It is a central driver of the life expectancy deficit, a massive fiscal drain on the health system, and a structural impediment to the health improvements — treatment cascade closure, TB control, NCD management — that the previous article identified as the highest-return investments in South Africa's HDI.

The connection between the safety and security pillar and the human development pillar is not metaphorical. It is arithmetic. Three to four years of life expectancy lost to violence. R30 billion to R50 billion in direct health costs. Eighty percent of surgical time in major trauma centres devoted to violence injuries. Seventy-five percent of mental health conditions untreated. A reinforcing cycle that connects violence to health system degradation to worse health outcomes to lower productivity to less fiscal space to less prevention to more violence.

Breaking this cycle requires treating violence as what the evidence says it is: a preventable public health crisis, amenable to evidence-based interventions, and inseparable from the health, economic, and human development outcomes that determine South Africa's future.

The next article in this series, [The Fiscal Arithmetic of Health](/articles/hd-2-3-the-fiscal-arithmetic-of-health), examines the economic constraints that determine how much South Africa can actually spend on health — and why economic growth is not a separate agenda from the health agenda but its fiscal prerequisite. The violence burden documented here makes that fiscal arithmetic even more pressing: every rand spent treating the consequences of violence is a rand unavailable for the preventive and curative care that could close the life expectancy deficit.

:::takeaway
Violence is a central driver of South Africa's human development deficit, not a peripheral concern. The three-to-four-year "violence tax" on life expectancy, the fiscal drain on the health system, and the reinforcing cycle between violence, health degradation, and economic stagnation mean that the HDI cannot be meaningfully improved without addressing violence as a public health crisis. The evidence base for violence prevention — from the WHO INSPIRE framework to South African-tested interventions like Stepping Stones and alcohol regulation — provides a roadmap. The question is whether South Africa will treat violence as what the data unambiguously shows it to be: a preventable epidemic, not an immutable feature of the social landscape.
:::

:::framework
**The Violence-Health Impact Assessment**

Use this framework to assess the health burden of violence in your community and to evaluate whether government is treating violence as the public health crisis the evidence shows it to be.

**Step 1: Map the violence burden.**
Source: SAPS crime statistics (station-level data, published annually); Stats SA Victims of Crime Survey.
Key questions: What is the homicide rate in your police precinct? How does it compare to the national average (45 per 100,000) and the global average (6 per 100,000)? What proportion of crimes are contact crimes (assault, sexual offences, robbery) versus property crimes? Is the trend improving or deteriorating?

**Step 2: Assess the health system impact.**
Source: District Health Barometer; hospital trauma unit reports (request through PAIA if not published).
Key questions: What proportion of surgical capacity at your nearest hospital is consumed by trauma cases? Are there dedicated GBV service points (Thuthuzela Care Centres) accessible from your area? What is the average response time for emergency medical services?

**Step 3: Check for prevention programmes.**
Source: Municipal Integrated Development Plans; SAPS Community Policing Forum minutes; Department of Social Development programme reports.
Key questions: Are any evidence-based violence prevention programmes operating in your area — community-based violence interruption, school safety programmes, Stepping Stones-type gender norms interventions, or alcohol regulation enforcement? If not, why not?

**Step 4: Evaluate the policy response.**
Key questions: Does your municipality's IDP identify violence as a public health issue, or only as a policing issue? Is the health budget allocated in proportion to the violence burden — are trauma-heavy facilities receiving additional resources? Are prevention budgets growing, or is all spending reactive?

**Step 5: Connect to the reinforcing cycle.**
Key questions: Can you trace the pathway from violence in your area to health system strain, to reduced economic activity, to lower municipal revenue, to poorer services, to more violence? Mapping this cycle in concrete, local terms makes the abstract framework real — and identifies the specific intervention points where breaking the cycle is most feasible.
:::
