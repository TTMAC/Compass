## The Economy Was Built This Way on Purpose

**South Africa's economy was not shaped by bad luck or inevitable market forces.** It was *deliberately structured for extraction* — first by colonial powers who wanted minerals and agricultural commodities shipped to Europe, then by an apartheid state that built a distorted industrial economy serving a minority market while suppressing black wages to subsidise white industry. Post-1994, policy choices intended to modernise the economy — GEAR, ASGISA, the NDP — accelerated deindustrialisation rather than redirecting the underlying structure. The result is the R7,000 economy described in [The R7,000 Economy](/articles/eg-1-1-the-seven-thousand-rand-economy): a middle-income country with upper-income infrastructure for some, low-income outcomes for most, and an unemployment rate that would constitute a national emergency in any other democracy on earth. Understanding how we got here is not an exercise in historical grievance. It is a prerequisite for understanding why certain reforms are structurally necessary and why others are cosmetic.

If you read the previous article in this series, you will have a sense of where South Africa stands today in comparative terms — a country with roughly the same GDP per capita as Thailand or Colombia, but with an economic complexity profile that suggests it is less diversified than either. This article explains why. The structure of the South African economy is not a natural phenomenon. It is the accumulated product of specific decisions made by specific people at specific moments, from the discovery of diamonds in 1867 through to the adoption of the National Development Plan in 2012. Each decision constrained the decisions that followed. Each policy path closed off alternatives. And each era left structural features embedded in the economy that persist today, long after the political systems that created them have been dismantled.

This is the story of those decisions and their consequences.

## The Colonial Extractive Economy: Minerals, Labour, and the Architecture of Dependence

Before diamonds were discovered near Kimberley in 1867, the southern African economy was predominantly agrarian. The Cape Colony and Natal exported wool, wine, and sugar. The Boer republics were pastoral economies with limited connection to global markets. Indigenous economies were based on cattle, agriculture, and regional trade networks. None of this was destined to produce what South Africa became.

The discovery of diamonds, and then gold on the Witwatersrand in 1886, transformed everything. Within two decades, southern Africa went from an economic backwater of the British Empire to its most strategically important territory. Gold from the Witwatersrand became the foundation of the international gold standard — the monetary system that underpinned global trade until 1971. By 1900, South Africa was producing roughly a quarter of the world's gold output. The mineral discoveries attracted massive inflows of British capital, created Johannesburg from nothing, triggered the South African War of 1899–1902, and ultimately drove the political unification of the four colonies into the Union of South Africa in 1910.

What matters for understanding today's economy is not the minerals themselves but the economic system built around them. The mining industry that emerged on the Witwatersrand was not a competitive market of independent operators. It was a cartel. The major mining houses — initially dominated by figures like Cecil Rhodes and later consolidated into giants like Anglo American, De Beers, Gold Fields, and Gencor — operated through interlocking directorates, shared infrastructure, and coordinated labour recruitment. They fixed wages collectively through the Chamber of Mines. They shared recruitment networks through the Witwatersrand Native Labour Association (WNLA) and the Native Recruiting Corporation (NRC), which recruited black workers from across southern Africa on fixed-term contracts at suppressed wages.

This was not a natural market outcome. It was deliberate policy. The mining houses needed cheap, abundant, controlled labour to extract gold profitably from the deep-level reefs of the Witwatersrand, where ore grades were relatively low and mining costs were high. The gold price was fixed internationally, so the mines could not raise the price of their product. The only variable they could control was the cost of labour. **And so the entire political economy of South Africa was organised around one objective: delivering cheap black labour to the mines.**

The 1913 Natives Land Act was the most consequential instrument in this system. By restricting African land ownership to roughly 7 per cent of the country's territory (later expanded to about 13 per cent under the 1936 Native Trust and Land Act), the law systematically destroyed the capacity of African communities to sustain themselves through agriculture. This was not an unintended side effect. The Beaumont Commission and the Lagden Commission before it had explicitly identified the "problem" that too many Africans were able to support themselves on their own land and were therefore unwilling to work on the mines and white-owned farms at the wages on offer. The Land Act solved this "problem" by making subsistence agriculture impossible for the majority, forcing millions into wage labour.

:::takeaway
The 1913 Natives Land Act was not primarily about land. It was an instrument of labour market policy. By restricting African land ownership to 7 per cent of South Africa's territory, the Act destroyed the capacity of African communities to sustain themselves through agriculture, forcing millions into the migrant labour system that the mining industry depended on. The economic structure created by this single piece of legislation — concentrated mineral wealth extracted by cheap, controlled black labour — defined the South African economy for the next century and continues to shape it today.
:::

The colour bar completed the system. A series of laws — beginning with the Mines and Works Act of 1911 and formalised by the "civilised labour" policies of the Pact government after 1924 — reserved skilled and semi-skilled jobs in mining (and later manufacturing) for white workers. Black workers were confined to unskilled manual labour, regardless of their capabilities. The colour bar served a dual purpose: it protected white workers from competition (ensuring their political support for the system) and it kept the cost of the much larger black workforce artificially low by preventing upward mobility.

The result was what economic historians call the *minerals-energy complex* (MEC) — a term coined by researchers at the School of Oriental and African Studies to describe the symbiotic relationship between mining, cheap coal-fired electricity generation (through Eskom, established as the Electricity Supply Commission in 1923), and basic metals processing. Gold mining required electricity. Eskom provided it at below-cost rates using cheap coal from the same geological formations that produced gold. The cheap electricity then attracted energy-intensive industries — steel, aluminium smelting, chemicals — that processed the country's mineral wealth into intermediate products for export. This was an economy built on moving raw materials out of the ground, through a minimal processing chain, and onto ships bound for Europe.

The MEC was extraordinarily efficient at what it was designed to do: extract value from South Africa's mineral endowment for the benefit of mining capital and the white minority that serviced it. But it had structural features that would prove disastrous in the long run. It was capital-intensive rather than labour-intensive — the mines employed large numbers of workers, but the downstream processing industries did not. It was concentrated in a narrow range of commodities and products, making the economy vulnerable to commodity price cycles. It required the systematic suppression of black wages and skills, which meant that the majority of the population was never developed as a consumer market. And it created an industrial structure oriented toward exporting raw and semi-processed materials rather than manufactured goods — the opposite of the path followed by the East Asian economies that would later achieve rapid, broad-based growth.

:::expert
Research by the Trade and Industrial Policy Strategies (TIPS) institute — including its *Real Economy Bulletin* quarterly series and sustained working-paper output on deindustrialisation from the mid-2010s onward — has documented how the minerals-energy complex created structural path dependencies that persisted long after the political conditions that gave rise to it had changed. TIPS's analysis of South Africa's industrial structure has shown that the MEC locked the economy into a pattern of resource dependence that was self-reinforcing: the infrastructure (rail, ports, electricity generation) was built to serve the mining sector; the financial system was oriented toward funding mining and related heavy industry; the skills system produced engineers and metallurgists rather than the diverse technical and entrepreneurial skills needed for a broad-based industrial economy. When the democratic government attempted to diversify the economy after 1994, it found itself working against an entire infrastructure, financial, and institutional architecture that had been optimised for extraction over more than a century.
:::

By the time of Union in 1910, the fundamental economic architecture was in place: an extractive economy based on mineral wealth, powered by cheap energy, staffed by cheap controlled black labour, managed by a white minority, and oriented toward export markets in Europe. Everything that followed — including apartheid's industrial policy and the post-1994 reform attempts — was built on this foundation.

## Apartheid's Economic Distortions: Industrialisation for the Few

The National Party's election victory in 1948 did not create South Africa's extractive economy. It inherited the colonial system and added new layers of distortion that compounded the structural problems.

Apartheid's economic project had several distinct components, and understanding each one is important because their legacies manifest differently in today's economy. The first was import-substitution industrialisation — the deliberate creation of a domestic manufacturing sector behind high tariff walls. The second was the development of strategic industries for military and political self-sufficiency. The third was the systematic destruction of black human capital through Bantu Education. And the fourth was the forced development of alternative technologies in response to international sanctions.

Import-substitution industrialisation was not unique to South Africa. Many developing countries pursued similar strategies in the 1950s and 1960s, influenced by the prevailing development economics of the time. But South Africa's version had a distinctive and ultimately fatal flaw: it built a manufacturing sector sized for the white market only. The logic was straightforward, if perverse. Apartheid systematically excluded black South Africans from the formal consumer economy. Black wages were suppressed. Black urbanisation was controlled and restricted. Black South Africans were confined to purchasing basic necessities, while the white population — roughly 15 to 20 per cent of the total — had purchasing power comparable to a middle-income European country. The manufacturing sector that grew behind South Africa's tariff walls was designed to serve this small, wealthy market.

This produced factories that were capital-intensive (because labour was cheap but white consumers expected high-quality goods), operated at low volumes (because the market was small), and were uncompetitive by international standards (because they had never faced real competition). South Africa developed automotive assembly plants, textile factories, consumer electronics production, and a range of light and heavy manufacturing — but all of it was oriented toward a domestic market of four to five million white consumers rather than an export market or a mass domestic market of thirty million.

The strategic industries programme added another layer of distortion. Facing the prospect (and later the reality) of international arms embargoes, the apartheid government created Armscor in 1968 as a state-owned arms procurement and manufacturing agency. Armscor and its subsidiaries (later reorganised as Denel) built South Africa into a significant arms producer and exporter, developing indigenous capacity in artillery (the G5 and G6 howitzer systems), armoured vehicles (the Ratel and Rooikat), missile systems, and military electronics. The arms industry employed tens of thousands of skilled engineers and technicians and developed genuine technological capabilities — but it was oriented entirely toward military production. Unlike in countries such as South Korea, where defence industrialisation was deliberately linked to civilian industrial development (Samsung and Hyundai both have roots in defence contracting), South Africa's military-industrial complex remained largely isolated from the civilian economy. The skills and technologies developed by Armscor and Denel were not systematically transferred to civilian manufacturing.

Sasol represents perhaps the most dramatic example of sanctions-era forced industrialisation. South Africa had abundant coal but no domestic petroleum. When international sanctions began to restrict oil imports in the 1970s, the government invested massively in Sasol's coal-to-liquids (CTL) technology — the Fischer-Tropsch process originally developed in Germany in the 1920s. Sasol II and Sasol III, built in Secunda in the 1970s and 1980s, were among the largest industrial construction projects in the world at the time. They gave South Africa the capacity to produce roughly 30 per cent of its liquid fuel needs from coal — a genuine technological achievement, but one driven by political necessity rather than economic efficiency. The Sasol plants were enormously energy-intensive and contributed significantly to South Africa's carbon emissions profile, which remains one of the highest per capita in the developing world.

But of all apartheid's economic distortions, the one with the most devastating and lasting consequences was the deliberate destruction of black human capital. The Bantu Education Act of 1953 removed black education from the control of missionary societies (which had provided, however unevenly, a liberal education curriculum) and placed it under a government department explicitly tasked with providing an inferior education designed to prepare black South Africans for manual labour. Hendrik Verwoerd, then Minister of Native Affairs, was entirely transparent about the purpose: "There is no place for [the Bantu] in the European community above the level of certain forms of labour."

The numbers tell the story. **By the 1970s, per-pupil spending on white education was roughly ten times that on black education.** White schools had qualified teachers, science laboratories, libraries, and sports facilities. Black schools were overcrowded, under-resourced, and staffed by teachers who had themselves been products of an inferior education system. The curriculum for black schools deliberately excluded mathematics and science at higher levels, creating a systematic skills deficit that persists to this day. When the democratic government inherited the education system in 1994, it inherited a population in which the vast majority of working-age adults had received an education designed to produce unskilled labourers, not skilled workers, entrepreneurs, or professionals.

:::expert
The Southern Africa Labour and Development Research Unit (SALDRU) at the University of Cape Town has produced extensive research quantifying the economic cost of Bantu Education. Their analysis of the National Income Dynamics Study (NIDS) data has shown that the educational deficits created by Bantu Education are intergenerational — they did not end when the policy was abolished. Adults who received Bantu Education went on to raise children in households with lower literacy, fewer books, less exposure to numeracy, and weaker educational expectations. SALDRU's research has demonstrated that even controlling for household income and school quality, the children and grandchildren of Bantu Education recipients have measurably lower educational outcomes than comparable populations in other middle-income countries. The economic cost of Bantu Education is not a historical curiosity. It is a present-day structural constraint on productivity, earnings, and economic growth. SALDRU's estimate suggests that if South Africa's human capital profile matched that of countries at similar income levels, GDP per capita would be significantly higher — a gap attributable directly to the multi-generational damage of deliberately inferior education.
:::

The apartheid economy, then, was a peculiar hybrid. It was simultaneously industrialised and extractive. It had first-world infrastructure — world-class highways, a modern telecommunications network, nuclear power, advanced financial markets — but these served a minority population while the majority was deliberately excluded. It had a manufacturing sector, but one designed for a small domestic market and uncompetitive internationally. It had genuine technological capabilities in mining, energy, military hardware, and financial services, but a workforce in which the vast majority had been systematically denied education and skills development.

When you hear commentators describe the South African economy as "dual" — with a modern, productive formal sector existing alongside a vast informal and unemployed population — they are describing a feature, not a bug, of the system that apartheid built. The economy was designed to function with a small, skilled, well-paid white workforce and a large, unskilled, cheap black workforce. It was never designed for mass employment, mass consumption, or broad-based prosperity.

## Post-1994: The Policy Trajectory That Accelerated Deindustrialisation

The democratic government that took power in 1994 inherited an economy that was, in many respects, already in crisis. GDP growth had averaged less than 1 per cent per year during the 1980s. International sanctions, capital flight, and political instability had starved the economy of investment. Inflation was running above 10 per cent. The fiscal deficit was large and growing. And the accumulated costs of apartheid — the infrastructure backlogs in black communities, the education deficit, the healthcare inequities — represented an enormous claim on future government spending.

The ANC's initial economic framework was the Reconstruction and Development Programme (RDP), adopted in 1994. The RDP was a broad social-democratic programme that prioritised meeting basic needs — housing, water, electricity, healthcare, education — while restructuring the economy for greater equity and participation. It envisaged an active role for the state in economic development, including public investment in infrastructure, redistribution of land and other assets, and the development of small and medium enterprises.

The RDP was never fully implemented as an economic strategy. Within two years, the government had adopted the Growth, Employment and Redistribution (GEAR) strategy, announced in June 1996 by then-Finance Minister Trevor Manuel. GEAR marked a decisive shift in economic policy orientation. It was, in essence, a macroeconomic stabilisation programme built on fiscal discipline, trade liberalisation, inflation targeting, privatisation (or "restructuring") of state-owned enterprises, and labour market flexibility. GEAR was presented as non-negotiable — Manuel famously declared it was "not up for discussion" — and it was adopted without the broad consultation that the ANC's alliance partners expected.

The context for GEAR matters. In early 1996, the rand had crashed by roughly 25 per cent against the dollar in a matter of weeks, triggered by capital flight after the departure of Chris Liebenberg as Finance Minister and broader concerns about the ANC's economic intentions. The GEAR strategy was, in significant part, a signal to international financial markets that South Africa would pursue orthodox macroeconomic policy. And in narrow macroeconomic terms, GEAR delivered results. The fiscal deficit was brought under control. Inflation was reduced. Government debt as a share of GDP stabilised and then declined. The bond market stabilised. Foreign direct investment increased, though not at the levels GEAR had projected.

But GEAR's trade liberalisation component had consequences that its architects either did not anticipate or considered acceptable. **South Africa reduced tariffs faster and further than its World Trade Organisation commitments required.** The average effective tariff rate fell from roughly 28 per cent in 1994 to below 10 per cent by the early 2000s. Industries that had been protected behind apartheid-era tariff walls were suddenly exposed to competition from manufacturers in China, India, and Southeast Asia who had lower labour costs, larger domestic markets, and (in many cases) more supportive industrial policies.

The result was deindustrialisation. **Manufacturing's share of GDP, which had been roughly 21 per cent in 1994, began a decline that has continued almost without interruption to the present day.** By 2023, manufacturing accounted for approximately 12 per cent of GDP (Stats SA GDP series, 2023 Q4) — **a loss of nearly half its relative weight in the economy over three decades.** The textile industry, which had employed hundreds of thousands of workers (many of them women), was devastated by cheap imports from China. The footwear industry collapsed almost entirely. Automotive component manufacturing survived only because of the Motor Industry Development Programme (MIDP) and its successor, the Automotive Production and Development Programme (APDP), which provided specific incentives for vehicle assembly and component production. Electronics manufacturing, consumer goods production, and light industrial manufacturing all contracted.

:::takeaway
South Africa's manufacturing sector shrank from roughly 21 per cent of GDP in 1994 to approximately 12 per cent by the mid-2020s. This deindustrialisation was driven by trade liberalisation that exposed uncompetitive, apartheid-era industries to global competition, combined with a failure to develop new labour-intensive export industries to replace the jobs lost. The pattern is the opposite of what successful developing economies like South Korea, Vietnam, and Bangladesh achieved — they industrialised by building labour-intensive export manufacturing; South Africa deindustrialised by dismantling its protected manufacturing base without building an alternative.
:::

GEAR's projected employment gains — **the strategy had promised 400,000 new jobs per year by 2000 — never materialised.** Instead, the economy shed manufacturing jobs while creating employment mainly in services (finance, retail, public administration) that required higher skill levels than the apartheid education system had produced in the majority population. The structural mismatch between the skills the economy demanded and the skills the education system had produced became the defining feature of post-apartheid unemployment.

The government's next attempt at an economic framework was the Accelerated and Shared Growth Initiative for South Africa (ASGISA), announced in 2006 under Deputy President Phumzile Mlambo-Ngcuka. ASGISA was more ambitious than GEAR in its growth targets — it aimed for 6 per cent GDP growth by 2010 and an unemployment rate halved by 2014 — but it was built on a different theory of change. Where GEAR had relied primarily on macroeconomic stabilisation and market liberalisation to drive growth, ASGISA identified six "binding constraints" on growth: the volatility and level of the currency, the cost and efficiency of the national logistics system, skills shortages, barriers to entry for small businesses, the regulatory environment, and deficiencies in state capacity.

ASGISA proposed addressing these constraints through large-scale public infrastructure investment (the forerunner of the infrastructure programmes later associated with the 2010 World Cup), sector strategies for key industries (business process outsourcing, tourism, biofuels, and others), and skills development initiatives. It was a more interventionist approach than GEAR, acknowledging that the market alone would not produce the structural transformation the economy needed.

But ASGISA ran headlong into reality. The global financial crisis of 2008–2009 hit South Africa hard — GDP contracted by 1.5 per cent in 2009, the first recession since 1992. More than a million jobs were lost, and the economy never fully recovered its pre-crisis growth trajectory. The infrastructure investment programme was partially delivered (new stadia, the Gautrain, road upgrades) but at costs that significantly exceeded original estimates and with limited spillover into broader industrial development. The sector strategies produced some successes — the business process outsourcing sector grew substantially, particularly in the Western Cape — but not at the scale needed to replace lost manufacturing employment.

Then came the National Development Plan (NDP), produced by the National Planning Commission chaired by former Finance Minister Trevor Manuel, and adopted by the Cabinet in 2012. The NDP was the most comprehensive economic vision yet attempted — a 500-page document that set out a detailed programme for reaching "full employment" and reducing inequality by 2030. It called for 11 million new jobs by 2030, expansion of the infrastructure network, radical improvement of education quality, a capable and developmental state, reduction of corruption, and targeted support for labour-intensive sectors.

The NDP was, on paper, a thoughtful and well-researched document. It correctly identified the key structural challenges — unemployment, inequality, education quality, state capacity, infrastructure — and proposed plausible policy responses. But the pattern that had characterised South African economic policy since 1994 repeated itself with the NDP: the plan was more ambitious than its predecessors, but less effective in execution. The NDP was adopted as government policy, but implementation was never systematically monitored or enforced. No department was given authority to coordinate implementation across government. No consequence was attached to failing to achieve NDP targets. **By the time the 2030 deadline approached, virtually none of the NDP's key targets had been met.**

The pattern across these three major policy frameworks — GEAR, ASGISA, NDP — is revealing. Each was more comprehensive than the last. Each correctly diagnosed many of the economy's structural problems. And each failed to produce the structural transformation it promised. The reasons for this failure are multiple — the global economic environment, the energy crisis that began with Eskom's load-shedding in 2008 and intensified dramatically after 2019, the state capture of the Zuma era (documented in [From Rainbow Nation to State Capture](/articles/ss-1-2-from-rainbow-nation-to-state-capture)), and the inherent difficulty of transforming an economy that was structurally designed for extraction and minority benefit.

But the most fundamental reason is that none of these frameworks adequately confronted the depth of the structural inheritance. GEAR assumed that macroeconomic stability and trade liberalisation would attract investment and generate growth. ASGISA assumed that removing "binding constraints" would unleash latent economic potential. The NDP assumed that a comprehensive plan, if implemented, would produce transformation. None of them grappled adequately with the fact that the South African economy was not a normal developing economy that needed better policies. It was an economy whose fundamental architecture — its industrial structure, its skills base, its infrastructure orientation, its patterns of ownership, its spatial geography — had been shaped by 150 years of extraction and racial exclusion, and that transforming this architecture required something more radical than better planning.

## Structural Unemployment: The Predictable Consequence of an Economy Never Designed for Mass Employment

If you have read [The R7,000 Economy](/articles/eg-1-1-the-seven-thousand-rand-economy), you know that South Africa's official unemployment rate hovers around 32 to 33 per cent — and that the expanded definition, which includes discouraged work-seekers, pushes it above 40 per cent. Among young people aged 15 to 34, the expanded unemployment rate exceeds 60 per cent. These numbers are staggering by any international standard. They represent a level of labour market dysfunction that is unusual even among countries at similar income levels.

Understanding how we got here requires recognising a simple but profound fact: **the South African economy was never designed to employ the majority of the population.**

The colonial economy needed cheap migrant labour for the mines and farms. It needed a limited number of workers — at the peak, the gold mines employed roughly 500,000 workers, and white agriculture perhaps another 1.5 million — drawn from a population that, by the mid-twentieth century, numbered over 20 million. The system required that the majority of black South Africans remain in the reserves (later homelands), surviving on subsistence agriculture supplemented by remittances from migrant workers. The economy did not need them all to work. It needed a controlled supply of cheap labour, and it needed the rest to remain in the reserves, out of sight and out of the urban areas.

Apartheid's industrial economy added manufacturing jobs, but as we have seen, this was manufacturing for a small white market, not mass-employment manufacturing. The capital-intensive nature of apartheid-era industry — heavy chemicals, metals processing, vehicle assembly, armaments — meant that the manufacturing sector employed far fewer workers per unit of output than the labour-intensive manufacturing that drove employment in East Asia. South Korea, Taiwan, and later China, Vietnam, and Bangladesh built their industrial transitions on labour-intensive export manufacturing — clothing, textiles, shoes, toys, electronics assembly — that absorbed millions of low-skilled workers into the formal economy. South Africa never developed this kind of manufacturing because the apartheid economy did not need it. The mines provided the foreign exchange. The protected manufacturing sector served the white market. And the black majority was not conceived of as a workforce to be fully employed but as a labour reserve from which workers were drawn as needed.

When apartheid ended and the economy was liberalised, *the structural mismatch became catastrophic.* The mines were already in long-term employment decline — mechanisation, exhaustion of the richest gold reefs, and declining commodity prices meant that **mining employment fell from over 750,000 in the 1980s to below 500,000 by the mid-2000s.** The protected manufacturing sector, exposed to international competition, shed hundreds of thousands of jobs. Agriculture, undergoing its own mechanisation and consolidation, reduced its workforce from roughly 1.6 million in the early 1990s to below 900,000 by the 2010s.

The sectors that grew — financial services, telecommunications, retail, public administration — required higher skill levels than the majority of the workforce possessed. The economy was creating jobs, but predominantly in the upper half of the skills distribution, while the working-age population that needed employment was concentrated in the lower half. This was not a temporary adjustment problem. It was a structural feature of an economy that had spent 150 years developing its capital-intensive, skills-intensive sectors while deliberately underdeveloping the human capital of the majority.

:::expert
The Research Project on Employment, Income Distribution and Inclusive Growth (REDI3x3), a collaboration between the University of Cape Town, Stellenbosch University, and the University of the Witwatersrand that ran from approximately 2012 to 2019 and whose working-paper archive remains available online, produced some of the most detailed research on the structural nature of South African unemployment. Their findings paint a picture of a labour market with a fundamental structural mismatch. REDI3x3's analysis has shown that South Africa's unemployment is not primarily cyclical (caused by economic downturns) or frictional (caused by workers transitioning between jobs). It is structural: the economy does not generate enough demand for low-skilled and semi-skilled labour to employ the available workforce. This structural deficit has deep historical roots — in the mining-oriented industrial structure, in the apartheid education system's failure to produce a skilled workforce, in the spatial geography that locates the majority of the unemployed far from economic opportunities, and in the capital-intensive development path that the economy followed. REDI3x3's research suggests that addressing structural unemployment requires not merely faster economic growth but a fundamental shift in the composition of growth — toward labour-intensive sectors, toward small and medium enterprises, and toward economic activity in the areas where the unemployed actually live.
:::

The spatial dimension of unemployment deserves particular attention because it is one of the most direct legacies of apartheid planning. The Group Areas Act and the homeland system created a spatial geography in which the majority of black South Africans lived far from economic opportunities — in townships on the urban periphery, in rural homelands, in dormitory settlements designed to house migrant workers at a distance from their workplaces. This spatial mismatch was deliberate: apartheid did not want black South Africans living near their workplaces because that implied permanent urbanisation, which contradicted the fiction that black South Africans were temporary migrants whose real homes were in the homelands.

The post-1994 government built millions of houses through the RDP housing programme, but it built them in the same locations — on cheap land at the urban periphery, far from employment centres. The spatial inheritance of apartheid was not dismantled; it was consolidated. Today, a young person living in Khayelitsha must spend two to three hours and a significant portion of their potential earnings on transport just to reach the Cape Town central business district. A resident of Soweto faces similar costs and time to reach Johannesburg's economic core. A person in a former homeland area like the former Transkei has effectively no access to formal employment without migrating.

The cost of transport, the time spent commuting, and the sheer distance between where the unemployed live and where jobs exist all act as structural barriers to employment that no amount of macroeconomic policy adjustment can overcome. They are physical features of the built environment, created by deliberate policy over many decades, that require massive spatial restructuring — public transport investment, mixed-income housing in well-located areas, economic decentralisation — to address.

## The Fiscal Structure You Inherited

The economic history described above also explains the fiscal structure analysed in [Following the Money](/articles/2-1-following-the-money). South Africa's tax base reflects the economy that colonial and apartheid policy created: concentrated in a narrow formal sector (mining, finance, manufacturing, retail) that employs a minority of the population, while the majority participate in the economy informally or not at all. When people point out that a very small number of individual taxpayers and corporations generate the majority of South Africa's tax revenue, they are describing the fiscal consequence of an economy that was designed to concentrate productive activity in a narrow segment of the population.

This narrow tax base places an enormous burden on the formal sector and constrains the government's capacity to fund the transformation programmes that the economy needs. It also makes the fiscal system vulnerable to economic shocks — when commodity prices fall, or when the formal sector contracts, government revenue drops disproportionately because there is no broad base of taxpayers to absorb the impact. The fiscal structure is not a policy choice that the democratic government made. It is an inheritance from an economy designed to concentrate wealth in a small formal sector while excluding the majority from productive economic participation.

## The Energy Trap

No account of how we got here is complete without addressing the energy dimension. The minerals-energy complex described earlier was built on cheap, abundant coal-fired electricity. Eskom was established in 1923 with a mandate to provide cheap electricity to the mining industry and, later, to the broader industrial sector. For decades, it delivered on that mandate. South Africa had some of the cheapest electricity in the world — a competitive advantage that attracted energy-intensive industries like aluminium smelting (through BHP Billiton's Hillside and Mozal smelters) and ferrochrome production.

But the cheap electricity model contained its own destruction. Eskom's coal fleet was built in waves — a massive construction programme in the 1960s and 1970s produced stations like Kendal, Matimba, and Lethabo that gave the country surplus capacity. That surplus led to a decision in the 1990s to halt new capacity investment on the assumption that the private sector would eventually build new generation capacity. The decision was wrong. The private investment did not materialise, demand continued to grow, and by 2008 the surplus had become a deficit. Load-shedding began in January 2008 and has recurred with increasing severity, reaching crisis levels with over 200 days of load-shedding in 2023.

The energy crisis is not merely a management failure (though management failures at Eskom have been extensively documented, particularly during the state capture era). It is a structural consequence of an economic model built on cheap coal power. The same minerals-energy complex that created the economy's industrial base also locked it into a pattern of energy-intensive, carbon-intensive production that is now colliding with the realities of climate change, ageing infrastructure, and the global energy transition. **South Africa's carbon emissions per unit of GDP are among the highest in the world** — a direct legacy of the MEC — and the country faces the challenge of transitioning to cleaner energy while its existing coal fleet deteriorates and demand continues to grow.

## What the Comparator Countries Did Differently

The next article in this series, [The Comparator Countries](/articles/eg-1-3-the-comparator-countries), will examine in detail how countries at similar income levels followed different paths. But it is worth previewing the comparison here because it sharpens the question of why South Africa's economic trajectory was not inevitable.

South Korea in 1960 had a GDP per capita roughly equal to that of many African countries. It had few natural resources, a devastated infrastructure after the Korean War, and an agrarian economy with limited industrial capacity. Within four decades, it became one of the world's leading industrial economies. How? Through deliberate, state-directed industrial policy that channelled investment into labour-intensive export manufacturing (textiles and wigs in the 1960s, electronics and ships in the 1970s, semiconductors and automobiles in the 1980s), combined with massive investment in education at all levels and systematic absorption of technology from more advanced economies.

South Africa had advantages that South Korea did not: abundant natural resources, an established (if distorted) industrial base, sophisticated financial markets, and world-class infrastructure in parts of the country. But it lacked the one thing that the East Asian economies had: an economic structure designed for mass employment and broad-based growth. The East Asian model was built on labour-intensive manufacturing that absorbed surplus agricultural labour into the formal industrial economy. South Africa's model was built on capital-intensive extraction that required a limited workforce and excluded the majority.

This comparison is not made to suggest that South Africa should have simply copied the East Asian model. The historical circumstances were profoundly different. But it illustrates that the path South Africa followed — from colonial extraction through apartheid industrialisation to post-1994 deindustrialisation — was not the only possible path. It was the product of specific choices made at specific moments, and understanding those choices is the first step toward understanding what different choices might achieve.

## Practical Framework: The Policy History Timeline

One of the most useful things you can develop as a citizen trying to understand South Africa's economy is the ability to trace any current economic condition back to its policy origins. This is not an academic exercise. It is a practical tool for distinguishing between two fundamentally different types of economic problems — and the distinction matters because the solutions are completely different.

The first type is inherited structural features. These are characteristics of the economy that were created by colonial and apartheid policy, are embedded in the physical infrastructure, spatial geography, industrial structure, and skills profile of the country, and cannot be fixed by better management or incremental policy adjustment. They require deep, long-term structural reform — changes to the built environment, the education system, the industrial structure, and the patterns of economic ownership that take decades to achieve.

The second type is recent policy choices. These are outcomes that result from decisions made by the democratic government — choices about trade policy, industrial policy, energy policy, fiscal policy, and institutional governance that could, in principle, be reversed or modified through different policy choices. They require course correction — different decisions by the same institutions, using the same constitutional and legal frameworks.

Here is how to use the timeline. When you encounter an economic condition — say, the high unemployment rate, or the energy crisis, or the decline of manufacturing, or the concentration of economic ownership — trace it backward through the policy history to identify its origins. Ask three questions.

First, when did this condition originate? If it has roots in the colonial or apartheid era, it is likely an inherited structural feature. If it emerged primarily after 1994, it is more likely the result of democratic-era policy choices (though it may still be influenced by inherited structures).

Second, what specific policy decisions created or contributed to this condition? You are looking for identifiable moments when decisions were made that shaped the outcome. For unemployment, the key decisions include the 1913 Land Act (which created the migrant labour system), the colour bar (which excluded the majority from skilled work), Bantu Education (which destroyed black human capital), GEAR's trade liberalisation (which exposed uncompetitive manufacturing to global competition), and the failure to develop labour-intensive export industries. For the energy crisis, the key decisions include the establishment of Eskom's coal-based generation model, the 1990s decision to halt new capacity investment, the failure to diversify the energy mix, and the state capture-era corruption at Eskom.

Third, is this condition primarily structural or primarily the result of recent choices? Most economic conditions in South Africa are a combination of both — inherited structures that constrain possibilities, combined with democratic-era choices that either reinforced or failed to address those structural constraints. The unemployment crisis, for example, is rooted in inherited structural features (the MEC, Bantu Education, the spatial geography of apartheid) but was significantly worsened by democratic-era policy choices (rapid trade liberalisation, failure to develop labour-intensive sectors, inadequate education reform).

This distinction matters because it shapes the realistic expectations you should have for policy reform. Inherited structural features cannot be fixed by firing a minister or changing a policy document. They require sustained investment, institutional reform, and multi-generational commitment. If someone tells you that unemployment can be solved by deregulating the labour market or lowering the minimum wage, they are probably underestimating the structural dimension of the problem. The labour market is one factor, but even a perfectly flexible labour market cannot overcome the fact that the majority of the workforce lacks the skills the economy demands, lives far from economic opportunities, and exists in an economy that was structurally designed to employ a minority.

Recent policy choices, by contrast, are potentially more amenable to correction. The energy crisis, while it has structural roots in the MEC, has been significantly worsened by specific democratic-era decisions — the failure to invest in new capacity, the corruption and mismanagement at Eskom during the state capture era, and the slow pace of renewable energy procurement. These are problems that better governance and different policy choices can address within a shorter timeframe.

To practice using this framework, consider five current economic conditions and trace each one through the policy history.

The decline of manufacturing traces back to apartheid-era import substitution (which created an uncompetitive manufacturing base sized for the white market), GEAR's trade liberalisation (which exposed that base to international competition), and the failure to develop an alternative industrial strategy for labour-intensive export manufacturing. It is a combination of inherited structure and democratic-era policy choice.

The skills deficit traces back primarily to Bantu Education (1953–1994), which systematically destroyed black human capital over four decades. Democratic-era education policy has improved access — virtually all children now attend school — but quality remains deeply unequal, and the intergenerational effects of Bantu Education continue to constrain the workforce. This is primarily an inherited structural feature, though democratic-era education policy has been inadequate in addressing it.

The spatial mismatch — the fact that the unemployed live far from economic opportunities — traces directly to the Group Areas Act, the homeland system, and the apartheid spatial geography. It has been consolidated rather than dismantled by democratic-era housing policy, which built RDP houses on the urban periphery. This is an inherited structural feature that democratic-era policy has failed to address and, in some respects, has reinforced.

The energy crisis traces to the MEC's reliance on cheap coal power, the 1990s decision to halt new capacity investment, and the corruption and mismanagement at Eskom during the state capture era. It is a combination of inherited structure (coal dependence) and recent policy failure (investment decisions, corruption, slow energy transition).

The concentration of economic ownership traces to the colonial-era mining cartels, apartheid-era state-owned enterprises, and the post-1994 patterns of black economic empowerment that concentrated new wealth in a small elite rather than broadening ownership. It is a combination of inherited structure and democratic-era policy design.

You will notice that most economic conditions in South Africa are not purely structural or purely the result of recent choices. They are layered — inherited structures that have been either reinforced or inadequately addressed by democratic-era policy. This complexity is the reality of governing an economy with South Africa's history, and it is why simple explanations and simple solutions are almost always inadequate.

The framework does not tell you what the right policy response is. That is the subject of the rest of this series — beginning with [The Comparator Countries](/articles/eg-1-3-the-comparator-countries) for international evidence, and then Part 2 (The Growth Strategy), which presents a staged pathway from $7,000 to $50,000 GDP per capita starting with [Resource Optimisation](/articles/eg-2-1-resource-optimisation). What it does is help you evaluate policy proposals by asking whether they address the structural dimension of a problem (which requires deep, sustained reform), the policy-choice dimension (which requires course correction), or both. A proposal that addresses only one dimension is likely to be insufficient. A proposal that pretends a structural problem can be fixed with a quick policy change is misleading. And a proposal that treats every problem as a structural inheritance, using history as an excuse for inaction, is equally misleading — because there are real policy choices within the constraints of the inherited structure that can make a meaningful difference.

:::framework
**The "Policy Origins" Decision Framework**

When you encounter an economic problem — unemployment, the energy crisis, manufacturing decline, concentrated ownership — use this four-step process to trace its origins, classify it correctly, and evaluate whether proposed solutions are adequate to the scale of the problem.

**Step 1: Trace the condition to its policy origins.**

Ask: when did this condition first emerge? Use the timeline in this article as your reference. If the condition has roots in the colonial period (pre-1910), the apartheid era (1948-1994), or the early democratic transition (1994-2008), it is likely an inherited structural feature embedded in the physical infrastructure, spatial geography, industrial structure, or skills profile of the country. If it emerged primarily after 2008, it is more likely the result of recent policy choices or institutional failures — though it may still interact with inherited structures.

**Step 2: Identify the specific decisions that created or deepened the condition.**

For each economic condition, identify the two or three most consequential policy decisions. For unemployment: the 1913 Land Act (migrant labour system), Bantu Education (skills destruction), and GEAR's trade liberalisation (manufacturing job losses). For the energy crisis: the 1990s decision to halt new capacity investment, and state capture-era corruption at Eskom. For deindustrialisation: apartheid-era import substitution (uncompetitive factories) and rapid post-1994 tariff reduction. Writing these decisions down forces clarity about causes and prevents vague attribution to "the economy" or "the government."

**Step 3: Classify the condition as structural, policy-driven, or both.**

Structural conditions — the spatial mismatch between where the unemployed live and where jobs exist, the skills deficit created by Bantu Education, the capital-intensive orientation of the minerals-energy complex — cannot be fixed by changing a single policy or firing a minister. They require sustained investment over decades. Policy-driven conditions — the pace of renewable energy procurement, the design of industrial incentives, the management of Eskom — can be corrected within a shorter timeframe through different decisions by existing institutions. Most conditions in South Africa are a combination of both. Getting the classification right determines whether you should expect a quick fix or a generational project.

**Step 4: Evaluate proposed solutions against the classification.**

When a politician or party proposes a solution, ask whether it matches the scale of the problem. If the problem is primarily structural (like the skills deficit), a solution that promises results within one electoral term is almost certainly overselling. If the problem is primarily policy-driven (like the pace of energy reform), a solution that defers action to a long-term plan may be an excuse for inaction. The most honest proposals acknowledge both dimensions: the structural inheritance that constrains what is possible in the short term, and the policy choices that can make a meaningful difference within the constraints.

This framework will not tell you which party to vote for. It will help you distinguish between politicians who understand the depth of South Africa's economic challenges and those who are offering slogans in place of structural analysis.
