## The Same Design Keeps Breaking in the Same Ways

In [The Eight Tests Every Government Institution Must Pass](/articles/ra-1-1-eight-tests), you learned a framework for diagnosing governance failure. You can now look at any government function — water, education, healthcare, housing, electricity — and run eight tests to determine where the institutional design is broken. But here is the next question: why does the design keep breaking in the same ways?

## The Five Ways Government Breaks

If you look across South Africa's governance failures — across different functions, different spheres, different provinces, different decades — you begin to see patterns. Not random patterns, but *recurring structural configurations* that produce failure with remarkable consistency. A housing project that stalls in Limpopo fails for structurally similar reasons to a water system that collapses in the Eastern Cape. A provincial education department that cannot improve matric results suffers from the same institutional architecture as a provincial health department that cannot staff its clinics. The functions are different. The people are different. **The failure pattern is the same.**

This article maps five such patterns. Think of them as diagnostic categories. When you encounter a government failure — when the tap runs dry, when the clinic turns you away, when the housing project is abandoned half-built — you can now ask a precise question: which of these five patterns is at work here? The answer will point you toward the right fix, because different patterns require fundamentally different remedies. Consolidating institutions fixes fragmentation but does nothing for capture. Strengthening enforcement addresses absence but is irrelevant to misalignment. Naming the pattern correctly is the essential first step toward prescribing the right reform.

Most real-world governance failures involve more than one pattern operating simultaneously. A municipality's water system may be fragmented (too many entities involved), suffer from absence (no enforcement mechanism), and be captured (political deployment of unqualified officials). But even in these compound cases, there is usually a primary pattern — the structural flaw that, if fixed, would create the conditions for addressing the others. Your task as a citizen-diagnostician is to identify that primary pattern.

:::takeaway
Government failures are not random. They fall into five recurring structural patterns — fragmentation, misalignment, absence, conflation, and capture — that repeat across functions and across the country. Identifying the pattern is the first step toward identifying the fix, because different patterns require fundamentally different remedies.
:::

## Pattern 1: Fragmentation — Too Many Cooks

Fragmentation is the most pervasive failure pattern in South African governance. It occurs when too many institutions are involved in delivering a single function, and no single entity owns the outcome end-to-end. The result is predictable: responsibilities fall through the gaps between institutions, accountability becomes diffuse to the point of meaninglessness, and the citizen at the end of the chain receives a broken or absent service while every entity involved can credibly claim to have done its part.

Human settlements is the canonical example, and it is worth tracing the full value chain to understand just how severe the fragmentation is. Start at the top: the national Department of Human Settlements sets the policy framework, defines the subsidy quantum (the amount allocated per housing unit), and channels conditional grants to provinces through the Human Settlements Development Grant. Move to the provincial level: the provincial department of human settlements approves project applications from municipalities, appoints or approves developers, and manages the provincial housing pipeline. Move to the municipality: the local authority identifies and zones suitable land, provides bulk infrastructure — water connections, sewerage networks, road access, stormwater drainage — and approves building plans. Now add the developer or contractor, who physically constructs the dwelling. Add the National Home Builders Registration Council (NHBRC), which is supposed to inspect construction quality and certify that dwellings meet structural standards. Add the Surveyor-General's office, which approves cadastral plans. Add the Deeds Registry, which registers title deeds in the names of beneficiaries. Add the Housing Development Agency, a national entity created in 2008 precisely because the existing value chain was not working, with a mandate to acquire and develop land for human settlements.

Count them. At minimum, you have five distinct entities across three spheres of government, plus two regulatory bodies and a development agency. At each handoff point — from national to province, from province to municipality, from municipality to developer, from developer to NHBRC, from completion to title registration — there is a gap where things can stall, fall through, or break down entirely.

And they do. In the 2022-23 financial year, the Auditor-General reported that the national Department of Human Settlements had underspent its budget by **R1.97 billion**, primarily on the Human Settlements Development Grant. Across provinces, the housing delivery pipeline showed persistent bottlenecks at multiple points: land identification, bulk infrastructure provision, developer appointment, construction quality, and title deed registration. The Department itself acknowledged that **the housing backlog — estimated at over 2.3 million units — was growing faster than delivery could reduce it.**

The title deed backlog is perhaps the most telling symptom of fragmentation. **By 2023, an estimated 900,000 government-subsidised housing beneficiaries had not received their title deeds** — meaning they occupied houses they did not legally own and could not sell, bequeath, or use as collateral. The title deed process requires coordination between the developer (who must complete the dwelling and apply for an occupancy certificate), the municipality (which must issue the certificate), the surveyor-general (who must approve the cadastral plan), and the deeds office (which must process the registration). Each entity has its own process, its own timelines, its own capacity constraints. Nobody owns the end-to-end title deed journey. The beneficiary waits.

:::expert
The Auditor-General's office has described the human settlements value chain as exhibiting "systemic fragmentation" where "the absence of a single point of end-to-end accountability" makes it "structurally difficult to hold any single entity responsible for housing delivery outcomes." The AG's 2022-23 consolidated report on national and provincial government noted that housing delivery failures are "not primarily attributable to individual incompetence or corruption, but to an institutional architecture that distributes functions across multiple entities without binding coordination mechanisms."
:::

Fragmentation is not unique to housing. Water services involve the Department of Water and Sanitation (national policy and regulation), Water Boards (bulk water treatment and supply), municipalities as Water Services Authorities (planning and regulation at local level), and municipalities or contracted entities as Water Services Providers (actual delivery to households). Public transport involves the national Department of Transport, provincial road authorities, municipal planning authorities, the Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa, bus operators, and the unregulated minibus taxi industry. In each case, the value chain is long, the handoff points are numerous, and the end-to-end ownership is absent.

The structural fix for fragmentation is consolidation — reducing the number of entities involved and creating a single point of end-to-end ownership. This does not necessarily mean a single mega-entity. It means identifying one entity that owns the outcome and holds the authority to coordinate all the inputs. In the RAARICLE™ framework, this means co-locating responsibility, accountability, and authority in the same institutional chain — the principle introduced in [The Eight Tests](/articles/ra-1-1-eight-tests).

## Pattern 2: Misalignment — The Wrong Entity Holds the Wrong Element

Misalignment occurs when the eight RAARICLE™ elements are distributed across different spheres or entities in a way that separates accountability from authority, or responsibility from resources. The entity that answers for the outcome does not control the inputs that determine whether the outcome is achieved. The entity that delivers the service does not set the rules under which it operates. The entity that is blamed for failure could not have succeeded even with perfect management, because the levers of control sit elsewhere.

Education is the clearest and most consequential example. South Africa's Constitution lists education as a *Schedule 4 concurrent competence* — shared between national and provincial government. In practice, the division works like this: the national Department of Basic Education sets the curriculum, determines the conditions of service for educators through the Employment of Educators Act, participates in collective bargaining through the Education Labour Relations Council, sets norms and standards for school funding, and administers the National Senior Certificate examination. Provincial departments of education employ the teachers, manage the schools, maintain the infrastructure, and are accountable — through the provincial MEC for Education — for educational outcomes, including the matric pass rate.

**This creates a structural misalignment of devastating proportions.** The provincial MEC signs a performance agreement committing to improve educational outcomes. But that MEC cannot determine teacher salary scales — those are bargained nationally. The MEC cannot change the curriculum — that is a national competence. The MEC cannot alter the teacher post provisioning model — that follows national norms. The MEC cannot discipline a poorly performing teacher through an efficient process — the disciplinary procedures are governed by national legislation and collective agreements. The MEC is accountable for an outcome that depends overwhelmingly on inputs controlled by national entities.

The results are visible in the data. In the 2023 National Senior Certificate examinations, the national pass rate was 82.9 per cent. But this aggregate figure conceals enormous provincial variation. Gauteng and the Western Cape consistently outperform. The Eastern Cape, Limpopo, and KwaZulu-Natal consistently underperform. The conventional explanation attributes this to provincial management quality — and management quality certainly matters. But the misalignment pattern suggests a deeper structural issue: provincial departments that serve predominantly rural, poor, and geographically dispersed populations face fundamentally higher per-unit costs for service delivery, yet the national frameworks that govern teacher employment, school funding, and infrastructure provision do not adequately account for these cost differentials. The province is accountable for outcomes it cannot fully control.

:::takeaway
Misalignment is the pattern that makes accountability performative rather than genuine. When the entity that signs the performance agreement does not control the budget, the staff, the procurement process, or the policy framework, it is being held responsible for results it cannot deliver. This is not a management problem — it is an architectural flaw.
:::

Healthcare exhibits an almost identical misalignment. Provincial health departments operate hospitals and clinics and are accountable for health outcomes. But the Occupation Specific Dispensation that determines health professional grading and salaries is national. The essential medicines list is national. The infrastructure grant for clinic construction is a conditional grant with national conditions. Community health worker programmes operate under national frameworks. The province manages the facilities but does not control the critical inputs that determine whether those facilities can function effectively.

The structural fix for misalignment is co-location — bringing accountability and authority into the same institutional chain. This can be achieved either by moving authority to the entity that is accountable (giving provinces more control over education employment conditions, for instance) or by moving accountability to the entity that holds authority (making the national Department of Basic Education directly accountable for educational outcomes rather than devolving accountability to provinces that lack the authority to deliver). Either approach addresses the misalignment. The current system, which separates accountability from authority, ensures that neither sphere can be held meaningfully responsible.

## Pattern 3: Absence — Nobody's Watching

Absence is the simplest failure pattern to understand and the most dangerous to tolerate. It occurs when one or more of the eight RAARICLE™ elements is not merely weak or poorly implemented but entirely missing. There is no enforcement mechanism. There is no information system. There is no accountability framework. The element does not exist, and consequently the governance function operates without a critical component that any well-designed system would include.

Enforcement is the most commonly absent element in South African governance. Across municipal services — water, waste management, electricity distribution, roads maintenance — non-compliance with national norms and standards carries no automatic consequences. The architecture of discretionary enforcement means that a municipality can fail to meet drinking water quality standards for years without triggering any mandatory corrective intervention.

Consider the Department of Water and Sanitation's regulatory role. The Water Services Act empowers the Minister to issue directives to failing water services authorities. The National Water Act provides for compliance monitoring and enforcement. The Blue Drop and Green Drop certification systems assess water and wastewater quality respectively. All of these mechanisms exist on paper. But enforcement is discretionary, slow, and politically sensitive.

The 2023 Blue Drop report assessed 958 water supply systems and found that **46 per cent had poor or bad microbiological water quality compliance**. In the Northern Cape, 87 per cent of water systems were in poor or critical condition. Only 26 systems nationally achieved Blue Drop certification, down from 44 in 2014. Yet in the face of this documented, systematic, worsening non-compliance, the number of formal enforcement actions taken by DWS against failing municipalities has remained negligible relative to the scale of the problem. The Department can issue directives — but it is not required to. *The decision to enforce is political, not automatic.* And when enforcement is political, it is inconsistent at best and absent at worst.

:::expert
The Water Research Commission has documented what it terms the "enforcement vacuum" in South African water services governance. In a 2023 research report, WRC researchers found that "the regulatory framework provides adequate legal powers for enforcement, but the institutional arrangements for exercising those powers are fundamentally inadequate." The report noted that DWS's regulatory capacity — the number of qualified inspectors, the monitoring infrastructure, the legal capacity to prosecute non-compliance — has declined in real terms over the past decade even as the number of failing water systems has grown. The result is a regulatory system that can identify failure (through Blue Drop assessments) but cannot compel correction (through binding enforcement actions with automatic consequences).
:::

The absence pattern is not limited to enforcement. Information is absent in many municipal contexts — municipalities that do not test their own water quality, that do not have functional billing systems, that cannot produce accurate financial statements because they lack the data to do so. Capability is absent where critical technical posts have been vacant for years — the process controller position at the water treatment works that has been unfilled since the last qualified incumbent resigned, leaving untrained operators to manage complex chemical treatment processes by trial and error.

What makes absence particularly dangerous is that it is self-reinforcing. When enforcement is absent, non-compliance becomes the norm. When non-compliance becomes the norm, the political cost of introducing enforcement increases — because enforcement would now affect a large number of entities rather than a few outliers. When the political cost is high, enforcement remains absent. The cycle feeds itself. This is why the Department of Water and Sanitation has not enforced Blue Drop standards aggressively even as compliance has deteriorated — enforcing against the majority of municipalities simultaneously would be politically and logistically overwhelming. The absence of enforcement in the early stages, when only a few municipalities were failing, allowed the problem to grow to a scale where enforcement now seems impractical.

The structural fix for absence is institution-building — creating the missing element from scratch. For enforcement, this means designing automatic triggers with pre-defined consequences, removing discretion from the enforcement process, and building the institutional capacity (inspectors, monitoring systems, legal processes) to implement the triggers. The SARS model demonstrates that automatic enforcement works in South Africa when it is properly designed and resourced. The challenge is extending that design principle to service delivery functions where the political dynamics are more complex.

## Pattern 4: Conflation — Referee and Player on the Same Team

Conflation occurs when the entity that delivers a service is also the entity that regulates its own performance. The provider and the regulator are the same institution, or fall under the same political principal. The result is that the entity sets its own standards, monitors its own compliance, and reports on its own performance — the governance equivalent of a company serving as its own auditor.

The Water Services Authority and Water Services Provider pattern in municipal water services is the canonical example. Under the Water Services Act, municipalities are designated as Water Services Authorities — the entities responsible for ensuring access to water services within their jurisdictions. The WSA function is regulatory in nature: it involves planning, setting standards, contracting providers, and overseeing compliance. But in the vast majority of South Africa's 144 Water Services Authorities, the municipality is simultaneously the Water Services Provider — the entity that actually operates the water treatment works, maintains the pipe network, and delivers water to households. The same municipal department that runs the water plant is supposed to regulate the quality of water that plant produces. The same municipal manager who is responsible for operational delivery is supposed to hold the operations team accountable for compliance with water quality standards.

This is a textbook governance failure. Regulation requires independence from the regulated entity. When the regulator and the provider share the same institutional home, the same budget, the same political leadership, and the same organisational culture, the regulatory function is inevitably compromised. A municipal water services department will not issue a compliance directive against itself. A municipal manager will not shut down a water treatment plant that the municipality operates, even if the plant is producing water that fails to meet quality standards — because shutting down the plant means cutting off water supply to the community, which is politically and operationally unacceptable.

The conflation pattern explains a puzzle that baffles many observers of South African water governance: how can water quality deteriorate so dramatically when there are legal standards, assessment systems, and reporting requirements in place? **The answer is that the entity responsible for meeting the standards is the same entity responsible for assessing whether the standards are met.** When the municipality tests its own water quality (if it tests at all), it controls both the testing process and the response to unfavourable results. When the results are bad, the incentive is not to report them accurately — because accurate reporting would trigger compliance obligations that the municipality may lack the capacity or willingness to fulfil.

:::takeaway
Conflation is the governance equivalent of asking a company to audit its own financial statements. When the service provider and the regulator are the same entity, the regulatory function is structurally compromised — not because the individuals involved are dishonest, but because the institutional design eliminates the independence that effective regulation requires.
:::

The conflation pattern extends beyond water. Municipal electricity distribution operates under a similar structure — the municipality is both the distributor and the entity responsible for ensuring distribution network safety and reliability. Municipal waste management involves the municipality as both the service provider (collecting refuse, operating landfill sites) and the entity responsible for ensuring compliance with environmental standards for waste disposal. In each case, the provider regulates itself.

The structural fix for conflation is separation — creating an institutional boundary between the regulatory function and the operational function. This does not necessarily require establishing new national regulators for every municipal function (though proposals for an independent economic regulator of municipal services have been discussed for years). At minimum, it requires that the regulatory function — setting standards, monitoring compliance, enforcing consequences — is institutionally separate from the delivery function, with its own reporting line, its own budget, and its own professional staff. The entity that runs the water plant cannot also be the entity that certifies the water is safe to drink.

## Pattern 5: Capture — When Institutions Serve the Wrong Master

Capture is the most discussed failure pattern in South African public discourse — and the most frequently misunderstood. The term "state capture" entered the national vocabulary through the Zondo Commission, and it is often used as a synonym for corruption. But capture, in the structural sense that the RAARICLE™ framework uses, is broader and more insidious than corruption alone. Capture occurs when an institution is redirected to serve interests other than its public mandate — and those interests may be political, commercial, labour-related, regulatory, or informational.

The RAARICLE™ framework identifies five capture vectors, each representing a distinct mechanism through which institutional purpose can be subverted.

*Political capture* occurs when political party interests override professional criteria in institutional decision-making. The most visible form is *cadre deployment* — the appointment of individuals to public sector positions based on political loyalty rather than professional qualification. But political capture extends beyond appointments. It includes the suppression of service tariffs for electoral reasons (keeping water or electricity prices below cost-recovery levels to win votes, while the infrastructure funded by those tariffs deteriorates), the manipulation of procurement to reward political allies, and the use of government resources — vehicles, buildings, staff time — for party political purposes. The Zondo Commission documented extensive evidence of political capture at national, provincial, and municipal levels, **finding that the deployment of politically connected but professionally unqualified individuals to critical positions was documented across multiple administrations and governing levels, indicating a systemic governance vulnerability rather than a partisan aberration.**

Commercial capture occurs when procurement systems and contractual relationships are manipulated to benefit connected private interests at the expense of public value. This is the terrain of tender fraud, inflated contracts, collusive bidding, and the extensive networks of intermediaries and facilitators that the Zondo Commission exposed in detail. The Gupta family's capture of state procurement processes — documented across Eskom, Transnet, the Free State province, and multiple national departments during roughly 2009 to 2018 — is one of several documented examples, but commercial capture operates at every sphere of government and through institutional vulnerabilities that any connected network could exploit under the same procurement architecture. The Auditor-General's reports consistently identify irregular expenditure in the tens of billions of rands annually, much of it linked to procurement non-compliance that benefits connected contractors.

Labour capture occurs when organised labour interests within the public sector override service delivery imperatives. This vector is less discussed but equally significant. In South African governance, public sector unions exercise substantial influence over employment conditions, discipline processes, and organisational restructuring. When collective bargaining agreements make it practically impossible to discipline a poorly performing teacher, or when union resistance prevents the restructuring of a municipal department that is manifestly dysfunctional, or when agreements on staff establishment sizes produce bloated headcounts in administrative functions while front-line service delivery posts remain vacant — labour capture is at work. The entity is serving its employees' interests rather than its users' interests.

Regulatory capture occurs when a regulatory body — an entity created to protect the public interest by overseeing an industry or function — instead serves the interests of the entities it is supposed to regulate. This can happen through revolving doors (regulators moving to positions in regulated industries and vice versa), through information asymmetry (regulated entities controlling the data that regulators depend on), or through institutional weakness (regulators lacking the resources and expertise to challenge the entities they oversee). In South Africa, concerns about regulatory capture have been raised regarding NERSA's regulation of electricity tariffs, the NHBRC's oversight of construction quality, and various sector-specific regulators that lack the institutional independence and technical capacity to hold powerful regulated entities accountable.

Information capture occurs when management controls the flow of data to oversight bodies, presenting a filtered or distorted picture of institutional performance. A municipal manager who reports to the council that service delivery targets have been met — while the underlying data shows deteriorating water quality, rising infrastructure backlogs, and growing consumer complaints — is engaging in information capture. The oversight body (the council) cannot exercise its function because it lacks accurate information. The Auditor-General's reports frequently identify "material misstatements" in financial and performance reporting — instances where the information presented to oversight bodies did not accurately reflect the institution's actual financial position or service delivery performance.

:::expert
The Zondo Commission's final report, released in multiple parts between 2022 and 2024, documented capture across all five vectors — though the Commission's primary focus was on political and commercial capture. The Commission recommended the establishment of a permanent, independent anti-corruption body with prosecutorial powers, the strengthening of procurement integrity measures, and fundamental reforms to the appointment of senior public servants. As of mid-2025, implementation of the Commission's recommendations has been partial. The National Prosecuting Authority's Investigating Directorate Against Corruption has secured some convictions, but the structural reforms recommended by the Commission — including changes to the appointment and removal processes for heads of public entities — have not been fully legislated. The lesson of the Zondo Commission is not just that capture happened, but that the institutional designs that made capture possible remain largely intact.
:::

**What makes capture structurally distinct from the other four patterns is that it is not primarily a design flaw — it is a design vulnerability.** Fragmentation, misalignment, absence, and conflation are problems with how institutions are designed. *Capture is what happens when institutions that are otherwise adequately designed lack the structural protections needed to resist subversion.* An institution can have clear responsibility, proper accountability, adequate authority, and sufficient resources — and still be captured if its appointment processes are politicised, its procurement systems are weak, its information flows are controlled by management, or its regulatory independence is undermined.

The structural fix for capture is building independence safeguards into institutional design — protections that make capture structurally difficult rather than relying on the goodwill of those in power. These include merit-based appointment processes with independent selection panels, procurement transparency measures that make corruption harder to conceal, information systems that provide direct access to oversight bodies without management filtering, and structural separation between regulatory and political authority. The RAARICLE™ framework's anti-capture architecture, which will be detailed in [Anti-Capture Architecture](/articles/ra-5-2-anti-capture-architecture), proposes specific protections against each of the five vectors.

## The Compounding Effect

In the real world, these five patterns rarely operate in isolation. Most governance failures involve multiple patterns interacting — and the interaction is not merely additive but multiplicative. Each pattern creates conditions that make the others more likely, producing compounding failure that is greater than the sum of its parts.

Consider a typical rural municipality's water services. Start with fragmentation: the water supply chain involves DWS (national policy and bulk water allocation), a Water Board (bulk water treatment and trunk distribution), the municipality as WSA (planning and regulation), and the municipality as WSP (local reticulation and household connections). Four entities, three spheres, no single end-to-end owner. Now add conflation: the municipality regulates and provides the service simultaneously, eliminating independent oversight. Add absence: there is no automatic enforcement mechanism that triggers when water quality declines — DWS enforcement is discretionary and rarely exercised. Add capability absence: the municipality's water treatment plant has no qualified process controller because the post has been vacant for two years, and the municipality cannot offer a competitive salary. Now add capture: the municipal manager was appointed through cadre deployment and lacks technical understanding of water operations; the procurement of water treatment chemicals has been awarded to a politically connected supplier at above-market prices; and the management reports to the council understate the severity of the water quality problem.

Each pattern reinforces the others. Fragmentation makes information flow harder, which enables information capture. The absence of enforcement means that conflation has no external check — nobody is compelling the self-regulating municipality to comply with standards it is supposed to set for itself. Capture depletes capability by replacing qualified officials with connected ones, and it weakens the information systems that would make the other failures visible. The compounding effect produces a municipality where water quality has collapsed, nobody is structurally responsible for fixing it, nobody has the authority to act decisively, nobody is monitoring the situation in real time, nobody has the skills to operate the treatment works, and nobody faces automatic consequences for the failure.

This is not a hypothetical. It is the documented reality for dozens of South Africa's municipalities. The Auditor-General's 2022-23 municipal audit outcomes showed that **only 41 of 257 municipalities received clean audit outcomes**. One hundred and ten municipalities had incurred fruitless and wasteful expenditure totalling **R27.3 billion**. Ninety municipalities had spent more than their available funding. The patterns described in this article — fragmentation, misalignment, absence, conflation, and capture — are not academic abstractions. *They are the structural explanation for why millions of South Africans receive services that are inadequate, unreliable, or absent entirely.*

:::takeaway
The five failure patterns compound each other. Fragmentation creates information gaps that enable capture. The absence of enforcement means conflation goes unchecked. Capture degrades capability, which deepens fragmentation. Understanding these interactions is essential for designing reforms that address root causes rather than symptoms — because fixing one pattern while leaving the others intact will not produce lasting improvement.
:::

The practical implication for reform is that effective interventions must address the primary pattern while also building protections against the others. A reform that consolidates water services delivery (fixing fragmentation) but does not separate regulation from provision (leaving conflation intact) will produce an entity that is less fragmented but still self-regulating. A reform that creates an independent regulator (fixing conflation) but does not build enforcement mechanisms with automatic triggers (leaving absence intact) will produce a regulator that can observe failure but not compel correction. The RAARICLE™ framework, as applied throughout the rest of this series, insists that all eight elements must be addressed simultaneously — because governance, like any engineered system, is only as strong as its weakest component.

:::framework
**The Failure Pattern Identification Checklist**

When a government service is failing, use these five questions to identify which structural failure pattern is at work. Most governance breakdowns fall into one or more of these categories.

**Question 1: How many entities are involved in delivering this service?**

List every entity that plays a role — national department, provincial department, municipality, state-owned entity, regulator, contractor. If you count four or more entities with no single end-to-end owner, the problem is likely **fragmentation**. The more handoff points, the more likely that outcomes fall through the gaps between institutions.

**Question 2: Does the entity that is accountable for the outcome also control the key inputs?**

Identify who signs the performance agreement and then check whether that same entity controls the budget, the staff, the procurement process, and the operational decisions. If accountability sits in one sphere (e.g., provincial) but authority over critical inputs sits in another (e.g., national), the problem is **misalignment**. This is the most common pattern in South Africa's concurrent functions.

**Question 3: Is any of the eight RAARICLE™ elements simply missing?**

Run through the eight tests from Article 1.1. Is there no identifiable person accountable for the outcome? Is there no real-time performance data? Is there no enforcement mechanism at all? If an element is not weak but entirely absent — no one monitors, no one enforces, no information system exists — the problem is **absence**.

**Question 4: Is the same entity both delivering the service and regulating its own performance?**

Check whether the provider and the oversight body are the same institution or fall under the same political principal. If the municipality is both the water services authority (regulator) and the water services provider, the problem is **conflation**. An entity cannot objectively hold itself to account.

**Question 5: Has the institution been redirected to serve private, political, or sectional interests instead of the public interest?**

Look for the five capture vectors: political deployment that overrides professional criteria, commercial relationships that benefit connected insiders, labour agreements that prioritise employee interests over service delivery, regulatory decisions that protect incumbents rather than users, or information suppression that hides poor performance. If any of these vectors are present, the problem is **capture**.

After identifying the pattern, you can propose a targeted solution: consolidation for fragmentation, co-location for misalignment, institution-building for absence, structural separation for conflation, and independence safeguards for capture. Naming the pattern precisely is the first step toward advocating for the right reform.
:::

## A Severity Rating System

Now that you can identify which failure pattern is at work, you need a way to assess how severe the failure is. Not all governance breakdowns are equal. A municipality where the water treatment plant has no qualified process controller (capability is entirely absent) is in a different situation from a municipality where the process controller is qualified but lacks a real-time monitoring system (capability is present but information is weak). Both fail the RAARICLE™ tests. But the severity differs, and the urgency of the required intervention differs with it.

The RAARICLE™ framework uses a five-point severity rating scale that will be applied throughout the rest of this series. For each of the eight elements — Responsibility, Accountability, Authority, Resources, Information, Capability, Legitimacy, and Enforcement — you can assign a rating from 1 (critical failure) to 5 (well-aligned). Here is what each rating means in practice.

A rating of **1 — Critical failure** means the element is absent or fundamentally broken. Systemic collapse is evident. You cannot identify a responsible entity at all. There is no performance agreement. The entity has no authority to act. Revenue has collapsed or is entirely diverted. There is no performance data. Critical posts are vacant with no prospect of being filled. The entity's mandate is actively contested. There are no consequences for failure whatsoever. When you see a 1, you are looking at an institutional void — a place where the governance function that the element is supposed to provide simply does not exist.

A practical example of a 1: a municipality whose water treatment plant has been abandoned. No entity is operating it. No entity is monitoring its condition. The plant produces no treated water. Residents rely on untreated sources or water tankers provided on an ad hoc basis. The function has collapsed entirely.

A rating of **2 — Severe gap** means the element exists on paper but fails in practice. There is major misalignment between the design and the reality. A performance agreement exists but contains no measurable targets. The entity has formal authority but cannot exercise it because of dependencies on other entities. Revenue is allocated but consistently underspent or misspent. Performance data is collected but not acted upon. Posts are filled but by unqualified incumbents. The mandate exists but is widely ignored. Enforcement mechanisms exist in legislation but are never activated.

A practical example of a 2: a municipality that has a designated Water Services Authority function and a water services department on its organogram, but where the water treatment works is operated by staff who lack the required qualifications, where water quality testing is sporadic and results are not reported to the council, and where the billing system has collapsed so that water revenue covers less than half of water service costs.

A rating of **3 — Moderate tension** means the element is partially present but with significant friction or inconsistency. The function works in some areas or some of the time but not reliably. Accountability exists but is divided across multiple entities. Authority is present for routine decisions but breaks down for significant interventions. Revenue covers operational costs but not capital replacement. Performance data exists but is delayed by quarters. Some critical posts are filled with qualified staff but others are vacant. Enforcement happens occasionally but inconsistently.

A practical example of a 3: a provincial education department that has a qualified head of department, produces quarterly performance reports, and meets most of its administrative targets — but cannot address the persistent underperformance of certain districts because the national employment framework prevents it from redeploying or replacing poorly performing district directors.

A rating of **4 — Functional** means the element is present and largely effective, with minor gaps remaining. The function works for most users most of the time. There is clear accountability with measurable targets. Authority is sufficient for operational decisions. Revenue covers both operational and most capital needs. Performance data is timely and used for decision-making. Critical posts are filled with qualified staff. Enforcement mechanisms exist and are occasionally activated.

A practical example of a 4: a metropolitan municipality's water services operation that has qualified engineers, real-time SCADA monitoring of its treatment works, a functioning billing system that recovers costs, and a track record of meeting Blue Drop standards — but that struggles with ageing infrastructure in certain distribution zones and has not fully implemented its capital replacement programme.

A rating of **5 — Well-aligned** means the element is fully co-located and operating as designed. The function works reliably and consistently. This is the benchmark — what the element looks like when the institutional design is right.

A practical example of a 5: SARS's enforcement architecture. When a taxpayer fails to comply, the consequences are automatic — penalties, interest, and escalating enforcement actions follow a prescribed sequence without requiring ministerial approval or political negotiation. *The triggers are pre-defined. The consequences are non-discretionary.* The system produces results: **R2.303 trillion in gross revenue collected in 2024-25**, with compliance revenue growing at a compound annual rate of 18.8 per cent over five years.

:::expert
The five-point severity scale is calibrated to South African conditions rather than abstract ideals. A rating of 5 does not mean perfection — it means the element functions as designed within the constraints of South Africa's governance system. SARS, for instance, still faces challenges with the informal economy, base erosion, and profit shifting by multinationals. But its enforcement element is well-aligned relative to the institutional design. The scale is designed to be applied by citizens and civil society organisations, not only by governance specialists. If you can answer the eight questions from [The Eight Tests](/articles/ra-1-1-eight-tests), you can assign a severity rating — and that rating gives you a precise, comparable measure of how broken or functional each governance element is across different functions and different institutions.
:::

When you apply this scale across all eight elements for a given function, you produce a diagnostic profile — a pattern of ratings that tells you not just that the function is failing, but exactly where the failures are concentrated and how severe each one is. A function with ratings of 1-2-2-3-1-2-3-1 has a very different reform profile from a function with ratings of 4-3-2-4-3-4-4-2. The first needs fundamental institutional reconstruction. The second needs targeted fixes to authority and enforcement. The severity profile guides the reform prescription.

The articles that follow in this series — covering education, healthcare, human settlements, water, electricity, waste management, and provincial and municipal governance — will apply both the five failure patterns and the severity rating system to each function. You will see the same patterns recurring across different functions, rated at different severities, producing different reform prescriptions. The diagnostic toolkit is now complete. What follows is the diagnosis itself.
