## The Number That Doesn't Mean What You Think

A government department announces that it spent 100% of its budget and built 15,000 houses last year. It sounds like success. The money was used. The houses exist. The target was met.

Now ask three questions the announcement does not answer. Are 15,000 families actually living in those houses, or are some standing empty because there is no water connection? Are the houses still standing, or have some already cracked because the foundations were poor? And was 15,000 the right number — enough to reduce the housing backlog, or a rounding error against a waiting list of millions?

The announcement measured **what the department did**. It told you nothing about **what changed for citizens**. This gap — between activity and result, between spending and delivery, between the number on the press release and the condition of your life — is the single most important thing to understand about holding government accountable. This article teaches you how to read across that gap.

## Why Measurement Is a Citizen's Tool, Not a Bureaucrat's Chore

In the first three articles of this series, you learned the architecture of the state, mapped who is responsible for which services, and traced how the spheres are meant to cooperate. You now know who to hold accountable. This article gives you the instrument for doing it: a working understanding of how government performance is measured, where the real numbers live, and how to tell an honest measure from a misleading one.

Measurement is not a technical subject reserved for auditors and economists. It is the difference between a complaint and a case. "Our clinic is terrible" is a feeling. "Our clinic's budget rose 12% over three years while the number of patients seen fell, and the province reported it as a success because it measured spending rather than service" is an argument — one that a councillor, a journalist, a portfolio committee, or a court can act on. Measurement is how you convert frustration into accountability.

The reason this matters so much in South Africa specifically is that the state produces an enormous volume of performance information, much of which is designed — intentionally or not — to flatter. Departments report against targets they set themselves. Numbers that sound impressive often describe inputs and outputs rather than outcomes. And the public is rarely taught the simple conceptual tools that would let them see through the presentation. The good news is that those tools are not complicated, and once you have them, government reporting becomes far easier to read critically.

## The Results Chain: The Most Useful Idea in This Whole Series

Almost every confusion about government performance dissolves once you understand a single framework: the **results chain**. It is the backbone of how the South African government is officially supposed to plan and measure, and it is the lens that lets you place any number you encounter into its proper context.

![The results chain runs from the inputs government controls to the impact citizens experience | Photo by Deng Xiang on Unsplash](/images/articles/1-4-measuring-what-matters/img-1.webp)

The results chain has five links, moving from what government controls completely to what citizens actually experience:

**Inputs** are the resources government puts in — money, staff, equipment, legal mandates. A provincial education department's budget, its number of teachers, and its school buildings are inputs.

**Activities** are what government does with those inputs — the processes and actions. Running teacher-training workshops, procuring textbooks, conducting school inspections: these are activities.

**Outputs** are the direct, countable products of those activities. Textbooks delivered, teachers trained, classrooms built, learners enrolled. Outputs are tangible and easy to count, which is exactly why government loves to report them.

**Outcomes** are the changes that result — the actual improvement in conditions. Learners who can read for meaning, pass rates that reflect real learning, schools that retain students through to matric. Outcomes are what citizens care about, and they are harder to measure and slower to appear.

**Impact** is the long-term, society-wide change — a more educated population, higher employment, reduced inequality. Impact unfolds over a decade or more and is influenced by many forces beyond any single department.

The crucial divide in this chain sits between outputs and outcomes. Everything to the left — inputs, activities, outputs — is largely within government's control and easy to count. Everything to the right — outcomes and impact — is what actually matters to your life and is harder to measure. **The defining feature of weak government reporting is that it stops at outputs and presents them as if they were outcomes.** A department that built 15,000 houses (output) reports it as having "delivered housing" (which implies the outcome of people adequately and securely housed). The two are not the same, and the distance between them is where service delivery quietly fails.

:::takeaway
The single most important question you can ask about any government statistic is: "Is this an output or an outcome?" An output is something government produced — houses built, kilometres of road laid, patients seen. An outcome is something that changed for citizens — families securely housed, communities connected, people made well. Government reporting overwhelmingly favours outputs because they are countable and controllable. Your job as a citizen is to keep asking the outcome question: not "what did you do?" but "what changed?"
:::

## Leading and Lagging: The Time Dimension of Measurement

The results chain organises measures by *what* they capture. A second distinction organises them by *when* they tell you something: the difference between **leading** and **lagging** indicators.

A **lagging indicator** confirms a result after it has happened. The matric pass rate, the annual unemployment figure, the year-end audit outcome — these tell you how things turned out. They are accurate and important, but by the time you have them, the period they describe is over. You cannot change last year's results.

A **leading indicator** is an early signal that predicts where things are heading while you can still act. The number of teacher vacancies filled by the start of the school year, the percentage of a municipality's infrastructure budget actually committed by mid-year, the rate at which a department is spending against its plan in the first quarter — these point forward. They give you, and oversight bodies, a chance to intervene before the lagging indicator delivers bad news.

Government naturally gravitates toward lagging indicators because they are concrete and defensible: the matric results are the matric results. But accountability lives in the leading indicators, because that is where problems are still fixable. When a department reports, three months before the end of the financial year, that it has spent only 40% of its infrastructure budget, that is a leading indicator screaming that the year-end output target will be missed. A citizen who knows to look for that signal can raise the alarm while it still matters. This is why the most effective oversight watches the early numbers, not just the final scorecard.

## How South Africa Is Supposed to Measure Itself

South Africa has, on paper, one of the more developed government performance systems in the developing world. Understanding its official architecture tells you where to find the targets government has set for itself — which is the starting point for holding it to them.


![Annual Performance Plans and quarterly reports turn government promises into documents you can check | Photo by Olena Kholina on Unsplash](/images/articles/1-4-measuring-what-matters/img-2.webp)

### The National Development Plan and the Medium-Term Framework

At the top sits the **National Development Plan (NDP)**, adopted in 2012 and looking toward 2030. Produced by the National Planning Commission, the NDP is the country's long-term vision document, setting broad goals for reducing poverty and inequality across every sector. The NDP is aspirational and high-level; it does not, by itself, hold anyone accountable. It is the destination, not the speedometer.

Because a twenty-year plan is too distant to manage against, each administration translates the NDP into a **medium-term plan** covering its five-year term — historically the Medium-Term Strategic Framework (MTSF), and in the current administration the Medium-Term Development Plan. This document distils the NDP's vision into a smaller set of strategic priorities for the term, against which national and provincial departments are expected to align their own plans. When you want to know what government has committed to over the current electoral cycle, the medium-term plan is the reference document.

### The Department That Watches the Others

Sitting in the Presidency is the **Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME)**, the institution responsible for tracking whether the rest of government is delivering on the plan. The DPME runs the government's outcomes-based monitoring system, maintains the Programme of Action that tracks progress against priorities, commissions independent evaluations of major programmes, and operates frontline service-delivery monitoring that sends officials to inspect clinics, schools, and home affairs offices in person. The DPME is, in effect, the government's own internal performance auditor for delivery, and its evaluation reports — many of which are public — are an underused resource for citizens.

### Annual Performance Plans: The Promises You Can Hold Them To

The document that matters most for practical accountability is the **Annual Performance Plan (APP)**. Required of every national and provincial department under the Public Finance Management Act and National Treasury's planning framework, the APP is where a department publishes, at the start of each financial year, its specific, measurable targets for that year: how many of each output it will produce, by when, at what cost.

The APP is a public commitment. It is tabled in Parliament or the provincial legislature, it is published online, and the department is required to report its actual performance against it every quarter and at year-end. This is the single most powerful accountability document available to an ordinary citizen, because it lets you compare what a department *promised* against what it *delivered*, in the department's own words and numbers. When a department misses an APP target, that is not your opinion — it is the department's own admission against its own published plan.

### The Municipal Equivalents

The municipal sphere has its own parallel set of planning and reporting instruments, established under the Municipal Systems Act and the Municipal Finance Management Act. The **Integrated Development Plan (IDP)** is the municipality's five-year strategic plan, developed (in principle) with community participation. The **Service Delivery and Budget Implementation Plan (SDBIP)** breaks the IDP and budget down into the in-year targets and milestones against which the municipality is measured. Monthly **Section 71 reports** track budget implementation, and the annual performance report and municipal annual report close the loop at year-end. If you want to know what your municipality committed to, the IDP and SDBIP are where those commitments are written down.

:::takeaway
Government tells you its own targets — you just have to know which document to open. For a national or provincial department, that document is the Annual Performance Plan (APP) and its quarterly performance reports. For a municipality, it is the Integrated Development Plan (IDP) and the Service Delivery and Budget Implementation Plan (SDBIP). These are public documents containing measurable promises. Accountability often starts with nothing more sophisticated than reading the plan, then asking whether the promise was kept.
:::

## The Auditor-General: What a "Clean Audit" Actually Certifies

No institution shapes public perception of government performance more than the **Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA)**, an independent body established under Chapter 9 of the Constitution. Each year the Auditor-General audits every national and provincial department, every public entity, and every municipality, and assigns each an audit outcome. Those outcomes are widely reported, and they are widely misunderstood. Understanding precisely what they do and do not certify is essential to measuring government honestly.

The audit outcomes form a ladder, from best to worst:

A **clean audit** (formally, a financially unqualified opinion with no material findings) means the financial statements are reliable, there are no material findings on the reported performance information, and there is no material non-compliance with key legislation. This is the top rung.

A **financially unqualified opinion with findings** means the financial statements are reliable, but the auditors found material problems with either the performance information or compliance with the law, or both. The money was accounted for; something else was not in order.

A **qualified opinion** means the financial statements contain material misstatements in specific areas that the department could not correct.

An **adverse opinion** means the financial statements are so materially misstated that they do not fairly present the department's finances at all.

A **disclaimer of opinion** is the worst outcome: the records are so poor or incomplete that the auditors could not even gather enough evidence to form an opinion. A disclaimer is, in effect, the auditor saying the books cannot be audited.

Here is the point that changes how you read every "clean audit" headline: **a clean audit measures whether money was handled and reported correctly, not whether it achieved anything useful.** A department can build a school in the wrong village, staff it with no teachers, and watch it stand empty — and still earn a clean audit, provided every rand was properly documented and the procurement followed the rules. Conversely, a department doing genuinely transformative work in difficult conditions might receive a qualified opinion because its record-keeping is weak. The audit is a measure of financial and procedural integrity. It is a necessary foundation for good government, but it is not a measure of impact on your life. Treating a clean audit as proof of good service delivery is one of the most common errors in South African public debate.

The Auditor-General does, however, reach beyond the pure financial opinion in ways that are highly useful to citizens. AGSA reports on **irregular expenditure** (money spent in contravention of the rules), **fruitless and wasteful expenditure** (money spent for nothing of value), and **unauthorised expenditure**. And since amendments to the Public Audit Act took effect, the Auditor-General has expanded powers to identify **material irregularities** — failures that cause or risk substantial financial loss or harm to the public — and to refer them and pursue recovery. These categories let you see not just whether the books balance, but where money is being lost.

:::expert
The Auditor-General's annual general reports on national, provincial, and local government are among the most candid official assessments of state performance that South Africa produces. Read across multiple years, they reveal a recurring structural pattern that no single year's headline captures. AGSA has repeatedly drawn attention to a disconnect between financial compliance and service-delivery outcomes — institutions can move up the audit ladder, improving their financial and compliance standing, while the services they exist to provide stagnate or decline. The Auditor-General's work on performance information specifically examines whether the targets departments report are useful (relevant and measurable) and reliable (supported by evidence), and the office has consistently found that a meaningful share of reported performance information fails one or both tests. The lesson for citizens is precise: use the audit outcome to judge financial integrity and the credibility of a department's own numbers, but never mistake it for a verdict on whether services actually improved. The two questions are different, and the gap between them is where much of South Africa's governance challenge lives.
:::

## Where the Real Numbers Live: The Data Ecosystem

Knowing the concepts is half the battle; knowing where to find trustworthy numbers is the other half. South Africa has an unusually rich public data ecosystem, and almost all of it is free. These are the sources worth knowing.

**Statistics South Africa (Stats SA)** is the country's official, independent statistics agency, operating under the Statistics Act. It produces the Census, the Quarterly Labour Force Survey (the source of the official unemployment rate), the General Household Survey (which tracks household access to water, electricity, sanitation, and services), the Consumer Price Index, and GDP figures. Stats SA data is the closest thing the country has to a neutral scoreboard, because it is produced independently of the departments whose performance it indirectly measures. When a department's self-reported outputs and Stats SA's household-survey outcomes diverge, the divergence itself is information.

**The Auditor-General's reports**, discussed above, are published annually and are searchable by department and municipality.

**The DPME's evaluations and Programme of Action** provide government's own assessment of progress against priorities, including independent evaluations of major programmes.

**National Treasury's budget documents** — the Estimates of National Expenditure (ENE) at national level, and the equivalent provincial estimates — set out not just what each programme is allocated, but its objectives and performance targets, making them a rich and underused source. Treasury also runs **Municipal Money** (municipalmoney.gov.za) and **Vulekamali**, public portals that present municipal and national budget and performance data in accessible form.

**Parliamentary and legislature committee records**, tracked and published by the **Parliamentary Monitoring Group (PMG)**, capture the questioning of ministers and officials and often surface performance problems before they appear in formal reports.

**The Public Service Commission (PSC)** monitors the health of public administration itself — ethics, grievances, the functioning of the public service.

And a growing ecosystem of **independent civil-society trackers and research institutes** — among them the Public Affairs Research Institute, the Institute for Race Relations, and various sector-specific monitors — translate and interrogate official data, often making it more legible than the source documents themselves.

The abundance of this data is genuinely a strength of South African governance. The weakness is that the data is fragmented across many sources and rarely connected for the public. Learning to triangulate — to read a department's self-reported output against an independent outcome measure from Stats SA, against the Auditor-General's verdict on the reliability of that department's numbers — is the core skill of measuring government well.

## How Measurement Goes Wrong

Numbers can mislead as easily as they inform. Five failure modes recur often enough in South African government reporting that recognising them is a practical defence.


![When a number becomes a target, the incentive shifts from improving reality to improving the number | Photo by Mauro Gigli on Unsplash](/images/articles/1-4-measuring-what-matters/img-3.webp)

**The first is mistaking outputs for outcomes** — the error this article has returned to repeatedly. A report celebrates kilometres of pipe laid; the outcome question is whether households now have reliable water. Pipes can be laid and still deliver nothing if the pumps fail, the dam is empty, or the connections were never made.

**The second is the spending-equals-success fallacy** — treating budget expenditure as an achievement in itself. "We spent 100% of our budget" tells you money left the account, not that it bought anything of value. Full expenditure with poor results can be worse than underspending, because it means resources were consumed without return.

**The third is gaming the metric**, captured by **Goodhart's Law**: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Once a single number carries high stakes, the incentive shifts from improving the underlying reality to improving the number — and the two come apart. The classic South African illustration is the matric pass rate. Reported as a headline figure each January, it is calculated as the percentage of those who *sat* the final exam and passed. It does not count the learners who started Grade 1 in the same cohort and dropped out, or were held back, before reaching the exam hall. A province can raise its reported pass rate while a large share of its original cohort never completes school at all. The honest outcome measure is **throughput** — how many of those who began the journey actually finish — and it tells a more sobering story than the pass rate alone. None of this means the matric pass rate is meaningless; it means a single output number, elevated to a target, conceals as much as it reveals.

**The fourth is the vanity metric** — a number chosen because it is large and flattering rather than because it captures what matters. "Two million people reached" sounds impressive until you ask what "reached" means and whether anything changed for them.

**The fifth is poor data quality** — targets that are unmeasurable, numbers unsupported by evidence, definitions that shift between years so that progress cannot be tracked. This is precisely the territory the Auditor-General examines when it tests whether performance information is useful and reliable, and its findings show the problem is widespread.

:::expert
The phenomenon that governance researchers call "isomorphic mimicry" describes institutions that adopt the *form* of high-performing organisations — the strategic plans, the performance frameworks, the dashboards and key performance indicators — without the *function* those forms are meant to serve. The South African state is, in important respects, a sophisticated producer of performance documentation: it generates plans, targets, quarterly reports, and audits in great volume and to a high formal standard. The persistent question raised by analysts of the South African public service is whether this elaborate measurement apparatus drives better outcomes or substitutes for them — whether the energy spent producing and reporting indicators crowds out the harder work of actually delivering. The practical implication for citizens is to treat the existence of a measurement system as the beginning of scrutiny, not the end of it. A department with a beautifully constructed Annual Performance Plan and a wall of green-rated indicators may or may not be changing anything in the communities it serves. Only the outcome data, read independently, can tell you which.
:::

## A Worked Example: Reading One Service End to End

Concepts become usable when you see them applied. Walk through how to evaluate a single service — say, water provision in a municipality — using everything above.

Start at the **input** end with the budget. Open the municipality's budget and IDP, and find what it allocated to water services and how that allocation changed over recent years. Rising budgets with falling service is a warning sign worth holding onto.

Move to **activities and outputs**. The SDBIP and annual performance report will state the municipality's targets and reported achievements: new connections installed, kilometres of pipe replaced, treatment plants upgraded. Note what it promised and what it claims to have delivered.

Now ask the **outcome** question, and this is where you leave the municipality's own reporting and triangulate. Stats SA's General Household Survey reports the percentage of households in each municipality with reliable access to water — an outcome measure produced independently. Compare it to the rosy output story. If the municipality reports laying pipe and installing connections year after year while the independent survey shows household access flat or declining, you have found exactly the output-outcome gap this article is about, and you have found it with public data.

Check the **audit outcome** for the municipality and read what the Auditor-General said specifically about its water-related infrastructure spending and the reliability of its performance reporting. A disclaimer or a string of findings on irregular expenditure around infrastructure tells you whether to trust the output numbers in the first place.

Finally, watch the **leading indicators** going forward. Is the municipality committing its infrastructure grant early in the year or scrambling to spend it in the final quarter? Are senior engineering posts filled or vacant? These early signals predict next year's outcome before it arrives.

By the end of this exercise you are no longer relying on a press release or a feeling. You have a documented, evidence-based picture of whether a service is improving, built entirely from public sources — and that picture is something a councillor, a journalist, or a court can be made to engage with.

## The Citizen's Measurement Toolkit

:::framework
**The Six-Question Performance Test**

You do not need to be an analyst to evaluate a government service. You need to ask six questions in order, each answerable from public documents.

**1. What did they promise?**
Find the target. For a department, open the Annual Performance Plan; for a municipality, the IDP and SDBIP. Write down the specific, measurable commitment for the period you care about. Accountability begins with the promise government made in its own words.

**2. What did they spend?**
Compare the budgeted amount to the actual expenditure, using the budget documents and in-year reports. Note both underspending (money that never reached the service) and full spending with poor results (money consumed without return).

**3. Is this an output or an outcome?**
For every number government reports, classify it. If it describes what government produced (houses, pipes, patients seen), it is an output — keep going. The outcome question, "what changed for citizens?", is the one that matters.

**4. What do the independent numbers say?**
Triangulate the self-reported outputs against an independent outcome source — most often Stats SA's household surveys. Where government's story and the independent data diverge, the divergence is your finding.

**5. Can you trust the numbers?**
Check the Auditor-General's outcome and findings for the institution. A clean audit means the financial reporting is sound; findings on performance information mean the institution's own numbers may be unreliable. Let this calibrate how much weight you give everything else.

**6. Where is it heading?**
Look at the leading indicators — early spending rates, vacancy rates, first-quarter progress against plan. These tell you whether next year's outcome is on track while there is still time to act on it.

Run these six questions and you will know more about a service's real performance than most of the commentary you will read about it.
:::
