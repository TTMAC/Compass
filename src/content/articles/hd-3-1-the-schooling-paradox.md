## Among the Highest Education Spending in the World, Among the Worst Results

South Africa spends approximately 6.5 per cent of its gross domestic product on education. That figure places it among the most generous education spenders in the world — above South Korea (4.7 per cent), above Germany (4.9 per cent), above the United Kingdom (5.5 per cent), and above most of its middle-income peers. Education consumes roughly 20 per cent of total consolidated government expenditure, making it the single largest line item in the national budget. By any reasonable fiscal measure, South Africa takes education seriously. The money is there.

And yet. The Progress in International Reading Literacy Study (PIRLS) 2021, the most authoritative international assessment of reading ability in the early grades, found that 81 per cent of South African Grade 4 learners cannot read for meaning. Not cannot read Shakespeare. Not cannot read academic text. Cannot read for meaning — the foundational skill upon which all subsequent learning depends. In the Trends in International Mathematics and Science Study (TIMSS), South African learners perform at levels comparable to students three to four years younger in countries that spend less on education. In the Southern and Eastern Africa Consortium for Monitoring Educational Quality (SACMEQ) assessments, South Africa is routinely outperformed by countries with a fraction of its per-learner expenditure — Zambia, Tanzania, Kenya.

This is the schooling paradox. A country that spends like a developed nation and gets outcomes like a fragile state. A system that puts children in seats for an average of 11.3 years — a figure that the Human Development Index dutifully records and rewards — while failing to teach a majority of them the basic literacy and numeracy that those years of seat-time are supposed to deliver. The HDI sees the years. It does not see what happens inside them. And what happens inside them, for the majority of South African learners, is a slow-motion catastrophe that shapes every other dimension of human development that this series examines.

Understanding why this paradox exists — why spending does not translate to learning — is not an exercise in blame. It is a diagnostic exercise. The education system is not failing because teachers are lazy, or because learners are incapable, or because the curriculum is wrong. It is failing because of a specific set of structural, institutional, and historical factors that interact in ways that make the current equilibrium remarkably stable. Breaking that equilibrium requires understanding it. This article is the diagnostic.

## The Spending Numbers

Let us begin with what South Africa actually spends, because the numbers matter and the public discourse around them is often imprecise.

In the 2023/24 financial year, the Department of Basic Education and provincial education departments collectively spent approximately R310 billion on basic education. The Department of Higher Education and Training spent an additional R130 billion on post-school education and training. Total public expenditure on education thus exceeded R440 billion — roughly 6.5 per cent of GDP and approximately 20 per cent of consolidated government expenditure. These figures have been broadly consistent for the past decade, rising in nominal terms but remaining relatively stable as a share of GDP and total expenditure.

To put that in international context: the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) average for public education spending is approximately 4.9 per cent of GDP. The European Union average is similar. South Korea, which has one of the world's highest-performing education systems, spends 4.7 per cent. Finland, regularly cited as a global education exemplar, spends 5.9 per cent. Brazil, South Africa's BRICS peer, spends approximately 6.0 per cent. Among major economies, only a handful — Israel, Costa Rica, some Nordic countries — spend a larger share of their GDP on education than South Africa.

Within that spending, the allocation is heavily skewed toward one input: teacher compensation. Personnel costs consume over 80 per cent of provincial education budgets. In some provinces — the Eastern Cape and Limpopo, in particular — the figure exceeds 85 per cent. This leaves less than 20 per cent for everything else: textbooks and learning materials, school infrastructure, maintenance, transport, nutrition, technology, training, and administration. The per-learner spending figure varies dramatically across provinces and quintiles, but even at the lower end, South Africa's per-learner expenditure exceeds that of most sub-Saharan African countries by a significant margin.

The question is not whether the money is being spent. It is being spent. The question is why the money is not producing learning. If you have ever wondered why South Africa's schools seem chronically underfunded despite a massive education budget, this allocation pattern is the answer: the money exists, but over 80 cents of every rand goes to one input — salaries — before a single textbook is purchased.

:::expert[World Bank Education Finance Analysis]
The World Bank's 2018 report on South African education spending, "An Incomplete Transition," documented a pattern that has since been confirmed by subsequent analyses: South Africa's education spending is high in aggregate but inefficiently allocated. The dominance of personnel costs crowds out spending on non-personnel inputs that research consistently identifies as important for learning outcomes — instructional materials, school management support, teacher professional development, and early childhood development. The report found that even within the personnel budget, allocation is suboptimal: teacher deployment does not track learner numbers effectively, resulting in some schools being overstaffed while others face chronic shortages in critical subjects. The conclusion was blunt — South Africa does not have an education funding problem; it has an education spending effectiveness problem.
:::

## What International Assessments Reveal

International assessments are the diagnostic tools that expose the gap between spending and outcomes. South Africa participates in three major international assessment programmes, and the results from all three tell a consistent and devastating story.

**PIRLS — Reading.** The Progress in International Reading Literacy Study, conducted by the International Association for the Evaluation of Educational Achievement (IEA) every five years, assesses reading comprehension among Grade 4 learners. South Africa has participated in PIRLS since 2006, and the results have been consistently alarming. In PIRLS 2016, 78 per cent of South African Grade 4 learners could not read for meaning — they could not extract basic information from a simple text. In PIRLS 2021, conducted during the COVID-19 pandemic, the figure worsened to 81 per cent. South Africa ranked last among the 57 participating countries and territories. Last. Below Iran. Below Egypt. Below South Africa's own 2016 results.

The PIRLS data is not just a headline number. It reveals a bimodal distribution that is the signature of South Africa's education system. A small cohort of learners — predominantly from well-resourced, historically advantaged schools — performs at or near international benchmarks. The majority performs far below them. The gap between the top and bottom performers in South Africa is among the widest in the PIRLS dataset, wider than the gap in any comparator country. This is not a normal distribution with a low mean; it is two separate distributions overlaid on each other. Two school systems, producing two radically different sets of outcomes, within one national education system.

**TIMSS — Mathematics and Science.** The Trends in International Mathematics and Science Study, also conducted by the IEA, assesses Grade 4 and Grade 8 learners in mathematics and science. South Africa has participated in TIMSS at the Grade 9 level since 1995 and at the Grade 5 level more recently. The results are comparable to the PIRLS findings. South African Grade 9 learners score an average of approximately 370 points on the TIMSS mathematics scale, against a scale centre-point of 500. For comparison, Singapore scores approximately 620, South Korea approximately 610, and even fellow middle-income countries like Turkey (approximately 500) and Malaysia (approximately 470) significantly outperform South Africa. The mathematics performance of South African Grade 9 learners is comparable to that of Grade 5 or Grade 6 learners in higher-performing countries — a learning gap of three to four years.

The science results follow a similar pattern. South African learners score well below the international average, and the distribution is again bimodal: a small top cohort performing competitively, and a large majority performing far below international benchmarks.

**SACMEQ — Regional Comparison.** The Southern and Eastern Africa Consortium for Monitoring Educational Quality provides a regional lens. SACMEQ assessments allow South Africa to be compared not with Finland or Singapore but with its immediate regional peers — Zambia, Tanzania, Kenya, Mozambique, Botswana. The results are uncomfortable: South Africa, despite spending significantly more per learner than any of these countries, does not consistently outperform them. In some assessment rounds, Kenya and Tanzania — countries with a fraction of South Africa's per-learner expenditure — produce reading and mathematics outcomes that match or exceed South Africa's. This comparison demolishes the argument that South Africa's outcomes are simply a function of being a developing country. They are not. They are a function of how the system converts spending into learning.

When you look at your child's school results — or remember your own schooling — you are seeing one of two distributions: the small cohort performing at international benchmarks, or the large majority performing far below them. Understanding which system your school belongs to is the first step to understanding what those results actually mean.

:::takeaway
International assessments — PIRLS, TIMSS, SACMEQ — consistently show that South Africa's education outcomes are among the worst in the world relative to spending. 81 per cent of Grade 4 learners cannot read for meaning. Mathematics performance lags comparator countries by three to four years. Countries that spend far less per learner produce comparable or better outcomes. The problem is not resources. It is what happens with the resources.
:::

## The Two School Systems

The single most important structural fact about South African education is that it is not one system. It is two. And understanding the paradox requires understanding the bimodal reality.

The first system — call it System A — consists of approximately 25 per cent of schools. These are predominantly former Model C schools (historically white or historically Indian schools that were partially privatised in the early 1990s), independent schools, and a relatively small number of well-managed township and rural schools. System A schools tend to share certain characteristics: functional governance (school governing bodies that are active and competent), adequate infrastructure (libraries, laboratories, sports facilities), a teaching corps with reasonable subject knowledge, relatively low learner-to-teacher ratios, and the ability to charge fees or attract additional resources. System A produces outcomes that are broadly comparable to upper-middle-income countries. When you see South Africa's top PIRLS or TIMSS performers, they are overwhelmingly System A learners.

The second system — System B — consists of approximately 75 per cent of schools. These are predominantly historically Black schools in townships, informal settlements, and rural areas. System B schools tend to share a different set of characteristics: weak or absent governance, inadequate infrastructure (the Department of Basic Education's own data shows that thousands of schools still lack adequate sanitation, water, libraries, and laboratories), high learner-to-teacher ratios, teacher absenteeism, weak subject knowledge among teachers (particularly in mathematics, science, and English), and no capacity to charge meaningful fees. System B produces outcomes that are comparable to low-income countries — and in some cases, to conflict-affected states.

The quintile system, which South Africa uses to classify schools by the poverty level of the surrounding community, formalises this division. Quintile 1 schools (the poorest) are declared no-fee schools and receive the highest per-learner allocation from government. Quintile 5 schools (the wealthiest) receive the lowest government allocation but can charge fees that often exceed the government's per-learner spending many times over. In practice, the quintile system has not equalised outcomes. The additional per-learner funding that Quintile 1 schools receive does not compensate for the accumulated deficits in teacher quality, infrastructure, governance, and the socioeconomic challenges that learners bring through the school gate.

If your child attends a Quintile 1, 2, or 3 school, you are almost certainly in System B — a system that produces outcomes comparable to low-income countries, not a middle-income nation. That is not a reflection of your child's potential. It is a reflection of the system's structural failure to convert spending into learning for the majority.

The bimodal distribution is not new. It is a direct structural inheritance from apartheid, which created separate education departments — the House of Assembly (white), the House of Representatives (coloured), the House of Delegates (Indian), and the Department of Education and Training (Black) — with radically different per-learner expenditure. At the peak of apartheid, the government spent roughly ten times as much per white learner as per Black learner. The post-1994 government unified the system administratively and equalised per-learner funding, but the accumulated capital — physical infrastructure, institutional capacity, teacher quality, community resources — built up over decades of unequal investment could not be equalised by policy fiat. Thirty years later, the two systems persist, and the outcomes gap between them has narrowed only modestly.

:::expert[Equal Education]
Equal Education, a membership-based civil society organisation, has documented the infrastructure gap through sustained advocacy and litigation. Their campaigns have produced court orders requiring the Department of Basic Education to publish and enforce legally binding norms and standards for school infrastructure — including minimum standards for classrooms, sanitation, water, electricity, libraries, and laboratories. As of 2023, the Department's own reports acknowledge that thousands of schools still do not meet these minimum standards, and that the infrastructure backlog will take decades to clear at current investment rates. Equal Education's research demonstrates that the infrastructure deficit is not randomly distributed — it maps almost perfectly onto the historical geography of apartheid, with the greatest infrastructure needs concentrated in the former Bantustans and historically Black urban areas. The infrastructure gap is not a legacy problem in the sense of being in the past. It is a present, ongoing, structural constraint on learning outcomes.
:::

## The HDI's Blind Spot

The Human Development Index, as [South Africa's Report Card](/articles/hd-1-2-south-africas-report-card) documents, uses two education indicators: mean years of schooling (the average number of years of education completed by adults aged 25 and older) and expected years of schooling (the number of years a child entering school can expect to complete).

South Africa's numbers on both indicators look respectable. Mean years of schooling is approximately 11.3 years — higher than Brazil (8.1), higher than Indonesia (8.6), higher than India (6.7), and comparable to some upper-middle-income countries. Expected years of schooling is approximately 14.0 years, implying that the average child entering the system can expect to complete secondary school and some post-secondary education.

This matters directly for you. If you grew up in South Africa and completed secondary school, the HDI would count your education as roughly 11–12 years of learning. But what those years actually produced — in reading fluency, mathematical reasoning, critical thinking — is the number the index never asks.

These numbers are the statistical expression of the paradox. They measure seat-time. They count years spent inside school buildings. They do not measure what happened during those years. They do not distinguish between a year of high-quality instruction in a well-resourced school with competent teachers and functional governance, and a year of sitting in an overcrowded classroom with a teacher who did not arrive, using a textbook that was not delivered, in a building without adequate sanitation.

The World Bank's concept of "learning-adjusted years of schooling" attempts to correct this blind spot. The idea is simple: adjust the years-of-schooling figure by the quality of learning that actually occurs during those years, using international assessment data. When this adjustment is applied to South Africa, the results are brutal. South Africa's learning-adjusted years of schooling drops from approximately 11.3 to approximately 5.7 — roughly half. A South African learner who completes 11 years of schooling acquires, on average, the learning equivalent of less than 6 years in a well-functioning education system.

This is the HDI's blind spot writ large. The index rewards South Africa for putting children in school buildings for extended periods. It does not penalise South Africa for what happens — or fails to happen — inside those buildings. And because the HDI is a widely referenced summary measure, its education component creates a false impression of adequacy. South Africa's HDI education score suggests a country that has largely solved access to education. The learning data suggests a country in the grip of a capabilities crisis.

The implications for Amartya Sen's capabilities approach, which underpins the HDI's conceptual framework, are profound. Sen's argument was that development should be measured not by inputs (income, spending, years of schooling) but by capabilities — the substantive freedoms that people have to do and be what they value. A child who spends 11 years in a school system that does not teach her to read has not had her capability expanded by 11 years of schooling. She has had her time consumed by 11 years of a system that failed her. The capability was not developed. The freedom was not expanded. And the HDI, designed to capture Sen's insight, misses it entirely.

:::takeaway
The HDI measures years of schooling, not quality. South Africa's 11.3 mean years look respectable. But when adjusted for learning quality, the figure drops to approximately 5.7 — half. The HDI rewards seat-time, not capability development. This blind spot masks one of the most severe capabilities failures in the middle-income world.
:::

## Why Spending Doesn't Translate to Outcomes

If the money is being spent and the children are in the buildings, why is so little learning happening? The diagnostic literature points to a specific set of interacting causes, and understanding them requires resisting the temptation to identify a single villain.

If you have wondered why your child seems to be falling behind despite attending school every day, the factors below are the most likely structural causes — not your child's ability, and not yours as a parent.

**Teacher quality and subject knowledge.** The most consequential input in any education system is the quality of the teacher. A substantial body of international research — including the work of Eric Hanushek at Stanford, whose findings on teacher effectiveness have reshaped education policy globally — demonstrates that teacher quality is the single most important in-school determinant of learning outcomes. A good teacher can compensate for poor infrastructure, limited materials, and large class sizes. A poor teacher cannot be compensated for by any amount of spending on other inputs.

In South Africa, teacher subject knowledge is a documented crisis. Multiple studies — including research by Nic Spaull at Stellenbosch University, the National Education Evaluation and Development Unit (NEEDU), and the Department of Basic Education's own diagnostic reports — have found that a significant proportion of teachers, particularly in mathematics and science, cannot themselves pass the assessments they are required to teach. The NEEDU diagnostic report found that in a sample of schools, a majority of mathematics teachers at the Foundation Phase level could not correctly answer questions at the level they were teaching. The Spaull research, using SACMEQ teacher assessment data, found that South African mathematics teachers scored lower than their counterparts in several poorer neighbouring countries.

This is not a question of teacher motivation or work ethic, though absenteeism (discussed below) is a separate and significant problem. It is a question of the pipeline that produces teachers. The initial teacher education system — the universities and colleges that train new teachers — has historically produced graduates with inadequate subject knowledge, particularly in mathematics and science. The closure of the teachers' training colleges in the 1990s and the transfer of teacher education to universities was intended to raise the quality of teacher preparation. In practice, it reduced the quantity of new teachers without consistently improving quality, and created a pipeline bottleneck that the system has not fully resolved.

**Teacher absenteeism.** South Africa has one of the highest teacher absenteeism rates in the world. Studies by the Department of Basic Education, the Human Sciences Research Council, and independent researchers have consistently found that between 10 and 20 per cent of teachers are absent from school on any given day, and that a further proportion of teachers who are physically present are not actively teaching — they are in staff rooms, doing administrative tasks, or otherwise not in the classroom. The effective teaching time lost to absenteeism is enormous. Over a school year, the cumulative effect is that many learners receive substantially fewer hours of instruction than the curriculum assumes.

The causes of absenteeism are multiple: illness (including the impact of HIV/AIDS, which disproportionately affects the teaching workforce in some provinces), administrative obligations (training, union meetings, workshops that pull teachers out of classrooms), weak management (principals who do not monitor or enforce attendance), and a culture of impunity in some schools where absenteeism carries no consequences. The interaction between strong union protections and weak school-level management creates an environment where addressing teacher absenteeism is politically and practically difficult.

:::expert[Nic Spaull, Stellenbosch University]
Professor Spaull's research programme, drawing on both international assessment data and South Africa-specific studies, has produced some of the most cited findings on the education crisis. His analysis of the SACMEQ teacher assessment data revealed that South African mathematics teachers scored below their Kenyan and Tanzanian counterparts — countries with dramatically lower education budgets. His work on the "two school systems" thesis provided the empirical foundation for understanding the bimodal distribution of outcomes. And his research on "binding constraints" in education identified teacher content knowledge as the constraint with the highest leverage — the one input that, if improved, would have the largest impact on learning outcomes. Spaull's argument is not that other inputs do not matter but that they matter less than teacher quality, and that South Africa's education debate has been disproportionately focused on inputs (spending, infrastructure, technology) rather than on the one input that the evidence identifies as most consequential.
:::

**Curriculum implementation.** South Africa has undergone several curriculum changes since 1994 — from the initial Outcomes-Based Education (OBE) framework, to the Revised National Curriculum Statement, to the current Curriculum and Assessment Policy Statements (CAPS). Each change was accompanied by rhetoric about improved quality and relevance. Each change also imposed significant adjustment costs on a system with limited adaptive capacity. Teachers who were already struggling with content knowledge were required to learn new pedagogical frameworks. Schools that lacked basic instructional materials were expected to implement curricula designed for well-resourced environments. The gap between the intended curriculum (what CAPS prescribes), the implemented curriculum (what teachers actually teach), and the attained curriculum (what learners actually learn) is enormous in System B schools.

The pace of the CAPS curriculum is itself a problem. CAPS prescribes a specific sequence and pace of instruction — so many topics per term, so many assessments per period. This pace is calibrated for a learner who begins each grade with the foundational knowledge required for that grade. In System B, many learners begin each grade without the foundational knowledge they should have acquired in the previous grade. A Grade 4 teacher who must begin teaching Grade 4 reading comprehension to learners who cannot yet decode words faces an impossible choice: follow the curriculum pace and leave most learners behind, or slow down to address foundational gaps and never cover the prescribed content. Most teachers, under pressure from district officials to cover the curriculum, choose the former. The result is a compounding deficit: learners who were behind get further behind with each passing year.

**School governance and management.** The quality of school leadership — specifically, the effectiveness of the principal and the school governing body — is a strong predictor of school performance. Research in South Africa and globally consistently shows that well-managed schools produce better outcomes than poorly managed schools, even after controlling for socioeconomic factors and resource availability. In South Africa, school management quality is profoundly uneven. Some principals are capable instructional leaders who create cultures of accountability and learning. Others are administrators who occupy the position without the skills or inclination to manage effectively. The appointment of principals is sometimes influenced by political considerations — union affiliations, factional alignments within governing structures — rather than competence.

**Union dynamics.** South Africa's teacher unions — primarily the South African Democratic Teachers' Union (SADTU) and the National Professional Teachers' Organisation of South Africa (NAPTOSA) — are powerful institutional players. SADTU, the larger of the two, is an affiliate of the Congress of South African Trade Unions (COSATU), which is part of the governing tripartite alliance. This political positioning gives SADTU significant influence over education policy, teacher employment conditions, and — critically — the appointment of principals and district officials. The corruption in the teacher appointment process has been documented in detail by multiple investigations, including the ministerial task team report that found evidence of "selling" principal and teaching posts in several provinces. When appointments are driven by patronage rather than competence, the effects cascade through the system: incompetent principals fail to manage effectively, schools underperform, and learners pay the price.

This is not to suggest that unions are the sole or even primary cause of the crisis. Teacher unions in Finland and South Korea are also powerful, and those systems perform well. The difference is the institutional environment in which the unions operate. In a system with strong accountability mechanisms, transparent appointment processes, and effective school oversight, union power can coexist with high performance. In a system where accountability is weak, appointments are opaque, and oversight is patchy, union power can become a vehicle for patronage rather than professional development.

## The Foundation Phase Crisis

If there is a single leverage point in the entire education system — one intervention that would have more impact than any other — it is early-grade reading. If you are a parent of a child in Grade R, 1, 2, or 3, the most important question you can ask their teacher right now is: can my child read a short paragraph aloud and tell me what it means? The answer to that question predicts their educational trajectory more accurately than any test score or report card grade. The evidence on this point is overwhelming and converges from multiple research traditions.

The logic is straightforward. In the Foundation Phase (Grades R to 3), children are learning to read. From Grade 4 onwards, they are reading to learn. The transition from "learning to read" to "reading to learn" is the hinge point of the entire educational trajectory. A child who cannot read with fluency and comprehension by the end of Grade 3 cannot access the curriculum from Grade 4 onwards — because the curriculum is delivered through text. Mathematics problems are presented in words. Science concepts are explained in textbooks. History and geography require reading comprehension. A child who cannot read is locked out of every subject.

In South Africa, the Foundation Phase crisis is the crisis. The PIRLS data confirms that 81 per cent of Grade 4 learners cannot read for meaning. This means that 81 per cent of learners enter the Intermediate Phase (Grades 4 to 6) without the foundational skill required to access the curriculum. From this point, the deficit compounds. Each year, the gap between what the curriculum requires and what the learner can do widens. By Grade 9, the cumulative deficit is so large that meaningful remediation within the normal school system is virtually impossible. The learner may remain in the system — South Africa's progression policies ensure that most learners advance through grades regardless of performance — but they are in the building without being in the learning.

The Foundation Phase crisis has a linguistic dimension that makes it particularly intractable. South Africa has 11 official languages, and language-of-instruction policy requires that learners be taught in their home language in the Foundation Phase, with a transition to English (or Afrikaans) as the language of learning and teaching (LoLT) from Grade 4. This policy is pedagogically sound — the research evidence strongly supports mother-tongue instruction in the early grades. But the implementation is deeply uneven. Many home-language textbooks are of poor quality. Teacher training in home-language literacy instruction is inadequate. And the transition to English in Grade 4 is often abrupt and traumatic for learners whose English proficiency is limited.

The result is a double crisis: learners who are not learning to read effectively in their home language in the Foundation Phase, and who are then expected to read to learn in English from Grade 4 — a language in which many of them are not yet proficient. The linguistic transition compounds the foundational literacy deficit, and the combined effect is the PIRLS catastrophe.

:::expert[Research on Socioeconomic Policy (RESEP), Stellenbosch University]
RESEP's research on early-grade reading in South Africa has produced the most detailed mapping of the Foundation Phase crisis. Using data from the Early Grade Reading Assessment (EGRA) and linked longitudinal studies, RESEP has shown that the reading deficit emerges early — many learners are already significantly behind by the end of Grade 1 — and compounds rapidly. Their research has also documented the "bifurcation point": around Grade 3 or Grade 4, learners effectively sort into two trajectories — those who have achieved basic reading proficiency and continue to develop, and those who have not and plateau or regress. The research implies that interventions after Grade 3 face diminishing returns, because the window for building foundational reading skills is closing. This finding has profound policy implications: it suggests that the highest-return investments in education are those targeted at the Foundation Phase, particularly Grades 1 to 3, where the foundations of all subsequent learning are laid or, more commonly in South Africa, not laid.
:::

## What Works: Evidence from South Africa and Globally

The diagnostic is bleak. But the evidence base on what works is surprisingly robust, and some of the most compelling evidence comes from South Africa itself. What this means for you, as a parent, a community member, or a voter, is that the question worth demanding answers to is not "what are we spending?" but "which of these evidence-based approaches is this school — this district — this department — actually implementing?"

**Structured learning programmes.** The most rigorous evidence from South Africa comes from the Early Grade Reading Study (EGRS), a series of randomised controlled trials conducted in partnership between the Department of Basic Education and research organisations. The EGRS tested the impact of structured learning programmes — lesson plans that prescribe what the teacher should do in each lesson, with accompanying learner materials, training, and coaching support. The results were striking. In the EGRS Phase I trial, learners in schools that received the structured programme showed significant improvements in reading outcomes compared to control schools. The EGRS Phase II trial, which tested similar interventions in Setswana (a home language), also showed positive results. The effect sizes were not small: in some specifications, the structured programme closed one-third to one-half of the gap between the lowest-performing learners and the PIRLS minimum benchmark.

Structured learning programmes work because they address the binding constraint directly. If the primary problem is that teachers lack the content knowledge and pedagogical skill to teach reading effectively, then providing them with a detailed, lesson-by-lesson guide — with scripted lessons, sequenced activities, and aligned materials — compensates for that deficit without requiring years of pre-service training to fix it. Critics of structured programmes argue that they "de-professionalise" teaching by turning teachers into script-readers. The evidence-based response is that in a system where the alternative is no effective reading instruction at all, structured programmes are not a ceiling on teacher quality — they are a floor.

**Teaching at the right level.** The Pratham foundation in India developed a pedagogical approach called "Teaching at the Right Level" (TaRL), which has since been tested in multiple African countries including South Africa. The core insight is simple: in schools where learners are at vastly different levels, teaching to the curriculum grade level means teaching over the heads of the majority. TaRL instead assesses each learner's actual level and groups learners by ability rather than grade, providing targeted instruction at the level each learner is actually at. The evidence from India, where TaRL has been evaluated through multiple randomised controlled trials, shows large and sustained improvements in foundational literacy and numeracy. Pilots in South Africa have shown similar promise, though at smaller scale.

**School leadership development.** Schools with effective principals perform better, even in challenging contexts. The evidence from South Africa includes both quantitative studies (correlating principal quality indicators with school performance) and qualitative case studies of "turnaround" schools — schools that moved from persistent underperformance to acceptable outcomes. The common factor in turnaround cases is almost always leadership: a new principal who establishes expectations, monitors teaching, manages resources effectively, and creates a culture of accountability. Programmes that develop school leadership capacity — including the Department of Basic Education's own Advanced Certificate in Education: School Leadership — have shown positive results, though they have not been implemented at the scale required to transform System B comprehensively.

**What the evidence suggests about improving quality within existing budgets.** Perhaps the most important finding from the evidence base is that the highest-leverage interventions are not the most expensive ones. Structured learning programmes, teaching at the right level, and school leadership development are all interventions that can be implemented within existing budget envelopes — they do not require massive new expenditure. They require different expenditure: redirecting resources from low-impact activities (generic teacher workshops, administrative compliance exercises, hardware procurement) to high-impact activities (structured materials, coaching, leadership development). The evidence suggests that South Africa could achieve significant improvements in learning outcomes without spending a single additional rand — by spending existing resources differently.

:::takeaway
The evidence on what works is surprisingly clear. Structured learning programmes, teaching at the right level, and school leadership development have all shown significant impact in South African contexts. These interventions address the binding constraint — teacher quality — directly, and they can be implemented within existing budgets. The obstacle is not knowledge of what works. It is the political and institutional will to implement it at scale.
:::

## The Human Development Cost

The schooling paradox is not just an education problem. It is a human development problem with cascading consequences across every dimension that this series examines. You see it in the graduate who cannot write a coherent job application. You see it in the employer who cannot find qualified artisans. You see it in the 45 per cent youth unemployment rate that is not, at its root, a jobs problem — it is a capabilities problem.

A generation of learners is passing through the education system and emerging with credentials but not capabilities. They have matric certificates but cannot read a job advertisement. They have years of schooling but not the foundational skills required for post-secondary education, for employment, or for the kind of engaged citizenship that a constitutional democracy requires. They are, in the language of the capabilities approach, formally free — they have rights, they have access, they have certificates — but substantively unfree, because the system that was supposed to develop their capabilities failed to do so.

The economic consequences are explored in detail in [From Classroom to Paycheck](/articles/hd-3-3-from-classroom-to-paycheck). The health consequences are significant too: education is one of the strongest predictors of health outcomes, and the capabilities failure in education translates directly into poorer health literacy, lower uptake of preventive healthcare, and higher vulnerability to the kind of health crises documented in the health articles of this series.

But the deepest cost may be the one that is hardest to measure: the erosion of hope. When a young person spends 12 years in a system that was supposed to prepare them for life and emerges unprepared, the experience is not neutral. It is a betrayal of trust. The state promised education. It delivered schooling. The learner's time was consumed. The capability was not developed. And the learner enters adulthood knowing, at some level, that the system did not do what it was supposed to do — and that the certificate in their hand is not worth what it was supposed to be worth.

This is the schooling paradox's ultimate cost. Not just the fiscal inefficiency. Not just the lost economic productivity. But the erosion of the social contract between the state and its youngest citizens — a contract that says: if you show up, we will teach you; if you learn, you will have opportunities. In South Africa, millions of young people showed up. The system did not teach them. And the opportunities that were supposed to follow from education — employment, health, agency, dignity — remain beyond reach.

The reinforcing cycle that this series traces — from human development to economic growth and back — depends on the education system functioning as the transmission mechanism between investment and capability. When that mechanism is broken, the cycle does not reinforce. It degrades. More spending produces no more learning. No more learning produces no more capability. No more capability produces no more growth. And no more growth produces no more revenue for spending. The schooling paradox is not just a paradox. It is a trap.

:::expert[National Planning Commission]
The National Development Plan 2030, produced by the National Planning Commission and adopted by Cabinet in 2012, identified education as the "apex priority" for South Africa's development trajectory. The NDP's education chapter set specific targets — improving literacy and mathematics outcomes, increasing the number of learners achieving at competent levels in national assessments, improving teacher quality through enhanced initial education and ongoing professional development. More than a decade after its adoption, the NDP's education targets remain largely unmet. The gap between the NDP's vision and the system's performance is a case study in the challenge of converting policy ambition into institutional reality. The NDP identified the right problems and, in many cases, the right solutions. What it could not do — because no document can — is overcome the institutional, political, and structural barriers to implementation. The schooling paradox persists not because we do not know what to do, but because the system's equilibrium resists change.
:::

## What You Can Do With This Knowledge

Understanding the schooling paradox changes what you look for and what you demand. When politicians cite education spending as evidence of commitment, you now know to ask: what learning is that spending producing? When the HDI reports South Africa's education score, you know what it captures and what it misses. When school governing body elections happen in your community, you understand why who runs the school matters more than almost any other local governance decision.

The evidence on what works is public. The data on where the system is failing is available. The question is whether the citizens who fund this system — through their taxes and through the years of their children's lives — will demand that spending translates into learning. The schooling paradox is not inevitable. It is a policy choice sustained by institutional inertia, political interests, and insufficient public demand for change. The next article, [Learning in a War Zone](/articles/hd-3-2-learning-in-a-war-zone), documents the conditions outside the classroom that compound what happens — or fails to happen — inside it.

:::framework
**The School Quality Diagnostic**

Use this framework to assess whether a school — your child's school, a school in your ward, or a school you are evaluating — is producing learning rather than merely providing schooling.

**Indicator 1: Foundation Phase reading outcomes.**
Source: Annual National Assessments (when available); school-level PIRLS results (if the school participated); provincial systemic test results.
Key question: Can the majority of Grade 3 learners at this school read at grade level? If this data is not available, that itself is a red flag — a school that does not measure foundational literacy cannot know whether it is succeeding.

**Indicator 2: Matric results — beyond the pass rate.**
Source: Department of Basic Education's annual NSC results, published per school.
Key question: Do not look only at the pass rate. Look at the bachelor's pass rate (the proportion qualifying for university admission). Look at the mathematics and physical science pass rates. A school with a 90 per cent pass rate but a 15 per cent bachelor's pass rate is passing learners who have not acquired the skills for post-secondary education.

**Indicator 3: Teacher qualifications and subject knowledge.**
Source: School Governing Body reports; provincial education department data.
Key question: Are the teachers teaching subjects they are qualified in? In System B schools, it is common for teachers to be assigned subjects they did not study — a mathematics teacher who never passed university mathematics cannot teach it effectively.

**Indicator 4: School leadership and governance.**
Source: SGB meeting attendance records; school financial reports (which SGBs are legally required to present).
Key question: Does the school have an active, functional SGB? Does the principal attend community meetings? Are financial reports available and comprehensible? School leadership is the strongest single predictor of school performance in similar-context schools.

**Indicator 5: Learning environment.**
Source: Personal observation; Equal Education's school infrastructure reports; Department of Basic Education's NEIMS data.
Key question: Does the school have functional toilets, a library, a laboratory, and safe perimeter fencing? Are classrooms overcrowded (more than 40 learners)? Is the school free from visible gang activity and violence during school hours? These are minimum conditions, not aspirational targets.
:::
