## The Bottom Line, Up Front

The reinforcing cycle is not theoretical. It is not a model that exists only in academic papers and UNDP reports. Multiple countries have built it — from conditions as challenging as, and in several cases more challenging than, South Africa's current position. South Korea began with a per-capita income lower than most of sub-Saharan Africa. Botswana was one of the poorest countries on earth at independence. Rwanda rebuilt from literal genocide. Malaysia managed ethnic tensions and inequality that rival South Africa's own.

What these countries share is not culture, geography, or natural endowment. What they share is a pattern: establish basic safety and institutional order first, channel economic growth into deliberate human development investment, then let improved human development fuel the next phase of growth. Safety enables investment. Investment generates revenue. Revenue funds education and health. Education and health produce a more capable workforce. A more capable workforce attracts higher-value economic activity. Higher-value economic activity generates more revenue. The cycle reinforces itself — each turn building on the last, each link strengthening the others.

This article examines four countries that built this virtuous cycle from positions of profound disadvantage. For each, it traces the sequencing: what they did first, what they did second, what came later, and what conditions enabled each transition. The goal is not to suggest that South Africa should copy any of these models wholesale — the political, historical, and institutional contexts are too different for mechanical transplantation. The goal is to demonstrate that the reinforcing cycle is achievable, to identify the common principles that underlie diverse success stories, and to extract lessons that are relevant to South Africa's specific circumstances.

If you have followed this series from [Beyond GDP](/articles/hd-1-1-beyond-gdp) through the dimensions of human development in Parts 2 and 3, you now understand what human development is, how it is measured, and where South Africa stands on each dimension. Part 4 turns to the system dynamics — how the pieces fit together, why some countries achieve self-reinforcing progress while others experience self-reinforcing decline, and where South Africa falls in that framework.

The evidence from these four countries delivers a message that is simultaneously encouraging and sobering. Encouraging, because the virtuous cycle can be built from conditions of extreme adversity. Sobering, because building it requires sustained political commitment over decades — not election cycles — and demands the kind of institutional discipline that South Africa has intermittently demonstrated but never sustained.

## South Korea: From War to Wonder

In 1953, the Korean War ended with an armistice that left South Korea devastated. The country's infrastructure was destroyed. Its industrial base was obliterated. Per-capita income was approximately $67 — lower than most countries in sub-Saharan Africa at the time. Life expectancy was around 47 years. The adult literacy rate was below 30 per cent. By any measure — GDP, HDI, institutional capacity — South Korea was among the poorest and most damaged countries on earth.

The Human Development Index, had it been calculated for South Korea in 1953, would have been approximately 0.30 — placing it below contemporary figures for Chad, the Central African Republic, and South Sudan. Today, South Korea's HDI stands at 0.929, placing it in the "very high human development" category alongside Norway, Switzerland, and Australia. In seven decades, South Korea achieved a transformation in human welfare that has no historical parallel in terms of speed and comprehensiveness.

How did it happen? The answer is not a single policy or a single leader. The answer is a deliberate, sustained, state-directed reinforcing cycle that was built in identifiable stages over approximately four decades.

### Stage One: Safety and Land Reform (1953-1962)

The first priority was basic physical safety and institutional stabilisation. The armistice with North Korea did not end the threat — South Korea maintained one of the largest standing armies in the world throughout this period, funded substantially by American military aid. But within the country's borders, the state established a monopoly on legitimate violence, suppressed armed banditry and political insurgency, and created the minimal conditions for economic activity to resume.

Simultaneously, South Korea undertook comprehensive land reform. The Land Reform Act of 1949, implemented fully in the early 1950s, redistributed agricultural land from large landlords to tenant farmers. The maximum holding was set at three hectares. Compensation was paid to former landlords in government bonds, but at rates that effectively transferred wealth from the landed class to the farming population. By 1956, owner-cultivated land had increased from approximately 35 per cent to 70 per cent of all farmland.

The development significance of land reform cannot be overstated. It accomplished three things simultaneously. First, it created a broad-based agricultural economy where the majority of rural families had a direct stake in economic stability — a constituency for order rather than revolution. Second, it broke the economic power of the landed aristocracy, removing a class whose interests were tied to the extractive pre-war system. Third, it generated agricultural surplus that could be taxed and channelled into industrial investment. The parallels with South Africa's incomplete land reform programme are uncomfortable and instructive.

### Stage Two: Export-Led Industrialisation (1962-1979)

With basic safety established and agricultural reform providing a foundation, the Park Chung-hee government launched South Korea's industrialisation drive. The strategy was export-oriented industrialisation — producing goods for international markets rather than trying to substitute imports, as many developing countries were doing at the time.

The state played a directing role that would be anathema to free-market orthodoxy. The government selected strategic industries — initially textiles and light manufacturing, later steel, shipbuilding, automobiles, and electronics. It directed credit through state-controlled banks to firms in targeted industries. It provided export subsidies, tax incentives, and preferential access to foreign exchange. It protected domestic markets from foreign competition while demanding that protected firms meet export targets. Firms that met their targets received further support. Firms that failed had their support withdrawn. The system was disciplined — not a blank cheque for connected industrialists, but a performance-based compact between the state and the private sector.

The growth numbers were extraordinary. Between 1962 and 1979, GDP per capita grew at an average of approximately 7 per cent per year in real terms. Manufacturing's share of GDP rose from 14 per cent to 28 per cent. Exports grew from $55 million to $15 billion. Unemployment fell from double digits to below 4 per cent.

But the critical point for the reinforcing cycle is what the government did with the growth. It invested massively in human development.

### Stage Three: Education Investment as Development Strategy (1960s-1990s)

South Korea's education investment was not a side effect of growth. It was a deliberate, state-directed strategy to transform the quality of the labour force to support the next phase of industrialisation. The sequencing was precise.

In the 1960s, the focus was universal primary education. By 1970, primary school enrolment was nearly 100 per cent — remarkable for a country that had been largely illiterate two decades earlier. The government built schools, trained teachers, standardised curricula, and made primary education effectively compulsory.

In the 1970s, the focus shifted to secondary education, particularly technical and vocational training aligned with the industrial strategy. The government expanded secondary schools, created technical high schools linked to the targeted industries, and invested in teacher training colleges. By 1980, secondary school enrolment exceeded 80 per cent.

In the 1980s, the focus shifted again to tertiary education and research. University enrolment expanded dramatically. The Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology — KAIST — was established to produce the engineers and scientists needed for the technology transition. Research and development spending rose from 0.4 per cent of GDP in 1970 to 2.1 per cent by 1990.

Each stage of education investment was linked to the next phase of economic strategy. Primary education produced a literate workforce for labour-intensive manufacturing. Secondary education produced a skilled workforce for heavy industry. Tertiary education produced the engineers, scientists, and managers for the technology transition. The alignment between education policy and industrial policy was not accidental — it was designed.

:::expert[The Role of the Developmental State in South Korea's Transformation]
Development economists and political scientists — including Ha-Joon Chang at the University of Cambridge, Alice Amsden at MIT, and Robert Wade at the London School of Economics — have produced a substantial body of research on what they term the "developmental state" model exemplified by South Korea. The consensus in this literature is that South Korea's success cannot be explained by free markets alone. The state played a directing, disciplining, and investing role that was essential to the transition. This does not mean that markets were irrelevant — the export orientation ensured competitive discipline — but the state shaped the direction of investment, managed the allocation of credit, and invested in human capital at levels that the private sector would not have undertaken independently. For South Africa, the developmental state debate raises a fundamental question: does the South African state have the institutional capacity to play a directing role comparable to South Korea's, or would such an attempt simply create new opportunities for patronage and rent-seeking? The honest answer is that post-state-capture South Africa faces institutional constraints that South Korea did not — but the lesson that deliberate state investment in human capital is essential to the reinforcing cycle remains valid regardless of the specific institutional form it takes.
:::

### Stage Four: The Technology Transition (1990s-Present)

The final stage of South Korea's reinforcing cycle was the transition from heavy industry to technology-intensive production. Samsung, which started as a trading company, became a global leader in semiconductors and consumer electronics. Hyundai moved from construction to automobiles to technology. LG, SK, and other conglomerates similarly moved up the value chain.

This transition was possible only because of the preceding investments in human capital. You cannot build a semiconductor industry without thousands of highly trained engineers. You cannot develop a globally competitive automobile industry without a workforce capable of precision manufacturing and quality control. You cannot sustain a technology sector without continuous research and development, which requires universities producing world-class scientists.

South Korea's HDI trajectory mirrors its economic and education trajectory with remarkable precision. From approximately 0.30 in 1953, it rose to 0.52 by 1975, 0.73 by 1990, 0.86 by 2005, and 0.929 by 2022. Each acceleration in HDI corresponds to a specific phase of the reinforcing cycle — safety and land reform, industrialisation, education expansion, and technology transition.

The lesson is not that South Africa should replicate South Korea's specific policies. The lesson is that sustained, sequenced, state-directed investment in human development — linked to an economic strategy that creates demand for improved human capabilities — produces a self-reinforcing cycle that transforms a country within a generation.

## Botswana: The Diamond That Didn't Curse

If South Korea's story confounds the assumption that rapid development requires abundant natural resources, Botswana's story confounds the assumption that abundant natural resources inevitably curse a developing country. The "resource curse" — the observed tendency for countries rich in minerals and hydrocarbons to experience slower growth, worse governance, and deeper inequality than resource-poor countries — is one of the most robust findings in development economics. Nigeria, Angola, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Venezuela — the list of countries where resource wealth has fuelled corruption, conflict, and institutional decay is long and depressing.

Botswana is the exception that proves the resource curse is not inevitable — it is a product of institutional choices, not geological destiny.

At independence in 1966, Botswana was one of the ten poorest countries in the world. It had twelve kilometres of paved road. It had 22 university graduates. Its per capita income was approximately $70. Life expectancy was around 46 years. The British colonial administration had invested almost nothing in the territory — using it primarily as a labour reserve for South African mines.

Then diamonds were discovered. The Orapa mine opened in 1971, followed by Jwaneng in 1982. Botswana became one of the world's largest diamond producers by value. And instead of experiencing the resource curse, Botswana became the fastest-growing economy in the world between 1966 and 1990, averaging over 9 per cent GDP growth per year.

### How Botswana Escaped the Curse

The answer lies in institutional decisions made at independence and sustained over decades by successive governments. Three decisions were pivotal.

First, the Mines and Minerals Act of 1967 vested all mineral rights in the state, not in private landowners or traditional authorities. This meant that diamond revenues flowed to the national treasury rather than enriching a small class of mineral rights holders. The government then negotiated a joint venture with De Beers — Debswana — in which Botswana held a 50 per cent stake. This arrangement ensured that the state captured a substantial share of diamond rents while benefiting from De Beers' technical expertise and marketing network.

Second, the government established the Revenue Stabilisation Fund and later the Pula Fund — mechanisms for saving a portion of diamond revenues during boom periods to smooth spending during downturns. This counter-cyclical fiscal policy prevented the boom-bust pattern that characterises many resource-dependent economies. It also created a buffer that insulated public spending — including spending on health and education — from commodity price volatility.

Third, and most critically for the reinforcing cycle, the government invested diamond revenues in human development with unusual discipline. Education spending rose from less than 2 per cent of GDP at independence to over 8 per cent by the 1990s. The government built schools across the country, trained teachers (sending many abroad for training in the early years), and made primary and secondary education universally available. Health spending followed a similar trajectory, with a network of clinics and hospitals extending into rural areas.

:::expert[Botswana's Institutional Quality and the Resource Curse Literature]
The academic literature on Botswana's escape from the resource curse — contributed to by Daron Acemoglu, Simon Johnson, James Robinson, and by Botswana-focused scholars such as Ellen Hillbom at Lund University and Scott Beaulier at Troy University — emphasises a factor that is often underappreciated in popular accounts: pre-colonial institutional quality. Botswana's Tswana polities had relatively inclusive institutions, including the kgotla system of consultative decision-making, before colonisation. These pre-colonial institutions provided a foundation for post-independence governance that many other African states lacked. The implication for South Africa is nuanced. South Africa does not lack institutional infrastructure — its Constitution, its judiciary, its Chapter Nine institutions, its financial regulatory system are among the most sophisticated in the developing world. What South Africa lacks is the political culture of institutional discipline that characterised Botswana's post-independence leadership. Institutions exist; the question is whether the political elite will respect and strengthen them or capture and hollow them out.
:::

### The HDI Trajectory

Botswana's HDI trajectory is instructive but also contains a cautionary tale. From approximately 0.28 at independence, Botswana's HDI rose steadily to 0.59 by 1990 — a remarkable trajectory driven by education, health, and income improvements funded by diamond revenues. The reinforcing cycle was operating: mineral wealth funded institutional quality and human development, which supported economic diversification, which broadened the revenue base.

Then HIV/AIDS struck. Botswana's HIV prevalence rate peaked at approximately 27 per cent of the adult population in the early 2000s — one of the highest rates in the world. Life expectancy plummeted from 65 years in 1990 to 46 years by 2002. The HDI dropped from 0.59 to 0.49. Decades of health investment were undone in a single decade by a pandemic that the health system was not equipped to address.

Botswana's response to the HIV crisis, however, demonstrates the resilience of the reinforcing cycle once it is established. The government launched one of Africa's first universal antiretroviral treatment programmes in 2002, funded substantially from diamond revenues and the fiscal buffers the Pula Fund had built. By 2020, Botswana's antiretroviral coverage exceeded 80 per cent. Life expectancy recovered to approximately 62 years. The HDI climbed back to 0.69 by 2022.

The lesson is twofold. First, external shocks can break the reinforcing cycle even in well-governed countries. Second, the institutional and fiscal capacity built during the virtuous cycle enables recovery — a capacity that countries without that foundation lack. When COVID-19 hit South Africa, the state lacked the fiscal buffers, institutional capacity, and social trust that would have enabled a more effective response. That deficit was not an accident — it was the accumulated cost of decades of underinvestment in the reinforcing cycle.

### What Botswana Did Differently

Relative to other resource-rich African states, Botswana made four distinctive choices. It saved rather than spent windfall revenues. It invested in human development rather than prestige projects. It maintained institutional quality rather than allowing state capture. And it diversified its economy — albeit incompletely — rather than assuming diamonds would last forever.

South Africa has diamonds, platinum, gold, manganese, chrome, coal, iron ore, and vanadium. It has a mineral endowment that dwarfs Botswana's. And yet its mineral wealth has not funded a reinforcing cycle of development. The reasons are not geological but institutional: who captures the rents, how they are allocated, and whether they are invested in human capabilities or consumed by patronage, corruption, and waste.

## Rwanda: From Genocide to Growth

Rwanda's inclusion in a discussion of development success is inherently controversial, and it should be. The Rwandan model raises questions about the relationship between democratic governance and development outcomes that are uncomfortable for anyone committed to both values simultaneously. But the development outcomes are real and must be examined honestly.

In April 1994, Rwanda experienced one of the most catastrophic events of the twentieth century. Over approximately 100 days, an estimated 800,000 to one million people were murdered in a genocide targeting the Tutsi population and moderate Hutus. The state collapsed entirely. Infrastructure was destroyed. The economy contracted by over 50 per cent. The social fabric was shredded. Rwanda's HDI, estimated at approximately 0.28, was among the lowest ever recorded for any country.

Three decades later, Rwanda's HDI stands at approximately 0.53 — still in the "low human development" category, but representing an improvement of nearly 90 per cent from the post-genocide nadir. Life expectancy has risen from approximately 28 years in 1994 to over 66 years. Under-five mortality has fallen by more than 70 per cent. Primary school enrolment is near-universal. GDP per capita has increased more than fivefold. Poverty rates have fallen from approximately 78 per cent to under 40 per cent.

These numbers represent real improvements in real people's lives. Understanding how they were achieved — and at what cost — matters for South Africa.

### Stage One: Security and Reconciliation (1994-2000)

The first imperative was physical safety. The Rwandan Patriotic Front, which ended the genocide, established security across the country, disarmed militia groups, and prevented further mass violence. The scale of the security challenge was immense — hundreds of thousands of perpetrators lived alongside survivors, and the risk of retaliatory violence was ever-present.

The Gacaca court system — community-based tribunals that tried genocide cases locally — was a radical institutional innovation. Between 2001 and 2012, Gacaca courts processed approximately 1.9 million cases. The system was imperfect. Critics have raised legitimate concerns about due process, consistency of sentencing, and the conflation of justice with political control. But the system accomplished something that conventional justice mechanisms could not have achieved in a generation: it processed the vast majority of genocide cases, provided a measure of accountability, and — critically for development — allowed the country to move beyond the paralysis that would have resulted from hundreds of thousands of pending criminal cases clogging a destroyed judicial system.

The development relevance is direct. A country where a substantial portion of the adult male population is awaiting trial or imprisoned cannot build a productive economy. A country where survivors live in constant fear of unpunished perpetrators cannot attract investment. The Gacaca system, whatever its flaws, resolved the security-justice bottleneck that would otherwise have trapped Rwanda in a vicious cycle of fear, recrimination, and economic paralysis.

### Stage Two: Governance Reform and Health Investment (2000-2010)

With basic security established, the Rwandan government under Paul Kagame pursued aggressive governance reform and health investment simultaneously.

Governance reform focused on eliminating corruption, improving public service delivery, and creating an efficient state apparatus. Rwanda consistently ranks among the least corrupt countries in Africa on Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index. Government services were digitised. Performance contracts — imihigo — were introduced at every level of government, linking civil servants' evaluations to measurable development outcomes. District mayors who failed to meet health and education targets were publicly held accountable and replaced.

The health investment was centred on the Mutuelle de Santé — Rwanda's community-based health insurance system. Launched in 1999 and expanded nationally by 2006, the Mutuelle covers approximately 91 per cent of the population. Annual premiums are tiered by income — the poorest pay nothing, the wealthiest pay approximately $8 per year. The system covers consultations, hospitalisation, and essential medicines at accredited health facilities.

The results are striking. Rwanda's under-five mortality rate fell from 196 per 1,000 live births in 2000 to approximately 45 by 2020. Maternal mortality fell by over 75 per cent. Malaria deaths fell by over 85 per cent. HIV treatment coverage reached over 90 per cent. These improvements were achieved at a total health expenditure of approximately $55 per capita — a fraction of what South Africa spends.

:::expert[The Rwanda Paradox: Development Without Democracy]
Political scientists and human rights scholars — including Filip Reyntjens at the University of Antwerp, Susan Thomson at Colgate University, and Anjan Sundaram — have documented significant restrictions on political freedom, press freedom, and civil liberties in Rwanda. Opposition politicians have been imprisoned or have disappeared. Independent media operate under severe constraints. The RPF exercises effective one-party control. Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International have documented extrajudicial killings, forced disappearances, and restrictions on freedom of expression. This creates a genuine analytical dilemma. Rwanda's development outcomes are real and have materially improved millions of lives. But they have been achieved under conditions of political repression that are incompatible with the democratic values enshrined in South Africa's Constitution. The question is not whether South Africa should replicate Rwanda's political model — it should not. The question is whether the development outcomes Rwanda has achieved through authoritarian efficiency can be achieved through democratic accountability. The evidence from Botswana and, to a lesser extent, from Costa Rica and Mauritius suggests that democratic development is possible — but it is slower, messier, and requires more sophisticated institutional design. For South Africa, the Rwanda case study is a provocation, not a prescription: it demonstrates what focused governance and health investment can achieve, while raising the question of whether South Africa's democratic institutions can be made to deliver comparable focus and discipline.
:::

### Stage Three: Education and Economic Growth (2010-Present)

Building on improved health outcomes and governance capacity, Rwanda invested heavily in education — particularly in aligning education with economic strategy. The country transitioned its medium of instruction from French to English in 2008, a decision driven by the assessment that English would better connect Rwanda to the East African and global economy. Digital literacy programmes were expanded. Technical and vocational education was reformed to produce graduates with skills demanded by the growing services and manufacturing sectors.

Economic growth has averaged over 7 per cent per year for most of the past two decades. The services sector — particularly information technology, tourism, and financial services — has grown substantially. Kigali has become a hub for international conferences and investment, earning the label "the Singapore of Africa."

The reinforcing cycle in Rwanda operates as follows: security enables governance reform; governance reform enables effective health investment; improved health produces a healthier workforce and reduces household health expenditures; education investment produces a more skilled workforce; the combination of health, education, and governance quality attracts investment; investment generates revenue; revenue funds further development investment.

### The Trade-Offs

Rwanda's development trajectory involved trade-offs that must be acknowledged openly. Political repression. Restrictions on civil society. A concentration of power that creates succession risk — what happens after Kagame? Ethnic identity was effectively banned from public discourse, which some scholars argue suppresses rather than resolves the underlying tensions. These trade-offs are not acceptable models for South Africa, which has a constitutional democracy, an independent judiciary, a free press, and a vibrant civil society.

But the development outcomes demand explanation, not dismissal. If Rwanda can reduce child mortality by 75 per cent at $55 per capita health expenditure, why does South Africa — which spends approximately $500 per capita on public health — achieve worse outcomes on many indicators? The answer lies not in political systems but in institutional effectiveness, corruption, and the quality of implementation. Those are factors that democratic South Africa can address without abandoning democratic governance.

## Malaysia: The Multi-Ethnic Development State

Malaysia's relevance to South Africa is perhaps the most direct of the four case studies. Both countries have deep ethnic divisions. Both have histories of racially structured economic inequality. Both have grappled with affirmative action policies designed to redistribute economic opportunity to historically disadvantaged groups. And both have had to manage the tension between ethnic redress and economic growth.

At independence in 1957, Malaysia was an ethnically stratified society. The Malay majority held political power but was largely rural and economically marginalised. The Chinese minority dominated commerce and urban economies. Indians, brought as plantation workers under British colonialism, occupied the lowest economic rung. The economic structure mirrored South Africa's racial hierarchy in important respects — political power and economic power were held by different groups, creating a combustible dynamic.

In 1969, race riots in Kuala Lumpur killed several hundred people. The riots were a direct consequence of economic inequality that had been allowed to fester since independence. The government's response was the New Economic Policy — the NEP — launched in 1971 with two explicit objectives: reduce poverty irrespective of race, and restructure society to eliminate the identification of race with economic function.

### The NEP: Affirmative Action Plus Growth

The NEP is sometimes characterised as purely redistributive — a policy of taking from the Chinese minority to give to the Malay majority. This characterisation is wrong, and understanding why it is wrong matters for South Africa.

The NEP was redistributive, but it was redistribution within a context of deliberate, rapid economic growth. The explicit strategy was to enlarge the economic pie fast enough that the Malay share could increase without the absolute position of other groups declining. This was not just rhetoric — it was the mathematical logic of the policy. If the economy grows at 8 per cent per year and Malay ownership of corporate equity rises from 2 per cent to 30 per cent over twenty years, the absolute value of Chinese-owned equity still increases substantially. Nobody's absolute wealth declines; what changes is the relative share.

The growth strategy was export-oriented industrialisation, similar in some respects to South Korea's but adapted to Malaysia's circumstances. The government created free trade zones to attract foreign direct investment in electronics manufacturing. It invested heavily in infrastructure — roads, ports, telecommunications — to make Malaysia an attractive manufacturing location. It used petroleum revenues from Petronas, the state oil company, to fund development investment. And it invested massively in education — particularly in Malay-medium universities and technical training institutions — to build the human capital needed for the Malay population to participate meaningfully in the modern economy.

### Education as the Engine of the Cycle

Malaysia's education investment was central to both the equity and the growth objectives of the NEP. The government expanded secondary education dramatically — secondary school enrolment rose from approximately 28 per cent in 1970 to over 70 per cent by 1990. Tertiary education was expanded through new universities, polytechnics, and scholarship programmes that sent tens of thousands of Malaysian students to universities in the United Kingdom, Australia, and the United States.

The Majlis Amanah Rakyat — MARA — was established to provide educational support specifically for Bumiputera (ethnic Malay) students. Residential schools, scholarship programmes, and preferential university admission policies were used to accelerate Malay educational attainment. These policies were controversial — they created resentment among Chinese and Indian Malaysians who faced higher barriers to university admission — but they were effective in building a Malay professional class that could participate in the modern economy.

The results were transformative. Bumiputera ownership of corporate equity rose from 2.4 per cent in 1970 to approximately 19 per cent by 1990. The poverty rate fell from 49 per cent in 1970 to 8 per cent by 1995. The Malay share of professional and technical employment rose from approximately 5 per cent to over 30 per cent. Malaysia's HDI rose from approximately 0.47 in 1970 to 0.72 by 1995 and 0.803 by 2022.

:::expert[Malaysia's NEP and South Africa's BEE: A Comparative Assessment]
Comparative studies by scholars including Edmund Terence Gomez at the University of Malaya, Vishnu Padayachee at the University of KwaZulu-Natal, and Roger Memory Sobane at Stellenbosch have examined the parallels between Malaysia's NEP and South Africa's BEE policies. The critical difference these scholars identify is not in the equity objectives — which are broadly similar — but in the growth context. Malaysia's NEP operated during a period of sustained economic growth averaging 7-8 per cent per year, which meant that redistribution could occur without shrinking the absolute economic position of any group. South Africa's BEE has operated during a period of low growth — averaging below 2 per cent per year for much of the 2010s and 2020s — which means that redistribution has often been zero-sum or negative-sum. When the pie is not growing, every slice given to one group comes from another, generating resentment and resistance. The implication is not that BEE should be abandoned but that BEE without growth is economically and politically unsustainable. The Malaysian lesson is that equity and growth must be pursued simultaneously, with each reinforcing the other — not sequentially, and never in the expectation that equity alone will generate growth.
:::

### The Reinforcing Cycle in Practice

Malaysia's reinforcing cycle operated as follows. The NEP established social stability by addressing the inequality that had produced the 1969 riots. Social stability — the safety link — enabled domestic and foreign investment. Investment drove growth. Growth funded education and health investment. Education produced a more skilled workforce. A more skilled workforce attracted higher-value FDI — Malaysia moved from simple assembly operations in the 1970s to semiconductor fabrication and electronics design in the 1990s. Higher-value production generated higher incomes and tax revenues. Higher revenues funded further development investment. Each turn of the cycle strengthened the next.

The result, over five decades, was a transformation from a low-income, ethnically fractured, commodity-dependent economy into an upper-middle-income, industrially diversified economy with an HDI in the "very high" category. Not without problems — Malaysia faces challenges of institutional quality, democratic governance, and environmental sustainability that are real and serious. But the development trajectory is undeniable.

## The Common Pattern

Stepping back from the specifics of each case, a common pattern emerges. It is not identical across countries — the details vary enormously — but the structural logic is consistent.

### Principle One: Safety and Stability First

Every country that built a successful reinforcing cycle established basic safety and institutional order before attempting sustained economic growth or large-scale human development investment. South Korea established physical security and land reform. Botswana built institutional quality from pre-colonial foundations. Rwanda achieved security through military victory and the Gacaca process. Malaysia addressed the ethnic instability that produced the 1969 riots through a combination of political accommodation and economic policy.

This sequencing is not ideological — it is pragmatic. Investment, whether domestic or foreign, requires a minimum level of predictability. Human development programmes require institutional capacity to design and implement. Neither is possible in conditions of generalised violence, institutional collapse, or social instability. The safety-first principle does not mean that development must wait until conditions are perfect — it means that a threshold level of order is necessary before the other elements of the cycle can function.

For South Africa, this principle has direct implications. The country's safety crisis — documented in the safety and security series from [Order Through Oppression](/articles/ss-1-1-order-through-oppression) through the reform proposals in Part 4 — is not merely a quality-of-life issue. It is a development issue. High crime drives investment flight, skills emigration, and fiscal reallocation from development spending to security spending. Addressing the safety deficit is not a precondition that must be completed before development can begin — but it is a necessary component of any reinforcing cycle. The two must advance together, with safety improvements enabling development investments that in turn reduce the conditions that produce crime.

### Principle Two: State-Directed Investment in Human Capital

In every case, the state played a central role in directing investment toward human development — particularly education and health. The private sector did not do this independently. Markets, left to their own devices, underinvest in human capital because the returns are diffuse, long-term, and partially captured by people other than the investor. A company that trains a worker bears the cost but may lose the benefit when the worker moves to a competitor. A family that invests in a child's education bears the cost for eighteen years before seeing returns. These dynamics create systematic underinvestment in human capital that only the state can correct.

South Korea invested massively in education at each stage of its industrial development. Botswana channelled diamond revenues into schools and clinics. Rwanda built a national health insurance system at minimal cost. Malaysia used NEP instruments to expand education access for the Bumiputera majority. In every case, the scale of human development investment far exceeded what the private sector would have undertaken independently.

For South Africa, the implication is not that the state should spend more — South Africa already allocates a substantial share of its budget to education and health, approximately 20 per cent and 12 per cent respectively. The implication is that the state must spend more effectively. South Africa's education spending per student, adjusted for purchasing power, is among the highest in the developing world. Its education outcomes are among the worst. The gap between spending and outcomes — the implementation deficit — is the critical constraint, not the level of funding.

### Principle Three: Growth Strategy Linked to Development Outcomes

Each successful country linked its growth strategy explicitly to human development outcomes. South Korea's industrial policy demanded a workforce with specific skills, and the education system was redesigned to produce those skills. Malaysia's FDI strategy required English-speaking technical workers, and the education system was reformed accordingly. Botswana used mineral revenues to fund universal education and health services, creating the human capital base for economic diversification. Rwanda aligned its education reforms — including the switch to English-language instruction — with its economic strategy of becoming a services and technology hub.

The link between growth strategy and human development is not automatic. Many countries have achieved economic growth without human development — the Gulf oil states being the most obvious example — and some have achieved elements of human development without sustained growth — Cuba being the classic case. The reinforcing cycle requires deliberate alignment: the growth strategy must demand improved human capabilities, and the development strategy must supply them.

### Principle Four: Long Time Horizons

Every successful case operated on a time horizon of twenty to forty years. South Korea's transformation from 1953 to 1995 — approximately four decades. Botswana's post-independence trajectory — five decades and counting. Rwanda's post-genocide development — now three decades. Malaysia's NEP — explicitly designed as a twenty-year programme.

This time horizon is incompatible with the electoral cycle in democratic countries, which creates a structural challenge. Politicians face elections every four or five years and have incentives to prioritise policies with short-term visible benefits over long-term development investments whose returns materialise after they have left office. Building schools in rural areas produces no votes in the next election. Expanding a feeding scheme in an urban constituency produces votes immediately.

The countries that solved this problem did so in different ways. South Korea and Malaysia had authoritarian governments during key phases of the development cycle, which insulated economic policy from short-term electoral pressures. Botswana had a dominant party — the BDP — that won every election from independence until 2019, providing continuity without authoritarianism. Rwanda has effectively one-party rule.

For South Africa, which has a vibrant multi-party democracy and has already experienced a change of governing party (through coalition), the challenge is to build institutional mechanisms that sustain long-term development investments across changes in government. This requires embedding development commitments in legislation and institutional structures that are harder to reverse than policy documents — a point explored further in [Your Development Rights](/articles/hd-5-1-your-development-rights).

### Principle Five: Institutional Quality as Enabler

The reinforcing cycle does not function without institutional quality. State-directed investment requires a state capable of directing investment effectively — identifying strategic priorities, designing programmes, allocating resources, monitoring implementation, and holding implementers accountable. A state captured by patronage networks cannot do this. A state hollowed out by corruption cannot do this. A state that lacks technical capacity cannot do this.

Botswana's institutional quality was exceptional for a post-colonial African state. South Korea's bureaucracy, despite operating under authoritarian political leadership, was meritocratic and technically competent — entry to the senior civil service required passing examinations that were among the most competitive in the world. Rwanda's governance reforms created a performance-driven public service. Malaysia's civil service, while not immune to ethnic favouritism, maintained a core of technical competence.

South Africa's institutional challenge is that it once had substantial institutional capacity — in the Treasury, the South African Revenue Service, the Reserve Bank, the judiciary, and elements of the provincial and national civil service — and has seen that capacity degraded through a combination of cadre deployment, state capture, and skills flight. Rebuilding institutional capacity is not glamorous, and it is not fast. But without it, the reinforcing cycle cannot function, because the state cannot convert policy intentions into development outcomes.

## What South Africa Can Learn

South Africa has advantages that none of these four countries had at the beginning of their development trajectories. It has a Constitution widely regarded as one of the most progressive in the world. It has an independent judiciary that has repeatedly demonstrated its willingness to hold the executive accountable. It has sophisticated financial markets and regulatory institutions. It has a mineral endowment of extraordinary diversity and value. It has world-class universities and research institutions. It has a free press and vibrant civil society.

South Africa also has disadvantages that these countries did not face. The most significant is the depth and complexity of its inequality — not merely income inequality, but inequality structured along racial, spatial, and gender lines by three and a half centuries of colonialism and apartheid. Botswana was poor but relatively equal at independence. South Korea was devastated but did not face racially structured inequality. Malaysia had ethnic economic stratification, but at a scale significantly smaller than South Africa's.

The second disadvantage is the institutional damage inflicted by the state capture period. Whatever institutional capacity South Africa had built by the mid-2000s was substantially degraded between 2009 and 2018. Rebuilding it will take a decade or more.

The third disadvantage is the political economy of reform in a mature democracy with powerful interest groups. South Korea and Malaysia implemented key reforms under authoritarian governments that could override opposition. South Africa must build consensus — a slower, more difficult, and more fragile process, but one that is more likely to produce durable outcomes if it succeeds.

The lessons from the four case studies are not prescriptions but principles. Establish safety and institutional order as foundations for the cycle. Invest in human development at scale, with deliberate alignment between education policy and economic strategy. Link growth strategy to development outcomes so that each reinforces the other. Maintain the commitment over decades, not election cycles. And build and protect the institutional capacity needed to convert policy into outcomes.

## The Uncomfortable Lessons

There is a pattern in the case studies that must be confronted directly. Three of the four countries — South Korea, Malaysia, and Rwanda — achieved key phases of their development under authoritarian or semi-authoritarian governance. Only Botswana maintained a democratic system throughout, and even Botswana's democracy was characterised by single-party dominance that some scholars describe as soft authoritarianism.

This raises the question that Amartya Sen addressed in his work on development as freedom: is authoritarianism necessary for rapid development? Sen's answer — supported by substantial evidence — is no. Democratic governance and rapid development are compatible. India, despite its democratic messiness, has achieved substantial development gains. Costa Rica, a stable democracy with no army, has achieved an HDI of 0.806 with a fraction of the ecological footprint of comparable countries. Mauritius, a multi-ethnic island democracy, has built a diversified economy and high human development from a post-colonial base.

But the honest assessment is that democratic development is harder. It requires more sophisticated institutional design. It requires political leaders willing to invest political capital in reforms whose benefits accrue after they leave office. It requires citizens who understand the reinforcing cycle well enough to hold governments accountable for long-term development outcomes, not just short-term visible spending. And it requires institutional mechanisms — independent central banks, constitutional fiscal rules, statutory development commitments — that partially insulate development investments from the short-term incentives of the electoral cycle.

South Africa does not need an authoritarian government to build a reinforcing cycle. But it does need a citizenry that understands what the cycle is, how it works, and what it demands of government — which is, in part, the purpose of this series.

## The Sequencing Question

You cannot do everything at once. This is perhaps the most important practical lesson from the case studies. Every successful country made deliberate choices about sequencing — what to prioritise first, what could wait, and how to manage the transitions between phases.

The evidence from the four cases suggests a general sequencing framework, though the specifics must be adapted to each country's circumstances.

First, establish the minimum viable level of safety and institutional order. This does not mean eliminating all crime or building perfect institutions. It means achieving a threshold below which the other elements of the cycle cannot function — basic physical security, predictable property rights, a functioning judicial system, and a state that can collect revenue and allocate it through a budget process.

Second, invest in basic health and education simultaneously with the pursuit of economic growth. Do not wait for growth to fund development — the evidence suggests that basic human development investment (primary education, primary healthcare, clean water, sanitation) is affordable even at low income levels and actually enables growth rather than depending on it.

Third, align education investment with economic strategy as the economy develops. This is where the long-term sequencing matters — building the education system to produce the skills the economy needs at each stage of development, rather than treating education policy and economic policy as separate domains.

Fourth, use growth to fund increasingly sophisticated development investments — secondary and tertiary education, specialist healthcare, research and development — that in turn support higher-value economic activity. This is the self-reinforcing phase, where the cycle begins to generate its own momentum.

Fifth, protect the cycle institutionally. Embed development commitments in legislation. Build fiscal buffers that insulate development spending from economic shocks. Create independent institutions that monitor development outcomes and hold government accountable. Make the reinforcing cycle hard to reverse.

South Africa is not starting from zero. It has elements of each stage already in place — an advanced constitution, substantial education and health infrastructure, a diversified economy, sophisticated financial institutions. What it lacks is not the components of the cycle but the alignment, sequencing, and institutional discipline to make them reinforce each other rather than working at cross purposes.
