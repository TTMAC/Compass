## The Bottom Line, Up Front

South Africa's 257 municipalities are almost entirely absent from the justice architecture. As [Municipal Councils](/articles/3-3-municipal-councils) documents, municipalities are the sphere of government closest to communities — responsible for water, electricity, refuse removal, roads, and the daily infrastructure of life. Yet when it comes to justice — the resolution of disputes, the adjudication of minor offences, the restoration of community order — municipalities have no role. There is no municipal court. There is no community justice officer. There is no local mechanism for resolving the petty theft, the domestic dispute, the liquor-related altercation, the trespassing complaint, or the minor assault that constitutes the vast majority of criminal matters entering the system.

## The Missing Tier of Justice

Instead, every criminal matter — from a R50 shoplifting case to a complex murder — enters the same magistrates' court pipeline. Magistrates' courts handle approximately 95 per cent of all criminal cases in South Africa, yet they have the least resources, the most overloaded rolls, and the longest backlogs. As [The Broken Pipeline](/articles/ss-2-1-the-broken-pipeline) documented, case postponements are the norm rather than the exception, with cases routinely adjourned five, ten, or fifteen times before reaching trial. As [Justice Delayed, Rights Denied](/articles/ss-2-3-justice-delayed-rights-denied) documented, this backlog drives the remand detention crisis — tens of thousands of accused persons waiting years for trials that the system cannot schedule.

A municipal justice layer — community courts handling summary offences, incorporating restorative justice principles, and staffed by a new cadre of Community Justice Officers — could decongest the formal courts, deliver faster and more legitimate justice for minor offences, and reconnect the justice system to the communities it serves. This is not an exotic proposal. Community courts and restorative justice programmes operate successfully in dozens of countries. South Africa itself has precedents — the Hatfield Community Court in Pretoria has demonstrated the model's viability, and the Child Justice Act's diversion provisions show that the legal framework for alternative justice pathways already exists.

## The Case-Flow Triage System

Before examining community courts in detail, you need to understand how they fit into the broader case-flow architecture. The current system processes all criminal matters through essentially the same pipeline. The reform proposes a triage system — borrowed from hospital emergency department logic — in which every criminal matter entering the system is classified within 72 hours of arrest.

The triage sorts cases into four tracks. Track A covers summary offences and cases suitable for diversion — these flow to community courts for rapid resolution. Track B covers standard criminal matters requiring formal prosecution — these proceed through magistrates' courts as currently. Track C covers complex and serious matters — these proceed through provincial High Courts with Judicial Investigation Officers directing the investigation, as proposed in [An Independent Investigating Magistracy](/articles/ss-3-2-independent-investigating-magistracy). Track D covers national priority matters (organised crime, terrorism, major corruption) — these go to specialised national courts.

Each track has defined time limits. Track A matters must be resolved within 90 days. Track B within 18 months. Track C within 24 months. Track D within 30 months. If a case exceeds its track's time limit, charges are provisionally stayed pending review — constitutionalising the right to a speedy trial in a practical, enforceable way.

The triage system's impact on court congestion would be dramatic. If even 30 to 40 per cent of current magistrates' court matters could be diverted to Track A (community courts and diversion), the remaining tracks would have substantially more resources — more magistrate time, more courtroom availability, more prosecutorial attention — per case. The quality of justice for the serious matters that remain in the formal system would improve because the system would no longer be drowning in cases that do not require its full apparatus.

:::takeaway
The case-flow triage system classifies every criminal matter within 72 hours into four tracks: Track A (summary/diversion via community court), Track B (standard prosecution via magistrate), Track C (complex/serious via High Court with JIO), Track D (national priority via specialised court). Each track has defined time limits. Diverting 30–40 per cent of current matters to Track A would dramatically decongest the formal courts, improving the quality of justice for cases that remain in the system.
:::

## The Community Court Model

Municipal Community Justice Courts would be established under an amended Magistrates' Courts Act, empowered to handle summary offences with sentences up to two years. They would operate within the municipal governance framework — located in municipal buildings, funded partly through municipal budgets (supplemented by national conditional grants), and accountable to the local community through the same democratic structures that govern other municipal functions.

The courts would be presided over by Community Justice Officers (CJOs) — a new para-legal profession requiring a minimum of a three-year legal diploma (not a full LLB), specialised training in community justice, restorative justice facilitation, and dispute resolution. CJOs would be legally trained but not full magistrates — they would handle a defined category of summary offences and would refer matters that exceed their jurisdiction to the magistrates' court. The CJO training programme would draw on South Africa's existing para-legal training infrastructure, expanded to include the specific competencies required for community adjudication.

The offences within community court jurisdiction would include petty theft (below defined value thresholds), common assault (without serious injury), malicious damage to property, trespassing, liquor-related offences, certain drug possession charges, noise complaints with criminal dimensions, and other summary matters that currently clog the magistrates' courts without requiring a qualified magistrate's attention. The community court would have the power to impose fines, community service orders, compensation orders (requiring the offender to compensate the victim), restorative justice processes, and custodial sentences of up to two years (with a right of appeal to the magistrates' court).

The courts would be electronically linked to the national criminal records system, ensuring that community court convictions are recorded and that repeat offenders are identified and escalated to the magistrates' court when appropriate. This integration is essential — the community court must be part of the formal justice system, not a parallel informal system operating without records or accountability.

:::expert
The Centre for the Study of Violence and Reconciliation (CSVR) has researched community justice models across Africa and internationally, including South Africa's own experiences with the Hatfield Community Court and the diversion programmes established under the Child Justice Act. Their analysis has found that community courts achieve higher satisfaction rates among both victims and offenders than formal magistrates' courts for summary offences, primarily because community courts provide faster resolution (weeks rather than months or years), more participatory processes (victims have a voice in the outcome), and more meaningful outcomes (compensation and community service rather than fines that offenders cannot pay or short custodial sentences that disrupt lives without achieving any rehabilitative purpose). The CSVR has emphasised that the success of community courts depends on adequate resourcing, professional training of adjudicators, and genuine integration with the formal system — community courts that are established as under-resourced, informal alternatives to the formal system tend to produce second-class justice. Their research supports the model proposed here: community courts as a formal tier of the justice architecture, with defined jurisdiction, trained officers, and full integration with the criminal records system.
:::

## Restorative Justice: What It Is and How It Works

Restorative justice is a framework for responding to crime that focuses on repairing the harm caused rather than simply punishing the offender. In a restorative justice process, the victim, the offender, and affected community members come together — facilitated by a trained mediator — to discuss the impact of the offence, hear the offender's acceptance of responsibility, and agree on actions that the offender will take to repair the harm.

Restorative justice is not a soft option. When done properly, it is harder on the offender than the conventional system, because it requires them to face the person they harmed, hear the impact of their actions, and commit to meaningful reparation. A community service order imposed through a restorative justice process — in which the offender has heard directly from the victim and agreed to specific reparation — carries more moral weight and produces better compliance than a sentence imposed by a magistrate who heard the facts for fifteen minutes.

South Africa already has legislative frameworks that recognise restorative justice. The Child Justice Act of 2008 provides for diversion of juvenile offenders into restorative justice programmes as an alternative to prosecution. The Probation Services Act provides for victim-offender mediation. The Correctional Services Act recognises restorative justice as a component of rehabilitation. What is missing is the institutional infrastructure to deliver restorative justice at scale — and the community court provides that infrastructure.

Under the proposed model, restorative justice would be a formal sentencing input available to the community court. For appropriate cases — where the offender accepts responsibility, where the victim consents to participate, and where the offence is suitable for restorative resolution — the CJO could order a restorative justice process facilitated by trained mediators. The outcomes of the process — compensation agreements, community service commitments, behavioural undertakings — would be incorporated into the court's order and would be enforceable. Non-compliance with a restorative justice order would result in the matter being escalated to conventional sentencing.

Restorative justice is not appropriate for all offences. Serious violent crimes, sexual offences, and offences involving significant power imbalances between victim and offender generally require formal prosecution through the conventional system. The community court's jurisdiction would define the boundaries — Track A matters (summary offences and suitable diversions) are the appropriate domain for restorative justice, while Track B, C, and D matters proceed through the formal system.

## Traditional Justice: The Constitutional Integration Challenge

South Africa's traditional justice systems — dispute resolution mechanisms administered by traditional leaders under customary law — have operated alongside the formal justice system since long before colonialism. In many rural communities, traditional courts are the primary mechanism for resolving disputes and addressing minor offences. The Constitutional Recognition of Traditional Courts Bill and the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act provide legislative frameworks that recognise the role of traditional justice within the broader legal system.

The community court model provides an opportunity to integrate traditional justice mechanisms into a formal, constitutionally compliant framework. In areas where traditional leaders exercise dispute resolution functions, the community court could incorporate traditional justice processes as a recognised pathway within Track A — provided that the processes meet constitutional requirements.

The constitutional integration challenge is primarily about gender equality. Traditional justice systems have historically operated under patriarchal norms that disadvantage women — particularly in matters involving domestic violence, inheritance, and family law. Section 9 of the Constitution guarantees the right to equality, and Section 211 (which recognises customary law) is subject to the Bill of Rights. Any integration of traditional justice into the community court framework must ensure that constitutional rights — particularly gender equality — are protected. This means that traditional justice processes within the community court framework would be subject to oversight by the CJO, who would have the authority to intervene if a process produced an outcome that violated constitutional rights.

:::takeaway
Traditional justice mechanisms — already operational in many rural communities — can be integrated into the community court framework, provided they meet constitutional requirements. The CJO provides the oversight mechanism: traditional processes are recognised as legitimate pathways for dispute resolution within Track A, but they operate under the community court's authority, ensuring that constitutional rights (particularly gender equality) are protected. This integration respects traditional institutions while maintaining constitutional supremacy.
:::

## The Community Justice Map

If you want to engage with community justice in your area — whether that means accessing existing diversion programmes, advocating for a community court, or participating in restorative justice processes — this framework shows you what is currently available and where to push for change.

:::framework
**The Community Justice Map**

**What Exists Now:**
South Africa has limited but real community justice infrastructure. The Hatfield Community Court (Pretoria) operates as a pilot model for community-level adjudication. Child Justice Act diversion programmes operate in many jurisdictions, providing alternative pathways for juvenile offenders. Victim-Offender Mediation is available through the Department of Social Development's probation services in some areas. Community Advice Offices (funded by the Foundation for Human Rights and others) provide para-legal assistance and dispute resolution in many communities.

**How to Find Community Justice Resources in Your Area:**
Contact the Department of Social Development's local office and ask about diversion programmes, victim-offender mediation, and probation services. Contact your local Legal Aid South Africa office for information about alternative dispute resolution. Contact Community Advice Offices (a directory is available through the Association of Community Advice Offices). Ask your ward councillor whether any community justice or dispute resolution programmes operate in your ward.

**How to Advocate for a Community Court:**
If your area does not have community justice infrastructure, advocate for it through your municipal council. [Municipal Councils](/articles/3-3-municipal-councils) explains how municipal governance works and how to engage with council processes. Raise the concept of a community court at your Community Police Forum — CPFs are a natural starting point for community safety discussions. Contact the Department of Justice and Constitutional Development to ask about plans for community court pilots. Engage your Member of Parliament through the mechanisms described in [Making Public Submissions](/articles/4-3-making-public-submissions) to advocate for the legislative amendments that would enable community courts.

**The Legislative Changes Required:**
Community courts require amendment to the Magistrates' Courts Act (to create the jurisdiction), new enabling legislation (to establish the CJO profession and define community court procedures), and amendments to the Criminal Procedure Act (to establish the case-flow triage system). [Sequencing Reform](/articles/ss-4-1-sequencing-reform) maps these legislative requirements in detail.
:::
