## What the Constitution Intended vs What We Got

<!-- Section: The 1996 constitutional design logic vs the 2026 reality -->

:::framework
**The Provincial Performance Scorecard**

Use this framework to assess your own province's governance performance across its key functions — and to determine whether the failures you see are people problems (fixable by replacing officials) or design problems (requiring structural reform).

**Step 1: Identify your province's key concurrent functions.**

List the major services your provincial government delivers: basic education, health services, social development, roads and transport, human settlements, agriculture, and public works. These are the Schedule 4 functions where your province is responsible for delivery but shares authority with national government.

**Step 2: For each function, rate the eight RAARICLE elements on a scale of 1 to 5.**

Use the following scale: 1 = element is absent or entirely dysfunctional; 2 = element exists on paper but fails in practice; 3 = element functions partially with significant gaps; 4 = element functions well with minor gaps; 5 = element is fully co-located and effective. Rate each function across all eight elements: Responsibility, Accountability, Authority, Resources, Information, Capability, Legitimacy, and Enforcement. This gives you an 8-column scorecard for each function.

**Step 3: Check the Auditor-General's findings for your province.**

Look up the most recent AG audit outcome for each provincial department. Note whether the department received a clean audit, an unqualified opinion with findings, a qualified opinion, an adverse opinion, or a disclaimer. Map the AG's root cause findings — "lack of consequences," "slow response to poor audit outcomes," "key positions vacant or filled by unqualified personnel" — to the RAARICLE elements they correspond to.

**Step 4: Compare your province to the best-performing province for each function.**

For education, compare your province's matric pass rate, bachelor-pass rate, and district performance variation to the top performer. For health, compare your province's Ideal Clinic scores, maternal mortality ratio, and medicine availability to the best province. The comparison will reveal whether the performance gap is narrow (suggesting a people or management issue) or wide (suggesting a structural issue).

**Step 5: Check for the Section 100 pattern.**

Has your province ever been subject to a Section 100 national intervention in any department? If so, what was the outcome — did the intervention produce sustained improvement, or did performance revert after the intervention ended? If interventions fail to produce lasting improvement, the problem is structural, not managerial. Replacing the people without changing the design will produce the same results.

**Step 6: Identify whether the problem is provincial or systemic.**

If your province underperforms while others with the same constitutional structure succeed, the problem may be provincial — leadership, management, or capability. But if most provinces underperform on the same functions (as is the case with education, health, and housing), the problem is systemic — the constitutional design creates conditions that make provincial failure likely regardless of who governs.

This scorecard gives you a structured, evidence-based view of your province's performance that goes beyond anecdote and partisan blame — enabling you to distinguish between failures that require better people and failures that require a better design.
:::
