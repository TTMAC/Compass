## The Bottom Line, Up Front

The previous four articles in this series applied the RAARICLE™ framework to individual functions — [education](/articles/ra-2-1-fixing-basic-education), [healthcare](/articles/ra-2-2-fixing-primary-healthcare), [human settlements](/articles/ra-2-3-fixing-human-settlements), and [safety and security](/articles/ra-2-4-fixing-safety-and-security). Each produced a diagnosis of structural failure. Education, healthcare, and housing suffer from fragmentation and cross-sphere misalignment; safety and security suffers from the opposite pathology — excessive centralisation. All four identified the absence of enforcement as a root cause. Each proposed a reform blueprint that would co-locate authority with accountability at the delivery level.

## The Provincial RAARICLE™ Scorecard

But step back and look at the four diagnoses together, and a pattern emerges that is larger than any single function. The same RAARICLE™ elements fail consistently across all four — and across other provincial functions that this series has not yet examined in detail. Authority scores 1-2 across education, healthcare, housing, and safety. Enforcement scores 1 across all four. Capability scores 2 across all four. Information scores 1-3 depending on the function, but nowhere above moderate tension.

This consistency tells you something important: the problem is not function-specific. It is sphere-level. There is something about the way provincial government is designed — its relationship to national authority, its fiscal position, its enforcement architecture, its capability base — that produces systematic underperformance regardless of which function you examine. A province that underperforms on education also tends to underperform on health, housing, and roads. A province with a strong capability base tends to perform better across all functions. The variation is between provinces, not between functions within a province.

To make this pattern visible, consider a diagnostic scorecard that rates all eight RAARICLE™ elements across the four largest provincial functions: education, health, human settlements, and provincial roads. The ratings draw on the detailed diagnoses in the preceding articles and on the evidence presented in this one.

| Function | Responsibility | Accountability | Authority | Resources | Information | Capability | Legitimacy | Enforcement |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Education | 2 | 2 | 2 | 3 | 3 | 2 | 4 | 1 |
| Health | 2 | 2 | 2 | 3 | 3 | 2 | 3 | 1 |
| Human Settlements | 1 | 2 | 1 | 2 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 1 |
| Provincial Roads | 3 | 3 | 3 | 2 | 2 | 2 | 4 | 2 |

The pattern is unmistakable. Authority, enforcement, and capability are weak across every function. Resources are constrained across every function. Information is inconsistent. Only legitimacy — the constitutional mandate — consistently scores at or above moderate. The provincial sphere has a clear right to govern. It lacks the structural conditions to govern effectively.

:::takeaway
The provincial RAARICLE™ scorecard reveals a sphere-level design problem, not function-specific management failures. The same elements — authority, enforcement, and capability — score poorly across education, health, housing, and roads. This consistency means that fixing any single function in isolation will not solve the problem. The provincial sphere itself needs structural reform.
:::

## The Structural Problem

Why do provinces consistently underperform? The answer lies in the constitutional design of the provincial sphere and its relationship to national government — a design that was explored in [The Awkward Middle Child](/articles/3-1-the-awkward-middle-child) and that the RAARICLE™ framework now diagnoses with precision.

The Constitution lists the most important service delivery functions — education, health, housing, public transport, environment, agriculture — as Schedule 4 concurrent competences. "Concurrent" means both national and provincial government have legislative authority over these functions. In practice, this creates a structural condition where national government sets the framework (legislation, policy, standards, employment conditions, conditional grant rules) and provincial government implements within that framework. The province is accountable for delivery but does not control the design parameters that determine whether delivery succeeds or fails.

This is the misalignment pattern identified in [Why South Africa's Institutions Keep Failing](/articles/ra-1-2-why-institutions-fail) — and at the sphere level, it is not a bug in a single function's institutional architecture. It is a feature of the constitutional design itself. The concurrency model was adopted in 1996 as a compromise between those who wanted a unitary state (with a strong central government and weak provinces) and those who wanted a federal state (with constitutionally entrenched provincial autonomy). The compromise gave provinces the appearance of autonomy — their own legislatures, executives, and budgets — while retaining national control over the critical levers of service delivery. The result is a sphere of government that looks autonomous but operates as a constrained agent of national government.

The fiscal dimension deepens the structural problem. As documented in [Following the Money](/articles/2-1-following-the-money), provinces raise almost no revenue of their own — less than 5 per cent of their budgets in most cases. They depend almost entirely on transfers from national government through the equitable share and conditional grants. This fiscal dependence means that the accountability relationship runs upward (to National Treasury) rather than downward (to the citizens the province serves). A province that funded itself through taxes on its residents would face direct electoral consequences for poor service delivery — residents who pay for services expect to receive them. A province that receives 96 per cent of its funding from national transfers faces a weaker accountability link to its residents. The money arrives from Pretoria regardless of performance.

The enforcement architecture completes the structural trap. When a province fails — when educational outcomes decline, when health services deteriorate, when housing delivery stalls — what happens? Section 100 of the Constitution provides for national intervention in provincial affairs when a province "cannot or does not fulfil" an executive obligation. But Section 100 is discretionary — the national executive decides whether to intervene, when to intervene, and how to intervene. There are no automatic triggers, no prescribed thresholds, and no mandatory consequences. The decision to invoke Section 100 is political, and political decisions are subject to political calculations. A national government may be reluctant to intervene in a province governed by its own party. It may be eager to intervene in a province governed by an opposition party. The enforcement mechanism is contaminated by the very political dynamics it is supposed to override.

:::expert
The Financial and Fiscal Commission's annual submissions on the Division of Revenue have consistently identified what the FFC terms the "accountability gap" in provincial governance. The FFC's analysis shows that the equitable share formula allocates resources based on demographic proxies (population, school-age population, uninsured population) rather than on provincial performance. A province that delivers excellent health outcomes receives the same per capita allocation as a province that delivers catastrophic ones. There is no fiscal reward for good performance and no fiscal penalty for poor performance. The FFC has recommended incorporating performance incentives into the provincial allocation framework — rewarding provinces that achieve defined outcome targets with additional resources — but this recommendation has not been implemented. The result, the FFC argues, is a fiscal system that finances mediocrity: provinces have no fiscal incentive to outperform because performance does not influence funding.
:::

## The Evidence

The sphere-level diagnosis is not theoretical. It is documented in data that the Auditor-General, National Treasury, and sector-specific monitoring bodies publish annually.

The Auditor-General's provincial audit outcomes provide the broadest measure of provincial governance quality. In the 2022-23 PFMA audit cycle, the AG assessed 162 provincial government entities (departments and public entities). The results showed deep variation between provinces. The Western Cape achieved the highest proportion of clean audits — unqualified opinions with no material findings on financial reporting, compliance, or performance — across its departments. Gauteng and KwaZulu-Natal showed mixed results, with some departments performing well and others poorly. The Eastern Cape, Limpopo, and the Free State had the weakest audit outcomes, with multiple departments receiving qualified or adverse opinions and the AG identifying material irregularities, fruitless and wasteful expenditure, and inadequate financial controls.

The AG's root cause analysis is revealing. Across provinces, the AG identifies the same recurring causes of poor audit outcomes: lack of consequences for poor performance (enforcement absent), key positions vacant or filled by unqualified personnel (capability deficit), inadequate oversight by leadership (accountability diffuse), and slow response to AG findings from prior years (enforcement discretionary). These are not random findings — they are the RAARICLE™ elements that the scorecard identifies as consistently weak across the provincial sphere.

Education outcomes tell a parallel story. The 2023 matric results showed a national pass rate of 82.9 per cent, but the provincial variation was stark. The Free State achieved 90.1 per cent, Gauteng 85.7 per cent, and the Western Cape 83.7 per cent — while the Eastern Cape recorded 73.2 per cent and KwaZulu-Natal 83.4 per cent (which masks severe underperformance in rural districts). The bachelor pass rate — the more meaningful indicator, measuring the proportion of candidates qualifying for university entrance — was 37.5 per cent nationally, with similar inter-provincial variation. These differences persist year after year, creating a pattern of structural inequality in educational outcomes that maps directly onto the provincial capability and resource differences documented in the RAARICLE™ diagnosis.

Health outcomes follow the same geography of inequality. Maternal mortality ratios, infant mortality rates, HIV treatment adherence rates, and Ideal Clinic scores all show consistent patterns: the Western Cape and Gauteng outperform the national average, while the Eastern Cape, Limpopo, Mpumalanga, and the North West consistently underperform. The District Health Barometer published by the Health Systems Trust documents these disparities at district level, showing that the worst-performing districts are concentrated in the same provinces that have the weakest audit outcomes and the lowest educational results.

The Section 100 intervention record is itself evidence of the enforcement failure. Section 100 has been invoked in various forms across multiple provinces and functions since 1994. The most prominent cases include the Limpopo provincial government in 2011, where national government placed five departments (education, health, public works, roads and transport, and provincial treasury) under administration after a fiscal crisis; the Eastern Cape education department, which has been subject to various forms of national intervention; and multiple individual departments across the North West, Free State, and Mpumalanga. As of mid-2025, 21 municipalities had active Section 139 interventions (the municipal equivalent), with only six showing good progress.

The pattern is consistent. Interventions are triggered late — after years of documented failure, not at the first sign of deterioration. They are politically contested — provincial governments resist national intervention as an infringement on provincial autonomy. They are temporary — administrators come, stabilise the immediate crisis, and leave, often without addressing the structural causes. And they rarely produce sustained improvement. The Limpopo education intervention addressed the immediate textbook crisis but did not fix the structural misalignment that caused the province's education system to underperform in the first place. When the intervention ended, the structural constraints remained.

:::expert
Research published by the Public Affairs Research Institute has analysed Section 100 and Section 139 interventions over a twenty-year period and found that "interventions have consistently failed to produce sustained institutional improvement." The researchers identified three structural reasons: interventions address symptoms (fiscal crises, service delivery collapses) rather than causes (capability deficits, misaligned authority, absent enforcement); they are time-limited and discretionary, meaning the structural problems reassert themselves once the intervention team withdraws; and they do not alter the incentive structures that produced the failure — the same political dynamics, the same fiscal framework, and the same capability constraints remain after the intervention ends. The researchers concluded that "the intervention mechanism, as currently designed, is a crisis management tool, not a governance reform tool," and recommended that interventions be supplemented by automatic, outcome-triggered corrective mechanisms that operate continuously rather than episodically.
:::

Infrastructure spending confirms the pattern. Provincial roads budgets have been under pressure across all provinces as fiscal consolidation constrains real spending growth. The South African National Roads Agency manages the national road network, but provincial roads — the secondary and tertiary roads that connect communities to economic opportunities, schools, and clinics — are a provincial responsibility. The South African Institution of Civil Engineering's Infrastructure Report Card has rated provincial road conditions as poor to very poor, with maintenance backlogs growing in every province. Provinces that underspend their infrastructure budgets — because they lack the project management and engineering capacity to execute capital programmes — see their road networks deteriorate further, creating a capability-infrastructure death spiral.

## Province-by-Province Variation

If the structural constraints are the same across all nine provinces — the same Constitution, the same concurrent functions, the same equitable share formula, the same enforcement architecture — why do some provinces perform significantly better than others?

The answer is that structural design sets a ceiling, and provincial-specific factors determine how close to that ceiling each province performs. **No province can exceed the ceiling set by the structural design — even the best-performing provinces are constrained by the authority deficit, the enforcement vacuum, and the fiscal dependence.** But within those constraints, provinces vary in their ability to compensate.

Four factors explain most of the inter-provincial variation.

First, fiscal capacity. Gauteng and the Western Cape generate modest but meaningful own revenue — primarily through motor vehicle licence fees and gambling levies — and their populations include a larger proportion of taxpayers whose economic activity generates national revenue that flows back through the equitable share. More importantly, the metropolitan municipalities within these provinces have substantial fiscal capacity, which means that municipal services (water, electricity, refuse, roads) are generally better, creating a more functional service delivery environment within which provincial functions (education, health) operate. A provincial hospital in Johannesburg operates in a context where municipal water supply is reliable. A provincial hospital in a collapsed rural municipality operates in a context where it may need to source its own water.

Second, urbanisation. Gauteng is almost entirely urban. The Western Cape has a dominant metro (Cape Town) and relatively compact geography. Service delivery in urban areas is cheaper per unit than in rural areas — schools are closer together, clinics serve denser populations, infrastructure networks are shorter, and professional staff are easier to recruit and retain. The Eastern Cape, Limpopo, and KwaZulu-Natal have large rural populations dispersed across vast geographies. Reaching every school, every clinic, and every community with services costs more per unit and requires more logistical capacity. The equitable share formula partially compensates for this through its education and health components, but the compensation is insufficient to equalise the cost of delivery.

Third, administrative capability. Provinces with a history of strong administrative capacity — the Western Cape, parts of Gauteng — have been better able to attract and retain qualified senior managers, maintain institutional continuity through political transitions, and build the systems and processes that enable effective service delivery. Provinces with weaker administrative histories — the former homeland provinces that were incorporated into the Eastern Cape, Limpopo, and the North West after 1994 — inherited weak institutional foundations and have struggled to build capacity while simultaneously meeting the demands of service delivery at scale.

Fourth, political competition. The Western Cape — the only province consistently governed by a party other than the national governing party — has faced intense political competition that may have incentivised performance. Political competition creates pressure to deliver results because the governing party knows it can lose power. In provinces where the governing party holds a dominant electoral position, the electoral incentive to deliver is weaker — the party will win regardless of service delivery outcomes, reducing the political cost of underperformance.

:::takeaway
Provincial performance variation is real and significant — but it operates within structural constraints that no province can overcome through management alone. Better-resourced, more urban, more capable provinces perform better because they can partially compensate for the structural design flaws. But even the best-performing provinces are constrained by the authority deficit, the enforcement vacuum, and the misalignment that the concurrent function model creates. The structural ceiling limits all provinces — some just hit it later than others.
:::

## The Capability Gap

The capability deficit at provincial level deserves particular attention because it is both a symptom of the structural problem and a cause of service delivery failure in its own right.

The Public Service Regulations require that heads of department and senior managers meet prescribed minimum competency requirements — including qualifications, experience, and completion of prescribed management programmes. The regulations, strengthened in 2016, were intended to address the legacy of cadre deployment by ensuring that senior public servants possess the professional qualifications needed for their positions. But compliance has been uneven.

The Department of Public Service and Administration's data on senior management vacancies and competency compliance reveals persistent gaps. Across provincial departments, key positions remain vacant for extended periods — sometimes years. Acting appointments fill the gaps, but acting officials typically lack the authority and institutional investment of permanent incumbents. They are reluctant to make difficult decisions because their position is temporary. They may not initiate long-term projects because they do not know how long they will be in the role. The organisational culture suffers — staff members are reluctant to commit to a direction that may change when the next acting appointee arrives.

The capability gap is most acute in technical positions. Provincial departments of public works need registered professional engineers to manage infrastructure programmes. Provincial health departments need pharmaceutical managers, medical superintendents, and health information specialists. Provincial education departments need curriculum specialists, district directors with educational leadership qualifications, and financial managers who can navigate the complex conditional grant accounting requirements. These positions require scarce skills that the public sector struggles to attract at the salary levels on offer — particularly in provinces where the cost of living may be lower but the quality of life for professionals (schools, healthcare, amenities) is also lower.

:::expert
The Department of Public Service and Administration's annual reports on the state of the public service have documented that as of 2023, approximately 35 per cent of senior management positions in provincial departments were either vacant or filled by acting appointees who did not meet the prescribed competency requirements. The figures were worst in the Eastern Cape (approximately 42 per cent), the North West (approximately 40 per cent), and Limpopo (approximately 38 per cent). DPSA's analysis identified two structural causes: salary levels that are not competitive with the private sector for scarce skills, and appointment processes that are subject to political interference — with political considerations overriding professional qualifications in the selection of senior managers. The DPSA has recommended that appointment processes for heads of department and senior managers include mandatory participation by an independent Public Service Commission panel, but implementation of this recommendation has been limited.
:::

The capability gap is self-reinforcing. Provinces that cannot attract qualified senior managers produce weaker institutional performance, which creates a more difficult working environment, which makes it harder to attract the next generation of qualified managers. The provinces with the largest capability gaps are also the provinces with the highest staff turnover in senior positions — creating a revolving door that prevents institutional memory from accumulating and institutional culture from stabilising. The cycle cannot be broken by recruitment drives alone. It requires structural changes to appointment processes (removing political interference), remuneration frameworks (offering competitive packages for scarce skills in difficult postings), and institutional design (creating career pathways that reward professional expertise rather than political alignment).

The consultant dependency cycle — identified in the education diagnosis — is replicated across provincial departments. When a department lacks internal capacity, it hires consultants to perform core functions. The consultants deliver the immediate output (financial statements prepared, infrastructure projects managed, IT systems maintained) but the knowledge leaves with them. The department is no better positioned to perform the function the following year. The consultant engagement becomes permanent, draining resources that could have been invested in building internal capacity.

## What the Constitution Intended vs What We Got

The 1996 Constitution designed provinces as democratic institutions with three purposes: to check central government power by distributing it across multiple centres of authority, to provide a mechanism for regional democratic accountability (provincial voters holding their provincial government accountable for regional service delivery), and to bring government closer to the people by decentralising functions to the level closest to the communities served.

Thirty years later, none of these purposes is fully realised.

The check on central power has been weakened by political party dominance. Provincial premiers and MECs are, in practice, appointed by the national party leadership rather than emerging from genuine provincial political processes. The premier owes their position to the party president, not to the provincial electorate in any direct sense. This means that provincial executives are accountable upward (to the national party) rather than outward (to the provincial electorate). When the national party's interests conflict with the province's service delivery needs — when a politically connected but unqualified individual is deployed to a provincial HOD position, for instance — the national party interest typically prevails.

Regional democratic accountability is limited by the proportional representation electoral system. Provincial legislators are elected from party lists, not constituencies. Individual legislators owe their positions to the party list (determined by party structures) rather than to a defined group of voters. This weakens the accountability link between provincial representatives and the communities they serve. A legislator who fails to hold the provincial executive accountable for service delivery does not face direct electoral consequences from a constituency — they face consequences only if the party decides to drop them from the list.

Bringing government closer to the people has been partially achieved through the provincial structure, but the concurrent function model means that the province often cannot respond to local needs because the decision-making authority sits nationally. A provincial health MEC who wants to address a crisis in rural clinic staffing cannot restructure employment conditions or offer enhanced remuneration for rural postings — the employment framework is national. The province is closer to the people geographically but not closer in terms of responsive authority.

The constitutional vision was of provinces as sites of meaningful democratic governance — autonomous within a cooperative framework, accountable to their populations, capable of adapting national policy to regional needs. The reality is of provinces as constrained implementation agents — dependent on national transfers, bound by national policy frameworks, lacking enforcement tools, and politically subordinate to national party structures. The gap between the vision and the reality is the gap that structural reform must close.

:::framework
**The Provincial Performance Scorecard**

Use this framework to assess your own province's governance performance across its key functions — and to determine whether the failures you see are people problems (fixable by replacing officials) or design problems (requiring structural reform).

**Step 1: Identify your province's key concurrent functions.**

List the major services your provincial government delivers: basic education, health services, social development, roads and transport, human settlements, agriculture, and public works. These are the Schedule 4 functions where your province is responsible for delivery but shares authority with national government.

**Step 2: For each function, rate the eight RAARICLE™ elements on a scale of 1 to 5.**

Use the following scale: 1 = element is absent or entirely dysfunctional; 2 = element exists on paper but fails in practice; 3 = element functions partially with significant gaps; 4 = element functions well with minor gaps; 5 = element is fully co-located and effective. Rate each function across all eight elements: Responsibility, Accountability, Authority, Resources, Information, Capability, Legitimacy, and Enforcement. This gives you an 8-column scorecard for each function.

**Step 3: Check the Auditor-General's findings for your province.**

Look up the most recent AG audit outcome for each provincial department. Note whether the department received a clean audit, an unqualified opinion with findings, a qualified opinion, an adverse opinion, or a disclaimer. Map the AG's root cause findings — "lack of consequences," "slow response to poor audit outcomes," "key positions vacant or filled by unqualified personnel" — to the RAARICLE™ elements they correspond to.

**Step 4: Compare your province to the best-performing province for each function.**

For education, compare your province's matric pass rate, bachelor-pass rate, and district performance variation to the top performer. For health, compare your province's Ideal Clinic scores, maternal mortality ratio, and medicine availability to the best province. The comparison will reveal whether the performance gap is narrow (suggesting a people or management issue) or wide (suggesting a structural issue).

**Step 5: Check for the Section 100 pattern.**

Has your province ever been subject to a Section 100 national intervention in any department? If so, what was the outcome — did the intervention produce sustained improvement, or did performance revert after the intervention ended? If interventions fail to produce lasting improvement, the problem is structural, not managerial. Replacing the people without changing the design will produce the same results.

**Step 6: Identify whether the problem is provincial or systemic.**

If your province underperforms while others with the same constitutional structure succeed, the problem may be provincial — leadership, management, or capability. But if most provinces underperform on the same functions (as is the case with education, health, and housing), the problem is systemic — the constitutional design creates conditions that make provincial failure likely regardless of who governs.

This scorecard gives you a structured, evidence-based view of your province's performance that goes beyond anecdote and partisan blame — enabling you to distinguish between failures that require better people and failures that require a better design.
:::

## The Case for Structural Reform

The evidence assembled in this article leads to a conclusion that many governance practitioners and scholars have been reluctant to state directly: incremental improvement within the current provincial structure is necessary but insufficient. Better management, more training, stricter monitoring, and improved audit outcomes will make the system perform less badly — but they cannot overcome the structural constraints that the RAARICLE™ diagnosis has identified.

This is not a counsel of despair. It is a structural diagnosis that points to structural remedies.

The management improvement agenda is important and should continue. Every province should strive for clean audit outcomes. Every provincial HOD should meet prescribed competency requirements. Every critical post should be filled with a qualified permanent incumbent. These are prerequisites for any governance system to function, and they represent the minimum standard of institutional capability.

But management improvement cannot compensate for the authority deficit. A brilliantly managed provincial education department still cannot control teacher employment conditions, salary bargaining, or curriculum design. A competently led provincial health department still cannot give its facility managers procurement authority over essential medicines. A well-governed provincial human settlements department still cannot consolidate the fragmented value chain that distributes responsibility across six entities and three spheres. Management quality determines how well a province operates within its structural constraints — it does not eliminate those constraints.

Similarly, monitoring and oversight improvement — strengthening the DPME's performance assessments, expanding the Auditor-General's performance audit function, increasing the capacity of provincial legislatures to hold executives accountable — is valuable. But monitoring that identifies failure without triggering mandatory corrective action is diagnosis without treatment. South Africa does not lack oversight. It lacks enforcement. The country has more audit, monitoring, and evaluation capacity per government entity than most middle-income countries. What it lacks is the architecture of automatic consequences that converts identified failure into mandatory corrective action.

The structural reforms required fall into three categories, which the next two articles in this series will address in detail.

First, the concurrent function model must be redesigned so that authority is co-located with accountability — either by devolving meaningful authority to provinces (as proposed in the structured autonomy model for education) or by creating sub-provincial delivery entities that hold delegated operational authority within a framework of national standards and automatic enforcement (as proposed for healthcare and housing).

Second, the enforcement architecture must be transformed from discretionary to automatic. Section 100 interventions should be supplemented — or, for defined functions, replaced — by automatic triggers with prescribed thresholds, mandatory responses, and defined exit criteria. The triggers should be outcome-based (linked to service delivery results, not just financial compliance) and the responses should be technical (mandatory support, ring-fenced funding, prescribed remedial action) rather than political (provincial executive takeover).

Third, provincial fiscal autonomy must be strengthened — not necessarily through new provincial taxes, but through mechanisms that create a stronger accountability link between provincial performance and provincial resources. The FFC's recommendation for performance-based allocations within the equitable share framework deserves serious consideration. If a portion of the provincial allocation were linked to defined outcome indicators — rewarding provinces that improve educational outcomes, health indicators, or infrastructure condition — the fiscal incentive structure would shift from financing mediocrity to rewarding performance.

:::takeaway
Incremental improvement is necessary but insufficient. The structural constraints that the RAARICLE™ diagnosis identifies — the authority deficit, the enforcement vacuum, the fiscal dependence — cannot be overcome through better management alone. Structural reform means redesigning the concurrent function model, replacing discretionary enforcement with automatic triggers, and strengthening the fiscal accountability link between provincial performance and provincial resources. The next two articles in this series propose specific designs for making these reforms work.
:::

The provincial sphere is not beyond saving. It is a sphere of government that was designed with democratic intent — to bring government closer to citizens, to provide a check on central power, to create regional accountability. The intent was sound. The execution — the concurrent function model, the equitable share formula, the discretionary enforcement architecture — created a structural trap that good management can mitigate but not escape. The reform agenda for provinces is about redesigning the trap, not about eliminating provinces. In [Making Concurrent Functions Work](/articles/ra-3-2-making-concurrent-functions-work), we examine how the concurrent model can be restructured to co-locate authority with accountability. In [When Provinces Fail](/articles/ra-3-3-when-provinces-fail), we examine how the enforcement architecture can be transformed from discretionary intervention to automatic correction.
