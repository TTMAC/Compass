## A Qualification Certifies That You Sat in a Room; A Skill Certifies That You Can Do the Work

There is a sentence that South African employers say, in one form or another, so often that it has become a cliché of the recruitment industry: "We get hundreds of applications and we cannot find anyone we can actually hire." Read carelessly, it sounds like a complaint about a lazy generation or an indictment of the schools. Read in the language of the [previous article](/articles/hd-4-1-the-conversion-problem), it is something more precise: it is a description of a **personal conversion factor** that is failing at scale. The applicants have qualifications. What they often lack is the thing a qualification is supposed to stand for — a skill an employer can put to work on Monday morning.

This article is about the layer of South Africa's human-capital system that sits between the school gate and the workplace: the post-school education and training system, and specifically the parts of it that are meant to turn general schooling into applied, employable capability. [From Classroom to Paycheck](/articles/hd-3-3-from-classroom-to-paycheck) established the foundation — that cognitive skills, not years of seat-time, are what drive growth, and that South Africa's basic-education system produces those skills for too few learners. This article picks up where that one stopped. It assumes a young person who has, somehow, cleared the basic-education hurdle, and asks the next question: what is supposed to happen now to make her employable, and why does it so often fail to happen at all?

The answer matters because the conversion problem cannot be solved at the level of basic schooling alone. Even a perfect school system would hand its graduates over to an economy that demands specific, applied skills — how to wire a distribution board to code, how to weld a pressure vessel that will not fail, how to diagnose a fault in a diesel engine, how to keep a set of books that will survive an audit. These are not things a general education delivers. They are things a _vocational and technical_ education delivers, through a combination of structured instruction and supervised practice. And the system South Africa built to deliver them is one of the more consequential underperformers in the country's institutional landscape.

## The Skill Above the Schooling

Begin with the distinction the title insists on, because it is easy to blur. Basic schooling is supposed to produce foundational and general cognitive skills: literacy, numeracy, reasoning, the capacity to learn. These are necessary for employability but they are not sufficient for it, and the gap between necessary and sufficient is where this article lives.

Consider what an employer actually buys when they hire. They are not buying twelve years of attendance, or even the cognitive skills those years were meant to build. They are buying a bundle of _applied capabilities_ — the ability to perform specific tasks to a standard, reliably, in a real workplace. Some of that bundle is technical (the trade, the software, the procedure). Some of it is what the literature calls "employability skills" or "work-readiness": showing up on time, working in a team, communicating with a supervisor, solving a problem that the manual did not anticipate. None of it is automatically supplied by a matric certificate or even a diploma. It has to be built, deliberately, on top of the schooling.

In the conversion framework from the [previous article](/articles/hd-4-1-the-conversion-problem), this applied-capability bundle is the heart of the _personal conversion factor_. A young person with a matric and no applied skill possesses a resource — the certificate — without the capability it is supposed to certify. Employers, who pay the price of getting this judgement wrong, have learned to discount the certificate accordingly. This is why South Africa exhibits the seeming paradox that [From Classroom to Paycheck](/articles/hd-3-3-from-classroom-to-paycheck) named: simultaneous mass unemployment and persistent unfilled vacancies in skilled trades. The unemployed hold credentials; the vacancies demand capabilities; and the machinery that is supposed to convert the first into the second is broken.

That machinery has three main components, and the rest of this article takes each in turn: the public TVET college system, which is supposed to provide the institutional training; the apprenticeship and learnership system, which is supposed to combine training with real workplace practice; and the skills-levy and SETA architecture, which is supposed to fund and coordinate the whole enterprise. Each was designed sensibly. Each, in execution, leaks.

## The TVET College: The Bridge That Carries Too Few

[From Classroom to Paycheck](/articles/hd-3-3-from-classroom-to-paycheck) introduced the public Technical and Vocational Education and Training colleges — 50 of them, enrolling roughly 700,000 students — and documented their central failures: completion rates below 30 per cent in many programmes, weak workplace integration, curricula designed in isolation from employers, instructors without industry experience, and a stigma that drives away the very learners who might benefit most. Rather than repeat that diagnosis, this article presses on the part of it that bears most directly on individual capability: the difference between a college that issues qualifications and a college that builds skills.

The South African TVET qualification landscape is genuinely confusing, and the confusion is itself a barrier. A prospective student confronts at least three parallel offerings: the older NATED or "Report 191" programmes (the N1–N6 engineering and business studies certificates, theory-heavy and historically employer-recognised in certain trades); the National Certificate (Vocational), or NC(V), introduced in 2007 as a more comprehensive school-equivalent vocational qualification; and an array of occupational qualifications administered under the Quality Council for Trades and Occupations. Each carries a different reputation in the labour market, and a student often discovers only after enrolling — and sometimes only after graduating — which one employers actually value for the trade she is pursuing. A system that asks an eighteen-year-old to make a high-stakes bet on an opaque qualification map, and then penalises her for betting wrong, is a system imposing a conversion cost before training has even begun.

The deeper problem is the one the previous TVET discussion identified and that bears restating in capability terms: a TVET qualification earned without integrated workplace practice is, for many trades, a half-built capability. You cannot become a competent electrician, fitter, or boilermaker by passing theory modules alone. The trade is in the hands, learned through supervised repetition on real equipment under someone who already has the skill. The NC(V) and NATED systems are strong on the classroom and chronically weak on the workplace component, and so they produce graduates who have studied a trade without having practised it — qualified, in the formal sense, and not yet capable, in the sense that matters to an employer or a customer.

This is why the apprenticeship route, where it functions, remains the gold standard, and why its decline is so costly.

## The Artisan and the Trade Test: Where the Real Skill Is Certified

In the trades, South Africa has an institution that does, in principle, certify genuine applied capability rather than mere attendance: the **trade test**, and the Red Seal qualification it confers. A person who passes the trade test administered through the National Artisan Moderation Body — historically at the INDLELA facility in Olifantsfontein and an expanding network of accredited centres — has demonstrated, practically, that she can do the work of a qualified artisan to a national standard. The Red Seal is portable, recognised, and respected; it is one of the few South African credentials that an employer can trust without re-testing the holder. It is, in capability terms, the system working as designed: a certificate that genuinely stands for a skill.

The route to it is the apprenticeship or, in its more recent form, the learnership — a structured programme combining institutional instruction with extended supervised workplace practice, culminating in the trade test. This is the model that produces real artisans, and it maps almost exactly onto the international gold standard, Germany's dual system, that [From Classroom to Paycheck](/articles/hd-3-3-from-classroom-to-paycheck) described. The design is sound. The problem is volume.

South Africa's _Decade of the Artisan_ campaign and the targets carried in the National Development Plan set an ambition of producing roughly 30,000 qualified artisans per year by 2030 — the number the economy is estimated to need to maintain and expand its infrastructure, its manufacturing, and its mines. The system has struggled to reach that figure consistently, and the bottleneck is rarely the classroom. It is the workplace placement: there are more young people who have completed the theoretical phase than there are employers willing or able to host them for the practical phase. An apprenticeship requires a host firm to commit supervision, equipment time, and a wage to a trainee for an extended period, and in a low-growth economy with subdued investment, too few firms make that commitment. The result is a queue of "theory-complete" young people who cannot reach the trade test because they cannot find the workplace experience that the trade test requires. They are stuck, once again, at a conversion factor — this time a social one, the availability of host placements — with the capability half-built and no way to finish it.

:::expert[National Artisan Moderation Body and the Trade Test System]
The National Artisan Moderation Body (NAMB), located within the Department of Higher Education and Training, was established to standardise and improve the quality of artisan certification after years in which trade testing was fragmented and inconsistent. Its creation addressed a real problem: under the old dispensation, trade-test pass standards and processes varied, and the credibility of the artisan qualification suffered. NAMB centralised moderation, expanded the number of accredited trade-test centres beyond the historic INDLELA facility, and introduced a single registration and certification process. The reform improved the integrity of the Red Seal. What it could not do, because the constraint lies outside the certification system itself, is increase the supply of workplace-based apprenticeships that feed candidates into the trade test. NAMB data consistently show a gap between the number of learners entering artisan programmes and the smaller number reaching and passing the trade test — a gap driven heavily by the shortage of employer host placements for the practical, workplace-based phase. The institutional lesson is precise: South Africa improved the gate at the end of the pipeline while the pipeline itself remained too narrow, because the workplace-learning component depends on employer participation that policy has not successfully mobilised.
:::

## The Skills Levy and the SETAs: Money That Does Not Reach the Skill

If the workplace-placement bottleneck is the proximate failure, the system built to fix it is the skills-levy architecture — and that architecture is the third, and in some ways most dispiriting, layer of the story.

The mechanics are worth understanding because most South Africans pay into this system without knowing it exists. Every employer with a payroll above a modest threshold pays a **Skills Development Levy** of 1 per cent of total payroll, collected by the South African Revenue Service. That money — billions of rands a year — is meant to fund exactly the training this article is about. A portion goes to the National Skills Fund; the larger share is distributed to the **Sector Education and Training Authorities**, the SETAs, of which there are 21, each responsible for skills development in a sector of the economy — manufacturing, wholesale and retail, finance, construction, and so on. The SETAs are supposed to use the levy to fund learnerships, apprenticeships, bursaries, and workplace training, channelling the money to where the sector's skills gaps are.

In design, this is an elegant solution to the host-placement problem: tax payrolls, and use the proceeds to subsidise firms that train, so that the cost of hosting an apprentice is shared rather than borne alone. In execution, the SETA system has been one of the more persistently troubled corners of South African public administration. The recurring criticisms, documented across years of Auditor-General reports, parliamentary oversight, and DHET reviews, cluster around a few themes: governance instability and board dysfunction; underspending of levy funds even as skills gaps persist; administrative complexity that deters precisely the smaller firms most able to absorb trainees; weak alignment between the training funded and the skills the economy actually demands; and, in several documented cases, irregular expenditure and outright maladministration. The net effect is a system in which money collected specifically to solve the workplace-training bottleneck frequently fails to reach the workplaces, or reaches them attached to so much paperwork that the training it funds is an afterthought to the compliance.

For the individual at the centre of this series, the consequence is stark. The funding meant to subsidise her apprenticeship exists. It has been collected. It is sitting in a system that, too often, cannot deploy it to the firm that would otherwise host her. Her half-built capability and the money that would complete it are in the same country, and the machinery to connect them does not reliably function. That is a conversion failure of a particularly frustrating kind, because nothing is missing except coordination.

:::takeaway
South Africa's post-school skills system is not starved of resources or sound design — it is starved of execution. The 1 per cent Skills Development Levy funds a R-billions training system; the trade-test architecture certifies genuine capability; the apprenticeship model matches the international gold standard. The failures are in the joins: TVET colleges that teach trades without practising them, a workplace-placement bottleneck that strands theory-complete learners short of the trade test, and a SETA system that too often cannot move levy money to the firms that would do the training. The skill, the money, and the learner all exist. The coordination does not.
:::

## The Missing Habit: Lifelong Learning in a Changing Economy

There is a further dimension to the skills problem that the TVET-and-apprenticeship frame, focused on the transition from school to first job, can miss. Employability is not a state you reach once and keep. In an economy being reshaped by automation, digitisation, and now artificial intelligence — the forces [From Classroom to Paycheck](/articles/hd-3-3-from-classroom-to-paycheck) described as steadily raising the minimum skill threshold for employment — the capability to earn depends increasingly on the capability to _keep learning_. The skill that secures a livelihood at twenty-five may be obsolete, or merely insufficient, at forty. A development system that builds skills once and then assumes they last for a working life is building on sand.

This is the dimension where South Africa is least equipped, because it barely features in the national conversation. The post-school system is organised almost entirely around first qualifications for young people, not around continuous reskilling for adults already in (or pushed out of) the labour market. The instrument that exists in principle to help — Recognition of Prior Learning, the process by which skills acquired through experience rather than formal study can be assessed and credentialed — is administratively cumbersome and thinly used, so that a worker who has genuinely acquired a capability on the job often cannot get it certified into a portable qualification. The result is a workforce in which a great deal of real, hard-won capability is invisible to the formal system, and in which mid-career workers displaced by technological change have few structured routes back into employability.

The cost of this gap is not evenly spread. It falls hardest on workers in exactly the sectors most exposed to change — clerical and administrative roles being automated, retail roles being reshaped by e-commerce, manufacturing roles being reorganised around new machinery. A worker displaced from such a role in her forties, with twenty years of working life still ahead of her, is precisely the person a functioning reskilling system would catch and redirect. In South Africa she typically falls through, because the system was never built to catch her: there is no standing entitlement to retraining, no portable learning account, and a Recognition of Prior Learning process too cumbersome to convert her two decades of accumulated capability into a credential she can carry to a new sector. Her experience, real and valuable, becomes invisible at the moment she most needs it to be legible.

The international comparators that South Africa likes to invoke for basic education have lessons here too, and they point in a useful direction. Singapore's _SkillsFuture_ initiative gives every adult citizen a learning credit, periodically topped up, that can be spent on accredited courses across a working life — a deliberate national bet that employability must be continuously rebuilt rather than issued once. Germany and Switzerland sustain dense systems of continuing vocational training that workers re-enter repeatedly. The common thread is a reframing that South Africa has not yet made: treating skills not as a stage of youth but as infrastructure to be maintained across the whole span of a working life. Until that reframing happens, the conversion problem will keep reappearing for each cohort, and reappearing again for the same workers as the economy moves under their feet.

## The Economics of a Trade: What a Skill Is Actually Worth

It is worth pausing on a question that the status hierarchy around education tends to suppress: in purely economic terms, what is a vocational skill worth to the person who holds it? The answer, in the current South African labour market, upends a good deal of conventional advice — and it explains why the artisan funnel discussed above is not merely an industrial-policy concern but a personal one.

Consider the comparison that a school-leaver is implicitly making. One path leads through a general university degree in a crowded field — a three-year qualification, often financed by debt, into a graduate labour market where, as the [previous article in this Part](/articles/hd-4-1-the-conversion-problem) noted, even degree-holders face meaningful unemployment and many end up working outside their field. The other path leads through an apprenticeship to a Red Seal in a scarce trade — electrician, millwright, fitter and turner, instrumentation technician — where qualified practitioners are in persistent short supply. The second path frequently pays the trainee a wage _during_ training rather than charging tuition, and it ends in a credential that is among the most reliably employable in the country. A qualified artisan in a scarce trade can, in many cases, command an income that compares favourably with that of a mid-tier graduate, and can do so years earlier and without the debt.

There is a further dimension that connects this article to the [one that follows](/articles/hd-4-3-the-missing-informal-economy). A genuine trade skill is one of the few capabilities that is readily _self-employable_. An electrician, a plumber, a panel-beater, a diesel mechanic, a welder — these are people who, in principle, can earn without an employer at all, by trading directly with customers who need the work done. The Red Seal is therefore not just a ticket into formal wage employment; it is a foundation for the kind of small, self-directed enterprise that, as the next article will argue, South Africa has far too little of. A general qualification rarely offers this optionality. A trade skill is portable across the formal and informal economy in a way that a desk-bound credential is not, which makes it doubly valuable in a country where the formal labour market absorbs so few.

This is not an argument that everyone should become an artisan, or that university education is a poor investment — for many people and many careers it remains an excellent one. It is an argument that the _relative_ return to vocational and technical skill is, in South Africa's specific labour market, far higher than the social status hierarchy implies, and that the persistent under-supply of artisans is in part a failure of information. Young people, and the adults advising them, systematically underestimate what a scarce trade is worth and overestimate the security of a generic degree. Correcting that misjudgement is one of the few moves available to an individual that does not require the whole broken system to be fixed first.

The catch, of course, returns us to the bottleneck. The reason the artisan path is so valuable is precisely that so few people complete it — and they fail to complete it not because they lack the aptitude but because the workplace placements that lead to the trade test are scarce. The high return to the skill and the difficulty of acquiring it are two faces of the same coin. This is why the personal advice that closes this article and the systemic reforms that the [Reform Agenda](/articles/ra-2-1-fixing-basic-education) pillar takes up cannot substitute for one another. An individual can navigate toward the high-value path; only a functioning system can widen it enough for everyone who wants it to walk through.

## What This Means for You

The post-school skills system is one of the few areas of the conversion problem where an individual, armed with an accurate map, can materially improve her own odds. The system is broken, but it is broken in specific, navigable ways.

If you are a young person choosing a post-school path, the single most valuable thing this article can give you is a question to ask before you enrol anywhere: _what does this qualification let me actually do, and does it include real workplace practice?_ For a trade, the route that ends in a trade test and a Red Seal — an apprenticeship or learnership with a genuine workplace-learning component — is worth far more than a theory-only certificate in the same trade, even if it takes longer and is harder to secure. When you compare colleges or programmes, ask for two numbers: the completion rate and the proportion of graduates placed in work within a year. A programme that will not give you those numbers is telling you something by its silence.

If you are a parent advising a school-leaver, resist the status hierarchy that ranks a weak university degree above a strong artisan qualification. The labour market does not share that hierarchy. A qualified artisan with a Red Seal is, in much of the current economy, more reliably employable and more readily self-employable than the holder of a general degree from a crowded field. The stigma attached to vocational training is a social conversion factor working against your child's interests; the most useful thing you can do is refuse to pass it on.

If you are an employer, you are the missing link in the most expensive bottleneck in the system. The workplace placement that a theory-complete learner cannot find is something only a firm can provide, and the skills levy you already pay is meant to subsidise the cost of providing it. Engaging seriously with your SETA, hosting apprentices and learners, and treating entry-level training as a pipeline rather than a charity are the actions that would do most to widen the artisan funnel — and firms that build that pipeline secure scarce skills their competitors cannot find.

If you are a worker already in the labour market, the lifelong-learning gap is yours to manage in part by yourself, because the system will not manage it for you. Keep an eye on where your trade or your sector is heading, and treat the maintenance of your own skills as a recurring investment rather than a one-time event. Where Recognition of Prior Learning can certify a capability you have built through experience, the portable qualification is worth the administrative effort, because it converts invisible skill into a credential the labour market can read.

:::takeaway
A qualification is a claim about capability; only applied, practised skill makes the claim true. South Africa's post-school system is strongest where it certifies genuine practised capability — the trade test and the Red Seal — and weakest where it should feed that certification: too few workplace placements, a SETA system that cannot reliably move training money, and almost no infrastructure for the lifelong reskilling a changing economy demands. For the individual, the navigable lesson is to prize qualifications that include real workplace practice and to treat skill as something maintained across a working life, not issued once.
:::

:::framework
**The Employability Pathway Assessment**

Use this framework to evaluate whether a post-school training choice will actually build employable capability — or merely issue a credential. It applies to a learner choosing a programme, a parent advising one, or a community organisation steering young people.

**Assessment 1: Does the programme include real workplace practice?**
A trade or technical qualification without a structured, supervised workplace-learning component is a half-built capability. Ask explicitly: is there an apprenticeship, learnership, or internship built in, with a real employer, leading to a practical assessment such as the trade test? Theory-only programmes in hands-on trades are the single most common way time and money are spent without producing employability.

**Assessment 2: Is the qualification one employers in the trade actually recognise?**
South Africa's qualification map (NATED/Report 191, NC(V), occupational qualifications, Red Seal) is confusing, and the credential's labour-market value varies by trade. Before enrolling, ask working people in that trade — not the institution's marketing — which qualification they would hire on.

**Assessment 3: What are the completion and placement numbers?**
Request the programme's completion rate and the share of graduates employed within a year. Low completion is a warning that the programme loses most of the people who start it; low placement is a warning that even its graduates struggle to convert. An institution that cannot or will not provide these is answering the question by avoiding it.

**Assessment 4: Is the funding pathway clear?**
Identify which SETA covers the sector and what learnership, bursary, or apprenticeship funding exists. The skills levy is meant to subsidise exactly this training; a programme connected to that funding stream is more likely to include the workplace component that makes it worthwhile.

**Assessment 5: Does it build a skill you can keep updating?**
Favour foundational, transferable capabilities over narrowly proprietary ones, and treat the first qualification as the start of a learning habit, not the end of one. In an economy raising its skill threshold over time, the capability to keep learning is itself the most durable skill.
:::
