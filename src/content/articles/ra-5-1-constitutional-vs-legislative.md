## The Three Legal Pathways

Over the course of Parts 2, 3, and 4 of this series, you have seen a comprehensive programme of governance reform. Education needs a structured autonomy model that gives provincial departments operational authority over teacher employment. Healthcare needs semi-autonomous district PHC authorities with delegated procurement and staffing powers. Human settlements needs consolidated municipal delivery agencies with end-to-end ownership of the housing chain. Water and sanitation needs a clean separation between the Water Services Authority and the Water Services Provider. Electricity needs ring-fenced revenue and independent procurement pathways. Municipal capability needs mandatory professional qualifications and protected technical appointments. And binding all of these together, the enforcement architecture needs automatic triggers that replace the discretionary Section 100 intervention model.

That is a substantial reform agenda. The question that naturally follows — the question that will determine whether any of this actually happens — is: what does it take, legally, to implement each of these reforms? Does South Africa need to rewrite its Constitution? Does it need new Acts of Parliament? Or can some of these changes happen through the stroke of a ministerial pen?

The answer depends on which of three distinct legal pathways each reform requires. Understanding these pathways is not an academic exercise. It is the difference between knowing where to direct your advocacy energy and wasting years pushing for changes through the wrong channel. A reform that only needs a regulation change could be implemented within months — but if you spend your energy demanding a constitutional amendment, you will wait a decade and probably get nothing. Conversely, a reform that genuinely requires legislation cannot be short-circuited through a policy directive, no matter how well-intentioned the minister.

South Africa's legal system provides three pathways for governance reform, each with different requirements, different timelines, and different political dynamics.

### Pathway 1: Constitutional Amendment (Section 74)

The Constitution of the Republic of South Africa is the supreme law. Any reform that requires changing the actual text of the Constitution — altering a section, adding new provisions, moving a function from one schedule to another — must follow the amendment procedure in Section 74. For provisions affecting the Bill of Rights, the powers of provincial government, or the structure of the state, this requires a two-thirds majority in the National Assembly (267 of 400 members) and the support of six of nine provincial delegations in the National Council of Provinces. For other provisions, the threshold is two-thirds in the National Assembly alone.

These are extraordinarily high thresholds. Since 1996, the Constitution has been amended eighteen times, but most amendments have been technical or procedural — adjusting the number of councillors, clarifying judicial appointment procedures, extending the term of the Municipal Demarcation Board. The substantive architecture of the state — the three spheres, the allocation of functions in Schedules 4 and 5, the powers of provincial and local government — has remained essentially unchanged since 1996. No single party currently holds a two-thirds majority in the National Assembly, and the Government of National Unity formed after the 2024 elections, while representing a broad coalition, does not have the kind of ideological consensus on governance architecture that would make constitutional amendment straightforward.

The practical implication is stark: any reform that requires constitutional amendment is, for the foreseeable future, a long-term aspiration rather than a near-term plan. It is not impossible — constitutional amendments have passed, and a sufficiently broad coalition could assemble the votes. But it requires political alignment across multiple parties, agreement from at least six provincial legislatures (each with its own political dynamics), and sustained political will over several years. If you hear a politician promise a constitutional change within a year, you are hearing someone who either does not understand the process or is not being honest about it.

### Pathway 2: Legislation (Section 75 and Section 76)

Most governance reforms do not require changing the Constitution. They require passing new legislation or amending existing Acts of Parliament. The Constitution itself distinguishes between two types of legislation, and the distinction matters enormously for reform feasibility.

Section 75 bills are ordinary bills that do not affect provinces. They are passed by the National Assembly with a simple majority (201 of 400 members). The NCOP can propose amendments but cannot block the bill — if the NCOP and the NA disagree, the NA can override with a simple majority. Section 75 is the faster, easier pathway, but it is only available for legislation that does not "affect" provincial government.

Section 76 bills are ordinary bills that affect provinces. They require passage through both the National Assembly and the NCOP. In the NCOP, provinces vote as delegations — each province casts a single vote based on the mandate from its provincial legislature, and the bill requires five of nine provincial votes to pass. If the NA and the NCOP disagree, the bill goes to a mediation committee, and if mediation fails, the NA can only override with a two-thirds majority — the same threshold as a constitutional amendment. For all practical purposes, a Section 76 bill needs both chambers to agree.

The Section 75/76 classification is determined by the substance of the bill, not by what the drafter calls it. A bill that restructures how provinces deliver education, even if it does not amend the Constitution, will almost certainly be classified as a Section 76 bill because it affects provincial competences. This means it needs buy-in from at least five of nine provincial delegations in the NCOP — which, in turn, means it needs support from a majority of provincial governments.

The timeline for legislation is typically two to four years from drafting to promulgation. This includes policy development, public consultation, Cabinet approval of the bill, introduction in Parliament, committee deliberation, public hearings, passage through both chambers (for Section 76 bills), presidential assent, and commencement. Complex bills with significant stakeholder opposition can take longer. The NHI Act, for instance, was in legislative development for over a decade.

### Pathway 3: Regulation and Policy

The fastest pathway — and the one most frequently overlooked — is regulatory or policy change under existing legislation. Most Acts of Parliament include enabling provisions that authorise the relevant minister to make regulations on specific matters. Regulations have the force of law — they are binding and enforceable — but they do not require parliamentary approval. They typically require publication in the Government Gazette, a period for public comment, and the minister's signature.

Policy changes are even simpler. A national department can change its policy frameworks, guidelines, and administrative directives without formal regulatory process. A National Treasury circular can change how conditional grants are structured. A ministerial directive can change how existing powers are exercised. These instruments do not create new legal authority — they work within the authority that existing legislation already provides — but they can be remarkably powerful when the existing legislation is broad enough.

The critical question for every reform is: does the existing law already provide the authority to make this change? If the answer is yes, the reform can be implemented through regulation or policy — no new legislation required. If the answer is no, you need to determine whether new legislation or a constitutional amendment is necessary. This classification exercise is the backbone of this article.

:::expert
The National Treasury's Government Technical Advisory Centre (GTAC) has published analyses of the legislative and regulatory frameworks governing intergovernmental fiscal relations, noting that "a significant proportion of the governance improvements recommended by oversight bodies — the Auditor-General, the Financial and Fiscal Commission, the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation — can be achieved through regulatory amendments or policy changes under existing enabling legislation, without the need for new Acts of Parliament." GTAC's analysis found that the Division of Revenue Act, the Municipal Finance Management Act, and the Municipal Systems Act collectively provide sufficient regulatory authority to implement many of the governance reforms that have been debated for years — the constraint is not legal authority but political will and administrative capacity.
:::

:::takeaway
South Africa's governance reform pathways fall into three categories: constitutional amendment (Section 74, requiring a two-thirds majority and six of nine provinces — extremely difficult), legislation (Section 75 or 76, requiring parliamentary majorities and, for Section 76, NCOP concurrence — achievable but slow), and regulation or policy change (requiring only ministerial action under existing legislation — fast and feasible). The single most important analytical step for any reform advocate is correctly classifying which pathway a given reform requires, because misclassification wastes years of effort on the wrong channel.
:::

## Reform-by-Reform Legal Map

This section takes every reform proposed across Parts 2, 3, and 4 of this series and maps it to its legal pathway. The analysis works through the reforms function by function, identifying for each one whether it requires constitutional amendment, new legislation, or can be achieved through regulation and policy. This is the operational core of the article — the map that converts reform proposals into implementation plans.

### Education Reforms

The [education reform blueprint](/articles/ra-2-1-fixing-basic-education) proposed a structured autonomy model that transfers operational authority over teacher employment to provincial departments while maintaining national standard-setting and enforcement. This model has several distinct components, each with its own legal pathway.

The first component is provincial operational authority over teacher employment, including salary supplements and disciplinary processes. The Employment of Educators Act currently centralises employment conditions at the national level, with the Education Labour Relations Council setting binding bargaining outcomes for all nine provinces. Transferring operational employment authority to provinces — enabling a provincial department to offer scarce-skills supplements, manage deployment, and conduct disciplinary processes within nationally agreed parameters — requires amending the Employment of Educators Act. This is a Section 76 bill, because it directly affects how provinces exercise their education competence. It also requires restructuring the ELRC mandate to separate national salary bargaining (which would remain centralised) from operational employment matters (which would be devolved). The ELRC is a statutory body established under the Labour Relations Act, so its mandate restructuring may require amendments to either the Employment of Educators Act or the ELRC's own establishment provisions — both of which are legislative changes.

The second component is ring-fenced education funding. Currently, education is funded through the provincial equitable share (an unconditional allocation that provinces can spend as they choose) and several conditional grants (the Education Infrastructure Grant, the Maths, Science and Technology Grant, and others). Ring-fencing education funding within the equitable share would require changes to the Division of Revenue framework. The Division of Revenue Act is passed annually as a Section 76 bill, so the principle of ring-fencing could be introduced through the annual DORA process without separate enabling legislation. However, making the ring-fencing permanent and binding would likely require a dedicated legislative provision — either in the DORA itself (which is re-enacted each year) or in a standalone framework law. National Treasury has the regulatory authority to attach conditions to conditional grants, so restructuring the conditional grant framework to consolidate education grants into a single outcome-based grant can be achieved through the annual DORA process — a significant reform achievable without new standalone legislation.

The third component is the unconditional infrastructure block allocation. Replacing the current Education Infrastructure Grant (which comes with detailed national conditions on project selection, procurement, and spending timelines) with a block allocation that gives provinces discretion over how to spend infrastructure funding is a change to the Division of Revenue framework. This can be done through the annual DORA — no new legislation required, only a change in the grant design. National Treasury redesigns conditional grants regularly, and the shift from input-based to outcome-based conditions is a policy decision within Treasury's existing authority.

The fourth component is the national Education Support Unit with automatic triggers. Creating a new institutional entity — a dedicated support unit that deploys technical assistance to provinces when performance thresholds are breached — requires legislation. This could be established through amendments to the National Education Policy Act or through a standalone Education Support Unit Act. Either way, it is a Section 76 bill. However, much of the support unit's function could be piloted through executive action — the Minister of Basic Education could establish a support function within DBE using existing organisational authority, and only formalise it in legislation once the model is proven.

The fifth component is the Education Information System — a consolidated data platform that integrates data from multiple sources and publishes it in real time. This does not require legislation. DBE already collects education data through the Education Management Information System (EMIS), the Annual National Assessments (while they operated), and the School Monitoring Survey. Consolidating these into a real-time, publicly accessible dashboard is an administrative and technical task that falls within DBE's existing mandate. A ministerial directive could mandate the dashboard's development and require provinces to submit data in specified formats and timelines.

#### Suggested Sequencing

- **Month 1–3:** Minister of Basic Education issues a ministerial directive to develop the Education Information System, mandating standardised provincial data submissions and a publicly accessible dashboard. This requires no legislation and creates the information infrastructure that all subsequent reforms depend on.
- **Month 1–6:** National Treasury redesigns the Education Infrastructure Grant from input-based conditions to outcome-based targets in the next DORA cycle. Simultaneously, Treasury begins designing a consolidated outcome-based education grant to replace multiple conditional grants.
- **Month 3–6:** Minister of Basic Education establishes a pilot Education Support Unit within DBE using existing organisational authority, deploying technical support teams to the three lowest-performing provinces. This tests the support model before legislating it.
- **Month 6–12:** Ring-fenced education funding is introduced through the DORA process, initially as strengthened grant conditions requiring provinces to allocate a minimum percentage to frontline delivery before discretionary spending.
- **Year 1–2:** Draft the Employment of Educators Act amendment bill and ELRC mandate restructuring. Begin stakeholder consultation with teacher unions, provincial education departments, and the ELRC. This is the most politically contentious reform and requires the longest lead time.
- **Year 2–3:** Introduce the Employment of Educators Act amendment as a Section 76 bill. Simultaneously introduce the Education Support Unit Act to formalise the pilot established in Phase 1.
- **Year 3–4:** Once operational employment authority is legislated, provinces begin exercising scarce-skills supplements, deployment management, and expedited disciplinary processes within the nationally agreed framework.

### Healthcare Reforms

The [healthcare reform blueprint](/articles/ra-2-2-fixing-primary-healthcare) proposed semi-autonomous district PHC authorities with delegated procurement, staff recruitment authority, and ring-fenced PHC funding on a weighted capitation basis.

Establishing district PHC authorities as semi-autonomous entities with their own governance structures, delegated procurement powers, and staffing authority requires amending the National Health Act. The Act currently vests health service delivery in provincial departments, with districts functioning as management units within the provincial structure rather than as autonomous entities. Creating district authorities with their own legal personality, procurement authority, and governance boards is a structural change that requires legislative amendment. This is a Section 76 bill — it fundamentally alters how provinces organise and deliver health services.

Strengthening the enforcement capacity of the Office of Health Standards Compliance is partly legislative and partly regulatory. The OHSC was established under the National Health Amendment Act and has existing inspection and enforcement powers. However, its enforcement tools are limited — it can issue compliance notices but has weak sanctioning powers. Strengthening these powers (for example, enabling the OHSC to impose financial penalties on facilities that fail to meet minimum standards, or to trigger mandatory remedial support) requires legislative amendment to the National Health Act. The amendment would be Section 76 because it affects provincial health service delivery.

However, the OHSC can do significantly more within its existing mandate. It can publish inspection results more systematically, it can increase the frequency and coverage of inspections within its existing budget, and the Minister of Health can issue regulations under the National Health Act that strengthen the standards against which inspections are conducted. The Ideal Clinic Realisation and Maintenance programme — which sets operational standards for primary healthcare facilities — operates as a departmental programme, not a legislative requirement. Converting Ideal Clinic compliance into a formal regulatory requirement could be achieved through regulations under the National Health Act, without amending the Act itself.

Consolidating the multiple health conditional grants into a single outcome-based PHC grant follows the same pathway as the education grant consolidation — it is a Division of Revenue framework change achievable through the annual DORA process. The shift from input conditions to outcome targets with operational discretion for provinces is a grant design decision within National Treasury's authority.

Ring-fenced PHC funding on a weighted capitation basis — allocating PHC resources to districts based on population size, disease burden, and deprivation indices rather than historical budgets — is a more complex reform. The funding formula itself can be designed by National Treasury and introduced through the DORA. But making it binding and ensuring that provinces actually allocate the funding to districts as specified requires either legislative provisions in the National Health Act or enforceable conditions in the DORA. The NHI Act, to the extent it is being implemented, creates its own funding and contracting framework that would need to align with the district authority model — a point of potential legislative conflict that would need to be resolved through careful drafting.

The national Health Information Platform — consolidating data from the District Health Information System, the OHSC inspection database, and other sources into a publicly accessible dashboard — follows the same pathway as the Education Information System. It is an administrative and technical task within the existing mandate of the National Department of Health. No legislation is required. A ministerial directive or departmental policy can mandate the platform's development.

#### Suggested Sequencing

- **Month 1–3:** Minister of Health issues regulations under the National Health Act converting the Ideal Clinic programme from a voluntary departmental initiative into binding regulatory standards, with defined minimum facility requirements and compliance consequences (priority grant access for compliant facilities, mandatory remedial plans for non-compliant ones).
- **Month 1–6:** OHSC increases the frequency and public visibility of facility inspections under existing powers. Inspection results are published on a publicly accessible platform, creating reputational pressure for compliance.
- **Month 3–6:** Minister of Health issues a ministerial directive to develop the national Health Information Platform, consolidating data from the District Health Information System, OHSC inspections, and other sources into a single public dashboard.
- **Month 6–12:** National Treasury consolidates multiple health conditional grants into a single outcome-based PHC grant through the DORA process, specifying outcome targets (immunisation coverage, chronic disease management, maternal health indicators) with provincial operational discretion.
- **Year 1–2:** Draft the National Health Act amendment bill to establish semi-autonomous district PHC authorities. Coordinate drafting with the NHI Act implementation timeline to ensure the district authority model and NHI contracting framework are aligned. Begin designing the weighted capitation funding formula with Treasury.
- **Year 2–3:** Introduce the National Health Act amendment as a Section 76 bill, including OHSC enforcement strengthening provisions. Simultaneously introduce the weighted capitation formula through the DORA.
- **Year 3–5:** Phase in district PHC authorities across provinces, starting with provinces that have the strongest existing district management structures. National department transitions from operational oversight to standard-setting and enforcement.

### Human Settlements Reforms

The [human settlements reform blueprint](/articles/ra-2-3-fixing-human-settlements) proposed municipal housing delivery agencies with end-to-end ownership, consolidated grants, and mandatory title deed registration timelines.

Creating municipal housing delivery agencies requires amending the Housing Act. The current Act allocates housing delivery functions across national, provincial, and municipal spheres, with the province managing the subsidy disbursement pipeline and the municipality handling land, bulk infrastructure, and building plan approval. Consolidating these functions into a single municipal or regional delivery agency requires restructuring the institutional architecture in the Act. This is a Section 76 bill, because it affects the roles of both provincial and municipal government in housing delivery.

There is, however, a faster pathway for metros and capable municipalities. The Housing Act already provides for the accreditation of municipalities to administer national housing programmes — the Level 2 accreditation framework enables municipalities to take on functions that are otherwise provincial. Accelerating the accreditation process, expanding the scope of Level 2 accreditation, and ensuring that accredited municipalities receive direct funding transfers are changes that can be made through regulatory amendments to the accreditation framework and through the Division of Revenue. This regulatory pathway could deliver much of the consolidation that the reform envisages for metropolitan municipalities, without waiting for legislative amendment. The legislative change would then focus on extending the model to non-metropolitan areas and formalising the institutional structures.

Consolidating the HSDG and USDG into a single human settlements grant is a Division of Revenue framework change — achievable through the annual DORA process. The national Housing Delivery Platform (a consolidated project tracking system) is an administrative tool within DHS's existing mandate. Neither requires legislation.

Mandatory title deed registration timelines — requiring that a title deed be registered within a prescribed period after a house is completed and occupied — requires legislative amendment. The current Housing Act does not impose binding timelines on title deed registration, and the Deeds Registries Act governs registration procedures. A provision mandating title deed registration timelines could be inserted into the Housing Act through amendment. This is a Section 76 bill.

Strengthening NHBRC enforcement — including automatic triggers for construction quality failures — requires amending the Housing Consumers Protection Measures Act (which establishes the NHBRC). The NHBRC already has inspection and enforcement powers, but its capacity is limited and its sanctions are weak. Legislative amendment could strengthen its powers, mandate more frequent inspections, and create automatic consequences for builders who fail quality assessments. This is likely a Section 75 bill, because the NHBRC is a national regulatory body and the amendment does not directly affect provincial competences — though this classification could be contested.

#### Suggested Sequencing

- **Month 1–6:** National Treasury consolidates the Human Settlements Development Grant and Urban Settlements Development Grant into a single human settlements grant through the DORA process, with outcome-based conditions (houses completed, title deeds registered, bulk infrastructure connected).
- **Month 1–6:** DHS develops the national Housing Delivery Platform — a consolidated project tracking system covering land identification, construction progress, and title deed registration — as an administrative tool within its existing mandate.
- **Month 3–12:** DHS accelerates the Level 2 accreditation process for metropolitan municipalities through regulatory amendments to the accreditation framework. Accredited metros receive direct funding transfers, bypassing the provincial department. This regulatory pathway delivers consolidation for the most capable municipalities without waiting for legislation.
- **Month 6–12:** DHS expands the scope of Level 2 accreditation through regulatory amendment, enabling accredited municipalities to manage the full housing delivery chain (beneficiary selection, developer appointment, construction oversight, title deed registration) rather than only specific sub-functions.
- **Year 1–2:** Draft the Housing Act amendment bill to create municipal housing delivery agencies with end-to-end ownership, including mandatory title deed registration timelines. Use the accredited metro experience as proof of concept.
- **Year 1–2:** Draft the Housing Consumers Protection Measures Act amendment to strengthen NHBRC enforcement, including automatic triggers for construction quality failures and expanded inspection capacity.
- **Year 2–3:** Introduce both bills in Parliament. The Housing Act amendment is a Section 76 bill; the NHBRC Act amendment may qualify as Section 75 but should be prepared for Section 76 classification.
- **Year 3–5:** Extend the municipal delivery agency model to non-metropolitan areas, creating regional delivery agencies where individual municipalities lack capacity. Transition provincial departments from delivery management to standard-setting and monitoring.

:::expert
The Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation's review of the housing delivery chain has documented that "the gap between policy aspiration and implementation reality is widest at the point where legislative authority meets administrative capacity." DPME's assessment found that several reforms proposed by the housing White Paper process and subsequent policy reviews could have been implemented through existing regulatory provisions in the Housing Act and the Division of Revenue framework — but were never acted on because the political and administrative energy was directed toward legislative amendment processes that stalled in Parliament. The lesson is instructive: always exhaust the regulatory pathway before pursuing legislation, because the regulatory pathway is faster, requires fewer political stars to align, and can demonstrate proof of concept before the legislative debate begins.
:::

### Provincial Governance Reforms

The [structured autonomy model](/articles/ra-3-2-making-concurrent-functions-work) and the [automatic enforcement trigger system](/articles/ra-3-3-when-provinces-fail) proposed in Part 3 are cross-cutting reforms that apply to all concurrent functions. Their legal pathways are correspondingly cross-cutting.

The structured autonomy model — national standard-setting combined with delivery-level operational authority and automatic enforcement triggers — does not require constitutional amendment. Schedule 4 of the Constitution lists concurrent functions but does not prescribe the institutional architecture for delivering them. The Constitution does not require that provinces manage schools in any particular way, or that the national government control teacher employment conditions. These arrangements are products of ordinary legislation — the Employment of Educators Act, the National Health Act, the Housing Act — and can be changed through ordinary legislation. The structured autonomy model is implemented through the function-specific legislative amendments described above (education, healthcare, human settlements) and through the Division of Revenue framework changes that shift conditional grants from input conditions to outcome targets.

The Framework for Concurrent Function Enforcement Act — a dedicated law establishing the general principles of automatic enforcement triggers, graduated responses, and binding exit criteria — is a new piece of legislation. Its Section 75/76 classification depends on its drafting. If the Act establishes a national framework that directly prescribes how provincial functions are monitored and enforced, it is almost certainly a Section 76 bill. If it is drafted more narrowly, as a framework that empowers national departments to establish function-specific triggers through regulation, it might qualify as Section 75 — but this is a classification that would likely be contested, and the safer assumption is Section 76.

However, much of the enforcement architecture can be established without a dedicated framework law. The Intergovernmental Relations Framework Act already provides for intergovernmental structures and dispute resolution. Amending the IGRFA to incorporate automatic trigger thresholds and prescribed responses is one legislative vehicle. Alternatively, the enforcement triggers can be embedded in each function-specific statute — the Employment of Educators Act, the National Health Act, the Water Services Act — as each is amended to implement the structured autonomy model. The dedicated framework law would then serve a consolidating and standardising function rather than an enabling one.

The Provincial Performance Dashboard — the publicly accessible, automatically updated platform that consolidates performance data across all concurrent functions — does not require legislation. As discussed in [When Provinces Fail](/articles/ra-3-3-when-provinces-fail), the relevant data is already collected by the Auditor-General, National Treasury, DPME, and sector-specific departments. The dashboard is an integration and publication mechanism. A presidential directive or Cabinet resolution could mandate its development. The data-sharing protocols between departments could be established through intergovernmental agreements under the existing IGRFA.

#### Suggested Sequencing

- **Month 1–3:** President or Cabinet issues a directive mandating development of the Provincial Performance Dashboard, with DPME leading integration of data from the Auditor-General, National Treasury, DBE, NDoH, DWS, and sector departments. Define outcome thresholds for each concurrent function.
- **Month 3–6:** Provincial Performance Dashboard goes live, publishing each province's status against defined outcome thresholds. Even without legislative enforcement triggers, transparency generates reputational pressure and provides the data infrastructure that subsequent enforcement legislation requires.
- **Month 6–12:** National Treasury shifts conditional grants from input-based conditions to outcome-based targets through the DORA process. This is the fiscal mechanism that operationalises structured autonomy across all concurrent functions simultaneously.
- **Month 6–12:** Establish intergovernmental agreements under the IGRFA for voluntary enforcement pilots — one or two provinces agree to automatic enforcement trigger protocols for a specific function (e.g., education in the Western Cape, healthcare in KwaZulu-Natal), testing the model before legislation.
- **Year 1–2:** Draft the Framework for Concurrent Function Enforcement Act (or IGRFA amendments), using the dashboard data and pilot experience to inform threshold design and trigger mechanisms. Simultaneously, embed function-specific enforcement triggers in each sectoral amendment bill (Employment of Educators Act, National Health Act, Water Services Act).
- **Year 2–3:** Introduce the framework legislation as a Section 76 bill. The choice between a standalone Act and IGRFA amendments is a drafting strategy decision — the IGRFA route builds on existing institutional infrastructure; the standalone route creates a cleaner, more visible legal instrument.
- **Year 3–5:** Enforcement architecture is operational across all concurrent functions. The Dashboard provides the data. The legislation defines the triggers. The graduated responses (Tier 1 technical support, Tier 2 joint management, Tier 3 temporary takeover) activate automatically based on outcome performance.

### Municipal Function Reforms

The Part 4 reforms address water, electricity, waste, roads, and cross-cutting municipal capability. Their legal pathways vary.

For water and sanitation, the proposed WSA/WSP separation — creating a structural firewall between the Water Services Authority function (regulatory, standard-setting, oversight) and the Water Services Provider function (operational delivery) — requires amending the Water Services Act. The current Act allows a WSA to be its own WSP, which creates the conflation of regulator and provider that produces accountability failure. Legislating a mandatory separation, requiring that the WSP be a distinct entity (whether a municipal entity, a utility, or a contracted provider), is a structural change to the Act. The Water Services Act is a Section 76 bill because it directly affects how municipalities exercise their water services function.

Ring-fenced water revenue — requiring that revenue collected from water services be dedicated to water infrastructure maintenance and operations rather than absorbed into the municipality's general revenue — can be achieved through regulatory action. The Municipal Finance Management Act and the Municipal Systems Act already provide regulatory authority over municipal financial management. National Treasury can issue regulations or guidelines requiring ring-fencing of trading services revenue. The Municipal Standard Chart of Accounts, which Treasury prescribes, already requires separate accounting for water services — the step from separate accounting to binding ring-fencing is a regulatory one.

Blue Drop and Green Drop enforcement — making the Department of Water and Sanitation's water quality assessment programmes binding rather than voluntary — requires legislative amendment if the intention is to create automatic enforcement consequences. The Water Services Act would need to be amended to link Blue and Green Drop scores to mandatory remedial action. However, DWS can strengthen the reputational consequences of poor scores through policy — by publishing results more prominently, linking scores to conditional grant eligibility, or using poor scores as triggers for Section 63 interventions (the Water Services Act's own intervention provision).

Process controller qualifications — requiring that water treatment works be operated by qualified process controllers at prescribed levels — are already provided for in the Water Services Act regulations. The problem is not the absence of legal authority but the absence of enforcement. DWS can enforce existing qualification requirements through its regulatory powers without any legislative change.

For electricity, the proposed reforms include ring-fenced electricity revenue, independent power procurement pathways, and NERSA tariff reform. Ring-fenced electricity revenue follows the same pathway as ring-fenced water revenue — achievable through National Treasury regulation under the MFMA. Independent power procurement pathways for municipalities have already been partly enabled through amendments to the Electricity Regulation Act and NERSA's regulatory framework. Further reforms to enable municipalities to procure power from independent producers more easily may require additional NERSA regulatory changes rather than legislative amendment — NERSA has broad regulatory authority under the ERA to determine licensing and procurement frameworks. Tariff reform, to the extent it involves the structure of NERSA's tariff-setting methodology, falls within NERSA's existing regulatory authority and can be achieved through regulatory process.

For waste and roads, the structural reforms parallel the water model — ring-fenced revenue (regulatory pathway), professional qualifications for technical staff (regulatory pathway under existing municipal legislation), and strengthened enforcement of environmental standards (legislative amendment to strengthen enforcement provisions in the National Environmental Management: Waste Act, which is a Section 76 bill because it affects municipal competences).

For municipal capability, the reforms include mandatory professional qualifications for technical appointments, protected technical appointments insulated from political interference, shared services arrangements for small municipalities, and real-time operational dashboards. Mandatory professional qualifications are largely achievable through regulation. The Municipal Systems Act already provides for the competency requirements for senior managers, and the regulations made under Section 72 of the Act prescribe minimum competency levels. Extending these requirements to technical posts (engineers, planners, process controllers, financial managers) and strengthening the qualifications can be done through regulatory amendment. Protected technical appointments — provisions that prevent political office-bearers from overriding merit-based appointments or removing technical staff without cause — would benefit from legislative strengthening. Amendments to the Municipal Systems Act that create statutory protection for prescribed technical posts (comparable to the protection that the Municipal Finance Management Act provides for the CFO function) would be a Section 76 bill.

Shared services arrangements for small municipalities can be established through intergovernmental agreements under the existing Municipal Systems Act and the IGRFA. No new legislation is required — the legal framework already permits municipalities to share services. The constraint is administrative and political, not legal. Real-time operational dashboards for municipal services are administrative tools that fall within the existing mandate of municipal managers and can be mandated through National Treasury's municipal reporting requirements.

#### Suggested Sequencing

- **Month 1–3:** National Treasury issues MFMA regulations mandating ring-fenced revenue for municipal trading services (water, electricity, waste). Publish a three-year transition schedule for municipalities to reduce cross-subsidy extraction. This is the single most impactful regulatory change for municipal infrastructure — it protects the revenue base that funds maintenance and renewal.
- **Month 1–3:** DWS begins enforcing existing process controller qualification requirements through its regulatory powers — inspections, compliance notices, and enforcement action against water services authorities operating treatment works without qualified personnel.
- **Month 3–6:** Minister of Public Service and Administration extends competency requirements to prescribed municipal technical posts (chief engineers, heads of water/electricity services, town planners) through regulatory amendment under the Municipal Systems Act.
- **Month 3–6:** Minister of Cooperative Governance issues a directive to provincial COGTA departments to facilitate shared services arrangements in districts where municipalities lack capacity for key technical functions. Use existing Municipal Systems Act and IGRFA provisions — no new legislation required.
- **Month 6–12:** National Treasury mandates real-time operational dashboards for municipal services through its municipal reporting framework. NERSA publishes standardised IPP procurement templates for municipal use. DWS strengthens Blue Drop and Green Drop publication and links scores to conditional grant eligibility.
- **Year 1–2:** Draft the Water Services Act amendment bill for WSA/WSP separation — the highest-priority municipal legislation given the water crisis. Simultaneously draft the Municipal Capability Act (or Municipal Systems Act amendments) for protected technical appointments. Draft Waste Act amendments for environmental enforcement strengthening.
- **Year 1–2:** For electricity, pursue further NERSA regulatory changes to streamline municipal IPP procurement. Ring-fenced electricity revenue (already mandated in Month 1–3) begins stabilising electricity infrastructure investment. Tariff reform proceeds through NERSA's existing regulatory process.
- **Year 2–3:** Introduce the Water Services Act amendment, Municipal Capability Act, and Waste Act amendment as a coordinated municipal reform legislative package — all Section 76 bills. Introducing them together signals the coherence of the reform programme and enables Parliament to consider the cross-cutting municipal capability provisions alongside the function-specific structural changes.
- **Year 3–5:** WSA/WSP separation is implemented across municipalities, with transition arrangements for municipalities that currently conflate both functions. Protected technical appointments are operational. Regional water utilities are established where Section 78 assessments indicate municipal incapacity. The electricity distribution model evolves as the South African Wholesale Electricity Market matures and the regulated wire fee replaces the cross-subsidy.

## What Can Be Done Tomorrow

The reform-by-reform legal map reveals something that should give every reform advocate both hope and frustration: a remarkable number of the reforms proposed across this series can be implemented through regulation, policy change, or executive action under existing legislation. No new Acts of Parliament. No constitutional amendments. No multi-year legislative processes. Just political will and administrative competence applied to powers that already exist.

Here is what can be done within months, using existing legal authority.

National Treasury can restructure conditional grants from input-based conditions to outcome-based targets through the annual Division of Revenue process. This single change — which Treasury has the authority to make unilaterally in drafting the next DORA — would begin the shift from national micromanagement to structured autonomy for every function funded through conditional grants. Treasury already redesigns grants regularly. The shift to outcome-based conditions is a design choice, not a legal barrier. The Education Infrastructure Grant, the Health Facility Revitalisation Grant, the Human Settlements Development Grant, and the Municipal Infrastructure Grant can all be restructured to specify what outcomes must be achieved while giving the recipient entity discretion over how to achieve them.

National Treasury can mandate ring-fenced revenue for municipal trading services through regulations under the MFMA. Water, electricity, and waste services generate revenue that is currently absorbed into municipal general revenue and often diverted to fund other functions — leaving infrastructure maintenance unfunded. Treasury's existing regulatory authority over municipal financial management is broad enough to require that trading services revenue be ring-fenced for the maintenance, renewal, and expansion of the infrastructure that generates it. The Municipal Standard Chart of Accounts already requires separate accounting for each service; the step to binding ring-fencing is regulatory, not legislative.

The Minister of Basic Education can establish an Education Information System through ministerial directive. DBE already collects data through EMIS, matric examinations, and school monitoring surveys. Integrating these into a publicly accessible, near-real-time dashboard is a technical and administrative project. The minister can direct the department to develop the platform, mandate provinces to submit data in specified formats, and publish the results. No legislation is needed — this is within DBE's existing mandate.

The Minister of Health can strengthen Ideal Clinic enforcement through regulations under the National Health Act. The Ideal Clinic programme currently operates as a departmental initiative rather than a binding regulatory requirement. Publishing regulations that prescribe minimum facility standards and link compliance to specific consequences (priority access to conditional grant funding, mandatory remedial plans for non-compliant facilities) converts a voluntary programme into a regulatory floor. The OHSC can publish inspection results more systematically and frequently under its existing powers — transparency is not a legislative change, it is an administrative choice.

The Department of Water and Sanitation can enforce existing process controller qualification requirements. The regulations requiring qualified process controllers at water treatment works already exist. DWS has the regulatory authority to inspect, issue compliance notices, and pursue enforcement action against water services authorities that operate treatment works without qualified personnel. The problem is not legal authority but institutional will and capacity to exercise existing powers.

The President or Cabinet can mandate the development of the Provincial Performance Dashboard through a presidential directive or Cabinet resolution. The data is already collected by the AG, Treasury, DPME, and sector departments. The dashboard is an integration layer — it brings existing data into a single publicly accessible platform and automates the comparison against defined thresholds. This can be developed as a digital platform project under DPME or the Department of Communications and Digital Technologies, drawing on existing data feeds.

The Minister of Cooperative Governance can mandate shared services arrangements for small municipalities through existing provisions in the Municipal Systems Act and the IGRFA. The legal framework for inter-municipal cooperation already exists — municipalities can establish shared service centres, joint technical units, and cooperative procurement arrangements. What has been lacking is the national push to make these arrangements happen. A ministerial directive to provincial COGTA departments to facilitate shared services in districts where municipalities lack capacity to perform key technical functions — water treatment, financial management, infrastructure maintenance — can be issued under existing authority.

The Minister of Public Service and Administration can strengthen competency requirements for municipal technical posts through regulations under the Municipal Systems Act. The existing competency framework covers senior managers. Extending it to prescribed technical posts — chief engineers, heads of water services, heads of electricity, town planners — requires regulatory amendment to the competency regulations, which the minister has the authority to issue.

:::takeaway
A remarkable number of the reforms proposed in this series — ring-fenced municipal revenue, outcome-based conditional grants, mandatory performance dashboards, strengthened qualification requirements, Ideal Clinic enforcement, process controller compliance — can be implemented under existing legislation through regulation, policy change, or executive action. The legal authority already exists. The constraint is political will and administrative capacity. Exhausting the regulatory pathway first is not a compromise — it is a strategy that delivers results faster and builds the evidence base for subsequent legislative reform.
:::

## What Needs Legislation

After exhausting the regulatory pathway, a significant set of reforms still requires new legislation or amendment to existing Acts. These are the structural changes that cannot be achieved within the bounds of existing enabling provisions — they require Parliament to create new legal authority or fundamentally reshape existing institutional arrangements.

### Section 76 Bills (Affecting Provinces)

The most important legislative reforms require Section 76 bills — ordinary legislation that must pass through both the National Assembly and the NCOP, with provinces voting as delegations in the NCOP. This is a higher bar than Section 75 legislation, but it is the appropriate pathway for reforms that change how provinces exercise their constitutional competences.

The Employment of Educators Act amendments are the centrepiece of the education reform. Restructuring this Act to transfer operational employment authority to provincial departments — enabling provinces to offer scarce-skills supplements, manage teacher deployment, and conduct disciplinary processes within a nationally agreed framework — is a fundamental change to the employment architecture of education. The ELRC mandate restructuring, separating national salary bargaining from operational employment matters, sits within the same legislative package. This is politically sensitive because it affects the interests of teacher unions (who have strong representation at the ELRC and prefer centralised bargaining) and because it shifts power from the national department to provinces. But it is the single reform that would most directly address the authority-accountability split that the [education RAARICLE™ diagnosis](/articles/ra-2-1-fixing-basic-education) identified as the core structural failure.

The National Health Act amendments required to establish semi-autonomous district PHC authorities are the healthcare equivalent. Creating entities with their own governance structures, delegated procurement authority, and staffing powers within a provincial framework requires restructuring the institutional provisions of the Act. The OHSC enforcement strengthening — giving the office real sanctioning powers — sits within the same legislative package. If the NHI Act is being implemented concurrently, careful drafting is needed to ensure that the district authority model and the NHI contracting framework are aligned rather than creating parallel and potentially conflicting institutional arrangements.

The Water Services Act amendments for WSA/WSP separation are the most structurally significant municipal reform. Mandating that every Water Services Authority establish a structurally separate Water Services Provider — ending the conflation of regulator and operator that produces accountability failure — requires rewriting the institutional provisions of the Water Services Act. The Act would need to specify the governance requirements for WSPs, the regulatory relationship between the WSA and the WSP, the financial ring-fencing requirements, and the transition arrangements for municipalities that currently perform both functions through a single departmental structure. Given South Africa's water crisis, this legislation should be a high priority — but it is also politically complex, because it affects the organisational structure of every WSA municipality.

The Housing Act amendments for municipal delivery agencies — consolidating the fragmented housing delivery chain into end-to-end municipal or regional ownership — restructure the roles of national, provincial, and municipal government in housing delivery. The amendments would need to redefine the provincial role (from delivery management to standard-setting and monitoring), expand the municipal role (from land and infrastructure to full project management), and create provisions for regional delivery agencies in areas where individual municipalities lack capacity. The mandatory title deed registration timelines would sit within the same legislative package.

A Municipal Capability Act (or amendments to the Municipal Systems Act) to create statutory protection for prescribed technical posts, mandatory professional qualification requirements for all technical appointments, and legally protected appointment processes insulated from political interference is another Section 76 bill. This legislation would create, for technical municipal posts, the same kind of statutory protection that the MFMA provides for the chief financial officer — making it legally impermissible to appoint an unqualified person to a prescribed technical post, and creating legal consequences for political office-bearers who interfere with protected appointments.

The Framework for Concurrent Function Enforcement Act — establishing the general architecture of automatic triggers, graduated responses (Tier 1 technical support, Tier 2 joint management, Tier 3 temporary takeover), and binding exit criteria — is a Section 76 bill because it directly affects how national government monitors and enforces provincial performance. This is the legislative vehicle for converting the discretionary Section 100 model into an automatic enforcement architecture. Alternatively, the enforcement triggers can be embedded in each function-specific statute as it is amended, with the framework Act serving a consolidating function. The choice between a standalone framework Act and function-specific provisions is a drafting strategy decision, not a constitutional one.

Amendments to the Intergovernmental Relations Framework Act to incorporate the enforcement architecture are an alternative legislative vehicle. The IGRFA already establishes intergovernmental structures and dispute resolution mechanisms. Adding automatic trigger thresholds, mandatory support protocols, and binding exit criteria to the IGRFA would embed the enforcement architecture in the existing intergovernmental framework rather than creating a parallel legislative structure. This approach has the advantage of building on an existing Act with established institutional infrastructure (the forums and structures created under the IGRFA) rather than starting from scratch.

### Section 75 Bills (National Matters)

Some reforms may qualify as Section 75 bills — legislation that does not directly affect provincial competences and can be passed by the National Assembly alone. The classification is important because Section 75 bills face a lower political threshold: they need a simple majority in the NA and cannot be blocked by the NCOP.

The NHBRC Act amendments to strengthen construction quality enforcement — expanding the NHBRC's inspection capacity, strengthening its sanctioning powers, and creating automatic consequences for builders who fail quality assessments — may qualify as Section 75 because the NHBRC is a national regulatory body. However, because the NHBRC regulates housing construction that is funded through provincial programmes and delivered by provincial-accredited developers, the Section 75/76 classification could be contested. Prudent legislative strategy would prepare for a Section 76 process while arguing for Section 75 classification.

Certain provisions of the Framework for Concurrent Function Enforcement Act — particularly those that establish national monitoring institutions, define national reporting standards, and create the institutional infrastructure for the enforcement architecture — could potentially be separated into a Section 75 component. The national monitoring capacity and information integration functions do not directly affect provincial competences; they affect how the national government organises its own monitoring activities. Splitting the framework legislation into a Section 75 component (national institutional infrastructure) and a Section 76 component (enforcement triggers that affect provinces) is a legislative strategy that could accelerate implementation by progressing the less politically contentious components through the faster pathway.

:::expert
Parliamentary legal advisors have noted in National Assembly committee proceedings that the Section 75/76 classification is "frequently contested and has been the subject of multiple Constitutional Court rulings." The Joint Tagging Mechanism, which classifies bills before introduction, applies a purposive test — asking whether the substance of the bill affects provincial competences, regardless of its technical drafting. Legislative strategists working on governance reform must anticipate classification disputes and prepare for the possibility that a bill drafted as Section 75 will be reclassified as Section 76 by the Joint Tagging Mechanism or on challenge. The safest approach is to design legislation that can pass through either pathway — building the political support needed for Section 76 passage while pursuing Section 75 classification where legitimately arguable.
:::

## What Needs Constitutional Amendment

The most important finding of this legal mapping exercise is how little of the proposed reform programme actually requires constitutional amendment. Politicians and commentators frequently invoke constitutional barriers as reasons why governance reform is impossible or must wait for some future political dispensation. The reality is far more encouraging.

The Constitution of South Africa is remarkably flexible in its allocation of concurrent functions. Schedule 4 lists functions as concurrent — shared between national and provincial government — but it does not prescribe the institutional architecture for delivering those functions. The Constitution does not require that provinces employ teachers through a national bargaining framework, or that the national government control conditional grant conditions at the input level, or that provincial health departments manage district health services through internal departmental structures rather than semi-autonomous entities. These arrangements are products of ordinary legislation and can be changed through ordinary legislation.

The structured autonomy model — national standard-setting, delivery-level operational authority, automatic enforcement triggers — operates entirely within the existing constitutional framework. It does not move any function from one schedule to another. It does not change the powers of any sphere of government. It does not create new constitutional institutions. It restructures the legislative and fiscal architecture within which existing constitutional competences are exercised. This is precisely what ordinary legislation is for.

The automatic enforcement triggers for concurrent functions operate within Section 100's existing permissive framework. As [When Provinces Fail](/articles/ra-3-3-when-provinces-fail) demonstrated in detail, Section 100 does not prescribe the trigger mechanism, the decision-making process, or the exit criteria. It does not prohibit automatic triggers. It does not require that the decision to intervene be discretionary. The text is broad enough to accommodate a legislative framework that defines automatic triggers, prescribed responses, and binding exit criteria — provided the framework operates within the constitutional authority that Section 100 confers.

So what, if anything, actually requires constitutional amendment?

There are two areas where constitutional amendment would strengthen the reform programme, though neither is strictly necessary.

The first is the creation of constitutional status for the enforcement architecture. A constitutional provision explicitly establishing the automatic enforcement trigger system — mandating outcome-based monitoring, requiring graduated responses, and prescribing binding exit criteria — would give the enforcement architecture greater permanence and protection from political erosion. Without constitutional status, the enforcement framework exists in ordinary legislation that can be amended or repealed by a future parliamentary majority. With constitutional status, it would require a two-thirds supermajority to dismantle. The practical question is whether the risk of political erosion is high enough to justify the enormous difficulty of achieving a constitutional amendment. Given that the enforcement architecture serves the interests of citizens and oversight bodies (who want effective provincial governance) against the interests of underperforming political executives (who prefer discretionary, avoidable enforcement), there is a genuine risk that future political dynamics could weaken the framework through ordinary legislative amendment. Constitutional entrenchment would protect against this risk.

The second area is Schedule 4 and Schedule 5 reform. The current allocation of functions between the schedules reflects decisions made during the constitutional negotiations of the early 1990s. Some functions that are listed as concurrent in Schedule 4 might be more effectively delivered if they were moved to Schedule 5 (exclusive provincial competence) or assigned exclusively to national or local government. Conversely, some functions in Schedule 5 (exclusive provincial competence) might benefit from being moved to Schedule 4 to enable national standard-setting and enforcement. Any movement of functions between schedules requires constitutional amendment. However — and this is the critical point — the structured autonomy model achieves the practical effect of schedule reform without actually changing the schedules. By restructuring the legislative architecture within which concurrent functions are delivered, the model gives delivery entities the operational authority they need without formally changing the constitutional allocation. Schedule reform is a theoretical improvement but a practical distraction — it would consume enormous political energy for a change that can be substantially achieved through legislation.

The conclusion is straightforward: the entire reform programme proposed in this series can be implemented through legislation and regulation. Constitutional amendment would add belt-and-braces protection for the enforcement architecture, and schedule reform would tidy up the constitutional allocation of functions, but neither is a prerequisite for implementing the reforms. Anyone who tells you that governance reform requires constitutional change before anything can happen is either mistaken about the law or using constitutional complexity as a shield against reform they would prefer not to see.

:::takeaway
The most important finding of this legal mapping is that virtually none of the proposed reforms require constitutional amendment. The structured autonomy model, automatic enforcement triggers, ring-fenced funding, professional qualification requirements, and consolidated delivery agencies all operate within the existing constitutional framework and can be implemented through ordinary legislation and regulation. Constitutional amendment would strengthen the enforcement architecture's durability, but it is not a prerequisite. The Constitution is not the barrier to governance reform — political will is.
:::

## The Political Feasibility Test

Knowing the legal pathway is necessary but not sufficient. Every reform, regardless of its legal classification, must pass a political feasibility test. Legislation does not write itself. Regulations do not sign themselves. Executive action requires executive commitment. The political dynamics of reform — who benefits, who loses, who has veto power — determine whether legally achievable reforms actually happen.

The political landscape that any governance reform must navigate has several distinctive features.

The Government of National Unity formed after the 2024 national elections brings multiple parties into the national executive. The GNU creates an unusual dynamic for governance reform. On one hand, it broadens the base of political support available for reform — a multi-party coalition could, in principle, assemble the votes needed for Section 76 legislation more easily than a single party. On the other hand, coalition governance requires compromise, and reform proposals that threaten the interests of any coalition partner may be vetoed. The GNU's capacity for governance reform depends on whether the coalition partners share a common diagnosis of what is broken and a common interest in fixing it — or whether their governing agreement papers over fundamental disagreements about the structure of the state.

The NCOP is the critical institution for Section 76 legislation. In the NCOP, provinces vote as delegations — each province casts a single vote based on a mandate from its provincial legislature. This means that Section 76 legislation needs the support of at least five provincial governments. The political composition of provincial governments — which parties control which provinces, and how those parties are aligned with or opposed to the national government — determines the NCOP arithmetic. After the 2024 elections, provincial governments are controlled by different party combinations, some aligned with the national GNU and some governed by different coalitions. For any Section 76 bill, the reform champion must secure support from a majority of these diverse provincial governments — each with its own political dynamics, its own interests, and its own assessment of how the reform affects its power and resources.

The incentive structure for provincial governments facing reform proposals is complex. Structured autonomy, in theory, gives provinces more operational authority — which provincial governments should welcome. But it also introduces automatic enforcement triggers — which provincial governments may resist, because automatic enforcement removes the political discretion that currently allows underperforming provinces to avoid consequences. The net political calculus for each province depends on its self-assessment: well-performing provinces should welcome both more autonomy and automatic enforcement (because they will benefit from the autonomy and not be penalised by the enforcement), while poorly performing provinces may accept the autonomy but resist the enforcement. The political feasibility of the reform package depends on whether enough provinces perceive the net benefit as positive.

Labour unions are powerful stakeholders in several of the proposed reforms. The restructuring of the ELRC mandate — separating national salary bargaining from operational employment matters — directly affects teacher unions, which are among the most politically influential organisations in South Africa. Teacher unions prefer centralised bargaining because it gives them maximum leverage: negotiating with a single national counterpart for outcomes that bind all nine provinces. Decentralising operational employment matters to provinces would fragment the unions' bargaining position and reduce their national leverage. Expect strong union resistance to this reform, which will be expressed both through collective bargaining channels and through political pressure on the governing parties (several of which have formal or informal alliance relationships with the unions).

Similarly, reforms to municipal staffing — mandatory professional qualifications, protected technical appointments — threaten the patronage networks that some political parties use to reward supporters with municipal jobs. Creating statutory protection for technical appointments and making it legally impermissible to appoint unqualified persons to prescribed posts directly constrains the discretion of mayors and municipal councils to make political appointments to technical positions. This reform will face resistance from any political actor who benefits from the current system of political appointments to technical posts.

Civil society organisations, professional bodies, and oversight institutions are natural allies of governance reform. The Auditor-General, the Financial and Fiscal Commission, the Public Service Commission, professional engineering bodies, and civil society organisations like Section27, Equal Education, and the Legal Resources Centre have advocated for many of the reforms proposed in this series. Their support does not translate directly into votes in Parliament, but it creates political cover for reform champions, generates public pressure for action, and provides technical input that strengthens legislative drafting. A reform strategy that actively engages these allies — sharing draft legislation, seeking input on technical provisions, coordinating advocacy campaigns — has a better chance of building the political momentum needed to move legislation through Parliament.

The media plays a shaping role in reform politics. Governance reform is not inherently newsworthy — it lacks the drama of corruption scandals or political crises. But specific reform proposals, if communicated clearly and linked to tangible citizen impacts (your water quality, your child's education, your housing title deed), can generate media attention that creates political pressure. The GovCompass approach — making governance legible to ordinary citizens — is itself a reform strategy: citizens who understand what is broken and what the fix looks like are more likely to demand action from their elected representatives and more likely to hold those representatives accountable for progress.

## A Sequenced Legal Reform Programme

Converting the legal map and political feasibility analysis into a practical implementation timeline requires sequencing — doing the fastest, easiest, and most impactful reforms first, building momentum and evidence, and then tackling the more politically difficult legislative changes.

The following programme organises the reform agenda into three phases, sequenced by legal pathway and political feasibility.

### Phase 1: Regulatory Quick Wins (Year 1)

The first phase focuses exclusively on reforms achievable through regulation, policy change, and executive action — no new legislation required. These are the reforms that a committed national executive could begin implementing immediately, demonstrating progress and building the political case for subsequent legislative reform.

National Treasury restructures conditional grants from input-based conditions to outcome-based targets in the next Division of Revenue cycle. The Education Infrastructure Grant, Health Facility Revitalisation Grant, and Human Settlements Development Grant are redesigned to specify outcome targets (schools built to specification and occupied, clinics meeting Ideal Clinic standards, houses completed with title deeds registered) while giving recipient entities discretion over how to achieve them. This is the single most impactful regulatory change, because it begins shifting the intergovernmental fiscal relationship from control to accountability across all functions simultaneously.

National Treasury issues regulations requiring ring-fenced revenue for municipal trading services — water, electricity, and waste. Municipal revenue from these services is legally required to be dedicated to the maintenance, renewal, and expansion of the infrastructure that generates it, rather than being diverted to fund general municipal operations. This addresses one of the most critical causes of municipal infrastructure decay identified in the Part 4 articles.

The President or Cabinet mandates the development of the Provincial Performance Dashboard, directing DPME to lead the integration of data from the AG, Treasury, DBE, NDoH, DWS, and other sources into a publicly accessible platform. The dashboard includes defined performance thresholds for each concurrent function and publishes each province's status against those thresholds. Even without legislative enforcement triggers, the transparency created by the dashboard generates reputational pressure for performance improvement and gives citizens, media, and oversight bodies the information they need to hold provinces accountable.

The Minister of Basic Education establishes the Education Information System through ministerial directive, mandating provinces to submit specified data in standardised formats and publishing the results on a public platform. The Minister of Health does the same for the Health Information Platform and issues regulations strengthening Ideal Clinic standards as binding regulatory requirements. The Minister of Water and Sanitation directs enforcement of existing process controller qualification requirements and strengthens Blue and Green Drop publication and consequences.

The Minister of Public Service and Administration extends competency requirements to prescribed municipal technical posts through regulatory amendment, and the Minister of Cooperative Governance directs provincial COGTA departments to facilitate shared services arrangements in districts with small, capacity-constrained municipalities.

Phase 1 requires no parliamentary action. It requires only committed ministerial leadership and competent departmental execution. If the national executive implements Phase 1 comprehensively, citizens will see tangible changes within a year: better information about how their province and municipality are performing, ring-fenced revenue protecting infrastructure maintenance, and strengthened enforcement of existing quality standards.

### Phase 2: Legislation (Years 2-3)

The second phase introduces the legislation needed for structural reforms that cannot be achieved through regulation alone. Phase 2 legislation should be drafted during Phase 1, so that bills are ready for introduction as soon as the Phase 1 regulatory reforms are operational and generating evidence.

The legislative priority order reflects both impact and political feasibility. The Employment of Educators Act amendments and ELRC restructuring are the highest-impact education reform, but also the most politically contentious due to union opposition. They should be introduced early in Phase 2, allowing time for the extended consultative process that politically sensitive legislation requires.

The Water Services Act amendments for WSA/WSP separation address the most acute municipal crisis — water — and have broad support from oversight bodies, civil society, and well-governed municipalities. This legislation should be prioritised because the water emergency creates a political window for structural reform that may not remain open indefinitely.

The National Health Act amendments for district PHC authorities and OHSC enforcement strengthening should be sequenced alongside or shortly after the NHI implementation timeline, ensuring alignment between the two legislative frameworks. Introducing district authority legislation without coordinating with NHI implementation risks creating conflicting institutional mandates.

The Housing Act amendments for municipal delivery agencies and mandatory title deed timelines should be introduced in conjunction with the acceleration of Level 2 accreditation through the regulatory pathway in Phase 1. By the time the legislation reaches Parliament, the metros and capable municipalities will already be operating under expanded accreditation, providing a proof of concept for the legislative model.

The Framework for Concurrent Function Enforcement Act (or, alternatively, amendments to the IGRFA) should be introduced once the Provincial Performance Dashboard is operational and generating data. The dashboard provides the information architecture that the enforcement triggers require, and its operation during Phase 1 will reveal which thresholds and trigger mechanisms work in practice — informing the legislative design.

The Municipal Capability Act (or Municipal Systems Act amendments) for protected technical appointments and mandatory professional qualifications should be introduced alongside the regulatory strengthening of competency requirements in Phase 1. The regulations establish the principle; the legislation entrenches it with statutory protection.

All Section 76 bills in Phase 2 require active engagement with provincial governments through the NCOP. The reform champion (the relevant national minister, supported by the President or Deputy President) must secure mandates from at least five provincial delegations for each bill. This requires a political strategy for each province — understanding each province's interests, addressing each province's concerns, and where necessary, offering inducements (additional funding, technical support, transition assistance) to provinces that face adjustment costs from the reform.

### Phase 3: Constitutional Entrenchment (Years 3-5)

The third phase addresses constitutional amendment — not as a prerequisite for reform (which, as established, it is not) but as an optional strengthening measure to entrench the enforcement architecture against future political erosion.

If the Phase 2 legislation is working — if automatic enforcement triggers are activating, graduated responses are functioning, and provincial performance is improving — the case for constitutional entrenchment becomes both stronger (because there is a proven model to protect) and less urgent (because the model is already working through legislation). The political dynamics of constitutional amendment are unpredictable over a five-year horizon, and the two-thirds threshold in the National Assembly makes passage uncertain regardless of the reform's merits. Phase 3 should be pursued opportunistically — if a constitutional reform window opens (for example, through a broader constitutional review process or a political alignment that produces a two-thirds coalition), the enforcement architecture should be included. But the reform programme should not be held hostage to constitutional amendment. The legislative reforms in Phase 2 are sufficient to implement the full structured autonomy model with automatic enforcement.

Schedule reform — moving functions between Schedule 4 and Schedule 5, or creating new schedule provisions — falls in the same category: desirable as a constitutional tidying exercise but not necessary for the practical reforms. If a constitutional amendment process proceeds, schedule reform should be included in the package. If not, the legislative reforms achieve the practical effect of schedule reform without formal constitutional change.

**The Reform Pathway Classification Framework**

When you encounter a proposed governance reform — whether from a political party, civil society organisation, or your own analysis — use these four questions to classify it and assess its feasibility:

**Question 1: Does this reform change the text of the Constitution?**

If the reform requires altering the wording of any section of the Constitution — such as moving a function between Schedule 4 and Schedule 5, changing the powers of a sphere of government, or creating a new constitutional institution — it requires a constitutional amendment under Section 74. This means a two-thirds majority in the National Assembly and support from six of nine provinces in the NCOP. Very few reforms actually require this step, but politicians sometimes claim constitutional barriers to avoid acting on reforms that only need legislation.

**Question 2: Does this reform affect provincial competences?**

If the reform changes how provinces exercise their powers — even without amending the Constitution — it likely requires a Section 76 bill, which must pass through the NCOP where provinces vote as delegations. This is a higher bar than ordinary legislation but far more achievable than constitutional amendment. Most reforms to concurrent functions (Schedule 4) follow this pathway.

**Question 3: Can this reform be achieved through regulation or policy?**

Many governance improvements do not require new legislation at all. A national minister can issue regulations under existing enabling legislation, or a department can change its policy frameworks. For example, requiring municipalities to publish specific performance data can often be achieved through regulations under the Municipal Systems Act without amending the Act itself. Always check whether the existing law already provides the authority for the reform you want.

**Question 4: What is the realistic timeline?**

Constitutional amendments take a full parliamentary term or longer. New legislation typically takes two to four years from drafting to promulgation. Regulatory changes can happen within months. When you assess a reform proposal, classify it into one of these three timelines. If someone promises a constitutional change within a year, they are either uninformed or not being honest about the pathway.

With these four questions, you can cut through political rhetoric about governance reform and assess whether a proposal is achievable, what pathway it requires, and how long it will realistically take — so you can direct your advocacy energy where it will have the most impact.

## A Sequenced Legal Reform Programme — The Five Anti-Capture Vectors

Every phase of the reform programme must address the five anti-capture vectors that this series has identified as essential protections for institutional integrity.

Political capture is addressed across all three phases. In Phase 1, the Provincial Performance Dashboard makes performance data publicly visible, reducing the ability of political principals to control the narrative about provincial performance. In Phase 2, the Municipal Capability Act creates statutory protection for technical appointments, and the function-specific legislation embeds merit-based appointment requirements for senior positions in delivery entities. In Phase 3, constitutional entrenchment of the enforcement architecture protects the system from political erosion by future governments that might prefer discretionary enforcement.

Commercial capture is addressed through procurement transparency requirements embedded in both the regulatory changes (Phase 1) and the legislative reforms (Phase 2). The structured autonomy model gives delivery entities procurement authority — but within national framework contracts that reduce the scope for local procurement manipulation, and with mandatory publication of contract details, pricing, and supplier performance on accessible platforms. Ring-fenced revenue (Phase 1) reduces the scope for diversion of infrastructure maintenance funds to other purposes.

Labour capture is addressed primarily through the ELRC mandate restructuring in Phase 2, which separates national salary bargaining from operational employment matters. This reform gives delivery entities the authority to manage staff performance — including the authority to initiate and conclude disciplinary processes within prescribed timelines — without undermining the national employment framework. The Phase 1 regulatory changes (outcome-based conditional grants, performance dashboards) create the data infrastructure that makes labour productivity visible and measurable.

Regulatory capture is addressed through the structural separation of standard-setting from delivery in every function-specific reform. The national body that sets standards and monitors outcomes is institutionally separate from both the delivery entity and the political executive. The OHSC in health, the NHBRC in housing, the WSA function in water — each is strengthened and separated from the delivery function to prevent the regulated entity from influencing its own regulation.

Information capture is addressed from Phase 1 onwards through mandatory real-time data reporting to publicly accessible platforms. The Provincial Performance Dashboard, the Education Information System, the Health Information Platform, and the municipal operational dashboards collectively create an information architecture that prevents any single entity from controlling the narrative about its own performance. When outcome data is automatically collected and publicly visible, information capture becomes structurally impossible.

The sequenced programme is not merely a timeline for legislative action — it is a comprehensive strategy that builds political momentum through quick wins, demonstrates proof of concept through regulatory reform, uses evidence from early phases to inform legislative design, and protects the entire programme against the capture vectors that have undermined previous reform efforts.

The legal pathways are open. The regulatory authority exists. The legislative architecture is achievable. The constitutional framework is flexible enough to accommodate everything that has been proposed. What remains is the hardest part of governance reform — not the law, not the policy design, not the institutional architecture, but the sustained political will to implement a programme of change that serves citizens' interests above the interests of those who benefit from the current dysfunction. That political will does not emerge spontaneously. It is built — by citizens who understand what is broken, who know what the fix looks like, who can distinguish between genuine reform and rhetorical theatre, and who hold their elected representatives accountable for progress. The legal map in this article is your tool for that accountability. Use it.
