## Two Health Systems in One Country — One for the Rich, One for Everyone Else

South Africa spends approximately 8.5 percent of its gross domestic product on health — a figure that, in isolation, sounds reasonable and is indeed comparable to the OECD average. But that headline number conceals a structural divide that defines the country's health system more than any other single fact. Of that 8.5 percent, only approximately 4.1 percent flows through the public sector, which serves approximately eighty-four percent of the population. The remaining 4.4 percent flows through the private sector, which serves approximately sixteen percent of the population — those with medical scheme coverage. The arithmetic is stark: the public health system spends approximately R250 per capita per month to deliver healthcare to fifty million people. The private system spends approximately R1,100 per capita per month to deliver healthcare to ten million people. The result is two health systems in one country — one chronically underfunded and overstretched, the other well-resourced but accessible only to those who can afford medical scheme premiums.

This article is about the fiscal pipeline that determines how much money flows into the public health system — the system that serves the vast majority of South Africans and that is responsible for the life expectancy outcomes documented in the previous two articles. The pipeline has three stages: the economy generates GDP; the government collects revenue from that GDP; and the health sector receives its share of that revenue through the budget process. At each stage, there are constraints, trade-offs, and policy choices that determine the absolute rands available for public healthcare. And at the end of the pipeline, there is a fundamental question that South African health policy has been circling for two decades without resolution: can the country afford universal health coverage? The National Health Insurance (NHI) Bill, signed into law in 2024, promises to deliver it. The fiscal arithmetic presented in this article will allow you to judge for yourself whether that promise is fundable.

The argument is straightforward: economic growth is not a separate agenda from the health agenda. It is the health agenda's fiscal prerequisite. Without growth, the public health budget cannot expand in real terms. Without budget expansion, the health system cannot absorb a growing and ageing population, address the disease burdens documented in this series, and close the life expectancy deficit. Every political promise about improving healthcare — whether it comes from the governing party, the opposition, or civil society — must be evaluated against the fiscal arithmetic. If it does not come with a credible economic growth strategy, it is, however well-intentioned, a promise that cannot be kept.

## Health Spending in International Context

Before diving into South Africa's fiscal specifics, it is worth understanding how the country's health spending compares to its peers. International comparison provides the benchmark against which to assess whether South Africa spends too little, too much, or the wrong amount in the wrong places. When you hear politicians claim that the government is spending generously on health, these comparisons are what you need to hold them to account.

Total health expenditure as a percentage of GDP is the most commonly used comparison metric. South Africa's approximately 8.5 percent places it above the average for upper-middle-income countries (approximately 6.2 percent), above the average for sub-Saharan Africa (approximately 5.2 percent), and close to the OECD average (approximately 8.8 percent). By this measure alone, South Africa is not a low-spending country. It devotes a substantial share of national income to health.

But the composition of that spending is where South Africa becomes an outlier. In most countries at South Africa's income level, public health expenditure accounts for sixty to seventy percent of total health expenditure. In South Africa, public expenditure accounts for approximately forty-eight percent and private expenditure for approximately fifty-two percent. This private-heavy split is unusual for an upper-middle-income country and is characteristic of countries with highly unequal access to healthcare — the United States being the most prominent example among high-income countries, and South Africa being the most extreme example among middle-income countries.

Per capita public health spending provides an even starker comparison. South Africa's public sector spends approximately $250 per person per year (approximately R4,500) on healthcare. Brazil's public sector spends approximately $400 per person per year. Thailand — a country that has achieved universal health coverage at a per capita income lower than South Africa's — spends approximately $300 per person per year through its Universal Coverage Scheme. These comparisons are not exact (differences in purchasing power, disease burden, and health system structure all affect what a given amount of money can buy), but they establish a rough range: South Africa's public health spending per capita is in the lower range of what upper-middle-income countries typically spend through their public systems, while its private health spending per capita is in the upper range.

The efficiency of health spending — the health outcomes achieved per dollar spent — is the most important dimension of all, and the most difficult to measure precisely. But rough comparisons are instructive. South Africa spends more as a percentage of GDP on health than Thailand, yet achieves a life expectancy approximately fourteen years shorter. It spends more per capita (public plus private combined) than Vietnam, yet achieves a life expectancy approximately twelve years shorter. These comparisons suggest that South Africa's health spending problem is not solely about the quantum of money but about how that money is allocated (the public-private split), how efficiently it is used within the public system, and the non-health-system factors (HIV burden, violence burden, social determinants) that determine what any given level of health spending can achieve.

:::expert
The World Health Organization's health systems financing framework identifies three dimensions of progress toward universal health coverage: the proportion of the population covered (breadth), the range of services covered (depth), and the proportion of costs covered by prepaid pooled financing rather than out-of-pocket payments (height). South Africa's position on this framework is unusual. On breadth, the country has nominally universal coverage — the Constitution guarantees the right of access to healthcare, and the public system does not charge user fees for primary healthcare. On depth, the range of services theoretically available through the public system is comprehensive. But on height — the proportion of health costs covered by prepaid pooled financing — South Africa performs poorly because the public system is so underfunded relative to demand that many people effectively cannot access the services they are entitled to, and those who can afford it opt out into the private system. The result is a system that is universal in law but deeply unequal in practice. The NHI aims to address this by creating a single prepaid pool that funds healthcare for the entire population, but doing so requires either dramatically increasing the public health budget or dramatically reducing the cost of private-sector-equivalent care — or, more realistically, some combination of both. The fiscal feasibility of this ambition depends entirely on the economic growth trajectory that determines the size of the revenue pool available for public financing.
:::

## The Fiscal Arithmetic

To understand how much South Africa can spend on public healthcare, you need to follow the money through three stages: from the economy to the fiscus, from the fiscus to the health budget, and from the health budget to the facilities and programmes that deliver care. At each stage, constraints narrow the stream. Following this pipeline is how you move from abstract budget headlines to understanding why the clinic near your home is understaffed.

Stage one: from the economy to the fiscus. South Africa's GDP is approximately R7 trillion. The government collects revenue — primarily through personal income tax, value-added tax, corporate income tax, and excise duties — equal to approximately twenty-five to twenty-six percent of GDP, or approximately R1.8 trillion. This "tax-to-GDP ratio" is relatively high by upper-middle-income standards (the average for upper-middle-income countries is approximately twenty to twenty-two percent), reflecting the legacy of South Africa's relatively sophisticated tax administration and broad-based VAT system. The South African Revenue Service is generally regarded as one of the more effective tax authorities in the developing world, though its capacity was damaged during the "state capture" period and is still recovering.

But revenue collection is a function of GDP. If GDP does not grow, revenue does not grow — or, more precisely, revenue grows only to the extent that inflation pushes nominal GDP higher, which produces more rands but not more purchasing power. In real terms — adjusted for inflation — revenue growth tracks real GDP growth. And South Africa's real GDP growth has been anaemic: approximately 0.5 to 1.5 percent per year over the past decade, and below one percent in per capita terms (given population growth of approximately 1.2 percent per year). This means the fiscal base from which all government spending is funded — including health — has been essentially stagnant in real per capita terms.

Stage two: from the fiscus to the health budget. Of the approximately R1.8 trillion in revenue, the government must allocate spending across competing priorities: education (the largest single budget item, at approximately twenty percent of total spending), social protection (the second largest, driven by the social grants system), debt servicing (the fastest-growing item, now consuming approximately fifteen percent of revenue), defence, justice, infrastructure, and every other function of the state. Health typically receives approximately twelve to thirteen percent of the total consolidated government budget — a share that has been relatively stable over time but that, in a context of slow GDP growth and rising debt service costs, translates to real-terms stagnation or decline in per capita spending.

The debt servicing squeeze deserves particular attention. South Africa's debt-to-GDP ratio has risen from approximately thirty percent in 2008 to approximately seventy-three percent, driven by persistent fiscal deficits — the government spends more than it collects in revenue, and the difference is borrowed. The interest payments on this accumulated debt now consume approximately R350 billion per year — more than the entire health budget. Every rand spent on debt servicing is a rand unavailable for health, education, or any other productive expenditure. And as the debt burden grows, the debt service cost grows with it, creating a vicious fiscal cycle: higher debt leads to higher interest payments, which leads to larger fiscal deficits (if spending on other items is maintained), which leads to even higher debt.

Stage three: from the health budget to service delivery. The consolidated health budget — national plus provincial — is approximately R260 billion per year. This sounds substantial, and it is in absolute terms. But it must fund the entire public health system: over four thousand primary healthcare clinics, approximately four hundred hospitals (including tertiary academic hospitals that deliver the most complex and expensive care), the salaries of approximately 350,000 health workers, the procurement of all medicines and medical supplies, the maintenance and construction of health infrastructure, and the administrative overhead of a system that operates across nine provinces and fifty-two health districts.

The salary bill alone — the compensation of health workers — accounts for approximately sixty to sixty-five percent of the total health budget. This is not unusual by international standards (health is a labour-intensive sector), but it means that the "non-personnel" budget — the money available for medicines, equipment, infrastructure maintenance, and new programmes — is approximately R90 billion to R105 billion. And within that non-personnel budget, essential medicine procurement, including the massive antiretroviral and TB treatment programmes, consumes a large share. The fiscal space for new investments, system improvements, and expanded coverage is therefore extremely narrow.

:::takeaway
South Africa collects approximately R1.8 trillion in revenue, of which approximately R260 billion goes to the public health system. Debt servicing consumes more than the entire health budget. Health worker salaries consume sixty to sixty-five percent of the health budget, leaving approximately R90 billion to R105 billion for medicines, equipment, infrastructure, and everything else. In a context of stagnant real GDP growth and rising debt service costs, there is no pathway to expanding public health spending in real per capita terms without either economic growth or reallocation from other budget items — both of which involve difficult political choices.
:::

## The NHI Question

The National Health Insurance Bill, signed into law by President Ramaphosa in May 2024, represents the most ambitious health financing reform in South Africa's democratic history. Its stated objective is to achieve universal health coverage by creating a single publicly financed health system that covers the entire population — eliminating the two-tier system in which quality of care depends on the ability to pay medical scheme premiums.

The ambition is laudable. The question is whether it is affordable. If you are currently on a medical scheme, NHI determines what happens to your contributions and your access. If you depend on the public system, NHI determines whether the promise of equal care will ever be funded. Either way, this is a question that will directly affect you.

The NHI, as envisaged in the legislation and supporting policy documents, would be funded through a combination of sources: reallocation of existing public health spending, reallocation of existing medical scheme tax credits (currently worth approximately R30 billion per year), and additional revenue raised through a combination of a surcharge on personal income tax, a payroll tax on employers, and potentially an increase in the VAT rate. The total estimated cost of NHI at full implementation has been variously estimated at between R256 billion and R450 billion per year, depending on the scope of services covered, the efficiency assumptions used, and the timeline for implementation. The wide range of estimates reflects genuine uncertainty about what NHI would actually cost — uncertainty that has not been resolved by the actuarial modelling conducted to date.

Let us work through the arithmetic using the more conservative estimates. The current consolidated public health budget is approximately R260 billion. The medical scheme tax credit reallocation could contribute approximately R30 billion. That leaves a funding gap of approximately R0 to R160 billion that must be raised through new taxes, efficiency gains, or economic growth — or some combination of all three.

New taxes face significant constraints. South Africa's tax-to-GDP ratio of approximately twenty-five to twenty-six percent is already high by upper-middle-income standards, and the tax base is narrow — approximately seven million individuals pay income tax, out of a labour force of twenty-three million and a population of sixty-two million. Increasing the tax burden on this small base risks dampening economic activity, encouraging tax avoidance, and driving skilled professionals (including health professionals) to emigrate. The Treasury has repeatedly warned that there is limited scope for raising additional revenue through tax increases without harming economic growth.

Efficiency gains within the health system could contribute to closing the gap. There is considerable evidence that the public health system does not use its current resources optimally: drug procurement inefficiencies, inappropriate hospital admissions, poorly managed referral systems, and administrative waste all consume resources that could be better deployed. But even the most optimistic efficiency estimates suggest savings of perhaps ten to fifteen percent of the current budget — meaningful, but not sufficient to close a gap of R100 billion or more.

That leaves economic growth. And here the arithmetic becomes dispositive. If South Africa's GDP grew at four percent per year in real terms — a rate that was achieved briefly in the mid-2000s but that has not been sustained — tax revenue would grow at approximately the same rate (with some additional growth from "bracket creep" and improved compliance). A four percent real GDP growth rate would add approximately R70 billion to R80 billion in additional annual revenue within five years, some of which could be allocated to health. Combined with efficiency gains and the medical scheme tax credit reallocation, this growth scenario could plausibly fund a phased NHI implementation over a decade or more.

But if GDP grows at one percent per year — the recent historical trend — the additional revenue generated is approximately R18 billion to R20 billion per year, much of which would be absorbed by existing spending pressures (the growing social grant bill, debt servicing, infrastructure maintenance). In this scenario, there is no fiscal space for NHI at anything approaching its envisaged scope. The choice would be between implementing a nominal NHI that covers a narrow range of services at low quality (essentially rebranding the existing public health system), or raising taxes to levels that would almost certainly damage economic growth and trigger professional emigration.

:::expert
The international evidence on the relationship between economic growth and universal health coverage (UHC) is illuminating. Virtually every country that has achieved UHC has done so during a period of sustained economic growth that generated the fiscal space to expand public health coverage. Thailand introduced its Universal Coverage Scheme in 2001, after a decade of approximately seven to eight percent annual GDP growth that had expanded government revenue and built institutional capacity. South Korea achieved universal health insurance coverage by 1989, during the country's sustained high-growth period. Rwanda's Mutuelle de Sante — a community-based health insurance scheme that has achieved over ninety percent coverage — was implemented during a period of approximately eight percent annual GDP growth following the genocide. Even the United Kingdom's National Health Service was established in 1948 during the post-war economic expansion. The common thread is not ideological — these countries span the political spectrum from market-oriented (South Korea) to social democratic (Nordic countries) to developmental state (Rwanda) — but fiscal. They had the revenue to fund expanded coverage because their economies were growing. Countries that have attempted to legislate universal coverage without the fiscal base to fund it — including several Latin American countries and some African countries with constitutionally mandated health rights — have produced systems that are universal on paper and deeply unequal in practice. South Africa's Constitutional right to access healthcare, established in 1996, already demonstrates this pattern: the right exists, but the resources to realise it fully do not. NHI attempts to close this gap through legislation, but legislation cannot create fiscal space. Only economic growth can do that.
:::

## The Growth-to-Health Pipeline

The previous section established that NHI's feasibility depends on economic growth. This section broadens the argument: the entire trajectory of South Africa's public health system — with or without NHI — depends on economic growth. This pipeline matters to you because it explains why voting for economic growth policies is, in a very direct sense, a vote for better healthcare. The pipeline from GDP growth to health outcomes has several intermediate steps, each of which is worth understanding in its own right.

Step one: GDP growth generates government revenue. This is the most direct mechanism. When the economy grows, businesses earn more, workers earn more, and consumers spend more. Corporate income tax, personal income tax, and VAT collections all increase. The relationship between GDP growth and revenue growth is typically slightly greater than one-to-one — a phenomenon known as "tax buoyancy," which occurs because as incomes rise, a larger share falls into higher tax brackets (in a progressive tax system like South Africa's), and as consumption rises, a larger share of spending falls on VAT-liable goods and services. South Africa's tax buoyancy has historically been approximately 1.1 to 1.3 — meaning that for every one percent of GDP growth, revenue grows by approximately 1.1 to 1.3 percent.

Step two: increased revenue creates fiscal space for health spending. Not all additional revenue will flow to health — other spending priorities will compete for their share — but in a growing economy, health spending can increase in real terms without requiring a larger share of the budget. This is politically much easier than the alternative (increasing health's budget share by cutting other departments), which explains why health spending tends to expand during growth periods and stagnate during fiscal austerity.

Step three: increased health spending, if deployed effectively, improves health outcomes. This step is not automatic. More money does not automatically produce better health. The relationship between health spending and health outcomes is mediated by the efficiency and equity of the health system — how the money is spent, where it is allocated, and whether it reaches the populations and conditions where it can have the greatest impact. The District Health Barometer data documented in the previous article show that some South African health districts achieve significantly better outcomes than others with similar per capita funding levels, which demonstrates that management capacity and implementation quality matter as much as the absolute quantum of funding.

Step four: improved health outcomes contribute to economic growth. This is where the pipeline becomes circular — or, more precisely, where it can become a virtuous cycle rather than a vicious one. Healthier populations are more productive. Workers who are not debilitated by HIV, TB, or untreated chronic diseases work more hours, more productively. Children who are not stunted by malnutrition or impaired by untreated childhood diseases learn more effectively and accumulate more human capital. Women who have access to family planning and reproductive healthcare can participate more fully in the labour force. The economic return on health investment — the GDP growth attributable to improved population health — has been estimated at substantial multiples of the initial investment. The Lancet Commission on Investing in Health estimated that health investments in low- and middle-income countries yield economic returns of nine to twenty times the initial investment through improved productivity, reduced mortality, and the "demographic dividend" that occurs when declining fertility rates (associated with improved health and family planning access) produce a bulge in the working-age population.

The virtuous cycle, in summary, works like this: economic growth generates revenue, revenue funds health investment, health investment improves population health, improved health generates economic growth, and the cycle repeats. Each turn of the cycle lifts the HDI — through both the health dimension (directly) and the income dimension (through the growth effects of better health).

The vicious alternative — which more closely describes South Africa's recent trajectory — works in reverse: economic stagnation constrains revenue, constrained revenue limits health spending, limited health spending fails to address disease burdens, unaddressed disease burdens reduce productivity and growth, and reduced growth further constrains revenue. Breaking out of this vicious cycle requires either an exogenous boost to economic growth (structural reforms, favourable global conditions, investment-friendly policy changes) or a dramatic improvement in the efficiency of existing health spending — or, ideally, both.

:::takeaway
The pipeline from economic growth to health outcomes operates through four steps: growth generates revenue, revenue creates fiscal space, fiscal space enables health investment, and health investment both improves outcomes directly and contributes to further economic growth. South Africa is currently trapped in the vicious version of this cycle: stagnation constrains revenue, which constrains health spending, which fails to address disease burdens, which reduces productivity and growth. Breaking this cycle requires either faster economic growth, dramatically more efficient use of existing health resources, or both.
:::

## The Stagnation Trap

South Africa's recent economic performance has placed the health system in what can only be described as a fiscal stagnation trap. The arithmetic is relentless. If you have noticed that your local clinic seems no better — perhaps worse — than it was five years ago despite all the budget announcements, this is why: in real per-capita terms, the system has been going backwards.

Real GDP growth has averaged approximately one percent per year over the past decade. Population growth has averaged approximately 1.2 percent per year. This means that GDP per capita — the amount of economic output per person — has been declining in real terms. The pie has been growing more slowly than the number of people who need a slice.

Government revenue, while growing in nominal terms (driven by inflation), has been approximately flat in real per capita terms. The additional nominal rands collected each year are largely absorbed by inflationary increases in the cost of existing services rather than funding expanded services. Health worker salaries are adjusted for inflation (and often above inflation, as a result of collective bargaining agreements), medicine prices increase, infrastructure maintenance costs rise, and the result is that the health budget buys approximately the same quantum of services year after year, even as the population grows and the disease burden evolves.

Meanwhile, the competing claims on revenue have intensified. Debt service costs have tripled in real terms over the past decade as the government has borrowed to fund persistent fiscal deficits. The social grants system — which now reaches approximately eighteen million beneficiaries, up from approximately twelve million a decade ago — has expanded both in coverage and in the real value of individual grants. Infrastructure spending, while it has been cut significantly from its peak in the early 2010s, still absorbs a significant budget share. Education spending, which has been relatively protected as a share of the budget, competes for the same pool of resources.

The result is that health spending as a percentage of GDP and as a share of the budget has been approximately stable, but spending per capita in real terms has been declining. The number of people dependent on the public health system has grown (driven by population growth and by economic deterioration that has reduced the number of people who can afford medical scheme coverage), while the resources available per person have shrunk. This is the fiscal mechanism behind the overcrowded clinics, the stockouts, the staff shortages, and the long waiting times that characterise the public health system's daily reality.

The stagnation trap has a compounding quality. As per capita health spending declines, health outcomes stagnate or worsen. As health outcomes worsen, workforce productivity declines (sick workers produce less), economic growth suffers (unhealthy populations grow more slowly), and revenue growth slows further. The fiscal constraint tightens, and the cycle continues. This is not a hypothetical scenario. It is the current reality, and it has been the reality for the better part of a decade.

The COVID-19 pandemic exacerbated the trap. The 2020 GDP contraction of approximately 6.3 percent produced the sharpest revenue decline in democratic South Africa's history. Emergency spending on the pandemic response, social relief of distress grants, and vaccine procurement was funded largely through borrowing, pushing the debt-to-GDP ratio above seventy percent. The fiscal recovery since 2021 has been incomplete — revenue has recovered, but the accumulated debt and the higher debt servicing costs have permanently reduced the fiscal space available for health and other priorities.

:::expert
The concept of "fiscal space for health" — defined by the World Health Organization as "the capacity of government to provide additional budgetary resources for health without prejudicing the sustainability of its financial position" — has been analysed for South Africa by multiple researchers, including teams at the WHO, the World Bank, and South African academic institutions. Their analyses consistently identify the same constraints: limited scope for increasing the tax-to-GDP ratio (already high by peer-country standards), limited scope for reallocating budget from other sectors (education and social grants are politically protected, and debt service is a non-discretionary obligation), limited scope for reducing health system inefficiency in the short term (efficiency gains are real but take years to materialise), and limited external funding (South Africa's upper-middle-income status makes it ineligible for most concessional health financing, and donor funding for HIV/TB — primarily through PEPFAR and the Global Fund — is declining as South Africa is expected to transition to domestic financing). The conclusion across these analyses is remarkably consistent: the only sustainable pathway to substantially increased public health spending is economic growth. A sustained growth rate of three percent or above would, over a decade, generate sufficient additional revenue to fund meaningful expansion of health coverage without requiring tax increases or cuts to other essential services. A growth rate of one percent or below — the recent trajectory — makes even the maintenance of current per-capita spending levels difficult.
:::

## District-Level Resource Allocation

The fiscal arithmetic does not only determine how much money enters the health system. It also determines how that money is distributed — and the distribution mechanism is itself a significant driver of the health outcome inequalities documented throughout this series. How much your district receives per person is something you can look up in the District Health Barometer, and comparing it against the national average is one of the most direct ways to assess whether your community is being treated equitably.

Health funding in South Africa flows from the National Treasury to provincial governments through the "equitable share" formula — a constitutionally mandated mechanism that distributes nationally collected revenue among the three spheres of government. The provincial equitable share — the share allocated to provinces — is determined by a formula that takes into account each province's share of the national population, its economic activity, its poverty levels, and other factors. Health is not earmarked within the equitable share; provinces receive a lump sum and allocate it across their various functions (health, education, roads, agriculture, and others) through their own budget processes.

This means that the amount allocated to health varies by province, reflecting both the equitable share allocation and the provincial government's own prioritisation decisions. The Western Cape and Gauteng — the two wealthiest provinces, with the strongest tax bases and the most developed economic infrastructure — have historically allocated a relatively generous share of their budgets to health. The Eastern Cape, Limpopo, and Mpumalanga — poorer provinces with larger rural populations and weaker administrative capacity — have often allocated less per capita, despite having arguably greater health needs.

Within provinces, health budgets are allocated to districts and hospitals through mechanisms that vary in their transparency and rationality. Some provinces use needs-based allocation formulas that attempt to distribute resources according to population size, disease burden, and poverty levels. Others allocate resources on a largely historical basis — what was allocated last year, plus or minus a few percent — regardless of changes in need or performance. The result is significant variation in per capita health spending across districts. The best-funded districts may receive R2,500 or more per capita per year, while the worst-funded receive R800 or less.

This variation in resource allocation does not explain all of the variation in health outcomes — management quality, staff competence, and community factors also matter — but it is a significant contributor. A district clinic operating on R800 per capita cannot hire enough staff, maintain its infrastructure, and stock all essential medicines. The fiscal constraint at district level is not abstract: it translates directly into empty shelves in the medicine room, unmaintained buildings, unfilled posts, and patients who are turned away or receive inadequate care.

The conditional grants — earmarked funding from the national government to provinces for specific health programmes — partially offset the equitable share inequalities. The HIV/AIDS conditional grant (the largest, at approximately R28 billion per year), the Health Facility Revitalisation Grant, the National Health Insurance indirect grant, and others provide ring-fenced funding for priority programmes. But conditional grants cannot fully compensate for equitable share inadequacy, and their effectiveness depends on provincial capacity to spend them appropriately — a capacity that varies as widely as the health outcomes themselves.

## Health Worker Crisis

No discussion of the fiscal arithmetic of health is complete without confronting the health workforce challenge — arguably the most critical constraint on the health system's ability to translate funding into outcomes. If you have ever waited hours at a public clinic to see an overworked nurse managing a queue that would defeat anyone, you have experienced the vacancy rate in human terms.

South Africa has approximately 350,000 health workers in the public sector, including approximately 22,000 medical practitioners (doctors), approximately 140,000 nurses of various categories, and the remainder comprising allied health professionals, pharmacists, community health workers, and support staff. These numbers are inadequate for the population served. The doctor-to-population ratio in the public sector is approximately 0.3 per thousand (compared to approximately 2.7 per thousand in the private sector). The nurse-to-population ratio, while better, is still below WHO recommendations for the level of primary healthcare delivery South Africa's disease burden demands.

The vacancy rate — the gap between funded positions and filled positions — is a persistent and pernicious problem. Nationally, vacancy rates for professional nurses (the backbone of the primary healthcare system) average approximately twenty to twenty-five percent, with some provinces and some facilities experiencing vacancy rates of forty percent or more. For medical officers and specialists, vacancy rates in rural and underserved areas are even higher. Some rural district hospitals operate without a single specialist in key disciplines — no obstetrician, no paediatrician, no surgeon — relying on generalist medical officers to manage conditions that require specialist expertise.

The health worker distribution problem is fiscal at its core. Public sector salaries, while they have improved significantly since 2007 through the Occupation Specific Dispensation agreement, remain uncompetitive with the private sector for most categories of health professional. A specialist physician in the public sector earns approximately R1.2 million to R1.8 million per year; the same specialist in private practice can earn R3 million to R5 million or more. The income differential is a powerful pull factor toward the private sector and toward emigration. South Africa is a net exporter of health professionals — doctors, nurses, and specialists emigrate to the United Kingdom, Australia, Canada, the Middle East, and other destinations where salaries are higher, working conditions are better, and personal safety is greater.

The fiscal arithmetic of health workers is straightforward but constraining. The salary bill already consumes approximately sixty to sixty-five percent of the health budget. Hiring additional health workers — filling existing vacancies or creating new posts — requires either increasing the total health budget (which, as we have seen, requires economic growth) or reducing spending on other items (medicines, infrastructure, equipment). Increasing public sector salaries to competitive levels — to attract and retain health professionals who would otherwise enter the private sector or emigrate — would further increase the salary bill share, squeezing the non-personnel budget even more.

The health worker production pipeline adds a further constraint. South Africa produces approximately 1,500 to 2,000 new medical doctors per year from its medical schools. A significant proportion — estimates range from thirty to fifty percent — emigrate within ten years of graduation. The net addition to the domestic medical workforce is therefore far below what the training output suggests. Nursing training output has been more robust, but the shift from hospital-based nursing colleges to university-based training (following the implementation of the new nursing qualifications framework) temporarily reduced output and has not yet fully recovered.

The only sustainable solution to the health worker crisis is a combination of improved retention (requiring competitive compensation, better working conditions, and career development opportunities — all of which require additional funding), increased production (requiring investment in training institutions and student support), and improved productivity (requiring better management, appropriate task-shifting, and technology-enabled efficiency gains). All three pathways require fiscal resources that are, under current growth projections, not available.

:::takeaway
South Africa's public health workforce is inadequate for its disease burden, with vacancy rates of twenty to forty percent for critical cadres and a doctor-to-population ratio of 0.3 per thousand in the public sector. The salary bill already consumes sixty to sixty-five percent of the health budget, leaving minimal fiscal space for additional hiring or competitive salary increases. Emigration of health professionals — driven by the income differential between public and private sectors and between South Africa and destination countries — drains the training pipeline. Economic growth is the prerequisite for the competitive compensation, expanded training, and improved working conditions that would address the health worker crisis.
:::

## The Virtuous Alternative: Countries That Grew Into Universal Coverage

If the picture painted so far is bleak, it is intentionally so — because confronting the fiscal reality is the prerequisite for identifying a viable pathway forward. The countries below show you what is possible — not as inspiration, but as evidence that the pathway from stagnation to universal coverage has been walked before, and you can assess whether South Africa is on it. And viable pathways do exist. Other countries, facing constraints not entirely dissimilar to South Africa's, have achieved universal or near-universal health coverage by growing their way into it. Their experiences do not provide blueprints — every country's context is unique — but they provide proof of concept and instructive lessons.

Thailand's Universal Coverage Scheme (UCS), introduced in 2001 under Prime Minister Thaksin Shinawatra, is perhaps the most relevant comparator. At the time of introduction, Thailand's GDP per capita was approximately $2,000 — significantly lower than South Africa's today. But Thailand had achieved sustained economic growth of approximately seven to eight percent per year through the 1980s and 1990s (interrupted by the 1997 Asian financial crisis but recovering quickly), which had generated the fiscal space to expand public health coverage progressively. The UCS covers the approximately seventy-five percent of the Thai population not covered by the Civil Servant Medical Benefit Scheme or the Social Security Scheme. It is funded through general taxation and provides a comprehensive package of care — including primary care, hospitalisation, surgery, medicines, and prevention — at a per capita cost of approximately $140 per year. Thailand achieves a life expectancy of approximately seventy-seven years — fourteen years longer than South Africa — at a lower per capita health expenditure.

The Thai experience illustrates several principles relevant to South Africa. First, universal coverage was introduced during a period of sustained growth, not stagnation. The fiscal foundation was built over two decades of economic expansion before the political decision to universalise coverage was made. Second, the UCS was designed to be fiscally sustainable at Thailand's income level — it was not an aspirational promise contingent on future funding but a programme costed and funded from existing revenue. Third, Thailand invested heavily in the primary healthcare system in the decades preceding UCS — building a network of district hospitals and health centres, training a cadre of primary care professionals, and establishing a functional referral system — so that when universal coverage was announced, the delivery infrastructure was already in place.

Rwanda's Mutuelle de Sante provides a different model with different lessons. Rwanda, one of the poorest countries in the world, achieved health insurance coverage of over ninety percent through a community-based health insurance scheme financed by a combination of member premiums (scaled to income), government subsidies, and donor support. The scheme was implemented during a period of approximately eight percent annual GDP growth following the 1994 genocide — growth driven by post-conflict reconstruction, governance reforms, and strategic investments in human capital. Rwanda's per capita health expenditure is far below South Africa's, yet it has achieved health outcomes — including a life expectancy of approximately sixty-nine years, six years longer than South Africa's — that demonstrate the power of universal coverage combined with community health worker programmes, performance-based financing, and strong accountability mechanisms.

South Korea's path to universal health insurance, achieved by 1989, followed yet another trajectory. South Korea expanded coverage progressively over two decades — from large firms (1977) to smaller firms (1979) to the self-employed (1988) — during a period of sustained economic growth averaging approximately eight to ten percent per year. The growth generated the tax revenue to fund progressively expanding coverage, and the expansion of coverage itself contributed to improved health and productivity, reinforcing the growth.

The common denominator across these cases is not the specific policy design — Thailand, Rwanda, and South Korea used very different models of health financing — but the presence of sustained economic growth that generated the fiscal space to fund expanded coverage. No country has achieved universal health coverage during a period of prolonged economic stagnation. The lesson for South Africa is not that NHI is the wrong aspiration but that NHI without economic growth is an unfundable aspiration — and that the health policy agenda and the economic growth agenda are, in this fundamental sense, the same agenda.

:::expert
The sequencing question — whether universal health coverage drives economic growth (by improving human capital and productivity) or economic growth enables universal health coverage (by generating the fiscal space to fund it) — has been extensively debated in the health economics literature. The honest answer is: both. The evidence supports a virtuous cycle in which health investment and economic growth reinforce each other. But the evidence also suggests that the growth impulse typically comes first. Countries that have achieved UHC without first achieving growth have generally been heavily donor-dependent (which is not a sustainable model for a country of South Africa's size and middle-income status), or have achieved nominal but not effective coverage (laws on paper without services in practice). For South Africa, the implication is that the economic growth reforms described in the economic growth series of this publication — improving the business environment, fixing energy supply, building infrastructure, developing human capital — are not separate from the health agenda. They are the fiscal prerequisite for the health agenda. This does not mean that health investment should wait for growth — the case for immediate, efficiency-improving health investments is strong, and the health-to-growth feedback loop means that well-targeted health spending generates its own returns. But it does mean that anyone promising universal health coverage without a credible growth strategy is making a promise that the arithmetic cannot keep.
:::

## How Other Countries Finance Healthcare: Eight Case Studies

The countries examined above — Thailand, Rwanda, and South Korea — illustrate the growth-to-coverage pathway in broad strokes. But South Africa's health financing debate benefits from a wider lens. The eight countries profiled below represent the full spectrum of healthcare financing models: social health insurance, single-payer taxation, hybrid systems, and medical savings accounts. Each offers specific lessons — and specific warnings — for a middle-income country attempting to achieve universal coverage.

### Germany: The Bismarck Model and Social Health Insurance

Germany operates the oldest universal healthcare system in the world, dating to Chancellor Otto von Bismarck's Health Insurance Bill of 1883. The system is built on statutory health insurance (Gesetzliche Krankenversicherung), funded primarily through payroll contributions split equally between employers and employees at a combined rate of approximately 14.6 percent of gross wages, plus an income-dependent supplementary contribution averaging 1.7 percent. Approximately eighty-seven percent of the population is covered by statutory insurance, with the remaining thirteen percent — primarily higher earners, civil servants, and the self-employed — covered by private health insurance.

Germany spends approximately 12.7 percent of GDP on health — one of the highest rates in the OECD — with public sources (statutory insurance plus government transfers) accounting for approximately seventy-eight percent of total health expenditure. Per capita health spending is approximately $7,400 per year. Life expectancy is approximately eighty-one years.

The relevance for South Africa lies in the contribution-based financing model. Germany's system demonstrates that payroll-based contributions can fund universal coverage — but only in an economy with high formal employment, high wages, and effective contribution collection. Germany's formal employment rate exceeds seventy-five percent of the working-age population. South Africa's formal employment rate is approximately forty-three percent, with an unemployment rate above thirty percent. A payroll-based contribution model — one of the financing mechanisms proposed for NHI — would therefore capture a far smaller share of the population and generate far less revenue per capita than the German model. The lesson is not that payroll contributions are wrong for South Africa, but that they cannot bear the same financing weight in an economy with South Africa's employment structure.

### Canada: Single-Payer Through Provincial Administration

Canada's Medicare system, established through the Medical Care Act of 1966 and consolidated under the Canada Health Act of 1984, provides universal coverage for medically necessary hospital and physician services. The system is publicly funded through general taxation — primarily federal and provincial income taxes and sales taxes — and publicly administered, but care is delivered primarily by private providers (physicians are mostly self-employed, and hospitals are largely non-profit). There are no user fees for insured services.

Canada spends approximately 12.2 percent of GDP on health, with public sources accounting for approximately seventy percent of total health expenditure. Per capita health spending is approximately $5,900 per year. Life expectancy is approximately eighty-two years.

Two features of the Canadian model are particularly instructive for South Africa. First, Canada achieves universality through a single-payer model funded by general taxation — similar in principle to the NHI's proposed design — but the system was built incrementally over two decades, beginning with hospital insurance in Saskatchewan in 1947, expanding to physician services in 1962, and achieving national coverage only in 1966. The incremental approach allowed the system to be tested, refined, and politically consolidated before national rollout. Second, the Canadian system is administered provincially — each of the thirteen provinces and territories operates its own health insurance plan within federal standards — which provides a structural parallel to South Africa's provincial health system. Canada's experience shows that provincial administration can work, but also that it produces significant inter-provincial variation in access, waiting times, and quality — a challenge South Africa already experiences.

### Malaysia: Dual-Track Financing at Middle-Income Level

Malaysia is perhaps the most directly relevant comparator for South Africa because it is a middle-income country (GDP per capita approximately $12,500) that provides near-universal healthcare access through a dual public-private system remarkably similar in structure to South Africa's — but with vastly better outcomes. Malaysia spends approximately 4.1 percent of GDP on health — roughly half of South Africa's spending rate — yet achieves a life expectancy of approximately seventy-six years, thirteen years longer than South Africa's.

The Malaysian public health system is funded through general taxation and provides highly subsidised care at government hospitals and clinics. User fees are nominal — one ringgit (approximately R4) for an outpatient visit at a public clinic. The private sector serves approximately forty percent of outpatient visits and thirty percent of hospital admissions, funded through employer-provided insurance, out-of-pocket payments, and private health insurance. Malaysia has approximately seventy percent public and thirty percent private health expenditure — a split far more balanced than South Africa's near-equal divide.

The Malaysian lesson for South Africa is about efficiency and primary care investment. Malaysia invested heavily in a network of rural clinics and district hospitals from the 1960s onwards, building a primary healthcare backbone that handles the vast majority of the disease burden at low cost. The system is not without problems — waiting times in public hospitals are long, and there is a growing gap between public and private quality — but it demonstrates that a middle-income country can achieve high life expectancy at modest health spending through strong primary healthcare infrastructure and efficient resource allocation. South Africa spends more than double Malaysia's rate as a percentage of GDP and achieves fourteen fewer years of life expectancy — a comparison that underscores how much of South Africa's health crisis is about allocation and efficiency, not just funding.

### Rwanda: Community-Based Insurance in a Low-Income Setting

Rwanda's Mutuelle de Sant&eacute; (community-based health insurance) has been discussed earlier in this article, but its financing model deserves closer examination because it represents the most successful example of near-universal coverage achieved at an extremely low income level. Rwanda's GDP per capita is approximately $820 — less than a tenth of South Africa's — yet it has achieved health insurance coverage of over ninety percent and a life expectancy of approximately sixty-nine years, six years longer than South Africa's.

The financing model has three tiers. The poorest twenty-five percent of the population (identified through the Ubudehe community wealth ranking system) have their premiums fully subsidised by the government and development partners. The next forty percent pay a subsidised annual premium of approximately 3,000 Rwandan francs (approximately R55) per person. The remaining thirty-five percent pay the full premium of approximately 7,000 Rwandan francs (approximately R130) per person. Co-payments of ten percent apply at the point of care, with exemptions for the poorest category.

Rwanda spends approximately 7.2 percent of GDP on health, with government expenditure accounting for approximately forty-five percent, donor funding approximately forty percent, and private expenditure approximately fifteen percent. The heavy donor dependence is the model's principal vulnerability — as Rwanda transitions to middle-income status, donor funding will decline, and the government will need to replace it with domestic revenue. But the system's design principles — community-based enrolment, income-graduated premiums, performance-based provider payment, and strong community health worker networks — are applicable regardless of the funding source.

For South Africa, Rwanda's experience demonstrates three things. First, political will and implementation discipline can achieve universal coverage even at very low income levels — South Africa's failure to do so is not primarily a resource constraint but an implementation and governance constraint. Second, community health workers — Rwanda deploys over 58,000 — can dramatically extend the reach of the health system at low cost, a lesson directly relevant to South Africa's ward-based outreach team strategy. Third, performance-based financing — paying facilities based on the quantity and quality of services delivered rather than on inputs — can improve efficiency and accountability, addressing the governance failures documented throughout this series.

### Singapore: Medical Savings and Individual Responsibility

Singapore's healthcare financing model is unique globally and represents the polar opposite of the single-payer approach proposed by NHI. The system is built on three pillars. Medisave is a compulsory medical savings account into which employees and employers contribute eight to ten percent of wages — the funds belong to the individual and can be used to pay for hospitalisation and selected outpatient treatments. MediShield Life is a compulsory catastrophic health insurance scheme that covers large hospital bills and expensive treatments, funded by premiums deducted from Medisave accounts. Medifund is a government-funded safety net for patients who cannot afford their medical bills even after Medisave and MediShield Life.

Singapore spends approximately 4.1 percent of GDP on health — one of the lowest rates among high-income countries — yet achieves the highest life expectancy in the world at approximately eighty-four years. Government expenditure accounts for approximately fifty percent of total health expenditure, with the remainder funded through Medisave, insurance premiums, and out-of-pocket payments.

The Singaporean model's relevance for South Africa is limited but instructive. The model works in Singapore because of several preconditions that South Africa does not share: near-universal formal employment (enabling payroll-based Medisave contributions), very high wages (enabling meaningful savings accumulation), a small and compact geography (reducing healthcare delivery costs), exceptionally competent public administration, and a cultural context that accepts individual financial responsibility for healthcare. None of these conditions obtains in South Africa. However, Singapore's emphasis on cost control — the government regulates hospital fees, publishes price information, and uses means-tested subsidies rather than universal free care — offers lessons for South Africa's overpriced private sector. The Competition Commission's Health Market Inquiry recommended many of the same cost-control mechanisms that Singapore employs, and implementing them could help reduce the cost of extending private-sector-quality care to a broader population.

### Taiwan: Single-Payer Designed From Scratch

Taiwan's National Health Insurance (NHI), launched in 1995, is often cited as the most successful example of a single-payer system designed and implemented from scratch — as opposed to systems that evolved incrementally over decades. Before 1995, approximately forty-one percent of Taiwan's population lacked health insurance. Within one year of NHI's launch, coverage reached ninety-two percent. Today, coverage is approximately ninety-nine percent.

The system is funded through payroll-based premiums (shared between employees, employers, and government at varying ratios depending on employment category) supplemented by a two percent supplementary premium on non-wage income (bonuses, rental income, dividends). Total health expenditure is approximately 6.6 percent of GDP, with public sources accounting for approximately sixty-four percent. Per capita health spending is approximately $1,400 per year. Life expectancy is approximately eighty-one years.

Taiwan's experience is relevant to South Africa for several reasons. First, Taiwan demonstrated that a country can move from fragmented, unequal coverage to universal single-payer coverage rapidly — in contrast to the multi-decade incremental approaches of Germany, Canada, and South Korea. Second, Taiwan's NHI was explicitly designed with cost control as a central feature: a global budget system caps total expenditure, fee schedules regulate provider payments, and a smart card system (later digitised) tracks utilisation in real time. Third, Taiwan implemented its NHI at a GDP per capita of approximately $13,000 — comparable to South Africa's current level — demonstrating that single-payer universal coverage is fiscally achievable at middle-income levels. However, Taiwan's implementation was supported by several advantages South Africa lacks: a prior decade of approximately eight percent annual GDP growth, near-universal formal employment, high government administrative capacity, and a relatively homogeneous disease burden without South Africa's HIV/TB epidemic.

### France: The Hybrid Model of Solidarity

France's healthcare system, consistently ranked among the best in the world by the World Health Organization, operates a hybrid model combining statutory health insurance with supplementary private insurance. The statutory system (Assurance Maladie) covers the entire population and is funded primarily through employer and employee payroll contributions, a dedicated income tax (the Contribution Sociale G&eacute;n&eacute;ralis&eacute;e, or CSG, levied at 9.2 percent on employment income and at lower rates on other income), and earmarked taxes on tobacco, alcohol, and pharmaceutical companies.

France spends approximately 12.1 percent of GDP on health, with public sources accounting for approximately eighty-three percent of total health expenditure — one of the highest public shares in the OECD. Per capita health spending is approximately $5,500 per year. Life expectancy is approximately eighty-three years.

Two aspects of the French model are particularly instructive. First, the CSG — a broad-based health tax levied on all forms of income, not just wages — demonstrates an alternative to the narrow payroll-based contributions that would be problematic in South Africa's high-unemployment economy. A broad-based health levy that captures income from capital, property, and the informal economy could generate more revenue than a payroll tax alone. Second, France's system of complementary private insurance (mutuelle) — held by approximately ninety-five percent of the population and often provided by employers — covers the co-payments and services not fully reimbursed by statutory insurance. This layered approach allows the public system to control costs by not covering one hundred percent of every expense while ensuring that financial barriers to access are minimised. A similar layered model — in which NHI covers a defined package of essential services and voluntary private insurance covers additional services and amenities — may be more fiscally realistic for South Africa than the comprehensive single-fund model currently legislated.

### United Kingdom: The Beveridge Model and Tax-Funded Universal Care

The United Kingdom's National Health Service (NHS), established in 1948, is the archetype of the tax-funded, publicly provided healthcare model. The NHS is funded almost entirely through general taxation (approximately eighty percent) and National Insurance contributions (approximately eighteen percent), with minimal user charges (primarily for prescriptions, dental care, and optical services, with extensive exemptions). Care is free at the point of use for all UK residents. The NHS is the world's largest single-payer health system and the fifth-largest employer globally.

The UK spends approximately 11.3 percent of GDP on health, with public sources accounting for approximately eighty-three percent of total health expenditure. Per capita health spending is approximately $5,100 per year. Life expectancy is approximately eighty-one years.

The NHS model is the closest international analogue to the NHI's stated aspiration: a single publicly funded system providing comprehensive care free at the point of use. The UK's experience offers both encouragement and caution. The encouragement: the NHS demonstrates that tax-funded universal healthcare is achievable and can deliver good population health outcomes. The UK has achieved universal coverage for seventy-seven years, and the system retains broad public support despite persistent challenges. The caution: the NHS is under severe and growing strain. Waiting lists for elective procedures have reached record levels — over seven million people in England are waiting for treatment. Staff shortages are chronic, particularly for nurses, general practitioners, and specialists. Infrastructure is ageing, with a maintenance backlog estimated at over thirty billion pounds. These challenges arise despite the UK spending over eleven percent of GDP on health — a level that South Africa cannot approach under any realistic growth scenario in the medium term.

The NHS's current difficulties illustrate a critical lesson: even well-established universal systems require sustained and growing investment to maintain quality. The UK's health spending as a percentage of GDP declined in real terms during the austerity period of 2010 to 2019, and the consequences — longer waits, staff burnout, deteriorating infrastructure — emerged with a lag. For South Africa, this means that establishing NHI is only the beginning: sustaining it requires the ongoing fiscal commitment that only economic growth can guarantee. A universal system launched during stagnation will quickly reproduce the underfunding, overcrowding, and quality deficits that characterise the current public health system — merely extending them to the entire population rather than confining them to the eighty-four percent who currently depend on the public sector.

## The Two-Speed System: Public and Private

Any discussion of South Africa's health fiscal arithmetic must confront the defining structural feature of the system: the extreme bifurcation between public and private sectors. Wherever you sit in this system — whether you have medical scheme cover, use the public system, or move between both depending on what you can afford at any given time — the split shapes what care you receive and what it costs you.

South Africa's private health sector — comprising approximately eighty private hospital groups (dominated by Netcare, Mediclinic, and Life Healthcare), approximately 27,000 private medical practitioners, and a medical schemes industry covering approximately nine million principal members and their dependents — delivers healthcare of a quality comparable to high-income countries. Private hospitals are well-equipped, well-staffed, and well-maintained. Specialist access is rapid. Diagnostic technology is current. Outcomes for most conditions are comparable to those achieved in OECD countries.

This quality comes at a cost. Medical scheme contributions average approximately R2,500 to R6,000 per month per family, depending on the plan. Out-of-pocket payments for co-payments, non-covered services, and specialist consultations add to this cost. The Competition Commission's Health Market Inquiry, which reported in 2019 after four years of investigation, found that private healthcare costs in South Africa were high by international standards — particularly specialist fees and hospital facility fees — and that the private sector suffered from significant market failures, including information asymmetry, supplier-induced demand, and inadequate competition among providers.

The two-speed system has profound fiscal implications. The sixteen percent of the population with medical scheme coverage absorbs approximately fifty-two percent of total health expenditure. The eighty-four percent without coverage must rely on the public sector, which receives approximately forty-eight percent. The private sector employs approximately seventy percent of South Africa's specialist doctors and approximately thirty percent of general practitioners. This means that the public sector — serving five times as many people — has access to a fraction of the specialist workforce.

NHI's ambition to integrate these two systems — or, more precisely, to create a single purchasing fund that contracts both public and private providers — is, from a fiscal perspective, an attempt to redistribute health spending from the privileged minority to the underserved majority. The political economy of this redistribution is formidable: those who currently benefit from private coverage — who tend to be politically influential, economically productive, and tax-paying — will resist any change that degrades their access to healthcare. Medical schemes, private hospital groups, and private practitioners have powerful lobbying capacity.

The fiscal challenge is equally formidable. Expanding public-sector-quality coverage to the whole population — the only version of NHI that would be politically defensible — requires either dramatically increasing total health spending (to bring the public system up to private-system quality) or dramatically reducing private-sector costs (to bring private-system services within reach of public funding levels). The first option requires massive additional revenue. The second option requires overcoming the market failures and rent-seeking that the Competition Commission identified but did not resolve.

A middle path — often discussed but rarely articulated precisely — would involve gradually improving the public system's quality while creating regulatory incentives for the private sector to reduce costs and serve a broader population. This incremental approach is less dramatic than the NHI legislation's vision of a single fund, but it may be more fiscally realistic. It requires sustained economic growth to fund the incremental improvements, competent regulation to reduce private-sector costs, and patient political management of the expectations of both the currently insured and the currently uninsured.

## The Path Forward: Growth as Health Policy

This article has presented the fiscal arithmetic of South Africa's health system in unvarnished terms. The numbers are constraining but not hopeless. The path forward requires clarity about three things.

First, economic growth is health policy. This is not a metaphor or a political slogan. It is a fiscal fact. Without sustained GDP growth of at least three percent per year — and preferably higher — there is no pathway to expanding public health coverage, hiring the health workers the system needs, funding the treatment cascades documented earlier in this series, or implementing NHI in any meaningful form. The economic reforms discussed in [Three Engines of Growth](/articles/eg-3-1-three-engines-of-growth) and [Resource Optimisation](/articles/eg-2-1-resource-optimisation) are not separate from the health agenda. They are its fiscal foundation.

Second, efficiency matters as much as funding. Even within the current budget, there are opportunities to improve health outcomes by spending existing resources more effectively. Procurement reform — buying medicines at the best available prices through competitive tendering and international reference pricing — can stretch the medicine budget. Task-shifting — allowing nurses and community health workers to perform functions currently reserved for doctors — can extend the reach of the existing workforce. Differentiated service delivery — matching the intensity of clinic visits to patients' clinical needs — can reduce wasteful overcrowding and free capacity for patients who need it most. Technology — including electronic health records, mHealth adherence support, and telemedicine for specialist consultation — can improve coordination and reduce unnecessary referrals. None of these efficiency gains substitutes for additional funding, but they can improve the health outcomes achievable at current funding levels while the growth agenda works to expand those levels.

Third, the allocation of health spending matters as much as the total. The "points per rand" analysis from the previous article in this series — showing that closing the HIV treatment cascade gap is the highest-return investment in South Africa's HDI — should inform budget allocation decisions. If the health system cannot do everything, it should do first the things that produce the greatest health gain per rand spent. That means prioritising the treatment cascade, TB case-finding and treatment, maternal and child health (particularly neonatal care), and population-level NCD prevention — in roughly that order, based on the available cost-effectiveness evidence.

The fiscal arithmetic is not destiny. Countries have broken out of stagnation traps, grown their economies, and funded health systems that deliver universal coverage and high life expectancy. South Africa has the institutional infrastructure, the human capital base, and the policy knowledge to do the same. What it needs is the economic growth to fund it — and the implementation discipline to spend the money well when it comes.

The next article in this series shifts from health to education, examining [The Schooling Paradox](/articles/hd-3-1-the-schooling-paradox) — a system that spends more per student than most middle-income countries yet produces learning outcomes that rank among the worst in the world. If the health system's challenge is funding, the education system's challenge is efficiency — and both, ultimately, depend on the same fiscal arithmetic documented here.

:::takeaway
South Africa's health system cannot be fixed by money alone — but it cannot be fixed without more money. Economic growth of at least three percent per year is the minimum required to generate the fiscal space for meaningful health system expansion, health worker recruitment, and NHI implementation. In the interim, efficiency gains — procurement reform, task-shifting, differentiated service delivery, and evidence-based resource allocation — can improve outcomes within existing budgets. The fiscal arithmetic is clear: growth is not separate from the health agenda. It is the health agenda's prerequisite, and every health policy promise must be evaluated against whether the economic growth strategy exists to fund it.
:::

:::framework
**The Health Budget Evaluator**

Use this framework to assess whether health spending — at national, provincial, or district level — is being allocated and spent in ways that will close the life expectancy deficit.

**Question 1: What is the per capita public health expenditure in this province or district?**
Source: National Treasury Budget Review; provincial budget statements; District Health Barometer.
Benchmark: The national average is approximately R5,000 per uninsured person per year. Provinces and districts spending significantly below this average are structurally under-resourced. Compare to the approximately R30,000 per beneficiary spent in the private sector to understand the scale of the two-speed system.

**Question 2: What proportion of the health budget goes to personnel versus programmes?**
Source: Provincial budget statements (available on provincial treasury websites).
Red flag: If personnel costs exceed 65 per cent of the budget, the system has minimal fiscal space for medicines, equipment, maintenance, or new programmes. This is not the fault of health workers — it reflects insufficient total funding and, often, inefficient staffing patterns.

**Question 3: Is spending aligned with the highest-return interventions?**
Priority ranking (based on HDI points per rand): HIV treatment cascade closure (highest return), TB active case-finding and treatment, maternal and neonatal care, NCD screening and management.
Key question: Is the budget allocation consistent with this evidence-based priority ranking? If more is being spent on hospital infrastructure than on closing the treatment cascade gap, the allocation is misaligned with the evidence.

**Question 4: What are the health worker vacancy rates?**
Source: District Health Barometer; Department of Health HR reports.
Key question: What is the vacancy rate for professional nurses, doctors, and pharmacists in the facilities serving your community? Vacancy rates above 20 per cent indicate a facility that cannot deliver adequate care regardless of its budget allocation.

**Question 5: What is the GDP growth rate, and what does it mean for future health spending?**
Source: Stats SA GDP data; National Treasury fiscal framework.
Key question: Is GDP growth sufficient (at least 3 per cent) to generate the additional tax revenue needed for health system expansion? If growth is below 2 per cent, the health budget is likely to stagnate or decline in real terms — meaning that efficiency improvements are the only pathway to better outcomes in the medium term.
