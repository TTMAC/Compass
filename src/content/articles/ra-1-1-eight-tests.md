## The Bottom Line, Up Front

When water stops flowing from your tap, when the clinic has no medication, when your child's school has no textbooks — what is the first thing you think? Most South Africans reach for the same explanation: corruption. Or incompetence. Or both. And they are not entirely wrong. South Africa has no shortage of officials who have stolen public money, appointees who lack the qualifications for their posts, or politicians who treat government as a vehicle for personal enrichment rather than public service. The Zondo Commission's State Capture findings made that painfully, exhaustively clear.

## Why Government Fails Isn't a Mystery — It's a Design Problem

But here is a question that rarely gets asked: why does government fail even where officials are competent and honest? In the 2023-24 municipal audit cycle, only 41 out of 257 municipalities — sixteen per cent — achieved a clean audit. The Auditor-General identified R27.3 billion in fruitless and wasteful expenditure across 110 municipalities, and ninety municipalities had spent more than they had available. Yet even among the small number of municipalities that manage their finances well, many still struggle to deliver reliable water. Why? Why do provincial education departments with capable HODs still produce matric results that vary wildly from one district to the next? Why does the national Department of Human Settlements publish ambitious housing targets year after year, only to miss them by tens of thousands of units?

The answer is not corruption. The answer is design.

South Africa's governance system — the way responsibilities, powers, budgets, and consequences are distributed across institutions — contains structural flaws that would produce failure regardless of who occupies the positions. When the entity that is responsible for delivering a service does not control the budget for that service, it will underperform. When the entity that is accountable for outcomes lacks the authority to hire, fire, or procure, it will underperform. When there are no automatic consequences for failure, failure will persist. These are not moral failures. They are engineering failures — and they can be diagnosed, measured, and fixed.

This article introduces a framework for doing exactly that. It is called RAARICLE™, and it comprises eight elements that every government institution needs in order to function. Think of them as eight tests. When a government function passes all eight, services tend to work. When it fails even one, cracks appear. When it fails several simultaneously — which is the norm in South African governance — the result is the systemic collapse that millions of citizens experience every day.

:::takeaway
Government failure is not primarily a people problem — it is a design problem. Even competent, honest officials will fail when the institutional architecture scatters responsibility, accountability, authority, and resources across multiple entities with no binding coordination.
:::

The RAARICLE™ framework does not replace the need to fight corruption or improve individual competence. But it gives you something that outrage alone cannot: a structured way to diagnose exactly what is broken, where the misalignment lies, and what a working design would look like. If you have ever looked at a government failure and thought "someone should fix this," this framework will help you identify, with precision, what "this" actually is.

## Test 1: Responsibility — Who Does the Work?

The first test is deceptively simple: can you name one entity that owns the task end-to-end?

Responsibility, in the RAARICLE™ sense, means operational ownership. It is not about who sets the policy, who provides the funding, or who monitors the outcomes. It is about who actually does the work — who runs the water treatment plant, who employs the teachers, who collects the refuse, who builds the houses. Responsibility answers the question: if this function fails tomorrow, whose operational failure caused it?

In a well-designed system, the answer is clear. When you ask "who is responsible for collecting refuse in Johannesburg?" the answer should be one entity — the City of Johannesburg's waste management division, or a contracted service provider operating under the city's direct supervision. There should be no ambiguity. One entity, one mandate, end-to-end ownership from collection route to landfill site.

Now consider human settlements. Who is responsible for delivering a subsidised house to a qualifying beneficiary? The national Department of Human Settlements sets the subsidy framework and channels conditional grants to provinces. The provincial department approves project applications, selects developers, and manages the housing pipeline. The municipality identifies and zones suitable land, provides bulk infrastructure — water connections, sewerage, roads — and approves building plans. The developer or contractor physically constructs the dwelling. The Deeds Registry registers the title deed. That is at least five entities across three spheres of government, and we have not yet mentioned the National Home Builders Registration Council, which is supposed to ensure construction quality, or the Housing Development Agency, which was created precisely because the existing value chain was not working.

When a housing project fails — when the houses are built on unsuitable land, or the construction is defective, or the title deeds are never registered, or the bulk infrastructure is not connected — who is responsible? The honest answer is that nobody is, because nobody owns the outcome end-to-end. Each entity can point to its piece of the chain and say "I did my part." The national department approved the grant. The province appointed the developer. The municipality made the land available. The developer built the structure. And yet the beneficiary is living in a cracking house with no water connection and no title deed. Responsibility has been fragmented to the point where it functionally does not exist.

:::expert
The Auditor-General's office has repeatedly identified "lack of clear accountability and responsibility" as a root cause of poor audit outcomes in human settlements. In the 2022-23 MFMA audit cycle, the AG noted that the fragmentation of the housing value chain across spheres makes it "difficult to hold a single entity responsible for the end-to-end delivery of a housing unit."
:::

The test, then, is this: for any government function you care about, can you name one entity that owns the task from start to finish? If the answer requires you to name three or more entities, you have identified a fragmentation problem — and fragmentation is one of the most reliable predictors of service delivery failure in South African governance.

What does passing this test look like? Consider South Africa's own Revenue Service. SARS is responsible for tax collection end-to-end. It registers taxpayers, assesses returns, collects revenue, pursues non-compliance, and manages the IT systems that underpin all of these functions. You do not need to interact with three different agencies to file your taxes. One entity owns the task. That clarity of responsibility is a major reason why SARS — despite its own institutional crises during the Moyane era — was able to be rebuilt relatively quickly once competent leadership was restored. The institutional architecture was sound; it was the people that needed replacing. That is the difference between a design problem and a people problem.

## Test 2: Accountability — Who Answers for the Outcome?

Responsibility is about who does the work. Accountability is about who answers when it goes wrong — or, crucially, who gets credit when it goes right. The test here is: is there one name on the performance agreement?

Accountability requires that a specific, identifiable person or entity bears consequences for outcomes. Not processes. Not inputs. Outcomes. It is not enough to say "the municipality submitted its Integrated Development Plan on time." The question is whether residents received reliable water, whether refuse was collected on schedule, whether the roads are maintained. Accountability links a named entity to a measurable result.

In South African governance, accountability is often diffused to the point of meaninglessness. Consider basic education. The provincial Member of the Executive Council for Education is politically accountable for the province's matric results. That MEC signs a performance agreement with the Premier. But what does the MEC actually control? The curriculum is set nationally by the Department of Basic Education. Teacher salary scales and conditions of service are negotiated nationally at the Education Labour Relations Council. The South African Council for Educators handles professional registration and discipline nationally. Even teacher post provisioning — how many teachers each school gets — follows a nationally determined model. The provincial MEC is accountable for an outcome that depends overwhelmingly on inputs controlled by national entities.

This creates a perverse dynamic. When matric results decline, the MEC can credibly argue that the failure lies with national policy settings, national bargaining outcomes, or national funding norms. And the national minister can equally argue that delivery is a provincial competence. Both are correct. Both are also evading accountability, because the system is designed so that no single entity bears unambiguous responsibility for the outcome.

Effective accountability requires what organisational theorists call "the single throat to choke" — an inelegant phrase, but a powerful principle. When outcomes deteriorate, there must be one entity that cannot deflect blame elsewhere because it controls the inputs, owns the delivery chain, and has signed a binding performance agreement for the result. In South African municipal governance, the Municipal Systems Act places this accountability on the municipal manager, who is the accounting officer. But as we will see in later tests, the municipal manager often lacks the authority, resources, and capability to deliver on this accountability — which makes the accountability itself performative rather than real.

:::takeaway
Accountability without authority is theatre. If the entity that signs the performance agreement does not control the inputs that determine the outcome, the accountability is performative — it assigns blame but does not drive performance.
:::

What does genuine accountability look like? Singapore's public housing programme, operated by the Housing and Development Board, is a useful comparator. HDB is accountable for the entire housing delivery chain — land acquisition, design, construction, allocation, and estate management. When a housing estate has problems, there is no ambiguity about who answers for it. HDB cannot point to another agency. This alignment of accountability with operational control is a major reason why Singapore houses over eighty per cent of its population in public housing that is well-maintained, well-located, and well-regarded. The institutional design makes accountability meaningful because it is matched with operational control.

## Test 3: Authority — Who Has the Power to Decide, Act and Change?

This is arguably the most important test in the framework, because it is the one that South African governance fails most consistently. The test is: can the responsible entity execute a decision within 30 days using only its own powers?

Authority, in the RAARICLE™ framework, means the legal and operational power to decide, act, and change course without requiring permission from another entity. It is the power to hire the right person, fire the wrong one, procure the necessary equipment, redirect the budget, and change an operational process — all within a timeframe that is operationally meaningful.

The 30-day authority test is a practical benchmark. If a water services authority identifies that its water treatment plant needs a new chlorination system, can it procure and install that system within 30 days using its own budget, its own procurement authority, and its own technical capacity? If the answer is no — if it needs national Treasury approval for the expenditure, or provincial sign-off on the procurement, or MISA technical assistance to specify the equipment — then the entity does not truly have authority over its own function. It has nominal authority, constrained by dependencies that make timely action impossible.

South Africa's concurrent competence system, as established in [the constitutional framework](/articles/1-1-architecture-of-the-state), is the primary source of authority failures. Schedule 4 of the Constitution lists functions that are concurrent between national and provincial government — education, health, housing, public transport, environment, and many others. "Concurrent" sounds collaborative. In practice, it often means that neither sphere has complete authority. National government sets the framework, controls significant funding streams through conditional grants, and determines employment conditions. Provincial government delivers the service but within constraints set nationally. The result is a permanent authority deficit at the delivery level.

Consider a provincial health department that wants to restructure its district health services to improve primary healthcare outcomes. The province employs the health workers. But the Occupation Specific Dispensation that determines their grading and salary is national. The essential medicines list is national. The infrastructure grant for clinic construction is conditional — the province must comply with national specifications. The community health worker programme operates under a national framework. The province has formal authority over health service delivery, but the meaningful levers of operational control are held nationally. The province is, in effect, managing a franchise operation where the franchisor controls the product, the supply chain, and the brand — but the franchisee is accountable for the customer experience.

:::expert
Operation Vulindlela, the structural reform initiative housed jointly in the Presidency and National Treasury, has identified authority fragmentation as a binding constraint on service delivery. Their work on water services reform — including a Water Services Amendment Bill and the establishment of a National Water Resources Infrastructure Agency by April 2026 — and their Metro Trading Services Reform Programme across seven metropolitan municipalities explicitly aim to clarify "who has the power to act." National Treasury has acknowledged that implementation has been slow, recognising that South Africa's governance architecture distributes authority so widely that even reform itself is difficult to execute decisively.
:::

What does passing this test look like? Rwanda's Imihigo performance contracts offer an instructive example. Each district mayor signs an annual performance contract with the President, committing to specific, measurable outcomes. Crucially, the mayors have the authority to deliver on those commitments. They control their district budgets, manage their staff, and can reallocate resources within the year without requiring national approval. The authority is matched to the accountability. This does not mean Rwanda's system is perfect — it raises legitimate questions about democratic participation and upward accountability. But the principle of co-locating authority with accountability is sound, and it produces results that South Africa's more fragmented system consistently fails to achieve.

## Test 4: Resources — Who Controls the Means?

Money follows power, and power follows money. The fourth test asks: does revenue flow directly to the responsible entity without passing through another entity's accounts?

Resources, in the RAARICLE™ framework, encompass budget, people, and physical assets. But money is the most critical, because in South African governance, the way money flows determines who truly controls the function — regardless of what the Constitution or legislation says about responsibility and authority.

South Africa's fiscal architecture, governed by the Division of Revenue Act, channels money to subnational government through three main streams. The equitable share is an unconditional transfer — provinces and municipalities can, in theory, spend it according to their own priorities. Conditional grants are ring-fenced for specific purposes and come with compliance conditions set by the national transferring department. And own revenue — primarily property rates and service charges for municipalities, and a very limited tax base for provinces — is the only funding stream that the subnational entity fully controls.

The structural problem is this: for most municipalities, the equitable share and conditional grants together represent the majority of revenue. Own revenue is significant only for the metropolitan municipalities and a handful of well-managed secondary cities. This means that the entities responsible for service delivery are fundamentally dependent on transfers from another sphere of government. They do not control their own means.

The consequences are predictable and severe. When a municipality depends on conditional grants for water infrastructure, it must comply with the conditions set by the Department of Water and Sanitation — conditions that may not match local priorities, may change between financial years, and may come with reporting requirements that consume scarce administrative capacity. The municipality cannot redirect water infrastructure funding to address an urgent sanitation crisis, even if sanitation is the more pressing local need. The money comes with strings, and those strings run upward to national government, not outward to the community being served.

Even more problematically, municipal electricity surpluses have historically been used to cross-subsidise other functions. A municipality may generate a surplus from electricity reticulation — buying bulk power from Eskom at one price and selling it to consumers at a higher price — and use that surplus to fund roads, parks, or general administration. This cross-subsidisation means that electricity revenue is not ring-fenced for electricity infrastructure maintenance. When the distribution network deteriorates, the municipality faces a choice between maintaining electrical infrastructure and funding other services. The result, visible across dozens of municipalities, is ageing electrical infrastructure, rising technical losses, and deteriorating supply reliability.

:::takeaway
When revenue is not ring-fenced for the function it serves, the entity responsible for that function does not truly control its resources. Cross-subsidisation and conditional grants create dependencies that undermine operational autonomy and distort local priority-setting.
:::

What does passing this test look like? Consider Rand Water, the bulk water utility serving Gauteng and parts of neighbouring provinces. Rand Water generates its revenue directly from the municipalities it serves — it sells treated water at a tariff that covers its operational and capital costs. It does not depend on government grants for its core operations. This financial independence gives Rand Water operational autonomy that municipal water services authorities lack. It can plan capital expenditure over multi-year horizons, maintain its infrastructure proactively, and attract qualified engineers with competitive salaries. Rand Water is not perfect, but its institutional design passes the resources test in a way that most municipal water services do not.

## Test 5: Information — Who Has Visibility into Performance?

You cannot manage what you cannot see. The fifth test asks: can the responsible entity see real-time performance data for everything it operates?

Information, in the RAARICLE™ framework, is not about having a monitoring and evaluation unit or publishing an annual report. It is about operational visibility — the ability to know, in real time or near-real time, whether the function is performing as designed. Can the water services authority see today's water quality readings from every treatment plant? Can the provincial education department see this term's learner attendance and assessment data from every school? Can the municipality see which refuse collection routes were completed today and which were missed?

In South African governance, information failures are pervasive. The Department of Water and Sanitation's Blue Drop system tells a devastating story. The 2023 Blue Drop report — released after years of non-publication — assessed 958 water supply systems and found that 277 of them, across 62 municipalities, were in a critical state. Only 26 systems achieved Blue Drop certification, down from 44 in 2014. Forty-six per cent of systems had poor or bad microbiological water quality compliance, up from just five per cent a decade earlier. In the Northern Cape, 87 per cent of water systems were in poor or critical condition. Many municipalities could not report on their own water quality because they were not testing it. The entity responsible for delivering safe drinking water literally did not know whether the water it was delivering was safe.

This information deficit has a compounding effect. When the responsible entity lacks operational visibility, it cannot identify problems early. When it cannot identify problems early, small failures become large crises. When large crises emerge, they are visible only through the lagging indicators — disease outbreaks from contaminated water, matric results that reveal years of accumulated learning deficits, infrastructure collapses that reflect decades of deferred maintenance. By the time the information reaches the people who need it, the damage is done.

Information capture — where management filters, delays, or misrepresents data flowing to oversight bodies — is one of the five anti-capture vectors that the RAARICLE™ framework identifies as a structural threat. When a municipal manager presents the council with performance data that obscures declining water quality or rising revenue losses, the council cannot exercise its oversight function effectively. The information asymmetry between management and oversight creates space for poor performance to persist unchecked and, in the worst cases, for corruption to flourish undetected.

What does passing this test look like? South Africa actually has a good example in a different domain. The South African Reserve Bank's financial surveillance system provides real-time visibility into banking sector stability indicators. When a financial institution's capital adequacy ratio deteriorates, the Reserve Bank knows immediately — not quarterly, not annually, but in real time. This information architecture allows for early intervention before problems become crises. The same principle should apply to basic service delivery: the entity responsible for water treatment should know today's water quality, not last quarter's.

## Test 6: Capability — Are the Skills and Systems Present?

Having the right institutional design means nothing if the institution lacks the human capital and technical systems to execute. The sixth test asks: does every critical post have a professionally qualified incumbent?

Capability encompasses three dimensions: people (the right skills in the right posts), systems (the IT infrastructure, operational processes, and institutional knowledge that enable delivery), and institutional depth (the bench strength that allows the organisation to absorb shocks — retirements, resignations, restructuring — without losing operational continuity).

South Africa's capability crisis is well documented. The Auditor-General's annual reports consistently identify "lack of competencies" as a root cause of poor audit outcomes. Across local government, the AG has found that many municipalities lack the minimum financial management competencies required by the Municipal Finance Management Act. Chief financial officers who do not meet the prescribed qualifications. Technical directors without engineering or project management credentials. Municipal managers appointed on political rather than professional criteria.

The capability deficit is not evenly distributed. Metropolitan municipalities and well-resourced secondary cities can attract and retain qualified professionals. They offer competitive salaries, professional development opportunities, and working environments that are broadly functional. But in the majority of South Africa's 257 municipalities — particularly the rural and small-town local municipalities that serve some of the country's most vulnerable communities — the capability gap is acute. These municipalities struggle to fill critical posts, cannot compete with the private sector or larger municipalities for scarce skills, and often depend on external consultants for functions that should be performed by permanent staff.

The consultant dependency is itself a capability trap. When a municipality hires consultants to prepare its financial statements, it does not build the internal capacity to prepare financial statements in future years. The knowledge leaves with the consultants. The municipality must hire them again the following year. This cycle persists indefinitely, draining resources without building institutional capability.

:::expert
The South African Institution of Civil Engineering's Infrastructure Report Card has consistently rated South Africa's municipal infrastructure management capability as poor to very poor. The 2022 assessment noted that many municipalities lack the in-house engineering capacity to manage their own infrastructure assets, leading to reactive maintenance (fixing things after they break) rather than proactive asset management (maintaining things before they break).
:::

What does passing this test look like? South Korea's transformation of its civil service after the Asian financial crisis of 1997 is instructive. Korea invested heavily in professionalising its public service, establishing rigorous qualification requirements for technical posts, creating career pathways that rewarded expertise, and building institutional systems (knowledge management, standard operating procedures, digital infrastructure) that reduced dependence on any single individual. The result was a public service that could execute complex policy — from broadband rollout to pandemic response — with speed and competence. Capability was treated not as a nice-to-have but as critical infrastructure.

## Test 7: Legitimacy — Is the Mandate Recognised?

The seventh test moves beyond the operational and into the political: does the entity's authority derive from a clear democratic or statutory chain?

Legitimacy, in the RAARICLE™ framework, is the recognition by stakeholders — citizens, other government entities, the private sector, labour — that the entity has the right to exercise its functions. Legitimacy is not just legal (though legal mandate is the foundation). It is also social and political. An entity can have a clear statutory mandate but lack the social legitimacy needed to exercise it effectively.

This test matters because governance is not a purely technical exercise. Government functions operate within a political context where stakeholders can support, resist, or actively undermine institutional authority. An entity that lacks legitimacy — because its mandate is contested, its leaders are seen as politically compromised, or its historical conduct has eroded public trust — will struggle to govern effectively even if it passes all the other tests.

Consider the experience of Section 139 interventions in municipalities. The Constitution empowers provincial government to intervene when a municipality cannot or does not fulfil its constitutional obligations. In theory, this is a critical enforcement mechanism. In practice, as of May 2025, 21 municipalities had active Section 139 interventions — and only six showed good progress, while nine showed no progress at all. Interventions have been contested on legitimacy grounds repeatedly. Emfuleni Local Municipality in Gauteng has been under intervention since 2018 without achieving its recovery objectives. Mpofana Municipality in KwaZulu-Natal has been under intervention since 2017, spanning three successive administrations. Municipal councils have challenged whether the criteria for intervention were met. Communities have protested against administrators appointed from outside. Political parties have accused provincial governments of using Section 139 as a tool for political control rather than genuine governance rescue. The National Council of Provinces has acknowledged that there is no evidence that Section 139 interventions have yielded consistently positive results. The intervention may be legally valid, but its social and political legitimacy is contested — and that contestation undermines its effectiveness.

Legitimacy also operates between spheres of government. When national government creates new entities — like the Housing Development Agency or the Municipal Infrastructure Support Agent — to intervene in functions that are constitutionally assigned to other spheres, it implicitly signals that those spheres have failed. This can create institutional resentment and resistance. The province or municipality may view the national entity not as a partner but as an interloper, undermining cooperation and creating parallel systems rather than strengthening existing ones.

What does passing this test look like? The Independent Electoral Commission of South Africa is perhaps the country's strongest example of an entity with deep, broad-based legitimacy. The IEC derives its authority from Chapter 9 of the Constitution. Its mandate is clear and uncontested. Its operational independence is respected across the political spectrum. When the IEC announces election results, those results are accepted — even by losing parties. This legitimacy was not automatic; it was built over decades through consistent, competent, transparent performance. It demonstrates that institutional legitimacy is earned through conduct, not merely conferred by statute.

## Test 8: Enforcement — What Are the Consequences for Failure or Success?

The eighth and final test is the one that holds all the others together: are triggers automatic and consequences pre-defined?

Enforcement is the mechanism that ensures the other seven elements actually matter. Without enforcement, responsibility is advisory, accountability is rhetorical, authority is theoretical, and capability is optional. Enforcement means that when an entity fails to perform — when water quality drops below the standard, when financial statements are qualified, when infrastructure maintenance targets are missed — specific, pre-defined consequences follow automatically. Not at someone's discretion. Not after a political negotiation. Automatically.

This is where South African governance fails most dramatically. The architecture of enforcement in the South African system is overwhelmingly discretionary. The Department of Water and Sanitation can issue compliance directives to failing water services authorities — but it is not required to, and it rarely does so with meaningful consequences attached. The provincial government can invoke Section 139 to intervene in a failing municipality — but the decision to intervene is political, the timing is discretionary, and the intervention framework is vague. The Auditor-General can issue qualified or adverse audit opinions year after year — but there is no automatic trigger that converts a pattern of audit failures into a change in institutional arrangements.

:::takeaway
South Africa has abundant oversight but feeble enforcement. The country is rich in institutions that can identify failure — the Auditor-General, Chapter 9 institutions, parliamentary committees — but poor in mechanisms that automatically convert identified failure into mandatory corrective action.
:::

The contrast with SARS is instructive. When a taxpayer fails to file a return, SARS does not convene a committee to discuss whether to act. It does not publish a report noting the non-compliance and expressing concern. It issues an automatic penalty. When the penalty is not paid, interest accrues automatically. When the debt reaches a threshold, enforcement action — attachment of assets, garnishment of wages — follows through prescribed legal processes. The triggers are automatic. The consequences are pre-defined. Discretion is minimised. The results speak for themselves: in the 2024-25 fiscal year, SARS collected R2.303 trillion in gross revenue — a 6.9 per cent increase year on year. Compliance revenue has grown from R128.4 billion in 2019-20 to R304 billion in 2024-25, a compound annual growth rate of 18.8 per cent. This enforcement architecture is a major reason why South Africa's tax compliance rates are vastly better than its municipal service delivery compliance rates.

Now imagine if the same principle applied to water services. If a municipality's Blue Drop score fell below a defined threshold for two consecutive assessment periods, an automatic intervention would be triggered — not at the discretion of a provincial MEC, but as a mandatory consequence prescribed in legislation. The intervention would follow a standard protocol: appointment of a qualified technical team, ring-fencing of water revenue, and a recovery plan with measurable milestones. If the milestones were not met, the next level of intervention would trigger automatically. No political negotiation. No delays while national and provincial governments argue about jurisdiction. Automatic triggers, pre-defined consequences.

This is not a fantasy. It is how banking regulation works. It is how tax enforcement works. It is how electrical safety standards work (your circuit breaker does not hold a hearing before tripping). The technology of automatic enforcement with pre-defined consequences is well understood. South Africa simply has not applied it to the governance functions that affect citizens most directly.

## The Co-Location Principle

The eight tests described above are not a checklist to be ticked off independently. They form an integrated system, and the most important insight of the RAARICLE™ framework is this: all eight elements must be co-located in a single institutional chain, or coherently aligned across institutions with binding coordination mechanisms.

Co-location means that the entity responsible for the work is also the entity that is accountable for the outcome, that has the authority to act, that controls the resources, that can see the performance data, that possesses the necessary capability, that holds a recognised mandate, and that faces automatic consequences for failure. When all eight elements sit within one institutional chain, governance tends to work. When they are scattered across different entities, different spheres of government, different budget lines, and different political principals — governance fails predictably.

Think of it this way. Imagine a private company where the sales team is accountable for revenue targets, but the marketing team controls the advertising budget, the IT department owns the customer database, the HR department decides who gets hired onto the sales team, and the finance department can veto any deal over a certain size. No board of directors would design a company this way, because it is obviously dysfunctional. The accountability is disconnected from the authority. The resources are controlled by an entity that does not bear the consequences. The information sits with an entity that has no operational role.

Yet this is precisely how South Africa structures many of its most critical government functions. In education, the province is accountable but the national sphere controls the employment framework. In water, the municipality is responsible but the national department controls enforcement. In housing, nobody owns the outcome end-to-end because the function is fragmented across five or more entities. In healthcare, the facility manager cannot procure essential medicines because the provincial pharmaceutical depot controls the supply chain.

The co-location principle does not necessarily require that all eight elements sit within a single institution. In complex modern governance, some distribution of functions is inevitable and even desirable — you do not want the service provider to also be its own regulator, for instance. But where elements are distributed across institutions, there must be binding coordination mechanisms — legally enforceable agreements, shared performance frameworks with joint consequences, integrated information systems with mutual visibility — that ensure the distributed elements function as a coherent system rather than as disconnected fragments.

:::expert
Research from the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development on multi-level governance consistently finds that "the alignment of responsibilities, resources, and accountability across levels of government" is the single strongest predictor of public service quality. Countries that co-locate these elements — even imperfectly — outperform countries where they are fragmented, regardless of other factors like GDP per capita or democratic maturity.
:::

## How to Use These Tests

You now have a framework. The question is what to do with it. The short answer: apply these eight tests to any government function you care about. The longer answer follows.

The next time you experience a government failure — your water is cut off, the clinic has no medication, the potholes on your street have been there for eighteen months, your child's school does not have enough teachers — resist the impulse to reach immediately for blame. Instead, run the eight tests. Not because blame is unimportant, but because diagnosis must precede prescription. You would not accept a doctor who prescribed medication before examining you. You should not accept governance solutions — firing the minister, appointing an administrator, launching another commission of inquiry — without first understanding the structural failure.

Here is how to apply the tests in practice. Let us walk through an example using water services — the function that affects more South Africans, more directly, more frequently than perhaps any other.

According to Stats SA's 2024 General Household Survey, 48.4 per cent of South African households experienced water interruptions — nearly half the country. When your tap runs dry, start with Test 1: Who is responsible? Your municipality, as the designated Water Services Authority under the Water Services Act. If your municipality has established a separate water utility — as a few have done — then that utility is the responsible entity. Either way, you should be able to identify one entity that owns the delivery of water to your household.

Test 2: Who is accountable? The municipal manager is the accounting officer. The mayor and council provide political oversight. But is there a specific, binding performance agreement that commits a named individual to a measurable water service standard — not just "provide water services" but "maintain uninterrupted water supply to 95 per cent of connected households, with water quality meeting Blue Drop standards"? In many municipalities, the performance agreements are too vague to constitute genuine accountability.

Test 3: Can they fix it within 30 days? This is where the system often breaks down. If the water outage is caused by a burst pipe, the municipality may well be able to repair it within 30 days using its own maintenance team and budget. But if the outage is caused by a failing water treatment plant that needs a new component, the municipality may need to go through a procurement process that takes months. If the outage is caused by the bulk water supplier — Rand Water, or a dam managed by the Department of Water and Sanitation — the municipality has no authority to fix the upstream problem at all. Its authority stops at its jurisdictional boundary.

Test 4: Does the municipality control the money? Check whether your municipality ring-fences water revenue for water services. Many do not. Water tariff revenue may flow into the general municipal account and be used to fund other functions. If the water infrastructure needs a major capital investment, the municipality may need to apply for a Municipal Infrastructure Grant — a conditional grant from national Treasury with its own application process, conditions, and timelines.

Test 5: Does the municipality know what is happening? Does your municipality have real-time telemetry on its water network — pressure sensors, flow meters, quality monitors? Or does it discover problems only when residents complain? The information test separates municipalities that manage their networks proactively from those that lurch from crisis to crisis.

Test 6: Are the right people in the right posts? Does the municipality employ qualified process controllers for its water treatment works? Does it have registered professional engineers overseeing its water infrastructure? Or are critical technical posts vacant, filled by acting appointments, or occupied by people who do not hold the prescribed qualifications?

Test 7: Is the municipality's authority recognised? Do residents and stakeholders accept the municipality's right to govern water services — including the right to set tariffs, enforce water restrictions, and disconnect for non-payment? Or is the municipality's legitimacy so eroded by poor performance that residents have lost trust and resist its authority?

Test 8: What happens if the municipality fails? If water quality drops below the Blue Drop standard, what happens automatically? If the municipality posts its fifth consecutive qualified audit for water services, what consequences follow? In most cases, the answer is: nothing automatic. There are reports, recommendations, directives — but no automatic triggers that compel corrective action.

Run these eight tests for any function you care about, and you will have a more precise diagnosis of the governance failure than most official assessments provide. You will know whether the problem is fragmentation (too many entities), misalignment (the wrong entity holds the wrong element), absence (a critical element is missing), or conflation (the regulator and provider are the same entity). And once you know what is broken, you can begin to articulate what a fix would actually look like — not in the vague language of "improve governance" or "strengthen accountability," but in the specific language of institutional design.

:::framework
**The RAARICLE™ Governance Diagnostic**

When you encounter a government service failure, use these eight questions to pinpoint the structural breakdown before reaching for blame.

**Step 1: Identify the responsible entity.**

Ask: can I name one entity that owns this function end-to-end — from input to outcome? If the answer requires naming three or more entities across different spheres, you have a fragmentation problem. Write down every entity involved and note where handoff points create gaps.

**Step 2: Check accountability.**

Ask: is there one named person who has signed a binding performance agreement for a measurable outcome — not a process or an input, but a result that citizens can observe? If the performance agreement is vague ("provide water services") rather than specific ("maintain uninterrupted supply to 95 per cent of connected households"), the accountability is performative.

**Step 3: Test authority.**

Ask: can the responsible entity fix this problem within 30 days using only its own powers — its own budget, its own procurement authority, its own staff? If it needs approval from another sphere, a conditional grant application, or external technical assistance, it does not have real authority over the function.

**Step 4: Follow the money.**

Ask: does revenue for this function flow directly to the responsible entity, or does it pass through another entity's accounts? Is the revenue ring-fenced for this function, or can it be diverted to other purposes? If the entity depends on conditional grants or cross-subsidisation, it does not control its own resources.

**Step 5: Check information visibility.**

Ask: can the responsible entity see real-time or near-real-time performance data for the function — today's water quality, this week's refuse collection completion rate, this term's learner attendance? If it relies on quarterly reports or annual audits, problems will only surface after the damage is done.

**Step 6: Assess capability.**

Ask: does every critical post have a professionally qualified, permanent incumbent? Or are key positions vacant, filled by acting appointments, or staffed by people without the prescribed qualifications? If the entity depends on external consultants for core functions, it is in a capability trap.

**Step 7: Evaluate legitimacy.**

Ask: do citizens, other government entities, and stakeholders recognise this entity's right to exercise its function — including unpopular decisions like tariff increases, enforcement actions, or service disconnections? If the entity's mandate is contested or its credibility is eroded, it cannot govern effectively regardless of its design.

**Step 8: Look for automatic enforcement.**

Ask: if this entity fails — if quality drops below the standard, if audits are qualified, if targets are missed — do specific, pre-defined consequences follow automatically? Or does enforcement depend on political discretion, ministerial will, or intergovernmental negotiation? If there are no automatic triggers, failure will persist.

Once you have run all eight tests, identify the pattern: is the problem fragmentation (too many entities), misalignment (authority sits in a different sphere from accountability), absence (a critical element is missing entirely), or conflation (the regulator and provider are the same entity)? This diagnosis will tell you not just that something is broken, but precisely what needs to change — and that specificity is the difference between productive civic engagement and directionless frustration.
