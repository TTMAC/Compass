## Plans on Paper, Projects That Never Get Built

Every growth strategy in history — from the Asian Tigers to Bismarck's Germany to post-war Japan — has been built on an infrastructure foundation. Roads, ports, rail, energy, water, and telecommunications are not nice-to-have additions to an economy. They are the preconditions without which nothing else works. Factories cannot operate without reliable electricity. Mines cannot export without functioning rail and ports. Services firms cannot compete without broadband connectivity. Farmers cannot reach markets without road networks and cold chains. Infrastructure is the operating system on which the economy runs.

South Africa's operating system is crashing. **The Eskom crisis has cost the economy an estimated R1 trillion in lost output since 2007**, according to the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR). **Transnet's port and rail network has deteriorated to the point where mining exporters route cargo through Maputo, Mozambique,** to avoid the delays and inefficiencies at Durban — South Africa's own busiest container port. The National Infrastructure Plan (NIP) and its 18 Strategic Integrated Projects (SIPs), announced with great ambition in 2012, promised R827 billion in investment across energy, transport, water, and social infrastructure. A decade later, delivery has fallen far short of the targets. Against McKinsey's best-practice framework for infrastructure planning and delivery, South Africa scores reasonably well on strategic planning — the plans exist, the documents are thorough, the frameworks are in place — and poorly on execution. Projects are late, over budget, or simply undelivered.

This matters because, as you saw in [Resource Optimisation](/articles/eg-2-1-resource-optimisation), every intervention in Stage 1 of the growth strategy depends on infrastructure working. You cannot optimise agricultural exports if the rail line from the Mpumalanga highveld to Richards Bay cannot carry the freight. You cannot grow the renewable energy sector if Eskom's transmission grid cannot connect independent power producers to the national system. You cannot attract manufacturing investment if firms face 200 days of load shedding per year. Infrastructure is the binding constraint — the one thing that, if it does not improve, makes everything else impossible.

The uncomfortable truth is that **South Africa's infrastructure crisis is not primarily a funding problem.** The money has been allocated, the bonds have been issued, the budgets have been approved. **It is an institutional delivery problem** — a failure of project preparation, construction management, procurement integrity, and coordinated execution across the state. Understanding exactly where the system fails, sector by sector, is the first step toward fixing it.

## The Energy Crisis: How South Africa Lost the Lights

The story of South Africa's electricity crisis begins not in 2008, when load shedding first hit, but in 1998, when the government published a White Paper on Energy Policy that correctly identified the need for new generation capacity. The Department of Minerals and Energy's own modelling showed that Eskom's reserve margin — the buffer between total generation capacity and peak demand — would fall below safe levels by 2007 unless new power stations were commissioned. The modelling was sound. The decision to build was not taken.

Between 1998 and 2004, a combination of factors paralysed investment in new generation. Government was considering restructuring the electricity sector, potentially unbundling Eskom and introducing competition. While this policy debate continued — without resolution — no final investment decision was made on new base-load capacity. Eskom's board, uncertain about the utility's future structure, deferred capital expenditure. By the time Cabinet gave Eskom the green light to build in 2004, the reserve margin had already deteriorated dangerously, and the utility was forced into an emergency procurement programme.

The result was Medupi and Kusile — two of the largest dry-cooled coal-fired power stations in the world, with a combined planned capacity of approximately 9,600 megawatts (MW). Medupi, in Limpopo, was approved in 2007 with an initial budget of R79 billion and a completion date of 2012. Kusile, in Mpumalanga, followed with similar timelines. What happened next became a case study in how not to deliver mega-projects. **Medupi's first unit was only synchronised to the grid in 2015 — three years late. By 2023, the project's cost had ballooned to over R145 billion, nearly double the original estimate. Kusile fared even worse, with costs exceeding R161 billion**, persistent design defects (including flue gas desulphurisation failures), and units that repeatedly tripped offline after commissioning. Both stations were plagued by procurement irregularities, contractor disputes, welding defects, and project management failures that the Zondo Commission on State Capture would later document in detail.

While Medupi and Kusile limped toward completion, Eskom's existing fleet was ageing and under-maintained. The utility's Energy Availability Factor (EAF) — the percentage of installed capacity actually available to generate electricity — declined from above 90% in the early 2000s to below 60% by 2022 and 2023. Coal supply contracts became vectors for corruption, with politically connected intermediaries inserting themselves between mines and power stations, inflating costs and sometimes delivering substandard coal. Skilled engineers and technical staff left the organisation in significant numbers during successive rounds of institutional instability and leadership turnover. Between 2010 and 2023, Eskom cycled through more than ten chief executives and acting chief executives.

### The economic cost of darkness

The consequences of this institutional failure have been devastating. Load shedding — the controlled rotation of power cuts across the grid when demand exceeds available supply — became a recurring feature of South African life from 2008 onward, escalating dramatically in 2022 and 2023. **In 2023, South Africa experienced over 300 days of load shedding,** with stages reaching unprecedented levels (Stage 6 means 6,000 MW of demand must be shed from the grid simultaneously).

The CSIR estimated the direct economic cost of load shedding at between R60 billion and R120 billion per year in lost GDP during the worst periods. The cumulative cost since 2007 exceeds R1 trillion by most credible estimates. But the direct cost understates the damage. Businesses that invested in diesel generators, battery storage, or solar panels incurred costs that made them less competitive internationally. Small businesses and informal traders without the capital for backup power simply lost revenue — or closed. Foreign investors cited energy insecurity as the primary reason for not expanding operations in South Africa. The Manufacturing Circle, an industry body, estimated that **load shedding destroyed approximately 600,000 jobs between 2019 and 2023** through reduced production, cancelled investment, and business closures.

The damage extends beyond economics. Hospitals operated on backup generators during load shedding, with documented cases of equipment failure during power transitions. Water treatment works that depended on the grid sometimes failed to pump or purify water during outages, creating secondary public health risks. Traffic lights went dark, increasing road accidents. The social fabric of daily life was disrupted in ways that eroded trust in the state's basic capacity to function.

### The partial fix: REIPPPP and the path forward

Against this bleak backdrop, one programme stands out as a genuine policy success. The Renewable Energy Independent Power Producer Procurement Programme (REIPPPP), launched in 2011 under the Department of Energy and National Treasury, has been independently assessed by the World Bank, the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA), and the CSIR as one of the most successful renewable energy procurement programmes in any developing country.

The REIPPPP used competitive bidding rounds to procure renewable energy capacity from private developers. By 2023, across five bidding windows, the programme had allocated approximately 6,300 MW of renewable energy capacity from 102 independent power producers (IPPs), attracting over R210 billion in private investment. **The programme drove down the cost of solar photovoltaic (PV) energy by more than 80% between Bid Window 1 and Bid Window 5**, and onshore wind by a comparable margin. By Bid Window 5, the tariff for solar PV had fallen below R0.40 per kilowatt-hour (kWh) — cheaper than Eskom's average cost of generation from coal.

The REIPPPP worked because it combined several design features that addressed South Africa's institutional weaknesses. It used a transparent, rules-based competitive bidding process that limited discretion and corruption. It required bankable power purchase agreements (PPAs) backed by Eskom and guaranteed by Treasury. It imposed economic development requirements — local content thresholds, community ownership stakes, skills development plans — that ensured benefits reached host communities. And it was administered by a dedicated IPP Office within the Department of Energy, staffed with competent professionals and insulated from broader institutional dysfunction.

The Electricity Regulation Amendment Act, signed into law in 2024, took the reform further by enabling municipalities to procure power directly from IPPs, raising the threshold for embedded generation (generation for own use) without licensing requirements, and establishing the legal framework for a reformed electricity market with multiple buyers and sellers. The establishment of the National Transmission Company of South Africa (NTCSA) as a separate entity from Eskom began the long-discussed unbundling of the transmission function — a prerequisite for a competitive electricity market.

:::expert[CSIR Energy Analysis]
The Council for Scientific and Industrial Research's annual analysis of power system performance provides the most rigorous independent assessment of South Africa's electricity crisis. The CSIR's 2023 statistics showed that the total energy not served due to load shedding in 2023 was approximately 13,700 gigawatt-hours (GWh) — equivalent to the annual electricity consumption of a mid-sized city. The CSIR's modelling consistently demonstrates that the least-cost pathway to closing the generation gap involves a combination of renewable energy (solar PV and wind), battery storage, and flexible gas-to-power plants, with coal plant decommissioning managed over a 15-to-20-year transition period. This is not an ideological position — it is the output of techno-economic optimisation models that the CSIR has made publicly available for peer review.
:::

Yet the energy picture remains incomplete without addressing transmission. Even as new generation capacity comes online from IPPs, Eskom's transmission grid — the high-voltage network that carries electricity from where it is generated to where it is consumed — has become a bottleneck. Grid connection delays of two to three years for approved IPP projects are common. Transmission line capacity in the Northern Cape, where solar and wind resources are strongest, is insufficient to evacuate the power that new projects can generate. The Grid Development Plan estimates that R150 billion to R200 billion in transmission investment is needed over the next decade. Without it, new generation capacity remains stranded.

## The Logistics Crisis: When the Supply Chain Breaks

If energy is South Africa's most visible infrastructure crisis, logistics is its most economically damaging one. South Africa's economy is structurally dependent on the efficient movement of bulk commodities — iron ore, coal, manganese, chrome, agricultural products — from inland production sites to coastal ports for export. Transnet, the state-owned freight logistics company, operates the rail network (Transnet Freight Rail, or TFR), the major commercial ports (Transnet National Ports Authority, or TNPA), and the port terminals (Transnet Port Terminals, or TPT). When Transnet performs, the economy flows. When it does not, the economy chokes.

Transnet is not performing. **Rail freight volumes on the general freight business declined from approximately 81 million tonnes in 2016/17 to below 55 million tonnes by 2022/23 — a decline of more than 30% in six years.** The reasons are interconnected and cumulative. Locomotive procurement became entangled in state capture, with the Zondo Commission documenting how politically connected intermediaries inflated the cost of 1,064 locomotives procured from China South Rail (CSR) and China North Rail (CNR) by approximately R5 billion through kickbacks and inflated agency fees. Many of the delivered locomotives experienced operational problems, with some Chinese-manufactured units poorly suited to South African track conditions and gradients.

Cable theft and vandalism on the rail network escalated dramatically. Transnet reported that copper cable theft cost the utility billions in repairs and lost revenue annually, with certain rural and peri-urban sections of track repeatedly stripped. The shift from overhead electric traction to diesel on affected lines — as a stopgap against cable theft — increased operating costs and reduced capacity. Maintenance backlogs accumulated as capital was diverted to the troubled locomotive programme. Track conditions deteriorated, forcing speed restrictions that reduced throughput.

### Freight on the road

The consequence of rail decline is visible on every national highway: South Africa has experienced a dramatic shift from rail to road freight. The Road Freight Association estimated that trucks now carry approximately 85% of all freight in South Africa by volume, compared to roughly 65% two decades ago. This shift imposes enormous costs. Road freight is approximately three to four times more expensive per tonne-kilometre than rail for bulk commodities. Heavy trucks destroy road surfaces at a rate exponentially greater than passenger vehicles — a single fully loaded B-double (interlink) truck does the equivalent pavement damage of approximately 10,000 cars. The South African National Roads Agency Limited (SANRAL) estimates that the cost of road damage from overloaded trucks runs into billions annually, creating a vicious cycle: as rail deteriorates, freight shifts to road; road damage increases; road maintenance budgets are overwhelmed; road conditions deteriorate; transport costs rise across the economy.

For exporters, the logistics crisis translates directly into lost competitiveness. If it costs a South African manufacturer R3,000 more per container to ship goods from Johannesburg to Durban port compared to what a Vietnamese manufacturer pays to reach Ho Chi Minh City port, that cost difference makes the South African product less competitive in every global market. The logistics cost is embedded in the price of every exported product — and **South Africa's logistics costs as a percentage of GDP are among the highest in the world, estimated at between 47% and 51% of transportable GDP** by Stellenbosch University's logistics cost studies, compared to 30% to 40% in peer economies.

### Port efficiency: a comparative disgrace

South Africa's ports tell the story in numbers. Durban, the busiest container port in sub-Saharan Africa, handles approximately 2.5 to 2.7 million twenty-foot equivalent units (TEUs) per year. Tanjung Pelepas in Malaysia, a port that opened in 1999 — seven years after South Africa's democratic transition — handles over 10 million TEUs per year. Colombo in Sri Lanka handles over 7 million TEUs. Both are located in countries with smaller economies than South Africa's. The difference is not geography or demand. It is operational efficiency, investment, and institutional management.

Ship turnaround time — the total time a vessel spends in port from arrival to departure — averaged approximately 90 to 110 hours at Durban during 2022 and 2023, compared to 24 to 30 hours at world-class ports. Crane productivity, measured in moves per hour, averaged between 18 and 22 at Durban versus 30 to 40 at efficient global ports. Equipment downtime, caused by ageing cranes and straddle carriers, inadequate maintenance, and periodic power outages affecting port infrastructure, further reduced throughput. Shipping lines responded by imposing port congestion surcharges on Durban — additional fees of US$200 to US$400 per container that exporters ultimately bear.

The Durban port entrance channel, which requires periodic dredging to maintain depth for modern container vessels, has experienced maintenance challenges that limit the size of vessels that can call. As the global shipping industry has moved toward ultra-large container ships (vessels carrying 20,000+ TEUs), Durban's infrastructure constraints mean it cannot accommodate these vessels, forcing cargo consolidation at transhipment hubs elsewhere — adding time and cost.

:::takeaway
South Africa's logistics crisis is not abstract — it shows up in the price of every exported product. When rail does not work, freight shifts to road. When ports are slow, shipping lines add surcharges. When both fail simultaneously, exporters route cargo through neighbouring countries. Each of these workarounds adds cost, and each added cost makes South African goods less competitive in global markets.
:::

The partial reform has begun. The Transnet National Ports Authority Act, passed in 2024, separated the port authority function (regulating and managing the port estate) from the port terminal operating function (running the cranes and handling cargo). This structural separation is intended to enable private terminal operators to bid for concessions, introducing competition into port operations. The Durban Pier 2 container terminal concession process — the first major port terminal concession in South African history — was initiated in 2023 and 2024, though the process has been slow. Private sector participation in rail, through third-party access to the Transnet network, has also advanced, with the National Rail Policy providing a framework for slot allocation on key corridors. Whether these reforms translate into actual efficiency gains depends entirely on the quality and speed of implementation — a recurring theme, as you will see.

## The Digital Divide: Connectivity as Infrastructure

When you think about infrastructure, roads, ports, and power stations come to mind first. But in the 21st-century economy, digital connectivity — broadband internet access, mobile data coverage, undersea cable capacity, and data centre infrastructure — is equally fundamental. South Africa has made significant progress on some dimensions of digital infrastructure while falling behind on others.

On the positive side, South Africa is the primary landing point for undersea fibre-optic cables connecting Africa to global networks. The West Africa Cable System (WACS), the South Atlantic 3 (SAT-3), the Eastern Africa Submarine System (EASSy), and newer cables like 2Africa and Equiano have massively expanded international bandwidth capacity. Data centre investment in Johannesburg and Cape Town has grown rapidly, with South Africa emerging as the largest data centre market on the African continent. Mobile network coverage by the major operators (MTN, Vodacom, Cell C, Telkom) reaches over 95% of the population for basic 3G services.

The problem is in the last mile — and in the affordability gap. Fixed broadband penetration in South Africa remains low by international standards. The Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA) and the World Bank's data show fixed broadband penetration at approximately 2 to 3 subscriptions per 100 people, compared to 15 to 20 in peer upper-middle-income countries like Brazil, Turkey, and Mexico. Mobile broadband is more widespread, but data prices, while declining since the Competition Commission's Data Services Market Inquiry in 2019, remain a significant barrier for low-income users. A gigabyte of mobile data costs a larger share of monthly income for a South African earning the minimum wage than for a user in most peer countries.

The geographic dimension is stark. Fibre-to-the-home and fibre-to-the-business networks, deployed by operators like Vumatel, Openserve, and MetroFibre, are concentrated in urban areas and wealthier suburbs. Township areas and rural communities rely overwhelmingly on mobile data, often on congested networks with limited backhaul capacity. This digital divide constrains economic participation. Services exports — business process outsourcing, software development, data analytics, creative industries — require reliable, high-speed connectivity. Fintech applications require stable data connections. E-commerce requires both connectivity and digital payment infrastructure. Remote work, which expanded globally during and after the COVID-19 pandemic, requires broadband that most South Africans outside the urban middle class do not have.

South Africa's National Broadband Plan (SA Connect), published in 2013, set ambitious targets: 100% broadband access by 2020, with minimum speeds of 5 Mbps for 50% of the population. These targets were missed comprehensively. The programme suffered from the same implementation failures as other national infrastructure plans — inadequate project preparation, coordination failures across government departments, and slow procurement. The allocation of high-demand radio frequency spectrum for mobile broadband, delayed for over a decade by litigation and regulatory disputes, was only completed in 2022 through ICASA's spectrum auction. While this auction unlocked significant new capacity for mobile operators, the years of delay represented a substantial opportunity cost.

:::expert[World Bank Digital Economy Assessment]
The World Bank's Digital Economy for Africa (DE4A) diagnostic framework assesses countries across five pillars: digital infrastructure, digital skills, digital platforms, digital financial services, and digital entrepreneurship. South Africa's 2022 assessment scored the country relatively well on digital financial services (driven by a sophisticated banking sector and growing fintech ecosystem) and digital infrastructure backbone (undersea cables, data centres) but identified significant gaps in digital skills, last-mile connectivity, and the enabling regulatory environment for digital platforms. The assessment emphasised that digital infrastructure investment must be paired with digital literacy programmes and affordability interventions to translate connectivity into broad-based economic participation.
:::

## The McKinsey Gap: Planning Versus Delivery

To understand why South Africa's infrastructure consistently disappoints, it helps to have a diagnostic framework. McKinsey Global Institute's research on infrastructure productivity — drawn from analysis of infrastructure delivery across dozens of countries — identifies five dimensions that distinguish countries that deliver infrastructure effectively from those that do not. Assessing South Africa against each dimension reveals where the system breaks down.

### Dimension 1: Strategic prioritisation

This is the question of whether a country has a coherent, evidence-based infrastructure plan that identifies the right projects, in the right sequence, based on economic impact analysis. South Africa performs reasonably well here. The National Infrastructure Plan (NIP), developed by the Presidential Infrastructure Coordinating Commission (PICC) and adopted by Cabinet in 2012, identified 18 Strategic Integrated Projects (SIPs) across energy, transport, water, social infrastructure, and regional development. The plan was informed by economic modelling and aligned to the National Development Plan's (NDP) growth objectives. The Infrastructure Development Act of 2014 provided a legal framework for fast-tracking strategic projects.

The planning documents are sophisticated. The problem is not that South Africa lacks a plan. It is that having a plan is the easiest part of infrastructure delivery, and South Africa has historically confused planning with doing.

### Dimension 2: Project preparation and bankability

This is where South Africa's infrastructure pipeline begins to break down. Project preparation — the detailed engineering, environmental, financial, and legal work required to take an infrastructure concept from idea to investment-ready — is expensive, time-consuming, and unglamorous. It typically costs between 5% and 10% of total project value and takes two to five years for complex projects. It requires specialised skills in transaction advisory, environmental impact assessment, engineering design, and financial structuring.

South Africa has a chronic shortage of project preparation capacity in the public sector. National Treasury's Government Technical Advisory Centre (GTAC) and the Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA) provide project preparation support, but their capacity is limited relative to the pipeline. The Infrastructure Fund, established in 2019 as a blended finance mechanism housed within the DBSA, was designed in part to address the project preparation gap — but its operationalisation has been slow, with fewer projects reaching financial close than targeted.

The consequence is a phenomenon familiar across the developing world: a large pipeline of planned projects but a thin pipeline of bankable projects — projects that are sufficiently prepared, with completed designs, environmental approvals, secure land tenure, and viable financial structures, for construction to actually begin. Announcements of R827 billion in planned infrastructure investment mean little if only a fraction of that pipeline is genuinely investment-ready.

### Dimension 3: Delivery and construction management

This is South Africa's weakest dimension. The state's capacity to manage large construction projects has deteriorated significantly. Mega-projects like Medupi, Kusile, the Gauteng Freeway Improvement Project (e-tolls), and the Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa's (PRASA) rolling stock procurement programme have all experienced severe cost overruns, schedule delays, and quality failures. The pattern is consistent: underestimation of project complexity, inadequate risk allocation in contracts, insufficient owner's engineer capacity within the procuring entity, contractor disputes, change orders that inflate costs, and political interference in procurement decisions.

The skills pipeline for infrastructure delivery has also weakened. The number of registered professional engineers in South Africa, as tracked by the Engineering Council of South Africa (ECSA), has grown more slowly than the economy's needs. Government departments and state-owned enterprises have struggled to recruit and retain engineering and project management talent at public-sector salary scales, particularly when the private sector and international markets offer significantly higher compensation. The result is that state entities often lack the in-house technical capacity to effectively oversee contractors — creating a principal-agent problem where the client (the state) cannot independently verify whether the contractor's work meets specifications.

### Dimension 4: Private sector participation and PPP frameworks

South Africa has a sophisticated legal and regulatory framework for public-private partnerships (PPPs). Treasury Regulation 16 under the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) provides a structured approval process for PPPs, with defined feasibility, procurement, and delivery phases. The PPP unit within National Treasury (now housed within GTAC) has developed detailed guidelines, standardised contracts, and approval protocols.

Yet the output has been modest. Since the PPP framework was established in the early 2000s, South Africa has concluded approximately 30 to 35 national and provincial PPP projects — a fraction of the number in comparable jurisdictions. The approval process is rigorous but slow, with some projects taking five to eight years from inception to financial close. Transaction advisory costs are high, deterring smaller projects. The framework was designed primarily for social infrastructure (hospitals, office buildings, prisons) and has been less effective at accommodating economic infrastructure (ports, rail, energy) where the risk profiles are different.

The REIPPPP, discussed above, technically operates outside the formal PPP framework — it uses a bespoke procurement process under the Department of Energy. Its success, ironically, reinforces the point: South Africa can deliver infrastructure effectively when it creates fit-for-purpose institutional arrangements with clear mandates, ring-fenced capacity, and transparent rules. The PPP framework itself is not the problem — the problem is that few projects survive the preparation and approval process to reach construction.

### Dimension 5: Regulatory certainty

Investors — both public and private — need regulatory certainty to commit capital to long-lived infrastructure assets. A power station operates for 30 to 50 years. A port concession runs for 20 to 25 years. A toll road has a lifespan measured in decades. Investors need confidence that the regulatory rules governing tariffs, access, environmental compliance, and contract enforcement will remain stable over these timeframes.

South Africa's regulatory environment sends mixed signals. On one hand, the legal system is robust, with an independent judiciary, well-developed commercial law, and functioning arbitration mechanisms. On the other hand, policy uncertainty in key sectors — the on-again, off-again approach to the Integrated Resource Plan for energy, the decade-long delay in spectrum allocation, the unresolved questions about rail access and port concession terms — has deterred investment. The e-toll debacle in Gauteng, where a completed infrastructure project became politically toxic and the tolling mechanism was effectively abandoned after years of public resistance and non-payment, sent a damaging signal about regulatory commitment. If government can walk away from a completed project's revenue model, investors ask, what guarantees do I have for my project?

:::takeaway
South Africa's infrastructure failure is not a money problem — it is an institutional delivery problem. The plans exist. The funding frameworks exist. The legal structures exist. What is missing is the organisational capacity to prepare projects properly, procure them cleanly, build them on time and on budget, and create the regulatory certainty that attracts long-term private capital.
:::

## The National Infrastructure Plan and the 18 SIPs

To give you a concrete picture of what was promised and what was delivered, it is worth examining the National Infrastructure Plan in detail. The Presidential Infrastructure Coordinating Commission (PICC), chaired by the President and including Cabinet ministers and premiers, adopted the plan in 2012. It identified 18 Strategic Integrated Projects (SIPs) designed to transform the economy and address spatial inequality. These were not small interventions — they were supposed to be the backbone of South Africa's development trajectory for the decade ahead.

SIP 1 (Unlocking the Northern Mineral Belt) aimed to develop rail, water, energy, and road infrastructure to unlock mineral deposits in Limpopo, including the Waterberg coalfields and Bushveld platinum resources. SIP 2 (Durban-Free State-Gauteng Logistics and Industrial Corridor) focused on strengthening the logistics spine connecting the country's economic heartland to its primary port. SIP 3 (South-Eastern Node and Corridor Development) targeted the Eastern Cape, aiming to improve infrastructure in one of the most underserved provinces. SIP 4 (Unlocking Economic Opportunities in the North West Province) focused on road, water, and rail infrastructure. SIP 5 (Saldanha-Northern Cape Development Corridor) aimed to support the iron ore export corridor and industrial development around Saldanha Bay.

SIPs 6 through 9 focused on water and sanitation, the Integrated Urban Space and Public Transport Programme (including bus rapid transit systems), green energy, and electricity generation and transmission respectively. SIP 10 addressed electricity transmission specifically. SIP 11 targeted agri-logistics and rural infrastructure. SIPs 12 through 15 addressed revitalisation of public hospitals and clinics, national school build, higher education infrastructure, and expanding access to communication technology. SIPs 16 through 18, added later, covered the Square Kilometre Array (SKA) radio telescope, regional integration (cross-border infrastructure), and water and sanitation infrastructure.

The scope was breathtaking. The delivery was not. By the early 2020s, assessment of SIP delivery by the Department of Public Works and Infrastructure, the PICC secretariat, and independent analysts revealed a consistent pattern: early-stage progress on planning and design, followed by stalling during procurement and construction. Many SIPs lacked dedicated project management offices. Coordination across government departments and between national and provincial government was weak. The Infrastructure Development Act's provisions for "fast-tracking" approvals were underutilised. Individual projects within SIPs frequently encountered land acquisition delays, environmental impact assessment bottlenecks, and funding sequencing problems where budget allocations did not align with construction schedules.

The Infrastructure Fund, announced in the 2019 Budget and operationalised through the DBSA, was conceived as a mechanism to bridge the gap between public capital budgets and infrastructure financing needs. The concept was sound: use public capital (catalytic funding from the fiscus) to leverage private investment through blended finance structures — combining concessional (below-market) public funding with commercial private capital to make projects viable that neither sector could finance alone. The target was R100 billion in blended finance over a decade. By 2023, the Infrastructure Fund had made progress on several projects, including student housing and social infrastructure, but the pace of project development and financial closure remained below the trajectory needed to meet the target.

## The PPP Paradox: Sophisticated Framework, Sparse Results

South Africa's PPP framework deserves particular attention because it illustrates a broader pattern: the gap between institutional design and institutional performance. On paper, South Africa's PPP framework is among the best in the developing world. National Treasury's PPP Manual, published in 2004 and updated subsequently, provides step-by-step guidance through three phases: inception (feasibility study, Treasury Approval I), procurement (competitive bidding, Treasury Approval IIA and IIB), and delivery (contract management, monitoring).

The framework requires a rigorous feasibility study that includes an affordability assessment (can the government entity afford the PPP payments over the contract term?), a value-for-money assessment (is the PPP option better value than traditional procurement?), and a risk transfer analysis (are risks allocated to the party best able to manage them?). This rigour is a strength — it prevents badly structured deals. But it is also a barrier, because the feasibility study requirements demand specialised transaction advisory skills that many government departments do not have in-house and must procure from external consultants at significant cost.

:::expert[National Treasury GTAC Analysis]
The Government Technical Advisory Centre's assessment of PPP pipeline performance identifies project preparation capacity as the binding constraint. GTAC's analysis shows that the average time from PPP project registration to financial close in South Africa is approximately five to seven years, compared to two to four years in jurisdictions with dedicated PPP units that provide hands-on project development support rather than primarily regulatory oversight. GTAC has recommended a shift toward a more active project development role, where the centre co-develops projects with implementing departments rather than simply reviewing submissions. This shift requires additional funding, staffing, and a mandate expansion that has been slow to materialise.
:::

The successful PPPs in South Africa's portfolio — the Inkosi Albert Luthuli Central Hospital in Durban, the Chapman's Peak toll road, the Gautrain rapid rail link — demonstrate that the model can work when project preparation is thorough, the procuring institution is competent, and political commitment is sustained over the multi-year development cycle. The Gautrain, in particular, shows what is possible: a complex rail infrastructure project delivered roughly on time (it opened for the 2010 FIFA World Cup, as targeted) and operating successfully, though it required substantial public-sector capital contribution beyond the PPP concessionaire's investment.

The lesson is not that PPPs are inherently flawed or that the framework needs radical overhaul. The lesson is that PPPs are resource-intensive to prepare and manage, and South Africa has not invested sufficiently in the institutional capacity required to develop a robust PPP pipeline. The framework is a necessary but insufficient condition for infrastructure delivery through private participation.

## Water: The Crisis Nobody Talks About

While energy and logistics dominate the infrastructure headlines, South Africa faces a water infrastructure crisis that may ultimately prove more consequential. The country is classified as water-stressed by the United Nations — annual freshwater resources per capita are approximately 1,000 cubic metres, well below the global average and close to the threshold for water scarcity. The National Water and Sanitation Master Plan, published by the Department of Water and Sanitation in 2018, projected a 17% gap between water supply and demand by 2030 under a business-as-usual scenario.

The supply-side challenges are compounded by infrastructure failure. **Municipal water losses — the difference between water produced at treatment works and water billed to consumers — average approximately 41% nationally**, with some municipalities recording non-revenue water percentages above 60%. These losses result from ageing and poorly maintained pipe networks, illegal connections, meter failures, and billing system breakdowns. The cost of water lost through leaks alone has been estimated by the Water Research Commission at over R9 billion annually.

Bulk water infrastructure — the large dams, inter-basin transfer schemes, and treatment works that supply the system — also faces maintenance and investment backlogs. The Lesotho Highlands Water Project Phase II (LHWP II), which will augment supply to the Vaal River system serving Gauteng and surrounding provinces, has experienced repeated delays. The Vaal River system, which supplies approximately 60% of South Africa's economic output, is operating with declining assurance of supply. The Department of Water and Sanitation, which is responsible for bulk water resource management and dam safety, has itself experienced institutional difficulties — qualified audit opinions, irregular expenditure findings, and capacity constraints in its regional offices.

For the growth strategy, water infrastructure is a prerequisite that intersects with every other sector. Mining requires process water. Agriculture requires irrigation. Manufacturing requires reliable supply. Municipalities require bulk supply for residential and commercial users. Energy generation — including cooling for thermal power stations — requires water. The interdependence means that a water supply failure cascades across the economy.

## Where South Africa Actually Stands: A Comparative View

Comparing South Africa's infrastructure performance with peer countries sharpens the diagnosis. The World Economic Forum's Global Competitiveness Index — before it was restructured — ranked South Africa's overall infrastructure quality in a declining trajectory: from the 40s (out of approximately 140 countries) in the early 2010s to the 60s by the late 2010s. The World Bank's Logistics Performance Index (LPI) tells a similar story: South Africa's overall LPI score declined from 3.46 in 2016 to 3.38 in 2023 (on a 1-to-5 scale), with the infrastructure sub-component showing particular weakness.

Among upper-middle-income countries, South Africa's infrastructure investment as a percentage of GDP has been relatively low. Gross fixed capital formation — the broadest measure of investment in physical assets — has hovered around 18% to 20% of GDP, compared to 25% to 30% in East Asian economies during their high-growth phases. Public infrastructure investment specifically has averaged around 5% to 6% of GDP, below the 7% to 8% that development economists typically recommend for countries at South Africa's income level and infrastructure deficit.

The comparison is not uniformly negative. South Africa's road network is extensive — approximately 750,000 kilometres, of which approximately 22,000 kilometres are national roads managed by SANRAL. The national road network is generally well-maintained compared to the rest of the continent, though provincial and municipal road quality varies enormously. The financial sector infrastructure — payment systems, stock exchange technology, banking platforms — is world-class. Telecommunications backbone infrastructure, as discussed above, is strong. The picture is one of islands of excellence in a sea of mediocrity and decline — a pattern that reflects the broader institutional landscape of the South African state.

:::takeaway
South Africa's infrastructure challenge is not uniform across all sectors. The country has genuine strengths in financial infrastructure, telecommunications backbone, and parts of the national road network. The acute crises are concentrated in energy generation and transmission, rail freight, port operations, water supply and distribution, and last-mile digital connectivity. Prioritisation must focus on these binding constraints rather than trying to fix everything simultaneously.
:::

## Toward the Execution Machine

Everything you have read in this article points in one direction: South Africa does not lack infrastructure plans, legal frameworks, or even — in aggregate — financial resources. What it lacks is the institutional machinery to turn plans into projects, projects into construction sites, and construction sites into working infrastructure. This machinery — what [The Execution Machine](/articles/eg-3-2-the-execution-machine) will examine in detail — involves project preparation units, construction management capacity, procurement integrity, inter-governmental coordination, and the political will to sustain investment decisions across electoral cycles.

The path forward is not mysterious. The REIPPPP has shown that South Africa can deliver infrastructure at scale when it creates fit-for-purpose institutions with clear mandates, competitive procurement, and ring-fenced capacity. The Gautrain has shown that complex PPPs can work with adequate preparation and political commitment. The question is whether these successes can be generalised — whether the exceptional can become the normal.

For a deeper analysis of how South Africa's infrastructure plans compare with actual delivery outcomes, [Infrastructure Planning vs Reality](/articles/eg-4-3-infrastructure-planning-vs-reality) will examine the gap in granular detail. And as you saw in [Following the Money](/articles/2-1-following-the-money), the budget allocation process itself shapes which projects get funded and at what pace — understanding how infrastructure money flows through the intergovernmental fiscal system is essential to understanding why projects stall.

## Practical Framework: Infrastructure Report Card

The following framework provides a structured way to assess South Africa's infrastructure performance across six critical sectors. For each sector, four dimensions are evaluated: the gap between committed and actual spending, the rate at which planned projects are actually completed, how South Africa compares to an appropriate international benchmark, and the single most important constraint preventing improvement.

**Energy Generation and Transmission**

Committed versus actual spend: National Treasury's budget allocations to Eskom's capital programme and the REIPPPP have been substantial — over R150 billion in cumulative public capital transfers to Eskom between 2015 and 2023, plus R210 billion in private investment through the REIPPPP. However, Eskom's own capital expenditure efficiency (the ratio of productive capital spending to total capital budget) has been low, with significant portions absorbed by cost overruns on Medupi and Kusile rather than new capacity expansion.

Project completion rate: The REIPPPP has a strong completion rate — over 85% of projects awarded in Bid Windows 1 through 3 reached commercial operation, though with delays in some cases due to grid connection backlogs. Eskom's own build programme has a poor completion record, with Medupi and Kusile both years late and over budget. Transmission projects identified in the Transmission Development Plan have a completion rate estimated at below 50% of the planned programme.

Comparator benchmark: Vietnam added approximately 20,000 MW of solar capacity in a single year (2020), demonstrating what rapid renewable deployment looks like when grid access and regulatory approvals are streamlined. India's renewable energy programme has added over 60,000 MW of solar and wind capacity since 2015. South Africa's REIPPPP, while successful per project, has operated at a pace limited by bid window frequency and grid constraints.

Binding constraint: Transmission grid capacity and grid connection timelines are the primary binding constraint for energy infrastructure. Without accelerated transmission investment, new generation capacity — whether from IPPs or Eskom — cannot reach consumers.

**Ports**

Committed versus actual spend: Transnet's capital investment programme for ports has allocated approximately R30 billion to R40 billion over the past decade for port infrastructure upgrades, including the Durban dig-out port (a proposed new container terminal on the former airport site). Actual construction progress has been limited, with the dig-out port remaining in planning and feasibility stages for years.

Project completion rate: Equipment replacement programmes (new ship-to-shore cranes, straddle carriers) have progressed, though with procurement delays. Major capacity expansion projects have largely stalled.

Comparator benchmark: Tanjung Pelepas (Malaysia) handles four times Durban's container volume with significantly higher crane productivity and lower ship turnaround times. Mombasa (Kenya), after implementing a single-window clearance system and investing in port infrastructure, improved its ship turnaround time by approximately 50% between 2015 and 2020.

Binding constraint: The structural separation of the port authority from terminal operations (enabling private concessions) is the prerequisite reform. Until private operators can compete for terminal concessions and invest in equipment and technology, port efficiency gains will remain incremental rather than transformational.

**Rail Freight**

Committed versus actual spend: Transnet Freight Rail's capital programme has been significantly distorted by the locomotive procurement irregularities documented by the Zondo Commission. Of the approximately R50 billion spent on the 1,064-locomotive programme, a substantial portion was absorbed by inflated costs rather than productive capacity. Maintenance capital expenditure has been insufficient to prevent track deterioration on the general freight network.

Project completion rate: The dedicated iron ore export line (Sishen-Saldanha) and coal export line (Richards Bay Coal Terminal corridor) have maintained relatively higher performance levels than the general freight network, though both have experienced volume declines from their peaks. General freight network renewal projects have a poor completion record.

Comparator benchmark: India's Dedicated Freight Corridor programme — constructing two new 1,500-kilometre freight-only rail lines — provides a model for separating freight and passenger rail to improve throughput. Indonesia's rail investment under President Jokowi's infrastructure programme demonstrates that middle-income countries can scale rail investment when institutional arrangements are effective.

Binding constraint: Third-party access to the rail network — enabling private operators to run trains on Transnet's tracks — is the structural reform most likely to reverse the decline in rail freight volumes. The National Rail Policy provides the framework, but implementation of the slot allocation system and access pricing regime will determine whether private operators actually enter the market.

**Roads**

Committed versus actual spend: SANRAL's budget for national road maintenance and construction has been relatively stable, though the collapse of e-toll revenue in Gauteng created a significant funding gap. Provincial and municipal road budgets vary enormously, with many municipalities spending less than 50% of their roads maintenance budgets due to capacity constraints.

Project completion rate: National road projects managed by SANRAL have a reasonable completion record by South African standards. Provincial and municipal road maintenance programmes have significantly lower execution rates.

Comparator benchmark: South Africa's national road network quality compares favourably to most African peers but unfavourably to Latin American upper-middle-income countries like Chile and Mexico, which have invested more heavily in toll road concessions and maintenance regimes.

Binding constraint: The funding model for national roads — particularly the unresolved e-toll situation and the broader question of user-pays versus fiscus-funded models — is the primary constraint. Until funding certainty is restored, SANRAL's ability to plan and execute multi-year investment programmes is compromised.

**Water**

Committed versus actual spend: The Department of Water and Sanitation's capital budget has been consistently underspent, with actual expenditure falling short of budget allocations by 20% to 40% in multiple financial years, according to National Treasury expenditure reports. The gap reflects both procurement delays and institutional capacity constraints within the department.

Project completion rate: Major projects like the Lesotho Highlands Water Project Phase II and the Mokolo-Crocodile Water Augmentation Project have experienced multi-year delays. Municipal water infrastructure projects funded through the Municipal Infrastructure Grant have a mixed completion record, with the Department of Water and Sanitation reporting that many projects exceed their planned timelines by 50% or more.

Comparator benchmark: Israel, operating in a more water-scarce environment than South Africa, has achieved near-total water reuse, desalination capacity meeting approximately 80% of domestic water needs, and non-revenue water losses below 10%. While South Africa's context is different, Israel demonstrates what is achievable with sustained institutional commitment to water infrastructure.

Binding constraint: Municipal institutional capacity is the binding constraint for water distribution infrastructure. Even if bulk supply projects are completed, the last mile — getting water reliably from the treatment works to the consumer — depends on municipal pipe networks, maintenance capacity, and revenue management systems that are failing in many municipalities.

**Digital Connectivity**

Committed versus actual spend: SA Connect, the national broadband plan, has dramatically underperformed its budget and target projections. Of the planned broadband connections to government facilities and underserved areas, actual delivery has been a fraction of the target. Private sector investment in fibre and mobile networks has been more substantial, but concentrated in commercially viable areas.

Project completion rate: Private fibre network deployment has progressed well in urban areas, with fibre-to-the-home coverage expanding rapidly in Johannesburg, Cape Town, and secondary cities. Public-sector broadband projects have a very low completion rate.

Comparator benchmark: Rwanda's national fibre backbone and government broadband programme, achieved in a country with far fewer resources than South Africa, demonstrates that political commitment and institutional focus can deliver digital infrastructure rapidly. India's BharatNet programme, despite its own delays, has connected over 200,000 village councils with fibre.

Binding constraint: Last-mile connectivity to township and rural areas is the binding constraint. The commercial investment case for fibre in low-income areas is weak without public subsidy or innovative delivery models. Mobile data affordability, while improving, remains a barrier to full digital participation.

:::framework
**The "Infrastructure Accountability" Framework**

When government announces an infrastructure project, promises billions in investment, or claims progress on infrastructure delivery, use this five-step process to assess whether the announcement reflects reality or political theatre.

**Step 1: Distinguish between planned, committed, and delivered.**

Government infrastructure announcements typically conflate three very different things. "Planned" means the project appears in a strategy document or NIP list — this costs nothing and commits no one. "Committed" means a budget allocation has been made and procurement has begun — this is more serious but still no guarantee. "Delivered" means the infrastructure is built, operational, and serving users. When you see a headline about "R100 billion in infrastructure investment," ask: is this planned, committed, or delivered? The gap between these categories is where South Africa's infrastructure failure lives.

**Step 2: Check the project preparation status.**

A project that has not completed detailed engineering design, environmental impact assessment, land acquisition, and financial structuring is not ready for construction — no matter how many times it appears in a minister's speech. Ask whether the project has reached "bankability" — the point at which a financier would lend against it and a contractor would bid on it. The Development Bank of Southern Africa's Infrastructure Fund and National Treasury's GTAC track project preparation status. If a project has been announced but not prepared, add three to five years to any promised delivery date.

**Step 3: Apply the McKinsey delivery test.**

For any major infrastructure project, assess five dimensions. Does it have a dedicated project management office with qualified staff? Has risk been properly allocated between the public and private sectors in the contract? Does the procuring entity have in-house technical capacity to oversee the contractor (an "owner's engineer")? Is there a transparent procurement process with limited discretion? And is there regulatory certainty that the project's revenue model will be honoured over its lifespan? If the answer to any of these is no, expect cost overruns and delays. Medupi, Kusile, and PRASA's rolling stock programme all failed on multiple dimensions.

**Step 4: Compare against an international benchmark.**

For each infrastructure sector, identify a comparator country's performance. Energy: Vietnam added 20,000 MW of solar in a single year. Ports: Tanjung Pelepas handles four times Durban's volume with lower turnaround times. Rail: India's Dedicated Freight Corridor programme shows what middle-income rail investment looks like. Water: Israel achieves non-revenue water losses below 10% compared to South Africa's 41%. Digital: Rwanda built a national fibre backbone with far fewer resources. The comparator tells you what is achievable and exposes the gap between South Africa's performance and what competent delivery looks like.

**Step 5: Identify the binding constraint and the accountable institution.**

For each infrastructure sector, there is one constraint that, if removed, would unlock the most progress. For energy: transmission grid capacity and connection timelines. For ports: structural separation enabling private terminal concessions. For rail: third-party access to Transnet's network. For water: municipal institutional capacity. For digital: last-mile connectivity to townships and rural areas. Identify the constraint, identify the institution responsible for removing it, and track whether that institution is making measurable progress. If the binding constraint has not changed in three years, the institution responsible is failing regardless of what its annual report claims.

This framework turns infrastructure announcements from political events into accountability exercises. The plans exist. The legal frameworks exist. The money has often been allocated. What matters is whether projects move from announcement to delivery — and these five steps help you track that journey.
:::
