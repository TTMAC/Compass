## The Second Crisis Hides Behind the First

The previous article measured the visible crisis: millions of young South Africans stranded after school with no structured path into work. This article is about a second crisis that hides behind the first, less measurable but no less real. It is the absence, in a deeply divided society, of any shared experience that turns young residents of the same country into citizens of one nation — and the related absence of the structure, routine, and sense of common purpose that such an experience can build.

Call it the cohesion and discipline deficit. The two words sit together for a reason. A young South African today can grow up, attend school, socialise, worship, and enter adulthood almost entirely within a single race, class, and language group, never once sharing a sustained, structured experience with peers from the other South Africas that exist alongside their own. And a young person who leaves school with no job and no institution to enter loses not only an income but the daily architecture of purpose — the routine, the responsibility, the membership of something larger than themselves — that work or study would have provided. The first deprivation fragments the nation horizontally, across its divides. The second hollows out the individual, vertically, leaving capable young people without a scaffold for their days.

This article makes two arguments. The first is that these deficits are real, structural, and consequential for nation-building — not soft concerns to be addressed once the "real" economic problems are solved, but part of the same problem. The second is that South Africa has tried, repeatedly, to address pieces of this through youth programmes, and that those programmes — whatever their individual merits — have not closed either gap. Understanding precisely why they fell short is the most useful thing this article can do, because every failure mode it identifies is a design requirement for the proposal that follows in Part 2. A serious solution is not built by ignoring what came before. It is built by studying it honestly.

## A Nation That Has Never Been in the Same Room

Begin with cohesion, and begin with geography, because in South Africa geography is destiny in a way few other societies experience so sharply. Apartheid was, at its core, a project of separation — separating people by race into different places, different schools, different economic roles, and different futures, and enforcing that separation with the full machinery of the state. Democracy dismantled the laws. It did not, and could not quickly, dismantle the physical landscape those laws had built. The townships are still where they were placed. The former homelands still hold millions. The suburbs the laws reserved for one group are still, in large part, occupied along lines that track the old map, now sorted by wealth rather than by statute.

The consequence for young people is that the South Africa they grow up in is, for most, monochrome in its daily texture. A child in a township school is surrounded almost entirely by children of the same background. A child in a former Model C or private school may sit in a more mixed classroom but returns each afternoon to a neighbourhood sorted by income, which in South Africa still correlates heavily with race. The languages people speak, the schools they attend, the suburbs they can afford, the social networks they inherit — all of these continue to run along channels carved before democracy, and they keep young South Africans apart not by law but by the sheer inertia of the built environment and the economy.

This is not a sentimental complaint about people not mixing. It has measurable consequences for the thing political scientists call social cohesion — the web of trust, shared identity, and mutual obligation that lets a diverse society function as one. The Institute for Justice and Reconciliation has, for years, tracked these attitudes through its South African Reconciliation Barometer, one of the longest-running surveys of its kind on the continent. Its findings, read across editions, describe a society where formal commitment to a shared national identity coexists with low levels of interracial contact and fragile interpersonal trust across group lines. People can affirm "South Africa belongs to all who live in it" in the abstract while rarely, in practice, sharing sustained time, effort, or risk with someone from a different background. Cohesion affirmed in principle is not the same as cohesion lived in daily experience, and it is the lived version that holds a society together when it is tested.

There is a generational sharpness to this. The young people now leaving school are the "born-free" generation — born into or after 1994, with no personal memory of legal apartheid. They were promised, in effect, a country already reconciled, a non-racial democracy whose hardest work was done. What many of them have instead inherited is the constitutional promise alongside the unreformed geography: the freedom to live anywhere paired with the economics that keep most where the old map placed them. That gap between a generation's expectations and its lived reality is its own kind of risk. A young person raised on the language of a united nation, who then experiences a daily life as segregated in practice as their parents', is not unreasonable to feel the promise was thinner than advertised — and disappointment of that kind, at scale, is the raw material that division and scapegoating feed on.

It is worth being concrete about why low cohesion is not merely a moral disappointment but a practical liability. Trust is economic infrastructure. Societies where people extend trust across group lines transact more cheaply, cooperate more easily, and resolve disputes with less friction and less recourse to force; societies low in such trust pay a constant, invisible tax in suspicion, in contracts that must be policed, in cooperation that does not happen. A country trying to build — to invest, to reform institutions, to ask citizens to sacrifice now for a shared future — runs on exactly the reservoir of mutual trust that South Africa's divisions keep depleted. Cohesion, in other words, is not the reward a society gives itself once the economy is fixed. It is one of the conditions under which fixing the economy becomes possible at all.

Now connect this to youth specifically. A shared national identity is not transmitted by genetics or proclaimed into existence by a constitution, however admirable. It is built through shared experience — moments, ideally early in life, when people from different backgrounds are placed in the same room, given a common task, made to depend on one another, and emerge having learned in their bodies rather than from a textbook that the other South Africa is made of people much like themselves. Schooling could in principle provide this, but a schooling system sorted by geography and income mostly does not. Sport and culture provide flashes of it. National symbols and sporting triumphs provide occasional, powerful surges of common feeling — but a surge is not a structure. What South Africa lacks is an institution that reliably, for every young person, manufactures the sustained, cross-cutting, shared experience out of which a lived national identity is actually made. The absence of that institution is the cohesion deficit.

It is worth pausing on the word "manufactures," because it points to something important: cohesion at national scale is almost never an accident. Societies that achieved a strong sense of common identity across internal differences generally did so through deliberate institutions that mixed their young people and gave them a shared formative experience — universal schooling designed to build a common civic culture, large public undertakings that put people from different regions and backgrounds to work on a common task, and, in a number of countries, a period of national service through which an entire generation passed together. The specific mechanisms differed, but the principle was constant: a diverse society that wants its members to feel like one people builds an institution that deliberately gives them a shared experience, rather than hoping cohesion will emerge on its own. It rarely does on its own. Left to itself, a society sorts — by income, by language, by neighbourhood, by school — and the sorting hardens with each generation. South Africa, having inherited the most deliberate sorting machinery any modern state ever built, cannot expect cohesion to arise spontaneously from the wreckage of that machinery. If it wants a generation that has shared something across the divides, it will have to build the thing that makes the sharing happen. The later Parts of this series are about what that thing could be; the point here is only that the deficit is real, and that closing it requires construction, not hope.

One further distinction matters, because it separates a real cohesion-builder from a cosmetic one. Decades of research on what actually reduces prejudice between groups point to the same conclusion: simply placing different people in the same space does not, on its own, build trust — and can even harden suspicion if the contact is brief, unequal, or competitive. What works is contact of a particular kind: sustained cooperation toward a shared goal, on terms of roughly equal standing, where people genuinely need one another to succeed. A young person who merely passes someone from another background in a corridor learns nothing. A young person who spends months working alongside that person toward a common task they cannot accomplish alone — depending on them, being depended on, succeeding or failing together — learns, at the level of lived experience, that the categories they inherited do not predict who is competent, trustworthy, or worth respecting. This is why a shared *experience* of effort is so much more powerful than a shared *space*. It is also a demanding design requirement, and one most of South Africa's existing efforts do not meet: a matching platform or a brief stipended placement does not put young people from different worlds into sustained, equal-status, cooperative dependence on one another. An institution built deliberately to do exactly that — to make a mixed cohort of young people need each other to get through a common, demanding experience — is the kind of thing that builds cohesion rather than merely gesturing at it. Hold that requirement in mind; it is one of the tests the next Part must pass.

## The Word "Discipline" Needs Handling With Care

The second half of the deficit is harder to discuss in South Africa, because the word "discipline" carries a heavy and painful history here. For generations, "discipline" was the language of control — of an authoritarian state imposing order on people it had denied rights, of corporal punishment in schools, of a militarised society policing its own divisions. Any proposal that speaks of instilling discipline in young people has to acknowledge that history honestly, or it will rightly be heard as a call to return to control dressed up as character-building.

So let us be precise about what is meant, and what is not. The discipline deficit this article names is not about obedience, deference, or the breaking of young people's wills. It is about something the previous article touched on: the loss of the daily architecture of purpose. Work and study, for all their difficulties, give a young person a structure — a reason to rise at a particular hour, a set of responsibilities owed to others, a sequence of small obligations met and goals achieved, a membership in a collective enterprise. That architecture is not a luxury. It is one of the main ways human beings build the habits of reliability, self-direction, and follow-through that the rest of life depends on. A young person who spends years with no job, no study, and no structured role is not being given freedom; they are being denied the scaffold on which adult capability is normally built.

Consider two young people of identical ability who leave the same school in the same year. One finds a place — a job, a learnership, a college seat — and so acquires, without anyone calling it that, a daily discipline: an alarm that matters, a supervisor whose expectations must be met, colleagues who depend on them, a stream of small responsibilities that accumulate into competence and into a sense of being needed. The other finds nothing, and so acquires the opposite: days without shape, obligations owed to no one, a slow uncoupling from the rhythms of productive life. After two years, these two young people will differ markedly in their reliability, confidence, and employability — and an observer meeting them for the first time might attribute the difference to character. It is not character. It is environment. The first young person was placed inside a structure that built the habits; the second was left outside one. The habits we call discipline are overwhelmingly the residue of having been given a structured role, not a virtue some young people are born with and others lack. This is why the deficit is a design problem and not a moral one: provide the structure, and the habits follow; withhold it, and no amount of exhortation conjures them. A national service is, among other things, a deliberate machine for providing that structure to every young person rather than only to the lucky ones the market happens to place.

Understood this way, "discipline" is closer to its root meaning — a disciple is a learner — than to its authoritarian distortion. It is the difference between a person who has learned, through structured experience, to organise their time, work with others, take responsibility, and see a task through, and a person who, through no fault of their own, has never been given the structured experience in which those habits form. The deficit is not a moral failing in young people. It is the predictable result of leaving millions of them without any structured role at the precise age when such habits take root. And it compounds with the cohesion deficit, because the same missing institution — a shared, structured, purposeful experience — would address both at once.

This is the careful but firm claim of this article: South Africa's young people are short of two things that an institution could provide and that the market and the current state, left as they are, will not. They are short of shared experience across the country's divides, and they are short of the structured, purposeful membership in which adult capability and civic habit are built. Name these honestly, without paternalism and without nostalgia for control, and they describe a real gap that a well-designed national service is shaped to fill.

## The Honest Audit: What South Africa Has Already Tried

It would be both arrogant and inaccurate to present these problems as if no one had ever tried to address them. South Africa has, in fact, built a substantial and crowded landscape of youth programmes over three decades of democracy. Any proposal for something new has an obligation to study them — not to dismiss them, but to learn precisely why, despite real effort and real spending, they have not closed the cohesion, discipline, or opportunity gaps. What follows is an honest audit. Each of these programmes was created by people trying to solve a real problem, and several have done genuine good. The point is not to disparage them; it is to extract, from their shortfalls, the design requirements for something that would finally work.

**The National Youth Service.** In various forms since the 2000s, the state has run national youth service initiatives intended to engage young people in community service combined with skills development. The idea has been revisited and relaunched more than once. Its persistence is telling — the instinct that structured service could build both skills and citizenship is a sound one, and clearly recurring. But the successive iterations remained relatively small, fragmented across departments and programmes, and never became the universal, expected, prestigious rite the instinct implied. The good idea kept being tried at a scale and with a status that could not match the size of the problem.

**The National Youth Development Agency.** Established in 2009 through the merger of the Umsobomvu Youth Fund and the National Youth Commission, the NYDA is the state's dedicated youth-development body, offering grants, training, business support, and information. It is a real institution doing real work. But its reach, relative to the millions of young people who need support, is modest, and like much of the landscape it operates as one opt-in service among many — valuable to the young person who finds and qualifies for it, invisible to the many who do not.

**The National Rural Youth Service Corps (NARYSEC).** Launched around 2010 through the department responsible for rural development, NARYSEC recruited young people from rural areas for training and community-development work, explicitly blending skills, service, and a measure of structured discipline. It is, in many ways, the closest existing relative of the model this series will propose — which makes its limits instructive. It remained relatively small, rural-focused, and contained within a single department's mandate and budget, rather than becoming a national institution that every young person passed through. A promising template, confined to a pilot's scale for a structural problem.

**The Expanded Public Works Programme and the Community Work Programme.** The EPWP, running since 2004, and the CWP have provided large numbers of short-term, stipended work opportunities, and they reach real scale. But they were designed primarily as poverty relief and short-term work provision, not as a bridge into careers or a builder of cohesion. A stipended stint clearing alien vegetation or maintaining a community facility provides income and dignity in the moment, and that matters. But it is not structured to leave the participant with a durable credential, a network into the formal economy, or a transformative shared national experience. The design goal was relief, and relief is what it delivers.

**The Presidential Youth Employment Intervention and SA Youth.** Launched in 2020, the PYEI represents the most ambitious recent attempt to act at scale. Its SA Youth network and mobile platform, reachable without data costs, has registered millions of young people and connects them to opportunities, and its Basic Education Employment Initiative has placed very large numbers of young people as assistants in schools. This is genuine scale and genuine innovation, and it deserves credit. But notice its shape: it is principally a matching and placement engine — a way to connect young people to opportunities that exist — plus a large, valuable, but time-limited public-employment component. It is not a universal, sustained, cross-cutting institution that every young person enters and emerges from changed. It does important work within the existing landscape rather than replacing the missing floor beneath it.

## Why the Landscape Has Not Closed the Gap

Lay these programmes side by side and the pattern in their shortfalls is consistent. The failures are not mainly failures of intention or even of competence. They are failures of *design*, repeated across programme after programme, and each one names a requirement for whatever comes next.

**Fragmentation.** The programmes are scattered across many departments — basic education, higher education, rural development, public works, the Presidency, the NYDA — each with its own mandate, budget, eligibility rules, and data system. From the young person's side this is a maze, and as the previous article argued, the burden of navigating a maze falls hardest on those least equipped to navigate it. From the state's side it means duplicated administration, no single coherent journey, and no institution that owns the whole transition from school to adulthood.

**No universality.** Every programme is opt-in, reaching some young people and missing others. None is the default destination that everyone passes through. This guarantees two things: that the programmes will tend to reach the already-connected, and that they can never, by construction, build a *shared national experience*, because they are never shared by everyone.

**Stipend as relief, not pathway.** Several programmes are structured around a short-term stipend for short-term work. This provides immediate relief — which is genuinely valuable — but it is not designed to leave the participant with a durable credential, a network, or a transformed trajectory. When the stipend ends, the young person often returns to exactly where they started, now slightly older.

**No prestige.** This is the quietest failure and perhaps the most important. Because these programmes are framed, implicitly, as support for those with no better option, completing one carries no status. It is not something a young person competes to enter or is proud to have done. And without prestige, a programme can never become an aspirational rite that pulls in the whole cohort, including those who do have other options — which is exactly what building cohesion requires. The next Part of this series treats prestige not as a nice-to-have but as a core design requirement, precisely because its absence here has been so costly.

**Weak measurement.** Across the landscape, the evidence base on what actually works — who is reached, what they gain, where they are years later — is thinner than it should be. The Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation and the Auditor-General have, in various assessments, pointed to the difficulty of tracking outcomes across this fragmented field. A system that cannot measure its own results cannot reliably improve them, and cannot make the honest case for its own expansion.

**No transformation.** Underlying all of these is a deeper limitation: most of the programmes are designed to *help* young people where they are, not to *change* where they are. A matching platform connects a young person to an opportunity but does not alter the person's skills, networks, or sense of themselves. A short stipended placement provides income but returns the participant, when it ends, to roughly the trajectory they were already on. None of this is worthless — help is not nothing — but it is a different ambition from transformation. The cohesion and discipline deficits cannot be matched or stipended away; they can only be closed by an experience substantial enough to leave a young person genuinely different at the end of it: more skilled, more connected, more confident, and bound to compatriots they would never otherwise have met. An intervention measured in weeks and framed as relief cannot do that. An institution measured in a year of a young person's life, and designed for transformation rather than relief, might. The gap between those two ambitions is the gap this series is about.

Read this list again and notice something: it is not an argument that youth programmes are futile. It is an argument that the *shape* the country has given them — fragmented, opt-in, relief-oriented, low-status, weakly measured — is the wrong shape for the problem. Change the shape, and the same underlying instinct that produced all these efforts could finally deliver on its promise.

Notice, too, what this audit is *not*. It is not a charge against any single administration, minister, or party. These programmes were built and run across many years, many departments, and successive governments; their shared shortcomings are structural, baked into how the state approaches youth provision, not the personal failure of whoever happened to hold a particular office. That is precisely why swapping the people in charge has never fixed it, and why the next election's youth-policy promises, whoever makes them, should be read with suspicion if they leave the underlying shape untouched. A structural problem that has survived multiple governments will survive the next one too, unless the structure itself is redesigned. The remainder of this series is an attempt to specify that redesign — and to subject it, honestly, to the same six tests it asks of everything that came before.

## A Tool: Read Any Youth Programme Against the Six Tests

The most useful thing you can take from this article is a way to evaluate any youth intervention — including the one this series will propose, and including whatever a political party offers at the next election. Hold any programme up against six tests, each drawn directly from a failure mode above:

1. **Is it universal, or opt-in?** Does every young person pass through it by default, or must they find and qualify for it? Only a universal design can reach the disconnected and build a shared experience.
2. **Is it coherent, or fragmented?** Is it one institution owning the whole school-to-adulthood transition, or another separate scheme added to a crowded maze?
3. **Is it a pathway, or just relief?** Does completing it leave the participant with a durable credential, a network, and a changed trajectory — or only a temporary stipend?
4. **Is it prestigious, or low-status?** Is it something young people are proud to have done and others aspire to — or support framed for those with no better option?
5. **Is it measured, or unaccountable?** Does it track who it reaches and where they are years later, and publish the results — or does it run on good intentions and unverified claims?
6. **Does it transform, or merely help?** Is it substantial enough to leave a young person genuinely changed — more skilled, more connected, more confident, bound to compatriots they would never otherwise have met — or does it assist them where they are and return them to the same trajectory?

Run South Africa's existing programmes through these six tests and you can see, item by item, why the landscape has not closed the cohesion, discipline, or opportunity gaps: most fail on universality, prestige, and transformation, and the field as a whole fails on coherence and measurement. Run a serious new proposal through the same six tests and you have an immediate, honest way to judge whether it has learned from what came before — or is simply the next well-intentioned scheme destined to join the maze. Keep these six in your pocket; the next Part of this series should be read with them in hand, and so should every youth-policy promise you hear between now and the next election. A proposal that passes all six is rare, and worth taking seriously; one that quietly fails three or four of them, however generous it sounds, is the old maze wearing a new name.

The next Part of this series takes up exactly that challenge. It proposes an institution designed, from the first principle, to pass all five tests: universal, coherent, a genuine pathway, deliberately prestigious, and built to be measured. Whether it succeeds is for you to judge — and now you have the tests to judge it by.
