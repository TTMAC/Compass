## The Bottom Line, Up Front

Unemployment is the visible crisis; the quieter one is the absence of a shared, cross-cutting civic experience that turns young residents into citizens of one nation. Past programmes touched the edges of both and scaled poorly.

## Outline

- **BLUF / hook** — Open on the everyday reality that most young South Africans grow up, school, and socialise within a single race, class and language group — and never share a structured national experience.
- **Defining the cohesion deficit** — Spatial apartheid legacy, segregated schooling, trust deficits. *Sources: SA Reconciliation Barometer (IJR), Stats SA, social-cohesion literature.* Keep strictly non-partisan — describe structural patterns, not party blame.
- **The discipline / civic-order dimension** — Frame carefully and non-paternalistically: routine, purpose, teamwork, public-mindedness — what a shared service ritual builds, distinct from authoritarian "discipline."
- **Audit of existing programmes (the honest part)** — What each was meant to do and why it underdelivered at scale:
  - National Youth Service (NYS) and the NYDA
  - NARYSEC (rural development national youth service)
  - Expanded Public Works Programme (EPWP) and the Community Work Programme
  - Presidential Youth Employment Intervention / SA Youth (sayouth.mobi), Basic Education Employment Initiative
  *Sources: DPME evaluations, AGSA, departmental annual reports — figures to verify on full draft.*
- **Why they fell short** — Fragmentation across departments, no prestige, weak benefits, stipend-as-relief rather than pathway, poor M&E, no universal/aspirational identity.
- **Reader's struggling moment** — "Haven't we tried youth programmes before?" Answer directly and set up why a *dual-track, prestigious, universal-aspiration* design differs.
- **Close with the practical tool** — A comparison table of existing programmes (scale, cost, outcome) that motivates the Part 2 model.
