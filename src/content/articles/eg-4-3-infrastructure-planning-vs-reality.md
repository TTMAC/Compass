## More Plans Than Almost Any Emerging Market, Less Delivery

South Africa has produced more infrastructure plans than almost any other emerging market economy, and delivered less of them. The National Infrastructure Plan promised R827 billion across 18 Strategic Integrated Projects. The Infrastructure Fund promised R100 billion in blended finance over a decade. Infrastructure South Africa promised a new coordination model that would accelerate project preparation and unlock private capital. The plans are sophisticated. The documents are thorough. The frameworks are internationally benchmarked. And the gap between what was planned and what was built is one of the widest in the developing world.

If you have read [Infrastructure Foundations](/articles/eg-2-2-infrastructure-foundations), you already know the detailed story: the Eskom crisis and its trillion-rand cost, the Transnet decline and the shift from rail to road, the port inefficiencies that add hundreds of dollars per container, the water infrastructure decay, and the digital divide that locks millions out of the modern economy. This article does not retrace that ground. Instead, it asks the measurement question — the question that matters if you want to hold institutions accountable rather than simply lament the state of things. How do you track whether infrastructure is being delivered against plan? How do you score the gap between commitment and reality? How do you build a framework that tells you, sector by sector, exactly where the system is failing and by how much?

The answer requires three things: a diagnostic framework that identifies why delivery fails (McKinsey's five dimensions provide this), a quantified analysis of what was promised versus what was delivered (the NIP and SIP data provide this), and a sector-by-sector scorecard that maps current performance against commitments and benchmarks. This article provides all three. By the end, you will have a practical tracker you can use to assess infrastructure delivery in real time, using publicly available data from Treasury budget documents, ISA reports, SOE annual reports, and sector regulators.

The measurement dimension matters because it connects infrastructure delivery to the Municipal Performance Index framework introduced in [The Municipal Performance Index](/articles/eg-4-1-the-municipal-performance-index) and applied in [The Metro Rankings](/articles/eg-4-2-the-metro-rankings). Infrastructure is not something that exists in isolation from municipal governance. Municipalities are responsible for water reticulation, electricity distribution, local roads, and the last-mile connections that determine whether national infrastructure investments actually reach citizens. A bulk water pipeline that terminates at the municipal boundary achieves nothing if the municipality cannot maintain the distribution network that carries water to households. A new power station achieves nothing if the municipal electricity department cannot maintain the substations and transformers that deliver power to homes and businesses. The infrastructure delivery tracker you will find at the end of this article is designed to work alongside the MPI, giving you a national-level complement to the municipal-level measurement.

## The McKinsey Framework: Five Dimensions of Infrastructure Delivery

To understand why South Africa plans so well and delivers so poorly, you need a diagnostic framework that separates the different stages of infrastructure delivery and identifies where the system breaks down at each stage. McKinsey Global Institute's research on infrastructure productivity, drawn from analysis of dozens of countries, provides precisely this. The framework identifies five dimensions that distinguish countries that deliver infrastructure effectively from those that do not. What makes it useful is not its complexity but its specificity — it tells you where to look.

### Dimension 1: Strategic Prioritisation

This is the question of whether a country has a coherent, evidence-based plan that identifies the right projects in the right sequence, based on economic impact analysis. You need to know what to build, where to build it, and in what order.

South Africa scores well on this dimension. The National Infrastructure Plan, developed by the Presidential Infrastructure Coordinating Commission and adopted by Cabinet in 2012, identified 18 Strategic Integrated Projects across energy, transport, water, social infrastructure, and regional development. The plan was informed by economic modelling and aligned to the National Development Plan's growth objectives. The Infrastructure Development Act of 2014 provided a legal framework for fast-tracking strategic projects. The Integrated Resource Plan for electricity, the National Water and Sanitation Master Plan, the National Rail Policy, and the National Broadband Plan all represent substantive strategic planning exercises. South Africa's problem has never been a shortage of strategy documents.

If you were scoring this dimension on a scale from one to ten, South Africa would earn a seven or an eight. The plans exist. They are technically sound. They identify the right priorities. They are aligned to credible economic analysis. The weakness at this stage is sequencing — too many priorities competing simultaneously for limited institutional capacity, rather than a ruthless focus on the two or three projects that would unblock the most economic value.

### Dimension 2: Project Preparation and Bankability

This is where the South African infrastructure pipeline begins to fail, and it is worth understanding exactly why because it explains the vast majority of the planning-execution gap.

Project preparation is the detailed engineering, environmental, financial, and legal work required to take an infrastructure concept from idea to investment-ready project. It typically costs between five and ten per cent of total project value and takes two to five years for complex projects. It requires specialised skills in transaction advisory, environmental impact assessment, engineering design, and financial structuring. It is expensive, time-consuming, and unglamorous — which is precisely why governments underinvest in it.

South Africa has a chronic shortage of project preparation capacity in the public sector. National Treasury's Government Technical Advisory Centre and the Development Bank of Southern Africa provide project preparation support, but their combined capacity is limited relative to the pipeline of projects that need to be prepared. The consequence is a phenomenon that development economists call the "missing middle" of infrastructure finance: a large pipeline of planned projects at one end, available capital looking for investment opportunities at the other end, and a thin pipeline of bankable projects in between. Announcements of R827 billion in planned infrastructure investment mean very little if only a fraction of that pipeline has completed the preparation work necessary for construction to begin.

On this dimension, South Africa scores a three or a four out of ten. The institutional machinery for project preparation exists — GTAC, the DBSA's project preparation facility, the Infrastructure Fund's project development function — but the throughput is far too low. The number of projects that move from concept to bankable feasibility study to financial close in any given year is a fraction of what the NIP pipeline requires.

### Dimension 3: Delivery and Construction Management

This is South Africa's weakest dimension, and the one that produces the most visible failures. Delivery management encompasses procurement, contract management, construction oversight, quality assurance, and the operational coordination required to bring a project from groundbreaking to commissioning.

The pattern across South Africa's mega-projects is remarkably consistent. Medupi and Kusile: years late, costs doubled, persistent design and construction defects. The Gauteng Freeway Improvement Project: completed but rendered financially unviable by the e-toll political collapse. PRASA's rolling stock procurement: plagued by specifications that did not match South African rail conditions, delivery delays, and corruption allegations. Transnet's locomotive programme: costs inflated by billions through state-capture-linked intermediaries, with delivered units that experienced operational problems. The Mzimvubu Water Project in the Eastern Cape: announced repeatedly, budget allocated, construction never meaningfully commenced.

The skills dimension compounds the management problem. The Engineering Council of South Africa's registration data shows that the number of registered professional engineers has grown more slowly than the economy's engineering needs. Government departments and state-owned enterprises have struggled to recruit and retain engineering and project management talent at public-sector salary scales. The result is that state entities frequently lack the in-house technical capacity to oversee contractors effectively. When the client cannot independently verify whether the contractor's work meets specifications, cost overruns and quality failures become almost inevitable.

On this dimension, South Africa scores a two or a three out of ten. Individual projects can be delivered well — the REIPPPP programme demonstrates this conclusively — but systemic delivery management capacity across the state is critically weak.

### Dimension 4: Private Sector Participation and PPP Frameworks

South Africa has one of the most sophisticated PPP legal and regulatory frameworks in the developing world. Treasury Regulation 16 under the Public Finance Management Act provides a structured approval process with defined feasibility, procurement, and delivery phases. The PPP manual is detailed. The approval protocols are rigorous. The risk transfer frameworks are well-designed.

And the output has been modest. Approximately 30 to 35 national and provincial PPP projects have been concluded since the framework was established in the early 2000s — a fraction of the number in comparable jurisdictions like India, Brazil, or Turkey. The approval process takes five to eight years on average from inception to financial close. Transaction advisory costs are high, deterring smaller projects. The framework was designed primarily for social infrastructure and has been less effective at accommodating economic infrastructure where risk profiles differ.

The irony is that the REIPPPP — South Africa's most successful infrastructure programme by almost any measure — operates outside the formal PPP framework entirely. It uses a bespoke procurement process under the Department of Energy with a dedicated IPP Office. Its success reinforces the point that South Africa can deliver infrastructure at scale through private participation when it creates fit-for-purpose institutional arrangements, but the general PPP framework has not achieved this at comparable scale.

On this dimension, South Africa scores a four out of ten. The framework is sound, the legal protections are strong, but the throughput is too low and the preparation process too slow to generate a meaningful PPP pipeline.

### Dimension 5: Regulatory Certainty

Infrastructure investors — whether public or private — need confidence that the regulatory rules governing tariffs, access, environmental compliance, and contract enforcement will remain stable over the multi-decade life of infrastructure assets. A power station operates for 30 to 50 years. A port concession runs for 20 to 25 years. Capital will not flow without regulatory predictability.

South Africa sends mixed signals. The legal system is robust, with an independent judiciary and well-developed commercial law. But policy uncertainty in key sectors has deterred investment. The decade-long delay in spectrum allocation for mobile broadband. The on-again, off-again approach to the Integrated Resource Plan for energy. The unresolved questions about third-party rail access terms and port concession frameworks. And the e-toll debacle in Gauteng, where government effectively walked away from a completed project's revenue model after years of public resistance, sent a devastating signal to infrastructure investors. If the revenue model for a completed, operational project can be abandoned, what confidence does an investor have in a project that has not yet been built?

On this dimension, South Africa scores a four or a five out of ten. The legal foundations are strong, but sector-specific regulatory uncertainty in energy, transport, water, and telecommunications has materially slowed investment.

### The Composite Picture

If you aggregate across all five dimensions — weighting them equally for simplicity — South Africa's overall infrastructure delivery score sits at approximately four out of ten. The country plans well (seven to eight), prepares poorly (three to four), delivers weakly (two to three), underwhelms on private participation (four), and provides inconsistent regulatory signals (four to five). The pattern is unmistakable: the further you move from strategy toward execution, the worse South Africa performs. This is the infrastructure delivery gap, and it explains why a country with sophisticated plans, a deep capital market, and a large pipeline of identified projects consistently fails to build what it has committed to building.

:::expert[DBSA Infrastructure Barometer]
The Development Bank of Southern Africa's Infrastructure Barometer tracks the gap between planned and actual infrastructure investment across all three spheres of government and state-owned enterprises. The Barometer's methodology compares capital budget allocations in the Medium Term Expenditure Framework with actual expenditure reported in annual financial statements. Across the period from 2015 to 2023, the Barometer found that actual infrastructure expenditure averaged between 60% and 75% of budgeted allocations, with the shortfall concentrated in project preparation delays, procurement bottlenecks, and construction management failures rather than fiscal constraints. The Barometer provides one of the most systematic data sources for tracking the planning-execution gap over time, and its sectoral breakdowns allow comparison of delivery rates across energy, transport, water, and social infrastructure.
:::

## The Planning-Execution Gap Quantified

The McKinsey framework tells you why the gap exists. Now you need to see how large it is. The numbers are striking, and they tell a story of systematic underdelivery that goes far beyond individual project failures.

The National Infrastructure Plan, adopted in 2012, committed to R827 billion in infrastructure investment across 18 Strategic Integrated Projects over a ten-year horizon. This was not a vague aspiration — it was a costed programme endorsed by Cabinet, backed by the Presidential Infrastructure Coordinating Commission, and embedded in the Medium Term Strategic Framework. By the early 2020s, credible assessments of delivery against the NIP targets suggested that actual completion ranged from 20% to 40% of the original commitments, depending on how you define "completion" and which SIPs you include.

The variance is not uniform across the 18 SIPs. Some have seen meaningful progress. SIP 5 (the Saldanha-Northern Cape Development Corridor) benefited from the iron ore export line's relatively strong performance and industrial development zone investment around Saldanha Bay. SIP 9 (Electricity Generation) saw substantial progress through the REIPPPP, even as Eskom's own build programme faltered. SIP 15 (Expanding Access to Communication Technology) saw private-sector-driven fibre deployment in urban areas, though the public-sector component (SA Connect) dramatically underdelivered.

Others have seen minimal tangible progress. SIP 1 (Unlocking the Northern Mineral Belt) was hampered by Transnet rail capacity constraints on the Waterberg line and water supply challenges for mining development. SIP 3 (South-Eastern Node and Corridor Development) in the Eastern Cape made limited headway on the economic infrastructure that was supposed to catalyse industrial development in one of the poorest provinces. SIP 11 (Agri-logistics and Rural Infrastructure) — critical for agricultural competitiveness — progressed slowly on rural road and cold-chain infrastructure.

The SIP delivery data, while imperfect, reveals three consistent patterns. First, SIPs that had a single dominant implementing agent with clear accountability (like the Department of Energy for SIP 9) performed better than SIPs that required coordination across multiple departments and spheres of government. Second, SIPs that could leverage private investment (like energy and telecommunications) outperformed SIPs that depended entirely on public capital budgets. Third, SIPs in provinces with weaker institutional capacity (Eastern Cape, Limpopo, North West) consistently underperformed SIPs in provinces with stronger delivery track records (Western Cape, Gauteng).

Beyond the SIPs, the broader infrastructure expenditure data tells the same story. National Treasury's budget documentation shows that actual infrastructure spending by government and SOEs has consistently fallen short of budgeted allocations. The gap between capital budget and actual capital expenditure — what Treasury euphemistically calls "underspending" — has averaged between 15% and 30% across major infrastructure programmes over the past decade. For the Department of Water and Sanitation, underspending on capital projects reached 40% in some financial years. For PRASA, capital expenditure efficiency collapsed during the worst years of institutional dysfunction. Even SANRAL, which has a better delivery record than most, experienced disruptions from the e-toll revenue uncertainty that constrained its forward planning.

The cumulative effect of sustained underspending is not merely that projects are delayed. It is that the infrastructure deficit grows larger each year, because existing infrastructure continues to depreciate while new investment falls short. The DBSA has estimated that South Africa's infrastructure maintenance backlog — the cost of bringing existing infrastructure up to acceptable condition — exceeds R400 billion. This is separate from the cost of new infrastructure. Every year of underinvestment in maintenance adds to this backlog, creating a compounding problem that becomes progressively more expensive to address.

:::takeaway
The planning-execution gap is not a story about one or two troubled projects. It is a systemic pattern across every sector and every sphere of government. Of the R827 billion committed under the NIP, credible assessments suggest that between 60% and 80% was not delivered within the original timeframes. Capital budget underspending of 15% to 30% per year compounds into an infrastructure deficit that grows wider with each passing budget cycle. The question is not whether South Africa has plans — it is whether the institutional machinery exists to execute them.
:::

## Sector-by-Sector Scorecard

The following scorecard assesses infrastructure delivery across four critical sectors. For each sector, five dimensions are scored: the original NIP/SIP commitment, the current delivery status, the timeline variance (how far behind schedule delivery has fallen), the cost variance (how much costs have exceeded estimates), and the binding constraint that explains the gap. This structure is designed to be updated as new data becomes available, making it a living measurement tool rather than a static assessment.

### Energy: The Partial Turnaround

**NIP/SIP commitment:** SIP 8 (Green Energy in Support of the South African Economy) and SIP 9 (Electricity Generation to Support Socio-Economic Development) together committed to a fundamental transformation of South Africa's energy system — new generation capacity from both Eskom and independent power producers, transmission grid expansion, and the transition toward a diversified energy mix.

**Current delivery status:** The energy sector presents a split picture. The REIPPPP has been a genuine success, delivering approximately 6,300 MW of allocated renewable capacity across five bid windows, attracting over R210 billion in private investment, and driving solar PV costs down by more than 80% between the first and fifth bid windows. You can read the detailed analysis of the REIPPPP's design and performance in [Infrastructure Foundations](/articles/eg-2-2-infrastructure-foundations). On the Eskom side, Medupi and Kusile eventually added generation capacity, but at roughly double the original cost and years behind schedule. The Energy Availability Factor for the existing fleet declined to below 60% before showing signs of recovery in 2024 and 2025 as maintenance programmes took effect and some capacity was stabilised.

**Timeline variance:** For the REIPPPP, projects awarded in Bid Windows 1 through 3 experienced grid connection delays of one to three years beyond planned commissioning dates, primarily due to Eskom transmission constraints rather than developer performance. For Medupi and Kusile, the combined delay exceeds five years against original completion targets. Transmission grid expansion identified in the Transmission Development Plan is running approximately three to five years behind the investment trajectory needed to accommodate planned generation additions, with the Northern Cape and Eastern Cape corridors facing the most acute bottlenecks.

**Cost variance:** Medupi's final cost of over R145 billion represents an approximate 83% overrun against the original R79 billion budget. Kusile's costs exceeded R161 billion against similar original estimates. The REIPPPP, by contrast, has operated within its contracted cost structures, with tariff prices declining across successive bid windows. The contrast is instructive: competitive procurement with private risk allocation (REIPPPP) delivers on cost; state-managed mega-projects (Medupi, Kusile) do not.

**Binding constraint:** Transmission grid capacity is the binding constraint for the energy sector going forward. The CSIR's modelling shows that generation capacity additions from renewables and gas can close the supply gap over the medium term, but only if the transmission grid can evacuate power from generation sites (concentrated in the Northern Cape, Eastern Cape, and Limpopo) to demand centres (Gauteng, KwaZulu-Natal, Western Cape). The Grid Development Plan estimates R150 billion to R200 billion in transmission investment over the next decade. The establishment of the National Transmission Company of South Africa as a separate entity from Eskom is a structural prerequisite, but the entity's capitalisation, staffing, and operational independence remain works in progress.

### Transport: The Deepening Decline

**NIP/SIP commitment:** SIP 2 (Durban-Free State-Gauteng Logistics and Industrial Corridor) was the centrepiece of the transport commitment, aiming to strengthen the logistics spine connecting the economic heartland to the primary export port. SIP 5 (Saldanha-Northern Cape Development Corridor) addressed the iron ore export corridor. SIP 7 (Integrated Urban Space and Public Transport Programme) committed to bus rapid transit systems and commuter rail improvements in major cities.

**Current delivery status:** Rail freight volumes on the general freight network declined from approximately 81 million tonnes in 2016/17 to below 55 million tonnes by 2022/23 — a deterioration of more than 30% over six years, the opposite direction from the NIP's targets. The detailed story of Transnet's decline, including the locomotive procurement scandal and cable theft crisis, is covered in [Infrastructure Foundations](/articles/eg-2-2-infrastructure-foundations). Port efficiency at Durban remains poor by international standards, with ship turnaround times of 90 to 110 hours compared to 24 to 30 hours at world-class facilities. The Durban dig-out port — a proposed transformational capacity expansion — remains in planning stages after years of feasibility work. BRT systems in Johannesburg (Rea Vaya), Cape Town (MyCiTi), and other cities have been implemented in limited corridors but have not achieved the network coverage originally envisaged.

**Timeline variance:** The transport sector shows the largest timeline variances of any infrastructure sector. The Durban-Free State-Gauteng corridor improvements are years behind the NIP schedule. Rail capacity expansion on general freight corridors has not materialised; instead, capacity has contracted. The Durban dig-out port has been in planning for over a decade without construction commencing. PRASA's commuter rail modernisation programme — which was supposed to deliver over 3,600 new commuter coaches — experienced procurement irregularities, delivery delays, and a fundamental mismatch between coach specifications and South African rail conditions. Commuter rail services in Gauteng, KwaZulu-Natal, and the Western Cape have deteriorated rather than improved, with service cancellations, safety concerns, and vandalism reducing the usable network.

**Cost variance:** Transnet's locomotive procurement programme, documented by the Zondo Commission, saw costs inflated by approximately R5 billion through state-capture-linked intermediaries. The broader cost variance in transport infrastructure is difficult to quantify precisely because many projects have not advanced far enough for meaningful cost comparisons — the variance manifests as non-delivery rather than cost overruns. Where projects have progressed, the pattern mirrors other sectors: actual costs exceeding estimates by 30% to 80%.

**Binding constraint:** The transport sector faces a structural constraint that the NIP did not adequately address: the need for fundamental reform of the institutional model, not just capital investment. Third-party access to the rail network — enabling private operators to run freight trains on Transnet's infrastructure — is the single reform most likely to reverse the decline in rail freight volumes. The National Rail Policy provides the legal framework, but the implementation of slot allocation systems and access pricing regimes has been slow. For ports, the separation of the port authority from terminal operations and the introduction of private terminal concessions is the equivalent structural reform. Until these institutional changes take full effect, capital investment alone will not reverse the deterioration because the institutional model that caused the decline remains in place.

### Water: The Invisible Emergency

**NIP/SIP commitment:** SIP 18 (Water and Sanitation Infrastructure) and multiple components of other SIPs committed to addressing South Africa's growing water supply-demand gap. The National Water and Sanitation Master Plan projected a 17% gap between supply and demand by 2030 under business-as-usual conditions and identified the investments needed to close it.

**Current delivery status:** The water sector is arguably South Africa's most underperforming infrastructure domain relative to need. Non-revenue water — the difference between water produced at treatment works and water billed to consumers — averages approximately 41% nationally, with some municipalities recording losses above 60%. This means that for every 100 litres of treated water entering the distribution system, 41 litres are lost through leaks, illegal connections, meter failures, and billing system breakdowns. The Water Research Commission estimates the cost of physical water losses alone at over R9 billion annually.

The Blue Drop and Green Drop assessment systems, administered by the Department of Water and Sanitation, provide the most direct measurement of water and wastewater treatment quality. The Blue Drop programme assesses drinking water quality management at treatment works, while the Green Drop programme assesses wastewater treatment compliance. After a hiatus in publication, the most recent assessments showed a concerning deterioration. The national Blue Drop score declined from approximately 87% in 2014 to around 47% in the 2023 assessment, indicating a significant decline in the quality management systems at drinking water treatment works. The Green Drop results were even more alarming, with the majority of wastewater treatment works scoring in the "critical" risk category, meaning they were not treating wastewater to the standards required before discharge into rivers and other water bodies.

**Timeline variance:** The Lesotho Highlands Water Project Phase II, which will augment supply to the Vaal River system serving Gauteng, has experienced repeated delays measured in years against its original commissioning targets. The Mokolo-Crocodile Water Augmentation Project, critical for the Limpopo mining sector, has similarly fallen behind schedule. At the municipal level, water infrastructure projects funded through the Municipal Infrastructure Grant routinely exceed planned timelines by 50% or more, according to the Department of Water and Sanitation's own reporting.

**Cost variance:** The Department of Water and Sanitation's capital budget has been consistently underspent — actual expenditure falling short of allocations by 20% to 40% in multiple financial years. This is a different kind of cost variance: not projects that cost more than planned, but money allocated for projects that was never spent because projects could not be prepared, procured, or managed to the point of construction. The paradox of simultaneous infrastructure deficit and budget underspending is one of the clearest indicators of institutional capacity failure.

**Binding constraint:** Water infrastructure faces a binding constraint at the municipal level. Even if national bulk water projects are completed — dams built, pipelines laid, treatment works constructed — the last mile depends entirely on municipal capacity. Municipal pipe networks are ageing, with some reticulation systems in older towns exceeding their design life by decades. Municipal water and sanitation departments have lost skilled technical staff. Revenue collection for water services has declined in many municipalities, undermining the financial sustainability of operations and maintenance. The binding constraint for water is not national strategy or bulk infrastructure — it is municipal institutional capacity, which connects directly to the MPI framework's measurement of municipal service delivery performance.

:::expert[Water Research Commission Analysis]
The Water Research Commission's research programme on non-revenue water provides granular data on water losses at the municipal level. The WRC's methodology distinguishes between physical losses (leaks in the distribution network) and commercial losses (illegal connections, meter inaccuracies, billing failures). Their analysis shows that physical losses account for approximately 60% to 70% of non-revenue water nationally, with the remainder attributable to commercial losses. The WRC has mapped the age profile of municipal pipe networks and estimates that approximately 30% of South Africa's water distribution infrastructure has exceeded its design life. Replacing ageing infrastructure at the current rate of investment would take approximately 300 years — a figure that illustrates the scale of the maintenance backlog. The WRC recommends a three-pronged approach: immediate pressure management and leak detection (which can reduce physical losses by 20% to 30% at relatively low cost), medium-term pipe replacement programmes focused on the highest-loss networks, and long-term institutional reform to build and retain municipal engineering and maintenance capacity.
:::

### Digital: Private Progress, Public Failure

**NIP/SIP commitment:** SIP 15 (Expanding Access to Communication Technology) and the National Broadband Plan (SA Connect), published in 2013, committed to ambitious targets: 100% broadband access by 2020, with minimum speeds of 5 Mbps for 50% of the population. The high-demand spectrum auction, delayed for over a decade, was supposed to unlock mobile broadband capacity.

**Current delivery status:** The digital sector presents the starkest contrast between private-sector-driven and public-sector-driven infrastructure delivery. Private operators — Vumatel, Openserve, MetroFibre, and others — have deployed fibre-to-the-home and fibre-to-the-business networks rapidly in urban areas, with coverage expanding from negligible in 2015 to millions of premises passed by the mid-2020s. Mobile network operators, following the completion of the spectrum auction in 2022, have expanded 4G and 5G coverage substantially. Data prices have declined significantly since the Competition Commission's Data Services Market Inquiry in 2019, though affordability remains a barrier for low-income users.

The public-sector component has been a comprehensive failure. SA Connect's targets were missed by wide margins. The programme to connect government facilities, schools, and clinics in underserved areas with broadband delivered a fraction of its commitments. The programme suffered from precisely the same implementation failures as other national infrastructure plans: inadequate project preparation, coordination failures across government departments, and slow procurement. The decade-long delay in spectrum allocation — caused by litigation and regulatory disputes — represented an enormous opportunity cost in foregone mobile broadband investment and coverage expansion.

**Timeline variance:** SA Connect's 2020 targets were missed entirely. The spectrum auction, originally expected around 2012 to 2015, was completed only in 2022 — a delay of approximately seven to ten years. Private fibre deployment, by contrast, has broadly tracked or exceeded commercial deployment plans, demonstrating that the binding constraint is not technical or financial but institutional and regulatory.

**Cost variance:** The public-sector digital infrastructure programmes are difficult to assess on cost variance because delivery volumes were so low that cost-per-unit comparisons are not meaningful. The more relevant metric is opportunity cost: the economic value foregone during the decade of spectrum delay, which the CSIR and ICASA have estimated at tens of billions of rands in lost investment, employment, and productivity growth.

**Binding constraint:** The digital sector's binding constraint is last-mile connectivity to township and rural areas. The commercial investment case for fibre in low-income areas is weak without public subsidy or innovative delivery models. Mobile data, while more widespread, remains constrained by affordability and network congestion in high-density, low-income areas. Closing the digital divide requires either public investment in last-mile infrastructure (which SA Connect was supposed to deliver but did not) or subsidy mechanisms that make private investment in underserved areas commercially viable. This is fundamentally a policy design and implementation challenge, not a technology problem.

:::takeaway
The sector-by-sector scorecard reveals a consistent pattern: where competitive private participation has been enabled through well-designed procurement frameworks (energy, digital backbone), delivery has been reasonable to strong. Where delivery depends on state institutional capacity — particularly at the municipal level or through SOEs — performance has been poor to catastrophic. The implication for measurement is clear: tracking infrastructure delivery requires separate assessment of the private-participation and state-delivery components within each sector, because they follow fundamentally different performance trajectories.
:::

## Infrastructure South Africa and the Infrastructure Fund

Two institutional innovations were introduced in the late 2010s to address the infrastructure delivery gap. Understanding what they were designed to do and what they have actually achieved is essential for any measurement of the planning-execution gap.

### Infrastructure South Africa

Infrastructure South Africa (ISA), established within the Presidency as part of the Investment and Infrastructure Office, was designed to serve as a central coordination and facilitation body for strategic infrastructure projects. Its mandate included maintaining a pipeline of catalytic infrastructure projects, facilitating the removal of regulatory and institutional bottlenecks, coordinating across government departments and spheres, and providing a single window for investors seeking to participate in South African infrastructure.

ISA represented an acknowledgement that the PICC model — a political coordination body chaired by the President — was insufficient for the operational demands of infrastructure delivery. The PICC could convene ministers and premiers, but it lacked the technical capacity to drive project preparation, manage procurement pipelines, and resolve the day-to-day coordination failures that stalled projects. ISA was supposed to fill this operational gap.

The assessment of ISA's performance depends on what you measure it against. If the benchmark is the PICC's previous performance, ISA has provided incrementally better coordination, more systematic project tracking, and greater transparency about the infrastructure pipeline. If the benchmark is the scale and urgency of the infrastructure deficit, ISA has not yet demonstrated the capacity to fundamentally accelerate delivery. The office has been constrained by the same factors that limit infrastructure delivery more broadly: limited staffing relative to the pipeline it is supposed to manage, dependence on implementing departments and SOEs over which it has coordination authority but not line authority, and the inherent difficulty of accelerating project preparation — which remains the binding constraint in the pipeline.

ISA has published infrastructure project pipelines and progress reports that provide useful data for tracking delivery. These reports, available through the Presidency's website and tabled in Parliament, offer project-level information on status, timelines, and identified constraints. For the infrastructure delivery tracker described later in this article, ISA's pipeline reports are a primary data source.

### The Infrastructure Fund

The Infrastructure Fund, announced in the 2019 Budget and operationalised through the DBSA, was designed as a blended finance mechanism. The concept was straightforward in principle: use catalytic public capital (concessional funding from the fiscus) to leverage private investment through blended finance structures, making projects viable that neither the public nor private sector could finance alone. The target was R100 billion in blended finance over a decade.

The Infrastructure Fund was explicitly designed to address Dimension 2 of the McKinsey framework — the project preparation and bankability gap. Part of its mandate was to provide project development support, helping move projects from concept to bankable feasibility. This was a critical design feature because, as discussed above, the thin pipeline of bankable projects is the primary bottleneck in South Africa's infrastructure system.

By the mid-2020s, the Infrastructure Fund had made progress on several project categories, including student housing, social infrastructure, and embedded generation. However, the pace of project development and financial closure remained below the trajectory needed to reach the R100 billion target within the original timeframe. The fund faced challenges common to blended finance mechanisms globally: the difficulty of structuring deals that satisfy both the public sector's developmental objectives and private investors' return requirements, the time-intensive nature of project preparation even with dedicated resources, and the challenge of building a deal pipeline in an environment where implementing institutions have limited capacity to co-develop projects.

The Infrastructure Fund's performance data — available through the DBSA's annual reports and National Treasury's budget documentation — provides a direct measure of whether South Africa is closing the project preparation gap. The key metrics to track are: the number of projects that move from concept to feasibility study completion, the number that reach financial close, the ratio of public catalytic capital to private investment mobilised (the "leverage ratio"), and the time from project registration to financial close.

:::expert[National Treasury Infrastructure Analysis]
National Treasury's annual Budget Review and the Estimates of National Expenditure provide the most authoritative data on public infrastructure spending by department and entity. Treasury's infrastructure expenditure analysis distinguishes between "new and replacement" capital spending and "upgrades and additions," and tracks actual expenditure against budget allocations across the Medium Term Expenditure Framework. The expenditure data shows that the public sector's infrastructure spending as a percentage of GDP declined from approximately 7.3% in 2014/15 to approximately 5.4% by 2022/23, reflecting both fiscal consolidation pressures and the institutional delivery constraints discussed throughout this article. Treasury's analysis consistently identifies project preparation delays and procurement bottlenecks — rather than fiscal constraints — as the primary causes of capital budget underspending, supporting the McKinsey framework's diagnosis that South Africa's infrastructure gap is an institutional delivery problem, not a funding problem.
:::

## Why the Gap Persists

If the diagnosis is clear and the solutions are known, why does the planning-execution gap persist? The answer lies in a set of reinforcing institutional weaknesses that are individually understood but collectively resistant to quick fixes.

The first and most fundamental reason is the project preparation deficit. Preparing a complex infrastructure project for construction and financing requires sustained effort by a multidisciplinary team over two to five years. It requires engineering design, environmental impact assessment, social consultation, land acquisition, financial modelling, legal structuring, and procurement document preparation — each of which involves specialised skills, regulatory approvals, and coordination across multiple institutions. South Africa does not have enough of these teams, and creating them takes years of capacity building. GTAC, the DBSA, and the Infrastructure Fund collectively employ perhaps a few hundred professionals focused on project preparation. The pipeline requires thousands. No amount of political will can substitute for the patient, technical work of preparing projects to the point where construction can begin and investors can commit capital.

The second reason is delivery management weakness in the public sector. Even when projects are properly prepared, the procuring institution needs the technical capacity to manage the procurement process, oversee the construction contractor, manage contract variations, ensure quality compliance, and coordinate across the multiple parties involved in a complex infrastructure project. South Africa's public sector has lost significant engineering and project management capacity over the past two decades. The salary differential between public and private sector engineering roles is substantial, and the institutional instability that has characterised several key infrastructure entities — Eskom, Transnet, PRASA, the Department of Water and Sanitation — has driven further attrition. Rebuilding this capacity requires not just recruitment but the creation of career paths, institutional cultures, and knowledge management systems that retain and develop technical professionals over decades.

The third reason is the PPP preparation bottleneck. As described earlier, South Africa's PPP framework is rigorous but slow. The average time from project registration to financial close is five to seven years. This is partly by design — the rigour of the feasibility and approval process is intended to prevent badly structured deals. But the consequence is that very few projects survive the preparation process to reach construction. Streamlining the PPP process without compromising rigour requires a different institutional model: one in which a well-resourced project development entity co-develops projects with implementing departments, rather than simply reviewing submissions. This shift has been discussed for years but has been slow to materialise at the scale required.

The fourth reason is regulatory uncertainty in key sectors. Infrastructure investors need confidence that the rules will remain stable over the 20-to-50-year life of the asset. When the energy policy oscillates, when spectrum allocation is delayed by a decade, when toll road revenue models are abandoned after construction, and when rail access terms remain unresolved, investors — both public and private — defer commitments. Regulatory certainty is not just about having the right rules. It is about maintaining them consistently over time, which requires political discipline and institutional continuity that South Africa has struggled to provide.

These four factors interact and reinforce each other. Weak project preparation means fewer bankable projects enter the pipeline. Weak delivery management means that those projects that do proceed experience cost overruns and delays that damage the state's credibility with investors. The slow PPP process means that private capital cannot fill the gaps left by weak public delivery. And regulatory uncertainty makes both public and private investors cautious about committing to the long-lived assets that infrastructure requires. Breaking this cycle requires simultaneous progress on all four fronts — which is precisely why the challenge is so difficult.

## Connecting to the Municipal Performance Index

The infrastructure delivery analysis in this article operates primarily at the national level — national plans, SOE performance, sector-wide trends. But as you know from [The Municipal Performance Index](/articles/eg-4-1-the-municipal-performance-index) and [The Metro Rankings](/articles/eg-4-2-the-metro-rankings), much of the infrastructure that directly affects citizens' lives is delivered and maintained at the municipal level. Electricity distribution, water reticulation, local roads, stormwater management, and waste collection are municipal responsibilities. National infrastructure investments that do not account for municipal delivery capacity will fail at the last mile.

The connection works in both directions. The MPI measures municipal performance on service delivery outputs — the percentage of households with access to piped water, the percentage of electricity losses in the distribution network, the condition of municipal road surfaces. These municipal-level measures are, in part, outcomes of national infrastructure investment decisions. A municipality that receives bulk water from a well-maintained dam and pipeline system starts with an advantage over one that depends on an unreliable bulk supply. A municipality that benefits from stable grid electricity can focus its resources on distribution network maintenance rather than managing load shedding disruptions.

Conversely, municipal infrastructure performance feeds back into the national picture. A municipality with 60% non-revenue water means that 60% of the national investment in bulk water supply for that area is wasted before it reaches a consumer. A municipality that cannot maintain its electricity distribution network undermines the value of national generation investments. The infrastructure delivery tracker in this article is designed to complement the MPI by providing the national-level context within which municipal performance should be interpreted. When you assess a municipality's water service delivery score in the MPI, the infrastructure delivery tracker tells you what that municipality is working with in terms of bulk supply, pipeline condition, and sector-wide trends.

For the institutional capacity dimensions that [The Execution Machine](/articles/eg-3-2-the-execution-machine) examines in detail, infrastructure delivery performance is both an input and an output. Institutional capacity determines infrastructure delivery quality, and infrastructure delivery results provide one of the most concrete measures of institutional capacity. A government entity that consistently delivers projects on time and on budget is demonstrating institutional capacity. One that consistently underperforms its capital budget is revealing institutional weakness. The infrastructure delivery tracker, in this sense, is a proxy measure for institutional capacity at the sectoral level.

:::takeaway
Infrastructure measurement cannot be separated from municipal governance measurement. National infrastructure investments succeed or fail at the municipal level, where the last mile of service delivery occurs. The infrastructure delivery tracker in this article is designed to work alongside the MPI: the tracker measures national-level planning and delivery performance, while the MPI measures the municipal-level outcomes that depend on both national infrastructure and local institutional capacity. Used together, they provide a comprehensive picture of whether infrastructure investment is translating into improved services for citizens.
:::

## Practical Framework: Infrastructure Delivery Tracker

The following framework provides a structured tool for tracking infrastructure delivery against plans across four sectors. For each sector, five dimensions are assessed using publicly available data. The framework is designed to be updated quarterly or annually as new data is published, making it a living measurement tool.

**How to use this tracker:** For each sector, identify the most recent data for each of the five dimensions. Score the sector on a simple traffic-light system: green (delivery broadly on track, variance below 20%), amber (delivery partially on track, variance 20% to 50%), and red (delivery significantly off track, variance above 50% or fundamental delivery failure). The composite picture across all sectors gives you an overall infrastructure delivery assessment.

**Dimension 1: NIP/SIP Commitment.** What was originally promised? Identify the specific SIP or national plan commitment for the sector, including the investment quantum, capacity targets, and timeline. Source: PICC reports, NIP documentation, Medium Term Strategic Framework, ISA pipeline reports.

**Dimension 2: Current Delivery Status.** What has actually been delivered? Quantify the current state — megawatts commissioned, kilometres of rail operational, megalitres of water treated, premises connected. Compare the actual delivery to the original commitment. Source: SOE annual reports (Eskom, Transnet, Rand Water), sector regulator reports (NERSA, ICASA), ISA progress reports, departmental annual reports.

**Dimension 3: Timeline Variance.** How far behind schedule is delivery? Calculate the difference between the originally planned completion date and the current expected completion date (or actual completion date, if the project is finished). Express as months or years of delay. Source: Treasury budget documentation (which tracks project milestones), ISA reports, SOE integrated reports, Parliamentary Monitoring Group records of departmental briefings.

**Dimension 4: Cost Variance.** How much have costs exceeded original estimates? Calculate the difference between the original budget or cost estimate and the current estimated total cost (or final cost, if complete). Express as a percentage overrun. Source: Treasury Estimates of National Expenditure, SOE annual financial statements, Auditor-General reports, ISA project data.

**Dimension 5: Binding Constraint.** What is the single most important factor preventing delivery improvement? Identify whether the binding constraint is project preparation capacity, procurement bottlenecks, construction management weakness, regulatory uncertainty, funding shortfalls, or institutional dysfunction. This qualitative assessment should be informed by the quantitative data in dimensions 1 through 4 and by the institutional analysis in sector-specific reports. Source: GTAC analysis, DBSA Infrastructure Barometer, sector-specific research (CSIR for energy, WRC for water, Stellenbosch logistics studies for transport).

**Energy Sector Application**

NIP/SIP commitment: SIP 8 and SIP 9 committed to diversified generation capacity additions, transmission grid expansion, and transition to a lower-carbon energy mix. The IRP 2019 provides the current generation capacity plan. Delivery status: track REIPPPP MW awarded versus MW commissioned, Eskom fleet EAF trends, and transmission grid investment against the Transmission Development Plan. Timeline variance: compare REIPPPP bid window timelines with actual commercial operation dates; compare Eskom maintenance and new-build schedules with actual delivery. Cost variance: compare Eskom build programme budgets with actual costs; track REIPPPP tariff trends across bid windows. Binding constraint: transmission grid capacity and grid connection timelines. Data sources: NERSA, CSIR energy statistics, Eskom integrated report, IPP Office quarterly reports, Treasury budget documentation.

**Transport Sector Application**

NIP/SIP commitment: SIP 2 (Durban-Gauteng corridor), SIP 5 (Saldanha corridor), SIP 7 (public transport). The National Rail Policy and port reform framework provide current sector plans. Delivery status: track TFR freight volumes (million tonnes per annum), port container throughput (TEUs), ship turnaround times, BRT route kilometres operational. Timeline variance: compare planned port expansion milestones with actual progress; track PRASA rolling stock delivery against procurement schedule. Cost variance: compare Transnet capital programme budgets with actual expenditure and output. Binding constraint: structural reform (third-party rail access, port terminal concessions). Data sources: Transnet integrated report, TNPA port statistics, StatsSA freight data, Treasury budget documentation, Department of Transport annual report.

**Water Sector Application**

NIP/SIP commitment: SIP 18 and the National Water and Sanitation Master Plan's investment requirements. Delivery status: track non-revenue water percentage (national average and by municipality), Blue Drop and Green Drop scores, bulk water project completion (LHWP II, Mokolo-Crocodile). Timeline variance: compare bulk water project schedules with actual progress; track municipal infrastructure grant project completion rates. Cost variance: compare Department of Water and Sanitation capital budget allocations with actual expenditure. Binding constraint: municipal institutional capacity for water distribution and maintenance. Data sources: DWS annual report, Blue Drop and Green Drop reports, WRC research publications, municipal annual reports, Treasury budget documentation.

**Digital Sector Application**

NIP/SIP commitment: SIP 15 and SA Connect broadband targets. Delivery status: track fixed broadband penetration per 100 people, mobile broadband coverage percentage, fibre premises passed, data price trends. Timeline variance: compare SA Connect milestones with actual delivery; track spectrum deployment timelines. Cost variance: compare SA Connect budget allocations with actual expenditure. Binding constraint: last-mile connectivity to underserved areas. Data sources: ICASA quarterly reports, operator results presentations, Research ICT Africa data, Competition Commission monitoring reports, Treasury budget documentation.

**Updating the Tracker**

The tracker should be updated at two intervals. The annual update, aligned with the publication of Treasury's Budget Review (February), SOE integrated reports (typically June to September), and sector regulator annual reports, provides the most comprehensive data refresh. A mid-year check, using ISA quarterly reports and Treasury's Medium Term Budget Policy Statement (October), provides an interim assessment. Both updates should be compared with the previous period to identify trends — is the planning-execution gap narrowing or widening in each sector?

For the energy and transport sectors, Eskom's and Transnet's quarterly operational updates (published on their websites and reported in financial media) provide more frequent data points on key operational metrics like the EAF and freight volumes. These higher-frequency indicators serve as leading signals of whether underlying delivery trajectories are improving or deteriorating.

The tracker is designed to connect to the broader measurement architecture described across Part 4 of this series. The MPI provides municipal-level measurement. This tracker provides national-level infrastructure measurement. Together, they give you a two-level assessment system: how well is national infrastructure being planned and delivered (this tracker), and how well are municipalities translating that infrastructure into services for citizens (the MPI). [From Strategy to Reality](/articles/eg-5-2-from-strategy-to-reality) will draw these measurement threads together into an integrated assessment of whether South Africa's economic growth strategy is translating from plan to execution across all levels of government.

:::framework
**The Infrastructure Gap Diagnostic**

When infrastructure in your area is failing — load-shedding, water outages, potholed roads, slow broadband — use this five-question diagnostic to pinpoint where in the delivery chain the breakdown is occurring and what data to cite when demanding answers.

**Question 1: Was the project planned?**

Check whether the infrastructure need is recognised in the relevant national plan — the Integrated Resource Plan (energy), the National Water and Sanitation Master Plan (water), the National Rail Policy (transport), or SA Connect (digital). If the project is not in any plan, the failure is at the strategic prioritisation stage. Source: PICC reports, ISA pipeline reports, sector master plans.

**Question 2: Was the project prepared to bankability?**

A planned project that has not completed feasibility studies, environmental impact assessment, and financial structuring cannot attract funding or begin construction. Check whether the project has moved beyond concept stage. If it has been "planned" for five or more years without reaching financial close, the failure is at the project preparation stage — the "missing middle" that traps most South African infrastructure. Source: Infrastructure Fund reports, DBSA project preparation data, GTAC pipeline tracking.

**Question 3: Was the budget allocated and spent?**

Compare the capital budget allocation in the Estimates of National Expenditure or the Municipal Infrastructure Grant to actual expenditure reported in annual financial statements. If money was allocated but not spent (underspending above 20%), the failure is at the delivery management stage — procurement bottlenecks, construction management weakness, or institutional dysfunction. Source: National Treasury budget documentation, Vulekamali portal, municipal Section 71 reports.

**Question 4: Was the project delivered on time and on cost?**

For projects that did proceed to construction, calculate the timeline variance (months or years of delay) and the cost variance (percentage overrun). Compare against the original commitment. If costs doubled and timelines tripled — as with Medupi and Kusile — the failure is at the construction management stage. Source: SOE integrated reports, Auditor-General reports, ISA progress reports, Parliamentary Monitoring Group records.

**Question 5: Is the last mile working?**

Even when national infrastructure is built, it only reaches citizens through municipal distribution networks. Check your municipality's non-revenue water percentage, electricity distribution losses, and capital expenditure ratio. If bulk supply exists but the municipal network is failing, the binding constraint is municipal institutional capacity — and the MPI is your measurement tool. Source: Blue Drop and Green Drop reports, Municipal Money portal, AGSA municipal audit outcomes.

By identifying the specific stage at which delivery breaks down, you move from a general complaint about "no service delivery" to a precise diagnosis that tells you which institution is responsible, what data proves the failure, and where to direct your accountability demand.
:::
