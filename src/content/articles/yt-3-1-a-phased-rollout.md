## Big Programmes Fail When They Start Big

The first two Parts of this series described a problem and a model. This Part confronts the question that decides whether the model is a serious proposal or a daydream: could South Africa actually build it? This article answers the "how" — the sequence and machinery of construction. The next two answer the "how much" and the "how to pay." Together they are where a youth-service proposal earns the right to be taken seriously, because anyone can sketch an institution; the test is whether you can show, step by honest step, how it would come into being in a country with real fiscal limits and real capacity gaps.

Begin with the most important implementation principle, the one whose violation has wrecked more good policy in South Africa than any other: **big programmes fail when they start big.** The temptation, with an idea as ambitious as universal national service, is to announce it, legislate it, and switch it on at full scale — to go from nothing to hundreds of thousands of young people in a single national rollout. That instinct is the single most reliable way to guarantee failure. A programme launched at full scale before anyone has learned how to run it will fail at full scale: the logistics will buckle, the quality will collapse, the money will leak, and the political backlash from a visible, expensive failure will kill the idea for a generation. South Africa has watched this pattern play out across more than one well-intentioned mega-initiative.

The alternative is to treat the rollout itself as the policy: a deliberately phased path in which each phase proves something before the next begins, gated by evidence rather than by political timetable. The model is built the way any complex, high-stakes system should be built — proven small, scaled deliberately, and only universalised once it has earned the capacity and legitimacy to be universal. This article sets out three phases, the institutional machinery to run them, the law to enable them, and the risks that must be managed throughout. The phasing is not a hedge or a delay tactic. It is the difference between a proposal that could actually work and one that merely sounds good in a manifesto. It is also, deliberately, the opposite of how South Africa has too often launched its most ambitious schemes — announced at full scale, with a ribbon cut and a deadline set, before anyone had learned how to run them. This proposal asks to be judged by a stricter standard, and builds that standard into its own design.

## Phase One: Prove It

The first phase has one job: to learn. Its purpose is not scale, coverage, or political visibility — it is to discover, in the real world and at manageable cost, how the service actually works, what it actually costs, and what breaks.

In this phase the service runs as a **voluntary pilot**, deliberately limited in size and geography — on the order of a cohort in the low tens of thousands, concentrated in one or two provinces chosen to test the model across different conditions (an urban-and-rural mix, varying state capacity). Participation is voluntary, which sidesteps, for now, every constitutional question about compulsion and lets the pilot recruit young people who want to be there while the model is still being refined. The pilot runs the full design — the basic-training crucible, the choice of tracks, the civilian streams, the benefits — but at a size small enough to manage closely and fix as you go.

The defining feature of Phase One is that it is **over-resourced and over-measured relative to its size.** This sounds wasteful and is precisely the opposite. The entire value of a pilot is the learning it produces, and learning requires investment in measurement that a mature programme would not need per participant: detailed tracking of every cohort, independent evaluation, careful costing of every component, follow-up of graduates into work and enterprise. A pilot that skimps on measurement to look cheap produces no usable evidence and wastes the whole exercise. The money spent measuring Phase One is the cheapest money in the entire programme, because it prevents expensive mistakes at scale.

It is worth being concrete about *what* Phase One is trying to learn, because "run a pilot" is a phrase behind which a great deal of vagueness can hide. The pilot has to answer a specific set of questions whose answers no one can currently give with confidence. How long should the basic-training crucible actually be to deliver its cohesion and discipline effects? What does it genuinely cost to put one young person through the full cycle, once every component is added up? Which civilian placements work well and which generate make-work? How many graduates are in employment, study, or enterprise a year later, and how does that compare to similar young people who did not serve? What share of participants drop out, and why? Can the basic-training environment be run humanely and safely at this scale? Each of these is a question the model's eventual success depends on, and each can only be answered by running the thing and measuring it honestly. The pilot is, in effect, a controlled experiment whose findings determine the design of everything that follows.

The choice of where to pilot matters too. One or two provinces should be chosen deliberately to stress-test the model across South Africa's real variation — ideally a mix that includes both a high-capacity, more urban setting and a lower-capacity, more rural one, so that the pilot learns not only whether the model works under favourable conditions but whether it survives the harder conditions that much of the country presents. A pilot run only in the easiest possible setting teaches you how the service performs at its best, which is precisely the wrong thing to know before scaling to a country full of difficult settings.

Phase One must also define its **success criteria in advance**, before the first cohort starts, so that the decision to proceed is made against honest benchmarks rather than after-the-fact rationalisation. What completion rate would count as success? What share of graduates in work or study a year later? What cost per participant is acceptable? What quality threshold in the civilian placements? Setting these gates beforehand is what makes the phasing real: it creates the possibility of an honest "no" — of looking at the evidence and concluding that the model needs redesign before it scales, or that a component does not work. A phased rollout in which every phase automatically proceeds regardless of results is not phased at all; it is a full rollout in slow motion. The gates have to be able to bite.

## Phase Two: Scale It

If Phase One proves the model works and establishes what it costs, Phase Two scales it from a pilot into a national programme — still voluntary, but now operating across the country and at a size that begins to matter, on the order of a cohort in the low hundreds of thousands.

The central challenge of Phase Two is not the model — that was proven in Phase One — but **delivery capacity**. Running the service for a hundred thousand or more young people a year requires training facilities, instructors, placement infrastructure across education, health, and public works, administrative systems, and supervisory capacity at a scale far beyond the pilot. Phase Two is, above all, the phase in which that capacity is deliberately built: the SANDF's training capacity expanded toward the volumes universal service will eventually require, the civilian placement systems scaled up, the institutional machinery matured. This is why the phase takes years rather than months, and why it cannot be rushed — capacity built too fast is capacity built badly.

Phase Two remains **voluntary**, and this is a deliberate strategic choice, not merely caution about compulsion. A voluntary service that has to attract participants is a service under healthy pressure to be worth attending. If, in Phase Two, the benefits designed in Part 2 are real and delivered, young people will choose the service in large numbers; if they are not, the shortfall in volunteers is itself a signal that the model is not yet ready to be made compulsory. Voluntariness in Phase Two functions as a continuous market test of whether the service has earned the prestige and benefits that would justify eventually requiring it. A service that cannot attract volunteers has no business being made mandatory; one that attracts them in growing numbers is demonstrating exactly the legitimacy that the final phase would need.

It is worth naming the specific capacities Phase Two has to build, because this is where the SANDF-capacity question raised in Part 2 must finally be answered rather than deferred. South Africa's defence force, as it currently stands, has nowhere near the capacity to run basic training for a national cohort, and that constraint is one of the central practical facts the whole rollout must respect. Phase Two is the period in which that training capacity is deliberately expanded — instructors recruited and trained, training facilities built or repurposed, the throughput of the training pipeline grown year by year toward the volumes a near-universal service would eventually need. The same deliberate expansion applies on the civilian side: the systems that place and supervise tens of thousands of young people in schools, clinics, and build sites must be grown into systems that can place and supervise many times that number without the quality collapsing. This capacity-building is slow by nature and cannot be bought overnight; it is the single biggest reason Phase Two is measured in years.

The hardest discipline of Phase Two is the willingness to **scale only as fast as capacity allows**, even when political pressure pushes for faster, more visible expansion. A government that has invested in the service will be tempted to grow the numbers quickly to show results — and that temptation is exactly how quality collapses and the whole effort discredits itself. The phasing has to hold the line that the cohort grows only as fast as the capacity to train, place, supervise, and benefit them grows. Coverage that outruns quality is not progress; it is the manufacture of a future failure. Phase Two succeeds by growing at the right speed, not the fastest one.

Phase Two is also where the **benefits flywheel** from the previous article must be deliberately started. The first national cohorts' benefits must be real and visible, their graduates tracked and celebrated, so that the service builds the reputation that draws the next, larger cohort. Getting the flywheel turning in the right direction during Phase Two is what makes Phase Three conceivable at all.

## Phase Three: Universalise It

Only after the model is proven (Phase One) and the capacity and legitimacy are built (Phase Two) does the question of near-universal, legislated service arise. Phase Three is the destination, and it is reached, if it is reached, on a foundation of evidence and demonstrated capacity rather than on a leap of political faith.

This is the phase that engages the hardest questions, and honesty requires meeting them directly. Making service near-universal means, in some form, requiring it — and a legal requirement to serve runs into genuine constitutional considerations. South Africa's Constitution protects freedom of trade, occupation, and profession, and guarantees equality; a compulsory service scheme has to be designed and justified against those protections. This is not a fatal objection — many constitutional democracies operate compulsory or near-compulsory national service, and the Constitution permits limitations of rights that are reasonable and justifiable — but it is a real one, and it means Phase Three cannot be willed into existence. It must be legislated carefully, justified rigorously, and very likely tested in the courts.

Two design features do much of the work of making near-universal service defensible. The first is the **civilian track**, which — as the Israel comparison showed — provides the route through which freedom of conscience is respected: no one is compelled to bear arms against their convictions, because the civilian streams offer a full and honourable way to serve. The second is the **evidence base built in Phases One and Two**: a near-universal scheme justified by years of demonstrated benefit to participants and country stands on far firmer ground, legally and politically, than one imposed on faith. By the time Phase Three is contemplated, the service should be able to show what it has done for hundreds of thousands of young people who chose it — which is the strongest possible argument for extending it to all.

The word "near-universal" is chosen carefully, because no real system is literally universal, and pretending otherwise invites both impracticality and unfairness. Some young people genuinely cannot serve — those with disabilities or medical conditions incompatible with the training, those whose circumstances make service impossible. A humane and lawful scheme accommodates them through transparent, rule-based exemptions and, where appropriate, adapted roles that let willing participants with disabilities serve in ways suited to them rather than being simply excluded. The design challenge, and it is the one the South Korea comparison flagged as decisive, is to keep the exemptions *narrow, published, and honestly applied*, so that genuine accommodation never becomes the escape hatch through which the connected and the privileged quietly exit the obligation they impose on everyone else. The legitimacy of the whole edifice rests on this. A near-universal service in which the children of the powerful are visibly present, doing real service alongside everyone else, can bind the nation; one in which they are visibly absent, exempted by influence dressed as medical necessity, would do the opposite — confirming the very cynicism about elite impunity that corrodes South African public life. Getting exemptions right is therefore not an administrative detail at the edge of the design. It is, along with the benefits, one of the two or three things on which the entire proposal's legitimacy stands or falls, which is why the fairness safeguards run through every phase rather than appearing only at the end.

It is worth being candid that Phase Three may not arrive on any fixed timetable, and might be reached in stages — first near-universal basic training, with the deployment phase scaled to capacity, before full universality. The phasing is honest about the possibility that the country reaches Phase Two, runs a large, successful, voluntary national service, and decides — on cost, on capacity, or on principle — to stay there rather than compel. That would not be a failure. A large, prestigious, voluntary national service that reaches a substantial share of each cohort would already be a transformative achievement. Universality is the destination the model points toward; reaching Phase Two alone would still be worth the entire journey.

## The Machinery: Who Runs It

A phased rollout needs an institution to run it, and the design of that institution is itself a critical decision — because, as the Reform Agenda pillar of this series argues at length, South Africa's deepest governance failures are failures of institutional design, of authority misaligned with accountability.

There are two broad options. The service could be run by a **dedicated national service authority** — a single, purpose-built institution with clear responsibility for the whole programme. Or it could be run through an **inter-departmental model**, coordinating the defence force (for basic training and the military track), basic education (for the education stream), health (for the health stream), public works (for the infrastructure stream), and a central coordinating body. Each has a characteristic failure mode. A dedicated authority risks becoming a silo disconnected from the sectoral departments whose placements it depends on; an inter-departmental model risks the coordination failure that Part 1 identified as the curse of the existing youth landscape, where everyone is responsible and therefore no one is.

The design principle that should govern the choice is the one the Reform Agenda pillar insists on: **authority must be matched with accountability**. Whoever is responsible for delivering the service must have the actual authority over the resources and decisions that delivery requires, and must be held accountable for clear, measured outcomes. In practice this likely means a dedicated coordinating authority with real power — control over its budget and clear ownership of the end-to-end participant journey — working through binding service-level agreements with the sectoral departments that host placements, rather than either a powerless coordinator or a silo that tries to do everything itself. The exact form is a design question for the legislation; the principle is not negotiable. An institution whose accountability and authority are misaligned will fail in exactly the way South Africa's troubled mega-programmes have failed before.

Whatever its central form, the institution would not deliver alone. It would work through a wide network of **delivery partners** — TVET colleges and the SANDF for training, provinces and municipalities for civilian placements, schools and clinics as host sites, and reputable non-governmental organisations with track records in youth and community work. This is a strength and a risk at once. The strength is that the service can draw on existing capacity rather than building everything from nothing; the risk is that a sprawling partner network is exactly where coordination failures and quality variation creep in. The answer is, again, the authority-accountability principle applied downward: every partner operating under a clear agreement, to defined standards, with funding tied to delivery and the central authority retaining ownership of the participant's overall experience so that no young person falls through the cracks between institutions.

Underpinning all of it must be a single **data backbone**: one system that tracks every participant from intake through training, placement, completion, benefits, and into the years afterward. This is not a back-office nicety. It is what makes the gates of the phased rollout real — you cannot decide whether to proceed on evidence you did not collect — and it is what makes the benefits automatic and the system auditable against capture. Part 1 identified weak measurement as a failure mode of the entire existing landscape; the data backbone is the deliberate correction, and it has to be built into the institution from the first pilot cohort rather than bolted on once the numbers grow too large to reconstruct.

## The Law: An Enabling Act and Its Sequence

A national youth service of this kind requires a legislative foundation, and the law should be sequenced to match the phases rather than attempting everything at once.

The anchor is an **enabling Act** that establishes the service, creates and empowers the institution that runs it, defines the tracks and the benefits, sets the rules for fairness and exemptions, and provides for the funding mechanism. In the early phases, when the service is voluntary, the legislative requirements are lighter — a voluntary programme needs an enabling framework but not the compulsion provisions that raise the hardest constitutional questions. The heavier legislative lift, including any provisions for near-universal or compulsory service, belongs to the transition into Phase Three, by which time the evidence base exists to justify it and the country has had years to debate it democratically.

Surrounding the anchor Act, a number of existing laws would need amendment to enable the service to function — frameworks governing the defence force and its reserve, the employment of education and health assistants, public-works programmes, and the various funding streams the service would consolidate. The legislative programme is therefore not a single Bill but a sequenced package, introduced over the phases in step with what each phase actually requires. Sequencing the law to the rollout, rather than front-loading a single omnibus Act for a programme that does not yet exist at scale, is itself part of the disciplined, evidence-led approach the whole rollout embodies.

## How Long, and Why It Cannot Be Rushed

A reasonable reader will want to know how long all this takes, and the honest answer is uncomfortable in an age of five-year electoral horizons: this is a project measured in many years, not in a single term of government, and pretending otherwise would be the first step toward doing it badly.

Consider what each phase genuinely requires. Phase One — designing the pilot, running it for at least one or two full cohorts, and following those graduates long enough to know whether they ended up in work or enterprise — cannot honestly be compressed below a few years, because the outcome that matters most (where participants are a year or more after completing) takes that long to even observe. Phase Two — building the training and placement capacity to go from tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands, without quality collapsing — is the slowest phase of all, because capacity-building is inherently gradual and the discipline of scaling only as fast as quality allows forbids shortcuts. Phase Three — the democratic debate, the legislation, and very likely the constitutional litigation that near-universal service would attract — adds further years on its own. Add these honestly and the path from a first pilot to a mature, near-universal national service is plausibly a project of a decade or more.

This long horizon is not a weakness to apologise for; it is a feature to defend, and it has a specific political implication worth stating plainly. A programme that spans multiple electoral terms cannot belong to one party or one administration if it is to survive, because the next election will hand it to different hands. It therefore requires something South Africa finds difficult but not impossible: a measure of cross-party consensus that the institution, once begun, will be carried forward by whoever governs. This is a tall order, and it is fair to be sceptical about it. But it is also the kind of long-horizon, beyond-one-term commitment that genuine nation-building has always required — and the framing of the service as a national project rather than a party programme, serving every young South African regardless of who their parents vote for, is part of what could make such consensus reachable. A reform that cannot outlive a single government should not be attempted at this scale; one designed from the start to be a shared national institution has at least a chance of the longevity it needs.

## The Risks, Named Honestly

No proposal of this scale is risk-free, and a serious case names its risks rather than hiding them. Five deserve particular attention, each with a corresponding mitigation built into the design.

**Capture.** A large programme dispensing training, stipends, placements, and enterprise finance is a target for patronage and corruption. The mitigation is the anti-capture design the Reform Agenda pillar develops and the previous article applied to the benefits: rule-based rather than discretionary processes, transparency, published data, and independent oversight, built in from Phase One rather than retrofitted after the rot sets in.

**Fiscal slippage.** Large programmes have a way of costing more than promised. The mitigation is the phasing itself — small, measured early phases that establish real costs before large money is committed — and the disciplined costing the next article insists on.

**Safeguarding.** Putting hundreds of thousands of young people through training and placements creates a serious duty of care: their physical safety, their protection from abuse, the humane conduct of the basic-training crucible. This is a first-order requirement, not a compliance detail, and it must be designed in — vetting, oversight, complaint mechanisms, and a training culture built around safeguarding — from the very first pilot cohort.

**Quality dilution at scale.** A model that works well for twenty thousand may degrade badly at five hundred thousand if quality is not protected as volume grows. The mitigation is, again, the phasing — scaling only as fast as capacity and quality allow — and continuous measurement that can detect dilution before it becomes systemic.

**Civil-military boundaries.** A universal basic-military-training crucible touches the sensitive relationship between the armed forces and civilian society, in a country with a specific history there. The mitigation is careful constitutional and institutional design that keeps the service firmly under civilian democratic control, frames the military element as civic formation rather than militarisation, and maintains the equal status of the civilian tracks.

None of these risks is a reason not to proceed. Each is a reason to proceed *carefully, in phases, with the mitigations built in* — which is exactly what this rollout is designed to do.

## The Phase-Gate Roadmap

The whole rollout can be held in a single picture — a roadmap in which each phase ends at a gate that must be passed, on evidence, before the next begins:

1. **Phase One — Prove It.** *Build:* a voluntary pilot in one or two provinces, tens of thousands of participants, heavily measured. *Gate to pass:* agreed thresholds on completion, graduate outcomes, cost per participant, and placement quality — set in advance. *Honest option at the gate:* redesign, or stop, if the evidence says so.
2. **Phase Two — Scale It.** *Build:* a national, still-voluntary service in the low hundreds of thousands; training and placement capacity deliberately expanded; the benefits flywheel started. *Gate to pass:* demonstrated capacity, sustained voluntary uptake, maintained quality and outcomes at scale, and credible costs.
3. **Phase Three — Universalise It.** *Build:* near-universal, legislated service, justified by the evidence of the prior phases and designed to withstand constitutional scrutiny, with the civilian track protecting freedom of conscience. *Gate to pass:* the democratic and legal mandate to require service, earned rather than assumed.

Read the roadmap and notice what it refuses to do: it refuses to promise universal national service by a date, refuses to switch the whole thing on at once, and refuses to proceed past any gate the evidence has not opened. That discipline is not weakness in the proposal; it is the proposal's answer to the reader's most reasonable objection — *government cannot run anything this big.* The honest reply is: not all at once, it cannot. But proven small, scaled deliberately, and universalised only once earned, it could. This is also why the proposal is, at every stage, falsifiable: if the pilot fails its gates, you learn it cheaply and stop or redesign, rather than discovering the failure after committing the whole budget of a nation. A plan that can be stopped at the first honest "no" is a far safer thing to begin than one that can only be stopped after it has already failed at scale. The next article takes the roadmap and asks the question every gate depends on and every sceptic is entitled to demand answered: what would each phase actually cost?
