## The Bottom Line, Up Front

South Africa spends approximately 0.8% of its GDP on research and development. South Korea spends 4.8%. Israel spends 5.4%. The OECD average sits at 2.7%. That gap — between 0.8% and the 2.5–3% threshold where innovation begins to compound into sustained economic transformation — is the distance South Africa must travel over the next two decades. Every policy decision made in Stage 1 (resource revenue investment) and Stage 2 (education pipelines, regulatory quality, institutional efficiency) either narrows or widens that gap. There is no neutral gear.

Innovation-driven growth is not a buzzword. It is a specific economic stage — Stage 3 in Michael Porter's framework — where a country competes primarily on the creation of new knowledge rather than the extraction of natural resources or the efficient production of existing goods. It is the stage where value lives in intellectual property, not physical assets. Where a semiconductor design worth billions weighs less than a gram. Where a cybersecurity algorithm developed in a Tel Aviv office generates more export revenue per employee than a platinum mine. South Africa is not at this stage. But the countries that are — South Korea, Singapore, Israel, Finland — were not always there either. They arrived through deliberate, patient, compounding investment over 20 to 30 years.

This article maps the terrain of Stage 3: what innovation-driven growth actually requires, where South Africa stands on each dimension, what the benchmark countries did to get there, and what the practical implications are for citizens evaluating economic policy. If you have followed this series from [Resource Optimisation](/articles/eg-2-1-resource-optimisation) through [The Efficiency Transition](/articles/eg-2-3-the-efficiency-transition), you understand the logic: Stage 1 generates the revenue, Stage 2 builds the institutions and human capital, and Stage 3 is the destination where that investment pays off in sustained, broad-based prosperity. The question is not whether South Africa should aim for innovation-driven growth — every serious economic strategy implicitly assumes it. The question is whether the country is making the specific, measurable investments today that will get it there in 2040 or 2045.

The answer, as of now, is mixed. South Africa has genuine innovation pockets — in fintech, mining technology, biotechnology, and renewable energy. It has research universities that produce respectable output. It has a science council system (the CSIR, MRC, ARC, HSRC) with real capability. But the overall innovation ecosystem remains fragmented, underfunded, and poorly connected to commercial application. The venture capital ecosystem is nascent. University-industry collaboration is sporadic rather than systematic. Patent output is modest and declining relative to comparators. Closing these gaps is the work of a generation, not a parliamentary term.

## What Innovation-Driven Growth Actually Means

Michael Porter's competitive development framework, introduced in *The Competitive Advantage of Nations* (1990), identifies four stages through which national economies progress. Stage 1 is factor-driven growth, where competitiveness derives from natural resources and cheap labour — the stage covered in [Resource Optimisation](/articles/eg-2-1-resource-optimisation). Stage 2 is efficiency-driven growth, where competitiveness comes from superior production processes, better education, functioning institutions, and well-developed infrastructure — the stage covered in [The Efficiency Transition](/articles/eg-2-3-the-efficiency-transition). Stage 3 is innovation-driven growth, where competitiveness rests on the ability to produce innovative products and services at the global technology frontier using the most advanced methods.

The distinction matters because each stage requires fundamentally different capabilities and policies. In Stage 1, you compete by having something in the ground — platinum, coal, iron ore — and extracting it at reasonable cost. In Stage 2, you compete by doing things better than your competitors — manufacturing more efficiently, delivering services more reliably, educating workers more effectively. In Stage 3, you compete by doing things that nobody else is doing yet. You are not making existing products more cheaply; you are creating products and services that did not previously exist.

This shift has profound implications for where value sits in an economy. In a resource-driven economy, value is locked in physical assets — the mineral deposit, the farmland, the oil field. In an efficiency-driven economy, value shifts toward organisational capital — the factory floor layout, the supply chain management system, the trained workforce. In an innovation-driven economy, value concentrates in knowledge assets — the patent portfolio, the proprietary algorithm, the research team, the design capability. A country's most valuable economic assets become intangible.

Consider three concrete examples. South Korea's semiconductor industry, anchored by Samsung and SK Hynix, generated over $60 billion in export revenue in 2023. The raw materials in a semiconductor chip — silicon, copper, gold traces — are worth a few dollars. The value is almost entirely in the design knowledge, the manufacturing process (which requires knowledge of physics, chemistry, materials science, and engineering at extreme precision), and the intellectual property protecting both. Israel's cybersecurity sector, which exports roughly $10 billion annually, sells products that are pure knowledge — software code, threat detection algorithms, security architectures. The "raw material" is human ingenuity and mathematical capability. Singapore's biomedical sciences cluster, deliberately cultivated from the late 1990s, now generates approximately $17 billion in annual output, built on research capabilities in genomics, drug discovery, and medical devices.

None of these industries emerged spontaneously. Each was the product of sustained government investment in research and development, deliberate cultivation of human capital pipelines (particularly in STEM fields), creation of institutional frameworks that connected research to commercial application, and patient capital deployed over decades. South Korea began its semiconductor push in the early 1980s; it took 15 years before Korean semiconductors were globally competitive and another decade before the industry became the backbone of the Korean economy. Israel's Yozma venture capital programme launched in 1993; the cybersecurity cluster it helped nurture did not reach critical mass until the 2010s. Singapore's biomedical sciences initiative began in 2000; it took a full decade of investment before the cluster became self-sustaining.

The timeline matters. Innovation-driven growth is a 20-to-30-year project. If South Africa begins making the right investments now — and sustains them through multiple electoral cycles — the innovation-driven economy becomes achievable by the mid-2040s. If those investments are delayed, diluted, or disrupted by policy inconsistency, the timeline extends accordingly.

## R&D Intensity: The Fundamental Metric

Research and development intensity — measured as total R&D expenditure as a percentage of GDP — is the single most important input metric for innovation-driven growth. It is not sufficient on its own (you can spend money on R&D badly), but it is necessary. No country has achieved sustained innovation-driven growth with R&D intensity below 2% of GDP. The threshold for Stage 3 entry, based on the historical experience of countries that have made the transition, appears to be approximately 2.5% of GDP, with the most innovation-intensive economies spending 3% to 5%.

South Africa's R&D intensity, as measured by the most recent National Survey of Research and Experimental Development conducted under the auspices of the Department of Science and Innovation (DSI), sits at approximately 0.8% of GDP. This figure has been roughly stagnant for over a decade — it was 0.73% in 2009, rose to 0.83% in 2014, and has fluctuated around 0.8% since. In absolute terms, South Africa spends approximately R40 billion per year on R&D, of which roughly 40% comes from the public sector (government and higher education) and 60% from the private sector (business enterprise and non-profit organisations).

To understand how far South Africa must travel, consider the R&D intensity trajectories of countries that successfully transitioned to Stage 3. South Korea spent 0.8% of GDP on R&D in 1980, when its GDP per capita (in purchasing power parity terms) was approximately $2,000. By 1990, R&D intensity had risen to 1.7%. By 2000, it was 2.2%. By 2010, it reached 3.3%. Today, at 4.8%, South Korea has the highest R&D intensity in the world, and its GDP per capita has risen to approximately $33,000 — a sixteenfold increase over four decades. The correlation is not coincidence; it reflects the compounding relationship between knowledge investment and economic output.

Israel followed a similar trajectory, from approximately 2% of GDP in the mid-1980s to 5.4% today, though Israel's path was distinctive in its heavy reliance on defence-related R&D that subsequently spun off into civilian applications. Singapore moved from roughly 0.9% in 1990 to 2.2% today, with particularly aggressive government investment in targeted research clusters. Finland rose from 1.2% in 1985 to a peak of 3.7% in 2009 (before declining somewhat after Nokia's collapse, a cautionary tale about over-concentration in a single firm).

:::expert
The Department of Science and Innovation's National Survey of Research and Experimental Development reports that South Africa's gross expenditure on research and development (GERD) as a proportion of GDP has remained below 1% for over a decade. The 2020/21 survey recorded GERD at 0.75% of GDP, with business enterprise expenditure on R&D (BERD) accounting for the largest share at approximately 40% of total GERD. The government and higher education sectors together account for the majority of the remainder. The DSI's target, articulated in the 2019 White Paper on Science, Technology and Innovation, is to reach 1.5% of GDP by 2030 — itself an intermediate target well below the 2.5–3% threshold associated with innovation-driven economies.
:::

Where is South Africa's R&D money actually spent? The public research system is anchored by the science councils — the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR), the Medical Research Council (now the South African Medical Research Council, SAMRC), the Agricultural Research Council (ARC), the Human Sciences Research Council (HSRC), and several smaller entities. The CSIR, the largest, has an annual budget of approximately R3 billion and conducts research across defence, industry, the built environment, and natural resources. The university research system, funded partly through the National Research Foundation (NRF) and partly through direct government grants and contract research, produces the majority of the country's scientific publications.

Private sector R&D is concentrated in a small number of large firms, particularly in mining and minerals processing, financial services, telecommunications, and increasingly in information and communication technology. The structural challenge is that South Africa's private sector R&D is heavily skewed toward "development" (the D in R&D) — adapting existing technologies for local application — rather than "research" (the R) — generating fundamentally new knowledge. This is characteristic of an economy still operating primarily in Stage 2. Moving to Stage 3 requires a shift in the composition of R&D toward more basic and applied research, which in turn requires a much larger pool of researchers, better research infrastructure, and stronger incentives for firms to invest in frontier research rather than incremental improvement.

The path from 0.8% to 2.5% requires roughly tripling total R&D expenditure in real terms. Given that GDP itself will presumably grow over this period, the absolute increase is even larger. This is not achievable through public spending alone; no government budget can absorb that increase. The critical lever is private sector R&D expenditure, which must grow from approximately 0.4% of GDP to 1.5% or more. This requires the kinds of efficiency-stage reforms discussed in [The Efficiency Transition](/articles/eg-2-3-the-efficiency-transition) — regulatory quality that encourages long-term investment, IP protection that gives firms confidence their innovations will be protected, human capital pipelines that produce enough scientists and engineers, and tax incentives (such as South Africa's existing Section 11D R&D tax incentive, which allows a 150% deduction for qualifying R&D expenditure) that are accessible and effective.

:::takeaway
South Africa's R&D intensity of 0.8% of GDP has been stagnant for over a decade. No country has achieved innovation-driven growth below 2% of GDP. The path from 0.8% to 2.5% requires roughly tripling total R&D expenditure, with the bulk of the increase necessarily coming from the private sector. This is a 15-to-20-year trajectory that must begin now with reforms to R&D tax incentives, IP protection, and the human capital pipeline.
:::

## The Venture Capital Ecosystem

Venture capital (VC) is a specific form of financing where investors provide funding to early-stage, high-growth-potential companies in exchange for equity (ownership shares). Unlike bank loans, which must be repaid regardless of the company's performance, venture capital investors accept a high probability of losing their entire investment on any individual deal in exchange for the possibility of extraordinary returns if one of their portfolio companies succeeds spectacularly. This risk tolerance makes venture capital uniquely suited to financing innovation, where most attempts fail but the successes can create enormous value.

The venture capital model works through a cycle. Institutional investors (pension funds, university endowments, sovereign wealth funds, insurance companies) allocate a portion of their portfolios to VC funds managed by professional general partners. These general partners identify, evaluate, and invest in promising startups. They provide not only capital but also strategic guidance, industry connections, and management expertise. Successful portfolio companies eventually "exit" — through acquisition by a larger company or through an initial public offering (IPO) on a stock exchange — returning capital to the fund and its investors. The proceeds are then recycled into new funds and new investments.

The relevance to innovation-driven growth is direct. Most breakthrough innovations emerge from small, nimble companies rather than large incumbents. Large companies are typically better at incremental improvement than radical innovation — their organisational structures, incentive systems, and risk appetites are optimised for efficiency, not experimentation. Venture capital provides the mechanism through which radical innovation gets financed, scaled, and commercialised. The semiconductor revolution, the internet economy, biotechnology, renewable energy, artificial intelligence — every major technology wave of the past 50 years was substantially funded by venture capital.

South Africa's venture capital ecosystem is small but growing. The Southern African Venture Capital and Private Equity Association (SAVCA) reports annual VC investment of approximately R3–4 billion, concentrated heavily in fintech, e-commerce, and software-as-a-service companies. This is a tiny fraction of what comparator innovation economies deploy. Israel, with a population of 9 million (compared to South Africa's 62 million), invests roughly $25 billion per year in venture capital — approximately 100 times South Africa's level on a per-capita basis.

The structural challenges facing South Africa's VC ecosystem are substantial. The pool of investable startups is constrained by the human capital pipeline — there are not enough engineers, scientists, and technical entrepreneurs to generate a critical mass of deep-tech startups (companies built on fundamental scientific or engineering innovations, as opposed to business model innovations). The pool of experienced VC managers is small, and most domestic institutional investors (pension funds and insurance companies that control the bulk of South Africa's savings) have limited appetite for the risk profile of venture capital. Regulatory constraints under the Pension Funds Act and Regulation 28 limit the allocation that retirement funds can make to "alternative" investments including venture capital, though the thresholds have been progressively relaxed.

The most instructive comparator for South Africa is Israel's Yozma programme. In 1993, Israel faced a challenge remarkably similar to South Africa's today: it had strong research capabilities (particularly in defence-related technology and university research), a growing pool of technical talent (boosted by immigration from the former Soviet Union), but virtually no venture capital industry. The government created Yozma ("initiative" in Hebrew), a $100 million fund of funds that co-invested alongside international VC firms to establish ten new venture capital funds. Crucially, Yozma included a buyout option that allowed the private partners to purchase the government's share at a predetermined price if the fund performed well — giving the private investors amplified upside while de-risking their initial commitment.

The results were transformative. Within a decade, Israel went from having essentially no venture capital industry to being the world's most VC-intensive economy on a per-capita basis. The international VC firms that partnered with Yozma brought not only capital but also deal-making expertise, global networks, and knowledge of how to structure and scale technology companies. Israeli entrepreneurs gained access to Silicon Valley's playbook. The programme cost the Israeli government relatively little (the government made a profit on its Yozma investments) while catalysing an industry that now invests tens of billions of dollars annually and has produced companies like Check Point, Waze, Mobileye, and CyberArk.

South Africa has made tentative steps in this direction. The Technology Innovation Agency (TIA), established in 2008, provides some seed and early-stage funding. The Industrial Development Corporation (IDC) has a venture capital arm. The Department of Trade, Industry and Competition supports various incentive schemes. But these efforts are fragmented, underfunded relative to the scale of the challenge, and not structured with the kind of deliberate ecosystem-building logic that characterised Yozma. A systematic approach to venture capital ecosystem development — one that addresses the capital gap, the skills gap, the deal-flow gap, and the institutional investor appetite gap simultaneously — remains a missing piece in South Africa's innovation strategy.

:::takeaway
Venture capital is not charity for startups. It is the financial infrastructure through which breakthrough innovations get funded, scaled, and commercialised. Countries that lack functioning VC ecosystems rarely produce globally competitive innovation companies, regardless of how good their universities or research institutions are. Building this ecosystem requires deliberate government intervention to crowd in private capital — as Israel demonstrated with Yozma — not to replace private investment but to catalyse it.
:::

## University-Industry Collaboration: The Missing Bridge

South Africa's research university system produces respectable output. The country consistently ranks first in Africa for scientific publications. Universities such as the University of Cape Town, Stellenbosch University, the University of the Witwatersrand, and the University of Pretoria maintain research programmes that are competitive by global standards in selected fields — astronomy, palaeontology, infectious disease, materials science, and several others. The Square Kilometre Array (SKA) telescope, being built in the Northern Cape with its operational headquarters in Cape Town, positions South Africa at the frontier of radio astronomy. The country's HIV/AIDS research programme, born of tragic necessity, has produced globally significant work in virology, epidemiology, and vaccine development.

The problem is not the quality of research produced. The problem is the connection — or rather, the disconnection — between that research and commercial application. University technology transfer, the process by which research findings are converted into marketable products and services, remains weak and unsystematic in South Africa compared to leading innovation economies.

To understand why this matters, consider how university-industry collaboration works in successful innovation ecosystems. In the United States, the Bayh-Dole Act of 1980 allowed universities to patent inventions arising from federally funded research and to license those patents to private companies. This seemingly technical legal change unleashed a wave of university-based entrepreneurship. MIT, Stanford, and other research universities became engines of regional economic development, spinning off companies (including Google, which originated in a Stanford research project) and generating billions in licensing revenue. University technology transfer offices (TTOs) became sophisticated operations, staffed by people who understood both the research and the commercial landscape.

South Africa's Intellectual Property Rights from Publicly Financed Research and Development Act (the IPR Act, Act 51 of 2008) was designed to achieve something similar — to ensure that IP generated through public funding was identified, protected, and where possible, commercialised for the benefit of South Africa. The Act established the National Intellectual Property Management Office (NIPMO) within the DSI and required all institutions receiving public R&D funding to establish offices of technology transfer (OTTs).

The implementation has been uneven. Many university OTTs are understaffed, underskilled, and overwhelmed by administrative compliance requirements. The Act's provisions, while well-intentioned, created bureaucratic processes that can slow rather than accelerate technology transfer. Researchers often perceive the IP disclosure and protection process as burdensome rather than beneficial. The commercial skills needed to evaluate whether a research finding has market potential, to negotiate licensing deals, to structure spin-off companies, and to connect academic inventors with entrepreneurial talent are scarce.

The structural barriers go deeper than administrative efficiency. South African academics are primarily incentivised through publication metrics — the number and quality of research papers they produce, measured through the Department of Higher Education and Training's (DHET) research output subsidy system. This system, which channels billions of rands to universities based on publication counts, creates strong incentives to publish but relatively weak incentives to commercialise. An academic who spends a year developing a patent and licensing it to a company receives far less institutional recognition than one who publishes five journal articles in the same period. Until the incentive structure values commercial application alongside academic publication, the behaviour of researchers will not change at the scale needed.

The CSIR and other science councils face a different version of the same challenge. The CSIR has produced significant technologies — in laser-based manufacturing, biosensor development, satellite systems, and other domains — but the pipeline from CSIR laboratory to scaled commercial product remains leaky. Successful technology transfer requires not just a working prototype but a sustained commitment to product development, market testing, manufacturing scale-up, and customer acquisition — activities that sit outside the core competence and mandate of a research council.

What works in successful innovation economies is not a single mechanism but an ecosystem of connections. This includes joint research programmes where companies co-fund university research on problems relevant to their industry, with shared access to resulting IP. It includes industrial research chairs, where companies endow professorships focused on commercially relevant research domains. It includes research parks and incubators physically located on or adjacent to university campuses, creating daily interaction between researchers and entrepreneurs. It includes student placement programmes where doctoral candidates spend time in industry, building relationships and understanding commercial needs. It includes clear, streamlined IP ownership and licensing frameworks that make it easy rather than bureaucratically painful to move technology from lab to market.

:::expert
The National Advisory Council on Innovation's assessments of South Africa's national system of innovation have consistently identified the weak linkage between knowledge-producing institutions and the productive economy as a systemic constraint. The "innovation chasm" — the gap between research output and commercial application — is not primarily a funding problem but a structural and institutional problem. It reflects misaligned incentives, inadequate technology transfer capabilities, and insufficient interaction between researchers and the firms that could commercialise their work. Strengthening these linkages requires systemic interventions across the entire innovation value chain, not isolated programme-level fixes.
:::

## IP Generation and Patent Output

Patents are an imperfect but useful measure of innovation output. A patent is a legal right granted by a government that gives the inventor exclusive rights to make, use, and sell an invention for a specified period (typically 20 years). To obtain a patent, the invention must be novel (new), non-obvious (involving an inventive step), and useful (capable of industrial application). Patent data tells you something about the rate at which an economy is generating commercially relevant new knowledge.

South Africa's Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC) administers the national patent system. The country files approximately 7,000 to 8,000 patent applications per year with CIPC, though the majority of these are filed by foreign applicants seeking patent protection in South Africa for inventions developed elsewhere. The number of patents filed by South African residents is substantially lower — approximately 1,000 to 1,500 per year.

For international comparison, the more meaningful metric is filings under the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT), an international system administered by the World Intellectual Property Organisation (WIPO) that allows inventors to seek patent protection across multiple countries simultaneously. PCT filings are a good proxy for innovation that is competitive at a global level, since the cost and complexity of international patent applications mean they are typically only filed for inventions with significant commercial potential.

South Africa's PCT filings number approximately 300 to 400 per year. South Korea files approximately 20,000. Israel files approximately 2,000. Singapore files approximately 1,200. Even adjusting for population differences, South Africa's per-capita PCT filing rate is a fraction of these comparators. And the trend is not improving — South Africa's share of global PCT filings has been declining over the past decade as other emerging economies (particularly China, which now leads the world in PCT filings) have accelerated.

What does patent data reveal about South Africa's innovation capacity? The low volume of resident filings suggests that relatively little commercially relevant new knowledge is being generated domestically, or that knowledge being generated is not being captured through the patent system. The concentration of filings in a small number of institutions and firms — the CSIR, a handful of universities, and a few large companies — suggests that innovation activity is not broadly distributed across the economy. The sectoral distribution of filings (weighted toward mining and minerals processing, agriculture, and basic manufacturing) reflects the resource-and-efficiency-driven structure of the current economy rather than an emerging knowledge economy.

It is worth noting that patents are not the only form of intellectual property relevant to innovation. Trade secrets, copyrights (particularly for software), trademarks, and design rights all play roles. South Africa's software sector, for instance, generates significant innovation that is typically protected through copyright rather than patents. The fintech innovations discussed below are often protected through a combination of copyright, trade secrets, and first-mover advantage rather than patents. Nevertheless, patent data remains the most standardised international comparison metric, and South Africa's performance on this metric should be a concern for anyone tracking the country's innovation trajectory.

:::takeaway
Patent output is a lagging indicator — it reflects investments made five to ten years earlier in research, education, and institutional capacity. South Africa's current patent performance reflects the R&D investment (or underinvestment) decisions of the 2010s. The patent output of the 2030s will reflect the decisions being made now. This is why R&D intensity, education pipeline quality, and university-industry collaboration are leading indicators that deserve more attention than they typically receive in political debate.
:::

## South Africa's Existing Innovation Pockets

The picture painted so far — low R&D intensity, nascent venture capital, weak technology transfer, modest patent output — is accurate at the system level. But it would be misleading to suggest that South Africa has no innovation capacity. Several sectors demonstrate genuine, globally competitive innovation capability, and understanding these pockets is important for two reasons: they show what is possible, and they suggest where Stage 3 capabilities might most naturally develop.

**Fintech and digital financial services.** South Africa's financial services sector is, by some measures, the most sophisticated in the developing world. The Johannesburg Stock Exchange (JSE) is the largest in Africa and among the most advanced globally in terms of trading technology. South African banks were early adopters of mobile and digital banking. The country's fintech ecosystem has produced companies that operate across Africa and beyond, leveraging South Africa's combination of financial services expertise, technology talent, and understanding of emerging market conditions. Mobile payment systems, digital lending platforms, insurance technology (insurtech), and regulatory technology (regtech) are all areas where South African firms demonstrate genuine innovation — not merely copying Silicon Valley models but developing solutions adapted to the specific conditions of African markets.

**Mining technology.** South Africa's deep-level gold and platinum mines are among the most technically challenging mining operations in the world, operating at depths of up to four kilometres underground in extreme heat and pressure. The engineering challenges of deep-level mining have driven innovation in rock mechanics, ventilation, drilling technology, mine safety systems, and more recently in automation and robotics. Companies like Sandvik, Epiroc, and their South African partners have developed mining technologies in South Africa that are subsequently exported globally. The Mandela Mining Precinct, a collaborative research initiative, brings together the Minerals Council South Africa, the CSIR, and mining companies to develop next-generation mining technologies.

**Biotechnology and health sciences.** South Africa's response to the HIV/AIDS epidemic, while politically delayed, ultimately produced world-class capabilities in virology, epidemiology, clinical trials, and vaccine research. The establishment of the BRICS Vaccine Research and Development Centre in South Africa recognises this capability. During the COVID-19 pandemic, South African researchers at institutions including the KwaZulu-Natal Research Innovation and Sequencing Platform (KRISP) were among the first to identify new virus variants, demonstrating genomic surveillance capabilities that are among the best globally. Afrigen Biologics and Vaccines' work on developing mRNA vaccine manufacturing technology for Africa, supported by the World Health Organisation, represents a potential breakthrough in building domestic biopharmaceutical manufacturing capacity.

**Renewable energy.** The Renewable Energy Independent Power Producer Procurement Programme (REIPPPP), despite its political turbulence, has attracted significant investment and technology transfer into South Africa's renewable energy sector. Local content requirements have driven the development of domestic manufacturing capability in solar photovoltaic and wind energy components. South African engineering firms have developed expertise in renewable energy project design and construction that is increasingly being deployed across Africa. The country's exceptional solar and wind resources create natural advantages for innovation in renewable energy technology.

**Astronomy and space science.** South Africa's investment in the MeerKAT radio telescope (precursor to the SKA) has built world-class capabilities in radio frequency engineering, signal processing, data science, and precision manufacturing. These capabilities have commercial applications beyond astronomy — in telecommunications, defence, and data analytics. The South African National Space Agency (SANSA) maintains capabilities in satellite operations, earth observation, and space weather monitoring.

These pockets demonstrate that innovation-driven growth is not starting from zero in South Africa. The challenge is scaling from isolated pockets to a broad-based innovation ecosystem. Each of these sectors illustrates a common pattern: where South Africa has made sustained investment, developed deep domain expertise, and connected to global networks, innovation follows. Where investment has been sporadic, expertise thin, and connections weak, innovation stalls.

## The Political Challenge: Twenty-Year Investments in Five-Year Cycles

Here is the fundamental political problem of innovation-driven growth: the investments required take 20 to 30 years to mature, while democratic elections occur every five years. A government that increases R&D spending, invests in research infrastructure, reforms the university-industry interface, and builds venture capital ecosystems will not see the results during its term of office. The economic payoff accrues to whoever is in power two or three decades later. The costs — both the direct fiscal costs and the political opportunity cost of spending money on research rather than on more immediately visible programmes — are borne now.

This creates a systematic bias against innovation investment in democratic systems. It is always politically easier to spend money on things that produce visible results within an electoral cycle — housing, social grants, road construction — than on things whose benefits materialise in the distant future. This is not a criticism of any particular political party or government; it is a structural feature of democratic politics that affects every country.

The countries that have overcome this bias share certain characteristics. South Korea's innovation investment was sustained through multiple changes of government (and even changes of regime — from military dictatorship to democracy) because the broad strategic direction was embedded in institutions rather than dependent on individual leaders. The Korea Institute of Science and Technology (KIST), the Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology (KAIST), and the chaebols' R&D commitments created institutional momentum that survived political transitions. Singapore's approach relied on the continuity of the People's Action Party's governance, which — whatever its democratic deficiencies — allowed for genuinely long-term planning. Israel's innovation ecosystem was built through a combination of defence-driven necessity (which created bipartisan support for technology investment) and specific institutional innovations like Yozma that were designed to be self-sustaining once launched.

For South Africa, this challenge connects directly to the institutional architecture discussed elsewhere in this series. The Department of Science and Innovation, the National Advisory Council on Innovation, the Technology Innovation Agency, the National Research Foundation, and the science councils collectively constitute the institutional infrastructure for innovation policy. Their effectiveness — their funding levels, their leadership quality, their strategic coherence, their insulation from short-term political pressures — will determine whether South Africa's innovation investment is sustained over the decades required.

This is why the execution architecture matters, as explored in [The Execution Machine](/articles/eg-3-2-the-execution-machine). Innovation policy cannot be effective if it is subject to the same implementation failures that afflict other areas of government. R&D tax incentives that take years to process do not incentivise. Technology transfer offices that lack skilled staff do not transfer technology. Venture capital co-investment programmes that are mired in procurement bureaucracy do not stimulate venture capital. The quality of execution determines whether innovation policy achieves its intended outcomes or merely generates impressive-looking strategy documents.

:::expert
International experience, as documented by the World Bank's Innovation Policy Platform, demonstrates that successful national innovation systems require policy stability and institutional continuity spanning multiple political cycles. Countries that have transitioned to innovation-driven growth — including South Korea, Finland, and Israel — maintained broadly consistent innovation strategies for periods of 15 to 25 years, even as governments changed. This does not mean policies were never adjusted; it means the strategic direction, the institutional architecture, and the commitment to rising R&D investment were sustained. Countries where innovation policy was subject to frequent reversals, institutional restructuring, or stop-start funding patterns consistently underperformed relative to their research capabilities.
:::

:::takeaway
The gap between South Africa's current innovation performance and the Stage 3 threshold is large but not unprecedented. South Korea was in a comparable position in 1980. The critical variable is not starting position but sustained commitment. Every country that reached innovation-driven growth did so through 20 to 30 years of consistent, compounding investment in R&D, human capital, and institutional capacity. The question for South African citizens is whether the policy environment — across all parties and all levels of government — supports that kind of sustained commitment.
:::

## Practical Framework: The Innovation Ecosystem Scanner

Assessing whether South Africa is on track for innovation-driven growth requires tracking four interconnected dimensions. Each can be monitored using publicly available data, giving you — as a citizen, voter, or analyst — the tools to evaluate innovation policy claims against observable evidence.

**Dimension 1: R&D investment.** The core metric is gross expenditure on research and development (GERD) as a percentage of GDP, broken down by funding source (government, business enterprise, higher education, non-profit). The primary data source is the National Survey of Research and Experimental Development, conducted by the Centre for Science, Technology and Innovation Indicators (CeSTII) at the HSRC on behalf of the DSI. This survey is published every one to two years, with a significant time lag (the most recent data typically refers to expenditure two to three years prior). Supplementary data is available from the UNESCO Institute for Statistics, which maintains a global R&D expenditure database using standardised definitions, and from the OECD's Main Science and Technology Indicators database (South Africa is included as a partner country). The trend to watch is whether GERD is rising as a share of GDP — particularly whether business enterprise R&D expenditure (BERD) is growing, since this is where the largest increases must come.

**Dimension 2: Patent and IP output.** Patent filings are available from two sources. Domestic filings are published by CIPC in its annual reports and in the Intellectual Property Office Journal. International (PCT) filings are tracked by WIPO in its annual World Intellectual Property Indicators report and are searchable through WIPO's PATENTSCOPE database. The metrics to track are: total resident patent filings per year (trend and level), PCT filings per year (trend and level), patent filings by sector (which sectors are generating IP?), and patent filings by institution (are universities and public research institutions increasing their output?). WIPO's Global Innovation Index, published annually, provides a composite ranking that includes patent-related metrics alongside many other indicators.

**Dimension 3: Venture capital activity.** The primary domestic data source is the annual SAVCA Venture Capital Survey, which reports on funds raised, investments made, investment by sector, investment by stage (seed, early, growth), and exits. International comparisons are available from sources including the OECD's Entrepreneurship at a Glance publication, PitchBook (some data is publicly available), and the Global Entrepreneurship Monitor (GEM), which includes venture capital and entrepreneurship metrics. The key metrics are: total VC investment as a percentage of GDP, number of deals per year, sectoral distribution of investment (is VC reaching deep-tech or only fintech and e-commerce?), and the number and size of successful exits (which signal ecosystem maturity).

**Dimension 4: University-industry linkages.** This dimension is the hardest to measure with a single metric, but several indicators are informative. The annual reports of NIPMO track IP disclosures, patent applications, and licensing agreements arising from publicly funded research. University annual reports include information on research partnerships with industry, contract research revenue, spin-off companies, and technology transfer activities. The HSRC's innovation surveys periodically assess the extent of collaboration between firms and public research institutions. The metrics to watch are: number of IP disclosures per year from publicly funded research, number of licensing agreements, revenue from technology licensing, number of university spin-off companies, and the volume of contract research (which indicates direct industry demand for university research capabilities).

By tracking these four dimensions over time, you can form a grounded assessment of whether South Africa's innovation ecosystem is developing at a pace consistent with reaching Stage 3 within the target timeframe. A simple traffic-light approach works: if a dimension is improving at a rate consistent with reaching benchmark levels within 20 to 25 years, mark it green; if it is improving but too slowly, mark it amber; if it is stagnant or declining, mark it red. As of the early 2020s, an honest assessment would mark all four dimensions amber at best, with patent output and university-industry linkages closer to red.

This framework is not academic exercise. It is a tool for accountability. When a political party claims to support innovation-driven growth, you can ask: what has happened to R&D intensity under your governance? Are patent filings rising or falling? Is venture capital investment growing? Are university-industry linkages strengthening? The answers are in the data, and the data is publicly available. Use it.

**The Innovation Ecosystem Scanner**

When evaluating whether South Africa is making real progress toward innovation-driven growth, work through these four dimensions using publicly available data:

**Dimension 1: Is R&D investment rising?**

Check the National Survey of Research and Experimental Development (published by CeSTII at the HSRC on behalf of the DSI) for gross expenditure on research and development (GERD) as a percentage of GDP. Break it down by funding source — government, business enterprise, higher education, non-profit. The critical question is whether GERD is rising as a share of GDP, particularly whether business enterprise R&D expenditure (BERD) is growing. South Africa needs to move from 0.8% toward 2.5% of GDP. Supplementary data is available from the UNESCO Institute for Statistics and the OECD Main Science and Technology Indicators database.

**Dimension 2: Is patent output increasing?**

Check domestic filings through CIPC annual reports and international (PCT) filings through WIPO's PATENTSCOPE database and World Intellectual Property Indicators report. Track total resident patent filings per year, PCT filings per year, filings by sector, and filings by institution. Rising patent output — especially from universities and public research institutions — signals a growing innovation pipeline.

**Dimension 3: Is venture capital activity expanding?**

Check the annual SAVCA Venture Capital Survey for funds raised, investments made, sectoral distribution, investment by stage (seed, early, growth), and exits. Compare total VC investment as a percentage of GDP against international benchmarks. The key question is whether VC is reaching deep-tech sectors or remains concentrated in fintech and e-commerce.

Check NIPMO annual reports for IP disclosures, patent applications, and licensing agreements from publicly funded research. Review university annual reports for contract research revenue, spin-off companies, and industry partnerships. Rising numbers of disclosures, licences, and spin-offs signal that the bridge between research and commercial application is being built.

Apply a traffic-light assessment to each dimension: green if improving at a rate consistent with reaching benchmark levels within 20 to 25 years, amber if improving but too slowly, red if stagnant or declining. When a political party claims to support innovation-driven growth, use these four dimensions to test the claim against the evidence.

## Resources

The following sources provide the data and context needed to track South Africa's progress toward innovation-driven growth.

**Department of Science and Innovation** (www.dst.gov.za) — publishes national R&D survey data, the White Paper on Science, Technology and Innovation, and annual performance reports.

**National Advisory Council on Innovation (NACI)** — publishes periodic assessments of South Africa's national system of innovation.

**Technology Innovation Agency (TIA)** — publishes annual reports on its investment activities and technology transfer outcomes.

**National Intellectual Property Management Office (NIPMO)** — publishes annual reports on IP from publicly financed R&D, including disclosures, patents, and licensing activity.

**CSIR, SAMRC, ARC, and HSRC** — each publishes annual reports and research output summaries detailing their R&D activities and outputs.

**Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC)** (www.cipc.co.za) — provides access to patent filings and annual IP statistics.

**World Intellectual Property Organisation (WIPO)** (www.wipo.int) — publishes the annual World Intellectual Property Indicators report and the Global Innovation Index, and maintains the PATENTSCOPE patent search database.

**SAVCA** (www.savca.co.za) — publishes annual venture capital and private equity surveys for Southern Africa.

**Global Entrepreneurship Monitor South Africa** — published annually by Stellenbosch University Business School in partnership with the Small Enterprise Development Agency (Seda), includes data on entrepreneurship and early-stage financing.

**OECD Science, Technology and Industry Outlook** and **Main Science and Technology Indicators** — provide international R&D and innovation comparisons (South Africa is included as a partner country).

**UNESCO Institute for Statistics** (uis.unesco.org) — maintains a global database of science, technology, and innovation indicators with standardised definitions.

**Korea Institute of Science and Technology Evaluation and Planning (KISTEP)** — publishes English-language reports on South Korea's innovation trajectory.

**Israel Innovation Authority** — publishes annual reports on Israel's innovation ecosystem, including venture capital data and programme evaluations.

**National Research Foundation of Singapore** — publishes Research, Innovation and Enterprise plans detailing Singapore's innovation strategy.
