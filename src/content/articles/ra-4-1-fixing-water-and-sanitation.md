## The Function Where Governance Failure Kills People

Water is the function where municipal governance failure kills people. Not figuratively. In May 2023, a cholera outbreak centred on Hammanskraal, north of Pretoria, killed at least 47 South Africans and hospitalised more than a hundred. The root cause was not a natural disaster or an unforeseeable pathogen. It was the Rooiwal Wastewater Treatment Works — a City of Tshwane facility that had been dysfunctional for years, discharging polluted water into the Apies River, which flows into Leeukraal Dam, from which the Temba Water Treatment Works abstracts drinking water. The area had not had reliably clean water for almost two decades. The South African Human Rights Commission stated plainly: "It can happen anywhere in the country."

## The Water Value Chain

It can. And to understand why, you need to trace the water value chain from source to tap and see exactly where the governance architecture breaks.

The chain begins with the Department of Water and Sanitation at the national level. DWS is responsible for water as a resource — the National Water Act of 1998 vests all water in the nation and assigns national government legislative and executive authority over freshwater resources. DWS sets national water policy, issues water use licences, manages water resource infrastructure (dams, inter-basin transfers, bulk supply systems), and is supposed to regulate water services delivery through the Water Services Act of 1997.

The next link is the Water Services Authority. The Water Services Act designates certain municipalities — **currently 144 of South Africa's 278 municipalities** — as WSAs. A WSA has the legal duty to ensure access to water services within its area of jurisdiction. It must plan, regulate, and ensure the provision of water supply and sanitation services. It sets by-laws, determines tariffs, and is responsible for ensuring that the services meet national norms and standards.

Then comes the Water Services Provider. The WSA does not have to provide the services itself. The Water Services Act envisages that a WSA may appoint a separate entity — a water board, a private contractor, a municipal entity, or another municipality — as the WSP to actually deliver the services. *The WSA is the regulator. The WSP is the operator.* The WSA sets the standards. The WSP meets them. The WSA monitors compliance. The WSP reports performance.

That is the design. Here is the reality: **in almost every case, the municipality that is the WSA is also its own WSP.** The same entity that is supposed to regulate water services also delivers them. The same officials who set the standards are the ones who must meet them. The same council that should be holding the operator accountable is the political authority over the operator. The WSA writes the rules. The WSP — which is the same municipality — follows them. Or does not follow them. Because who is going to enforce compliance when the regulator and the provider sit in the same building, report to the same municipal manager, and answer to the same council?

This is the conflation pattern that [Article 1.2](/articles/ra-1-2-why-institutions-fail) identified as one of the five structural failure modes in South African governance. And water is where the pattern is most fully expressed.

Between the national department and the municipality sit the nine Water Boards — Rand Water, Umgeni Water, Amatola Water, Bloem Water, Lepelle Northern Water, Magalies Water, Mhlathuze Water, Overberg Water, and Sedibeng Water. Water Boards provide bulk water supply to WSAs. They abstract, treat, and distribute water in bulk. Some are well-managed and financially stable — Rand Water supplies bulk water to Gauteng municipalities with high reliability. Others are struggling with their own governance and financial challenges. But Water Boards do not deliver water to your tap. They deliver to the municipality. What happens between the municipal reservoir and your household is entirely in the hands of the WSA/WSP — which, as established, is usually the same entity.

The value chain, then, has four institutional layers: DWS (national policy and resource management) → Water Board (bulk supply) → WSA (regulation and planning) → WSP (reticulation and delivery to consumers). The critical handoff — the one that determines whether clean water comes out of your tap — is between the WSA and the WSP. And when those two functions are conflated in a single municipality, the handoff does not happen. There is no regulation. There is no oversight. There is a municipality that provides water to itself, monitors itself, and reports on its own compliance.

:::takeaway
The water value chain has four institutional layers, but the critical governance failure occurs at the municipal level where the Water Services Authority (regulator) and Water Services Provider (operator) are the same entity. This conflation means the municipality sets its own standards, monitors its own performance, and enforces its own compliance — which in practice means no real regulation at all.
:::

## RAARICLE™ Diagnosis

### Responsibility — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

Who does the work of delivering water to South African households? The answer depends on where you are and which part of the value chain you mean. DWS is responsible for water as a resource and for regulating water services — but in practice its regulatory capacity is limited and its attention is divided between resource management and service regulation. Water Boards are responsible for bulk supply — but they deliver to the municipal boundary, not to households. The municipality, as WSA, is responsible for ensuring that services are provided. The municipality, as WSP, is responsible for actually providing them.

The problem is not that nobody is responsible. It is that the responsibilities are layered in a way that creates gaps at every handoff. DWS sets norms and standards but cannot ensure that municipalities meet them. Water Boards deliver bulk water of a certain quality but cannot control what happens to it in the municipal reticulation network. The municipality receives the water, treats it further (or does not), stores it (or does not maintain the reservoirs), distributes it through pipes (many of which are leaking), and delivers it to the consumer — or fails to.

When the system fails — when cholera breaks out in Hammanskraal, when sewage flows untreated into rivers, when taps run dry — every entity in the chain can point to its own piece and say it did its part. DWS published the norms. The Water Board delivered the bulk water. The municipality is the WSA and the WSP and was supposed to handle everything from there. But the municipality that is responsible for everything from treatment to billing to pipe maintenance to wastewater management often lacks the capacity to do any of it well.

The severity rating of 2 reflects a system where responsibility exists on paper — you can point to the legislation that assigns each entity its role — but where end-to-end ownership of the outcome (clean water from source to tap) is functionally absent. No single entity wakes up in the morning responsible for the entire chain.

### Accountability — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

The municipal council is the democratic accountability point for water services. Councillors approve the water tariff. Councillors approve the budget that funds water operations. Councillors appoint the municipal manager who oversees the water department. In theory, if water services fail, the council is answerable to the community that elected it.

In practice, the accountability chain is weak at every link. Most councillors lack the technical knowledge to evaluate water operations. They see a budget line item for water services. They see a tariff proposal. They do not see Blue Drop scores in real time. They do not understand process controller classifications. They cannot evaluate whether the wastewater treatment works is meeting discharge standards. They rely on management to tell them whether water services are functioning — and management has every incentive to present a positive picture, because the alternative is admitting failure.

The Auditor-General audits municipal water functions as part of the annual MFMA audit cycle, but the AG's primary focus is financial compliance — whether money was spent according to regulations, whether procurement was lawful, whether financial statements are accurate. **The AG can identify irregular expenditure on water tankers (in 2023/24, 59 municipalities spent R2.32 billion on water tankers, of which R419 million was irregular expenditure) but cannot compel a municipality to fix its treatment plant.** *Financial accountability and service delivery accountability run on separate tracks.*

DWS has an accountability role as the national regulator — it publishes Blue Drop, Green Drop, and No Drop reports that rate municipal water performance. But these reports are assessment tools, not enforcement mechanisms. A municipality can receive a catastrophic Green Drop score and face no binding consequence. **The 2023 Green Drop assessment found that 64 per cent of the country's 850 wastewater treatment works were at high or critical risk of discharging partially or untreated water. Ninety of 144 WSAs were found to be discharging partially treated or untreated sewage.** And yet the same municipalities continue to operate the same failing plants, because the accountability mechanism stops at publishing the score.

:::expert
The Auditor-General's 2023/24 MFMA audit findings on water services revealed a system in fiscal and operational crisis. Across all Water Services Authorities, the AG reported R14.89 billion in water losses, with 74 municipalities exceeding the 30 per cent loss threshold that is already well above international benchmarks. The AG found that 99 per cent of water treatment plants failed at least one quality standard, 44 per cent breached chemical safety requirements, and nearly half of municipalities recorded unsafe drinking water systems. For wastewater, 90 per cent of treatment plants failed to meet at least one quality standard. The AG noted that municipalities spent an average of 3 per cent of the value of their property, plant and equipment on maintenance — less than half the recommended 8 per cent. These findings describe a system that is financially accountable (the AG can see the money) but operationally unaccountable (nobody is compelled to fix what the money reveals).
:::

### Authority — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

Apply the 30-day authority test. A municipal manager identifies that the town's water treatment plant is producing water that fails Blue Drop microbiological standards. Can the municipal manager fix this within 30 days using only municipal authority?

The municipal manager cannot increase the water tariff without council approval — and council may resist a tariff increase before elections. The municipal manager cannot hire a qualified process controller if the post is not on the approved organogram — and amending the organogram requires council resolution and consultation processes. The municipal manager cannot procure emergency chemicals or replacement equipment without following the Municipal Finance Management Act's procurement procedures — which, even with emergency provisions, can take weeks. The municipal manager cannot force the provincial government to approve additional funding. The municipal manager cannot compel DWS to provide technical assistance on an accelerated timeline.

What the municipal manager can do within 30 days is issue a boil-water notice and request emergency funding from the council. That is a symptom-management response, not a fix.

The authority deficit deepens when you consider tariff-setting. Municipalities set their own water tariffs, but there is currently no independent economic regulator for water services — no equivalent of NERSA for electricity. Municipalities determine tariffs without oversight or regulatory standards. Some municipalities set tariffs too low (often for political reasons — keeping tariffs low before elections), which means water revenue cannot cover the cost of treatment, distribution, and infrastructure maintenance. Some municipalities set tariffs that are notionally cost-reflective but fail to collect them — revenue collection rates in many municipalities fall well below 80 per cent.

The Water Services Authority has the legal authority to contract a separate WSP — a Water Board, a private operator, or a municipal entity — to provide services. But in practice, the political dynamics of municipal governance make this extremely difficult. Outsourcing water provision to a Water Board or a private operator means giving up political control over jobs, procurement, and tariffs — all of which are politically valuable patronage resources. The authority to appoint a separate WSP exists in law. The political will to exercise that authority is almost always absent.

### Resources — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

**Water revenue in South African municipalities is not ring-fenced.** This is the single most destructive design feature of the current system. When a household pays its water bill, that revenue enters the municipality's general account. It can be — and routinely is — used to cross-subsidise other municipal functions. A water surplus can fund general administration, pay councillor salaries, or cover shortfalls in other departments. The water infrastructure that generated the revenue does not receive preferential claim on it.

The consequences are predictable and devastating. **Municipalities spend an average of 3 per cent of the value of their water infrastructure on maintenance, against a recommended minimum of 8 per cent.** Treatment plants deteriorate. Pipes leak. Reservoirs are not cleaned. Pumps fail. And when the infrastructure degrades to the point of failure, the municipality turns to emergency measures — water tankers. In 2023/24, 59 municipalities collectively spent R2.32 billion on water tankers. That is R2.32 billion that could have been spent on maintaining the infrastructure that would have made the tankers unnecessary.

The national government transfers R62 billion annually to municipalities for water and sanitation through the equitable share and conditional grants. But the equitable share is unconditional — municipalities decide how to spend it. Conditional grants are ring-fenced at the grant level but flow into municipal systems where implementation depends on municipal capacity. The Municipal Infrastructure Grant funds new water infrastructure, but municipalities often struggle to spend it — not because the money is not there but because the capacity to plan, procure, and manage infrastructure projects is absent.

The infrastructure maintenance backlog illustrates the scale of the resource failure. **DWS estimates that fixing the maintenance backlog across 105 municipalities rated critical or poor will cost R400 billion.** This figure excludes metros and national or regional bulk systems, so the true total is higher. Against this R400 billion backlog, the entire national water and sanitation budget for 2025/26 is approximately R26 billion. At current spending rates, the backlog will grow, not shrink. Every year of deferred maintenance makes the eventual fix more expensive.

*Non-revenue water* — water that enters the system but is never billed for, because of leaks, theft, metering failures, or administrative losses — compounds the resource problem. **The 2023 No Drop report found that national non-revenue water had reached 47 per cent, up from 37 per cent in 2014.** Of the 4.39 billion cubic metres that entered municipal systems in 2022/23, 2.08 billion cubic metres — 47.4 per cent — was written off. Even Johannesburg, the country's economic hub, loses 48.4 per cent of its water. eThekwini loses 58 per cent. These are not minor leakages. They represent a system that wastes nearly half of the water it produces.

:::takeaway
The combination of non-ring-fenced water revenue, chronic underinvestment in maintenance (3 per cent of asset value versus the recommended 8 per cent), and 47 per cent non-revenue water creates a vicious resource cycle: revenue leaks out of the water function to subsidise other departments, infrastructure deteriorates, water losses increase, less revenue is collected, and the cycle deepens. The R400 billion infrastructure backlog is the accumulated cost of decades of this cycle.
:::

### Information — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

The information picture for water services is better than for most municipal functions — and still deeply inadequate. The Blue Drop, Green Drop, and No Drop reporting system, reinstated by Operation Vulindlela after lapsing for nearly a decade, provides the most systematic assessment of municipal water performance in any sector. The 2023 reports assessed 958 water supply systems (Blue Drop), 850 wastewater treatment works (Green Drop), and non-revenue water across all WSAs. These are valuable diagnostic tools.

But the Drop reports are periodic assessments, not real-time monitoring. The most recent reports, published in December 2023, covered the 2021/22 municipal financial year. By the time a reader sees a Blue Drop score, the data is one to two years old. In a system where infrastructure can deteriorate rapidly — where a treatment plant can go from functional to critical within months — annual or biennial reporting is too slow to serve as an early warning system.

More concerning is what the Drop reports reveal about information gaps within municipalities themselves. The Blue Drop assessment found that 46 per cent of water supply systems achieved poor or bad microbiological water quality compliance. But compliance requires testing — and many municipalities do not test consistently. A municipality that does not monitor its own water quality does not know whether its water is safe. The absence of monitoring is itself an information failure that the Drop reports can identify but not fix.

At the municipal level, financial information systems are often unable to produce the data needed for effective water management. Many municipalities cannot report accurately on the cost of water production per kilolitre, the age and condition of their pipe networks, the maintenance history of their treatment plants, or the revenue collected per connection. Without this operational data, municipal managers are making decisions in the dark — and councillors are approving budgets based on incomplete or unreliable information.

The Water Services Information System managed by DWS is supposed to aggregate municipal water data nationally. But the system depends on municipalities submitting data, and submission rates are inconsistent. The result is a national information picture that is patchy — comprehensive for some municipalities, missing for others, and usually lagging behind real-time conditions.

### Capability — Rating: 1 (Critical Failure)

The capability crisis in municipal water services is the element where the system most clearly fails. A water treatment plant is a complex chemical and biological process facility. A wastewater treatment works is even more complex — it manages biological processes that must be precisely controlled to prevent the discharge of partially treated or raw sewage into water courses. Operating these facilities requires qualified process controllers — professionals with specific technical qualifications classified under Regulation 3630 (formerly Regulation 17) of the Water Services Act.

The classification system assigns treatment works to classes (I through IV) based on size and complexity, and requires process controllers with corresponding qualifications. A Class IV works — the largest and most complex — requires a process controller with the highest qualification level. In practice, many municipalities cannot attract or retain qualified process controllers. The reasons are structural: municipal salary scales cannot compete with private sector or Water Board salaries for qualified engineers and process controllers. Rural municipalities face the additional challenge that qualified professionals do not want to live in remote towns with limited amenities. Political instability — municipal managers who are appointed and removed on political cycles — creates an environment where technical professionals feel insecure. And the pipeline of qualified process controllers is thin — training takes years and the institutions that provide the training have limited capacity.

The Green Drop 2023 assessment explicitly measures municipal wastewater management capacity. Its findings are stark: 64 per cent of the country's 850 wastewater treatment works are at high or critical risk. **An independent assessment in 2024 found that only 13 per cent of wastewater treatment works meet minimum discharge standards. Five provinces — Free State, Eastern Cape, Limpopo, Mpumalanga, and KwaZulu-Natal — have zero compliant wastewater treatment plants.** Zero. Every single plant in those five provinces fails to meet the minimum standard for treating wastewater before discharging it into rivers.

The capability failure is not limited to process controllers. Municipal water departments lack engineers to design and manage infrastructure projects. They lack financial managers who understand the cost structures of water provision. They lack asset managers who can maintain pipe networks and treatment plants. Buffalo City Metro, a large urban municipality, had its district engineer for sanitation post vacant for 24 months and its electricity engineering post vacant for 80 months. If a metro cannot fill these positions, the situation in smaller municipalities is worse.

This earns the most severe rating on the RAARICLE™ scale. The capability element is not partially present with friction — it is absent in a way that makes the entire system incapable of performing its basic function. You cannot operate a water treatment plant without a qualified operator. No amount of funding, information, or authority compensates for the absence of the person who knows how to run the plant.

:::expert
The Water Institute of Southern Africa, which serves as the sole custodian of the Professional Process Controller designation under SAQA, has documented a persistent gap between the number of qualified process controllers and the number required to operate South Africa's water and wastewater treatment works. Years of non-enforcement of qualification regulations resulted in the appointment of staff without the required technical qualifications at treatment plants across the country. The Green Drop assessment methodology scores municipal capability as one of its core assessment criteria, and the consistently poor capability scores — particularly in rural and small-town municipalities — confirm that the qualification crisis is structural, not temporary. WISA's analysis indicates that even if municipalities committed to filling all vacant process controller positions immediately, the training pipeline cannot produce enough qualified professionals to meet the demand within the next decade.
:::

### Legitimacy — Rating: 4 (Functional)

Water services have the strongest constitutional mandate of any municipal function. Section 27(1)(b) of the Constitution provides that "everyone has the right to have access to sufficient food and water." Unlike many socio-economic rights, which are subject to progressive realisation within available resources, the Constitutional Court has treated the right to water with particular seriousness. The Water Services Act gives legislative force to this right. Schedule 4 Part B of the Constitution lists water and sanitation services as a local government function.

The mandate is clear. The democratic chain from voter to councillor to water department is visible. Citizens know that the municipality is supposed to provide water. The legitimacy of the function is not in question. What is in question is whether the institutional architecture can deliver on the mandate.

This is the paradox of water governance in South Africa: the function with the strongest legitimacy — the most explicit constitutional right, the most direct link to human survival — has some of the weakest operational elements. Legitimacy scores 4 while capability scores 1. The right to water is constitutionally entrenched. The capacity to deliver water is structurally absent.

### Enforcement — Rating: 1 (Critical Failure)

DWS is supposed to regulate water services delivery. It publishes Drop reports. It issues directives when municipalities fail to comply with norms and standards. It can, under the National Water Act, open criminal cases against municipalities that pollute water resources. By 2025, DWS had opened 42 criminal cases under the National Water Act, and 56 municipalities faced criminal charges including cases from environmental departments.

But **criminal prosecution is a blunt instrument for systemic failure. You cannot arrest your way to clean water.** *The enforcement architecture that is needed — automatic, pre-defined consequences triggered by objective performance data — does not exist for water services.*

Consider what happens when a municipality receives a critical Green Drop score. DWS publishes the score. DWS may issue a directive requiring corrective action. DWS may send a letter demanding a corrective action plan. As of January 2026, 42 WSAs had not even submitted corrective action plans in response to DWS's demands. And for those that did submit plans, there is no automatic consequence for failing to implement them. DWS can escalate — issuing further directives, referring the matter to the Minister, potentially recommending a Section 139 intervention. But each escalation step is discretionary. Each requires a decision by a human official who is subject to political pressures, resource constraints, and competing priorities.

The result is predictable: municipalities that fail face no automatic penalty. Municipalities that succeed receive no automatic reward. The enforcement mechanism is entirely discretionary, which means it is entirely political. A national minister may be reluctant to intervene in a municipality governed by a political ally. A DWS official may lack the budget to travel to the municipality and verify compliance. A directive may be issued and ignored and the follow-up may never come.

In the 2023/24 audit cycle, 583 non-compliance cases were reported and 467 investigated — an 80 per cent achievement rate that sounds reasonable until you consider what "investigated" means. Investigation does not mean resolution. Investigation does not mean the treatment plant was fixed. Investigation means somebody looked at the file.

The Water Services Amendment Bill currently before Parliament proposes to strengthen enforcement by enabling criminal prosecution under the Water Services Act itself (currently only possible under the National Water Act) and introducing a licensing system for WSPs. These are steps in the right direction. But they remain discretionary mechanisms — they give officials the power to act, without requiring them to act. The RAARICLE™ framework insists on automatic triggers: when a Blue Drop score falls below a defined threshold for two consecutive assessment periods, a prescribed consequence activates without discretion. That architecture does not yet exist.

:::takeaway
The enforcement element for water services is the mirror image of legitimacy: the constitutional right to water is the strongest mandate in the system, but the consequences for failing to deliver on that right are the weakest. DWS can assess, report, direct, and prosecute — but none of these actions are automatic, pre-defined, or binding in a way that compels compliance. The result is a regulatory system that documents failure with precision but lacks the structural power to prevent it.
:::

## The Root Cause

The eight-element diagnosis reveals a clear structural picture. Legitimacy is strong — there is no question that municipalities must provide water. Information is moderate — the Drop reports provide useful diagnostics, even if they lag. Every other element scores 1 or 2. Responsibility is fragmented. Accountability is diffuse. Authority is constrained. Resources leak. Capability is catastrophically absent. Enforcement is discretionary.

The root cause is not any single element in isolation. It is the interaction of three structural failures that together create a system incapable of delivering on its constitutional mandate.

The first failure is *conflation*. The WSA/WSP model, as implemented, conflates the regulator with the provider. This is the structural condition identified in [Why Institutions Fail](/articles/ra-1-2-why-institutions-fail) as Pattern 4. **When the same entity sets the standard and delivers the service, self-regulation does not work.** The Water Services Act envisaged separation — it provides for separate WSPs — but the political economy of municipal governance ensured that almost every WSA appointed itself as its own WSP. The theoretical separation was never implemented.

The second failure is *resource absence* — not the absence of revenue itself, but the absence of any mechanism that ring-fences water revenue for water infrastructure. This is the structural condition identified in [Why Institutions Fail](/articles/ra-1-2-why-institutions-fail) as Pattern 3 — a critical RAARICLE™ element that simply does not exist. Water revenue flows into the municipality's general account and cross-subsidises other functions. Infrastructure maintenance is deferred because the money generated by water services is spent elsewhere. The assets deteriorate. Water losses increase. Revenue collection falls. **The R400 billion infrastructure backlog is the accumulated cost of three decades of unprotected resource extraction.**

The third failure is *capability collapse*. Even if you fixed the conflation problem and ring-fenced the revenue, you would still face a system that lacks the qualified people to operate treatment plants, maintain pipe networks, and manage the technical complexity of water and sanitation services. The capability pipeline is broken — training institutions have limited capacity, municipal salary scales cannot compete with the private sector, and political instability in municipal leadership deters qualified professionals.

These three failures are mutually reinforcing. Conflation means there is no regulator to demand that resources be ring-fenced or that capability gaps be filled. The absence of ring-fenced revenue means there is no money to attract qualified professionals or maintain the infrastructure that those professionals would operate. Capability collapse means there is nobody who can articulate the case for ring-fencing or design the systems that would make separation work.

**Breaking this reinforcing cycle requires addressing all three failures simultaneously.** Fixing one without the others will not produce lasting improvement. Separating the WSA and WSP without ring-fencing revenue will produce a separate operator that is still underfunded. Ring-fencing revenue without building capability will produce a funded operator that still cannot run the treatment plant. Building capability without separating the functions will produce qualified professionals who are still embedded in a structure that has no regulatory oversight.

## The Reform Blueprint

### Structural Separation: WSA and WSP Must Be Different Entities

The first and most fundamental reform is to make the theoretical distinction between WSA and WSP real. The regulator cannot be the provider. This is not a South African insight — it is a universal principle of governance design. Every country that has achieved reliable water services has separated the regulatory function from the operational function.

In England and Wales, Ofwat (the Water Services Regulation Authority) regulated privatised water companies from 1989 until the UK government announced in July 2025 that Ofwat would be replaced by two new integrated regulators — a reform driven by the recognition that even Ofwat's regulatory model had failed to prevent sewage discharge scandals. The lesson is not that regulation is easy but that it is necessary — and that the regulator must be structurally independent of the operator, with teeth to enforce compliance.

In Chile, the Superintendencia de Servicios Sanitarios created in 1990 achieved remarkable results — 100 per cent urban coverage for drinking water and sanitation, 99.7 per cent service continuity — through a model that rigidly separated the regulator from the operator and used innovative tariff regulation based on benchmarking against an efficient "model company."

For South Africa, the structural separation should take the form of *regional water utilities*. **The current model — 144 individual municipalities each operating as their own WSP — is structurally incapable of achieving economies of scale, attracting qualified professionals, or sustaining the technical capability required for water and wastewater treatment.** Most of these municipalities are too small, too poor, and too capacity-constrained to operate as water utilities.

The reform blueprint proposes consolidating water service provision into a smaller number of regional water utilities — perhaps 20 to 30, aligned with water management areas or district boundaries. These utilities would be professionally managed entities with ring-fenced budgets, qualified staff, and operational autonomy. They would not be municipal departments. They would be separate legal entities — water boards expanded to cover reticulation, or new entities created through Water Services Act amendments. The WSA function would remain with municipalities — councillors would still set the standards, approve tariffs, and hold the utility accountable. But the WSP function would be performed by a professionally managed entity that is structurally separate from the political authority of the municipality.

Operation Vulindlela has already moved in this direction. The National Water Resources Infrastructure Agency Act, signed by President Ramaphosa on 27 August 2024, consolidates national bulk water functions (DWS, the Trans-Caledon Tunnel Authority, and the Water Trading Entity) into a single state-owned company capable of raising funds on its own balance sheet. This is the right structural move at the bulk supply level. The same logic must extend to municipal reticulation — where the bulk of the service delivery failure occurs.

### Ring-Fenced Revenue: Water Money Stays in Water

The second reform is non-negotiable: water revenue must be ring-fenced for water operations and water infrastructure. Every rand collected through water tariffs, every rand transferred through the equitable share for water services, and every rand allocated through conditional grants for water infrastructure must flow into a separate water account that cannot be used to cross-subsidise other municipal functions.

Ring-fencing is not technically difficult. The municipal Standard Chart of Accounts (mSCOA) already requires separate accounting for trading services including water. **The problem is that ring-fencing is not legally mandated — municipalities can and do transfer water surpluses to their general account.** National Treasury has the regulatory power to mandate ring-fencing through the MFMA regulations. It has not done so.

The reform requires *a binding regulation* — not a guideline, not a recommendation, but a regulation with the force of law — that water revenue is ring-fenced and can only be spent on water operations, water infrastructure maintenance, and water infrastructure expansion. **This regulation can be issued under existing MFMA provisions. It does not require new legislation.**

### An Independent Economic Water Regulator

The third reform addresses the regulatory vacuum. South Africa has no independent economic regulator for water services. NERSA regulates electricity tariffs. The Independent Communications Authority of South Africa regulates telecommunications. Water — arguably the most essential service — has no equivalent regulator. Municipalities set their own tariffs without oversight.

At the 2025 Water and Sanitation Indaba, the government committed to establishing an independent economic water regulator within three years. This regulator should set tariff methodologies (ensuring cost-reflectivity while protecting indigent households), benchmark operational efficiency (identifying municipalities that are performing well and those that are failing), and enforce minimum standards for infrastructure maintenance spending.

The regulator must be genuinely independent of DWS. DWS currently has a conflict of interest — it sometimes acts as a service provider through its Water Trading Entity, while simultaneously being responsible for regulating water services. The NWRIA Act addresses part of this conflict by consolidating the Water Trading Entity into the new agency. But the economic regulator must be a separate entity with its own governance, its own funding, and its own enforcement powers.

### Automatic Enforcement Triggers

The fourth reform replaces discretionary enforcement with automatic triggers. The RAARICLE™ framework insists that enforcement must be pre-defined and non-discretionary. For water services, this means:

When a municipality's Blue Drop score falls below 50 per cent for two consecutive assessment periods, an automatic escalation activates: DWS must deploy a qualified assessor within 30 days, the municipality must produce a corrective action plan within 60 days, and the regional water utility (or Water Board) must be placed on standby to assume operational management if the plan is not implemented within 180 days.

When a municipality's Green Drop score places a wastewater treatment works in the critical category, the plant must be placed under a mandatory remediation programme within 30 days. If the municipality cannot demonstrate that it has a qualified process controller operating the plant within 90 days, operational management transfers automatically to the regional water utility or Water Board.

When non-revenue water exceeds 40 per cent for two consecutive years, the independent economic regulator must require a water loss reduction plan and may adjust the municipality's tariff to fund the required infrastructure repairs.

These triggers remove political discretion from enforcement. They do not ask a minister to decide whether to intervene. They define the conditions under which intervention occurs and prescribe the consequences. The only role for human judgement is in designing the thresholds — once set, the triggers are automatic.

### The Capability Pipeline

The fifth reform addresses the critical capability failure. Regional water utilities achieve part of this — by consolidating operations across multiple municipalities, a regional utility can employ a smaller number of qualified professionals who serve a larger area. A qualified Class IV process controller who would never work for a single small-town municipality might work for a regional utility that operates ten treatment plants.

But consolidation alone does not produce the professionals. The reform must also include a national professional development pipeline: bursaries for water engineering and process controller qualifications linked to return-of-service obligations in municipalities or regional utilities. This is not a new model — the South African military and several health professions use bursary-and-bond schemes to address skills shortages in underserved areas. The same approach can work for water.

Regulation 3630 must be enforced — not as a future aspiration but as a current requirement. Every treatment plant must have a qualified process controller with the appropriate classification. Where a municipality cannot fill the post, the regional utility or Water Board must be required to provide the capability as an interim measure — at the municipality's cost, funded from the ring-fenced water account.

:::expert
Operation Vulindlela's water sector reform programme represents the most significant structural reform effort in water governance since the Water Services Act of 1997. The programme has reinstated the Blue Drop, Green Drop, and No Drop monitoring system after nearly a decade of lapse, established the Water Partnerships Office for public-private partnerships, secured the NWRIA Act for bulk water infrastructure, and committed to an independent economic water regulator. The R185.2 billion committed to water and sanitation projects over three years signals fiscal commitment. The reform programme's limitation, however, is that it has focused primarily on bulk supply infrastructure (the NWRIA) and monitoring (the Drop reports) rather than on the municipal reticulation level where the most severe service delivery failures occur. The next phase of reform must extend structural separation, ring-fencing, and automatic enforcement to the municipal WSA/WSP level — which is where the water either reaches the tap safely or does not.
:::

### Anti-Capture Architecture

Every element of the reform blueprint must be tested against the five capture vectors.

Political capture is the most immediate risk. Municipal politicians have strong incentives to resist water reform because the current system gives them control over water jobs, water procurement, and water tariffs — all of which are patronage resources. The structural separation of WSA and WSP is itself an anti-capture measure — it removes operational control from political authority. But the regional utility must also have protected governance: a professional board appointed through a merit-based process, not a board appointed by politicians. The independent economic regulator must have similar protections — fixed-term appointments, transparent selection processes, and funding that does not depend on the entities it regulates.

Commercial capture — procurement fraud in water infrastructure projects — is addressed by the regional utility model, which consolidates procurement across municipalities (reducing the number of procurement points that can be captured) and by mandatory e-procurement with automated price benchmarking.

Labour capture — resistance to performance management, bloated non-technical headcount — is addressed by the professional management requirement for regional utilities and by Regulation 3630 enforcement, which requires qualified professionals in technical positions.

Regulatory capture — the risk that the independent economic regulator becomes too close to the entities it regulates — is mitigated by separate funding, cooling-off periods for staff moving between the regulator and regulated entities, and automatic enforcement triggers that remove regulatory discretion.

Information capture — management filtering or delaying data before it reaches oversight bodies — is addressed by automated data pipelines from treatment plants to the Drop reporting system. Treatment plant performance data should flow directly from monitoring instruments to a central database without passing through municipal management. This is technically feasible with modern SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition) systems. The investment in automated monitoring is modest compared to the cost of information failure.

:::framework
**The Water Governance Diagnostic Framework**

Use these five steps to assess how water services are governed in your municipality and identify where the system is breaking down:

**Step 1: Identify who plays each role in the water value chain.**

Find out whether your municipality is both the Water Services Authority (WSA) and the Water Services Provider (WSP). If the same entity regulates and delivers, you have a conflation problem — the regulator cannot objectively hold itself accountable. Check your municipality's Integrated Development Plan (IDP) or the DWS website for WSA designations.

**Step 2: Check the Blue Drop and Green Drop scores.**

The Department of Water and Sanitation publishes Blue Drop (drinking water quality) and Green Drop (wastewater treatment) reports. Find your municipality's scores at www.dws.gov.za. A score below 50% signals serious risk. If your municipality was not assessed at all, that is a red flag — it may mean the information element of RAARICLE™ has collapsed entirely.

**Step 3: Follow the money.**

Request your municipality's annual financial statements (available on the municipal website or from National Treasury's Municipal Finance data). Check whether water revenue is ring-fenced for water operations or whether surpluses are being used to cross-subsidise other municipal functions. If water revenue leaks to other departments, infrastructure maintenance will be deferred.

**Step 4: Check the capability pipeline.**

Look at the municipality's organogram and annual report for process controller vacancies, engineering posts filled by acting officials, and whether the municipal manager has relevant technical qualifications. A municipality without qualified water engineers cannot maintain treatment plants — no amount of funding fixes a capability gap.

**Step 5: Ask about enforcement.**

Submit a PAIA request to DWS asking what enforcement actions have been taken against your municipality in the last five years. If the answer is none despite poor Blue or Green Drop scores, the enforcement element has failed — and you should advocate for automatic enforcement triggers tied to published water quality data.

With these five steps, you can diagnose exactly where your municipality's water governance is failing and direct your advocacy at the specific structural problem — rather than simply demanding "better service."
:::

## Implementation Pathway

### Phase 1: Legal Foundation (Year 1)

The immediate priorities require no new primary legislation. National Treasury can issue a regulation under the MFMA mandating ring-fencing of water revenue — this can be done within months. DWS can enforce Regulation 3630 for process controller qualifications by issuing compliance notices to every WSA with unqualified staff at treatment plants. The Water Services Amendment Bill currently before Parliament should be enacted, strengthening enforcement provisions and introducing a WSP licensing system.

Simultaneously, the government should gazette the terms of reference for the independent economic water regulator, begin the appointment process for its board, and allocate seed funding. The 2025 Water and Sanitation Indaba commitment to establishing the regulator within three years provides the political mandate.

### Phase 2: Pilot Regional Utility (Years 2-3)

Select one or two water management areas — ideally one that includes a mix of well-performing and poorly-performing municipalities — and pilot the regional water utility model. The pilot should consolidate WSP functions across all municipalities in the area into a single professionally managed entity. The WSA function remains with each municipality — councillors continue to set standards and approve tariffs. But the operational management of treatment plants, pipe networks, and billing transfers to the regional utility.

The pilot tests the key assumptions: that consolidation achieves economies of scale, that a regional entity can attract qualified professionals that individual municipalities cannot, that ring-fenced revenue is sufficient to fund operations and maintenance, and that the WSA/WSP separation creates genuine accountability. The pilot should be independently evaluated after two years against defined performance indicators — Blue Drop scores, Green Drop scores, non-revenue water, tariff collection rates, and infrastructure maintenance spending as a percentage of asset value.

### Phase 3: National Rollout (Years 3-5)

Based on the pilot evaluation, amend the Water Services Act to require structural separation of WSA and WSP functions for all municipalities below a defined capacity threshold. Municipalities that can demonstrate adequate capability — metros with functional water departments, large municipalities with qualified staff and ring-fenced finances — may continue to act as their own WSP under the oversight of the independent economic regulator. Municipalities that cannot meet the capability threshold must transfer WSP functions to the relevant regional water utility.

Establish the full national architecture: an independent economic water regulator, 20 to 30 regional water utilities with professional management, automatic enforcement triggers tied to Drop report scores, mandatory ring-fencing of water revenue, and a national professional development pipeline for water engineers and process controllers.

The goal is not to take water away from municipalities. The goal is to separate the functions that municipalities should do (set standards, represent communities, hold providers accountable) from the functions that require professional technical capability (operate treatment plants, maintain pipe networks, manage wastewater). As explored in [Municipal Councils](/articles/3-3-municipal-councils), the democratic governance of municipal services is essential. What is not essential — and what is provably failing — is the assumption that every municipality must also be its own water operator.
