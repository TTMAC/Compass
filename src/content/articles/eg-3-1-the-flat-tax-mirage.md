Every few years, someone proposes that South Africa scrap its complicated, multi-bracket income tax and replace it with a single, low, flat rate — one percentage that everyone pays, no exemptions to argue about, no brackets to game. It sounds clean. It sounds fair. And the moment you actually run the numbers, it dissolves. A low flat rate collects far less from high earners while the state still owes exactly the same bills, so the lost revenue has to reappear somewhere — and in the South African fiscal structure, that "somewhere" is almost always VAT, the most regressive tax we have. Run honestly, a 10% flat tax with credits implies a VAT rate in the low-to-mid twenties, which would land hardest on exactly the low-income households the flat tax was sold to help. The lesson of this article is not "flat tax bad". It is that you can learn to check the arithmetic yourself — and that any tax proposal which does not say where the compensating revenue comes from is not a proposal at all. It is a slogan with a number attached.

This is the first article in Part 3 of this pillar, The Fiscal Levers. If you have come through Part 1 (where South Africa's economy stands and how it got there) and Part 2 (the staged growth strategy and the infrastructure and institutions it needs), you have spent a lot of time on what the country should build. The unavoidable next question is how to pay for it — and whether the tax system we have is even the right one. The flat tax is where that conversation almost always begins, because it is the simplest-sounding answer on offer. So it is the right place to start. We are going to take it seriously, give it its strongest case, and then do the one thing public debate almost never does: finish the calculation.

## How South Africa actually taxes income right now

Before you can judge a replacement, you need to see clearly what is being replaced. South Africa runs a *progressive* personal income tax, which means the rate rises in steps as taxable income rises. For the 2026/27 tax year, the first R99,000 of income (for taxpayers under 65) is taxed at nothing at all — that is the tax threshold, the point below which you are registered but pay no income tax. Above it, income is sliced into brackets, and each successive slice is taxed at a higher *marginal* rate: 18% on the lowest band, then 26%, 31%, 36%, 39%, 41%, and finally 45% on taxable income above R1,878,600. The crucial thing most people misread is that the top rate applies only to the rand *above* each threshold, not to your whole income. Someone earning R1 million does not pay 41% of a million; they pay nothing on the first R99,000, 18% on the next slice, and so on up — an *effective* rate well below the bracket they top out in.

Now the numbers that make flat-tax arithmetic behave the way it does. According to the South African Revenue Service's *Tax Statistics 2025* — the eighteenth annual edition, jointly released with National Treasury — roughly 26 million people are registered for personal income tax, but only a fraction of them, on the order of nine million, actually submit returns and are formally assessed in a given year. Those assessed taxpayers reported an aggregated taxable income of about R2.7 trillion for the 2024 tax year, on which their assessed tax liability came to R563.3 billion. Step back to the level of the whole fiscus and personal income tax raised about R733 billion in 2024/25 — close to 40% of all the tax the state collected, making it by a clear margin the single largest source of revenue in the country.

Here is the fact that does all the work in this article. That tax is paid by very few people. SARS's own distribution data for the 2024 tax year shows that almost half of assessed taxpayers earned below R95,750 and accounted for under 9% of total taxable income. At the other end, the 1.2% of assessed taxpayers earning more than R1.5 million a year — barely one in eighty — earned 15% of the taxable income but paid just over 30% of all the personal income tax. A small number of high earners carry a very large share of the load. That is not an accident of the system; it is the *point* of a progressive system. And it is precisely why flattening the rate behaves the way it is about to.

:::expert
The Davis Tax Committee, the independent body chaired by Judge Dennis Davis that reviewed South Africa's tax system through the 2010s, repeatedly stressed how narrow and concentrated the personal income tax base is. Judge Davis, who teaches tax law at the University of Cape Town, has made the point in public testimony that a system leaning this heavily on a thin layer of high earners is both a strength and a vulnerability: it raises a great deal from comparatively few people, which is efficient, but it also means the revenue is exposed to the fortunes — and the mobility — of that small group. Any reform that changes how the top of the income distribution is taxed therefore moves a disproportionate amount of money, which is exactly why flat-tax proposals cannot be waved through on the strength of how simple they sound.
:::

## The flat tax in its most appealing form

Let us state the proposal at its strongest, because a stress test is only honest if it tests the real thing. The flat-tax case is not stupid; it has been argued by serious people for decades. Replace the seven-bracket schedule with one rate — say 10% — that everyone above the threshold pays on every rand of taxable income. To make sure nobody currently below the threshold is made worse off, you grant a tax credit so the low-paid stay where they are. The pitch writes itself. Simplicity: a tax return becomes a single multiplication. Lower compliance costs: less time, fewer accountants, less for SARS to administer. Reduced avoidance: when the top rate is 10% rather than 45%, the reward for elaborate tax structuring shrinks, so in theory more income surfaces and is actually taxed. And transparency: one visible rate that every citizen can see and understand, rather than a schedule most people cannot reconstruct from memory.

Each of those claims has something to it. Simplicity has real value, both in administrative cost and in the harder-to-measure sense that a tax people understand is a tax people are more willing to pay. The avoidance argument is genuine too: a chunk of the energy that goes into tax planning at the top is a pure deadweight cost, lawyers and accountants paid to move income around rather than to create anything. If a flat rate made that activity pointless, the country would get some of that effort back. Hold on to these points, because the conclusion of this article is *not* that flat-tax advocates are foolish. It is that the appeal is real and the arithmetic still does not work — and that the gap between those two facts is the whole story.

## Running the numbers

So let us run them. The method is simple enough to follow on the back of an envelope, which is the point — you should be able to do this yourself the next time someone proposes it.

Start with the base: the roughly R2.7 trillion of aggregated taxable income that assessed taxpayers reported for the 2024 tax year. Apply a flat 10% to it. Ten percent of R2.7 trillion is about R270 billion. Now subtract the credits you promised to everyone below the threshold, which pulls the figure down a little further. Treasury-style modelling of exactly this exercise — the kind set out in the analysis underlying this part — lands at roughly R274 billion of collections once the credits are accounted for, in the same ballpark as our envelope sum. Call it a little over R270 billion either way.

Now compare that to what the current schedule actually raises from the same base: R563.3 billion in assessed liability for that year. The flat 10% tax collects less than half. The shortfall is on the order of R290 billion — a number larger than the entire annual budget of most government departments, opening up in a single stroke. The state's obligations have not changed. The grants still have to be paid, the public-service salaries still fall due, the debt still has to be serviced. So the R290 billion does not vanish. It has to be found somewhere else.

The reason the gap is so large is the concentration we saw earlier. When a small group of high earners pays 30% of the income tax at marginal rates reaching 45%, dropping their rate to 10% removes most of what they were contributing. A flat rate is, by construction, a large tax cut for the people who currently pay the most, and that cut is not made up by the modest amounts collected from everyone else. The arithmetic is not a matter of opinion. It falls out of the distribution of income in a country as unequal as South Africa.

## The VAT consequence, and why this is a mirage

Where does R290 billion come from? In principle, a government has several instruments. In South African practice, a personal-income-tax shortfall of this size is closed with one of them far more readily than the others: value-added tax. VAT is the second-largest tax after personal income tax, it raised about R477 billion in 2024/25, and — crucially — it is the instrument the fiscus reaches for when it needs a lot of money reliably and quickly, because it is broad, hard to avoid, and collected continuously rather than assessed once a year. Corporate tax is volatile and internationally mobile; a wealth tax raises comparatively little and is administratively hard; fuel levies and sin taxes are already stretched. VAT is the workhorse. When the sums have to balance, VAT is where the country looks.

So how much VAT? The standard rate is 15%, and Treasury's own estimates put the yield of each percentage point at roughly R30 billion. To raise R290 billion you would need something close to ten additional points — pushing VAT from 15% into the mid-twenties. The modelling underlying this part, using slightly different inputs, put the figure at around 23%; on the most recent revenue numbers it lands a little higher, somewhere in the 24–25% region. The exact figure depends on the assumptions you feed it, and you should distrust anyone who quotes it to the decimal. But the range is the message: to keep a 10% flat income tax revenue-neutral, VAT has to roughly *double* in the low-to-mid twenties. That is the mirage. The flat tax appears to cut the tax burden. For most households it does no such thing — it *moves* the burden, from a tax many of them barely pay (income tax, which kicks in only above R99,000) to a tax they pay on almost everything they buy.

And that move is regressive, which is the part that should give every reader pause regardless of their politics. VAT takes the same 15% — or 24% — from the rand spent by a domestic worker as from the rand spent by an executive. But the domestic worker spends almost all of her income to live, so almost all of it is exposed to VAT, while the executive saves and invests a large share of his, sheltering it. As a proportion of income, a consumption tax therefore falls hardest on the poor. South Africa softens this with a zero-rated basket — brown bread, maize meal, rice, vegetables, paraffin and a list of other essentials carry no VAT precisely to protect low-income households — but zero-rating only blunts the effect; it does not reverse it.

Make it concrete with two households. Consider a worker earning R8,000 a month who, like most low-income South Africans, spends essentially everything she earns and saves nothing — she cannot afford to. A meaningful share of her spending is on zero-rated food, which is protected, but the rest — transport, electricity, airtime, clothing, cleaning products, school supplies, a takeaway meal — carries full VAT. Under a flat tax she pays no income tax (she was below the threshold anyway, so the flat tax changes nothing for her there), but the VAT rate on most of what she buys jumps from 15% toward 24%. Her cost of living rises measurably and she gets no income-tax cut to offset it, because she had no income tax to cut. Now consider an executive on R250,000 a month. Under the current schedule he pays a large income-tax bill at marginal rates up to 45%; under a 10% flat tax that bill collapses. He spends a far smaller fraction of his income — much of it goes to savings, investments, a bond, school fees that are partly VAT-exempt — so the higher VAT touches a smaller slice of his money. He is a large net winner; she is a net loser. The "flat tax for fairness" has quietly run the transfer in reverse. Push VAT into the twenties and you are raising the price of nearly everything the poorest half of the country buys, in order to fund a tax cut whose largest beneficiaries are the high earners who were paying 45%. The households that would pay more under a flat-tax-plus-higher-VAT swap are disproportionately poor and, given the shape of South African income distribution, disproportionately Black — the very households the "fairness" pitch claims to rescue.

:::expert
Professor Ingrid Woolard, an economist at the University of Cape Town, a research associate of the Southern Africa Labour and Development Research Unit, and a member of the Davis Tax Committee, has spent much of her career measuring exactly who bears which taxes. When the VAT rate was last seriously in play, she chaired the independent panel that reviewed the list of zero-rated items, work grounded in detailed household-expenditure data. The consistent finding of that tradition of research is that VAT, even with a generous zero-rated basket, is mildly regressive — it takes a larger share of income from poorer households than richer ones — whereas the personal income tax is strongly progressive. Swapping a progressive tax for a larger dose of a regressive one is, in distributional terms, a transfer from the bottom of the income distribution to the top, whatever the intention behind it.
:::

## The break-even rate: the number nobody quotes

There is a cleaner way to see the trap, and it does not require you to touch VAT at all. Ask a different question: forget keeping the rate low — what *single* flat rate, applied to the same R2.7 trillion base, would raise the R563 billion the current system raises, so that no other tax has to move? Divide one by the other. R563 billion over R2.7 trillion is about 20.8%. So the revenue-neutral flat rate — the rate at which a flat tax pays for itself without raising VAT or anything else — is around 20 to 21%.

Sit with that for a second, because it is the whole argument compressed into one number. The flat tax is attractive at 10%. To actually fund the country, it has to be about 21%. The gap between the rate that makes the proposal appealing and the rate that makes it solvent is roughly the size of the proposal itself. A flat tax is only "low" if someone else absorbs the shortfall — and we have just seen who that someone is. Every flat-tax pitch you will ever hear lives in the space between 10% and 21%, and the honest question to ask its author is simply: which number are you actually proposing, and if it is the low one, who pays the difference?

## The R1 million variant: progressivity wearing a disguise

The more sophisticated flat-tax advocates know all this, and they have a fix. Soften the regressive edge, they say, by exempting income below a high threshold entirely — say the first R1 million a year — and applying a single flat rate only to income above it. Now the low-paid and the middle class pay no income tax at all; only genuine high earners contribute. Doesn't that answer the objection?

It is worth taking seriously, so let us model it. The South African income distribution is brutally top-heavy, which cuts both ways here. Recall that only 1.2% of assessed taxpayers earn above R1.5 million, and the cohort above R1 million, while somewhat larger, is still a thin slice of the country — a few hundred thousand people. Exempt everything below R1 million and your entire income-tax base collapses to that slice, and within it you can only tax the rands *above* R1 million, since the first million is now exempt for everyone. To raise anything close to the R563 billion the system currently needs from that narrow band of income, the rate on it would have to be punishingly high — far above the "low, pro-growth" rate that was the entire selling point. Alternatively, you accept that you will raise dramatically less and run a much smaller state. There is no third option hiding in the arithmetic.

Two honest observations follow, and both matter. The first is almost a definitional point that the label obscures: a schedule of "0% up to R1 million, then a single rate above it" *is not a flat tax*. It is a two-bracket progressive tax. The high exemption is progressivity — coarser than the current seven-bracket schedule, but the same idea, taxing higher incomes at a higher average rate. The advocate who reaches for the R1 million threshold to fix the regressivity problem has, without quite saying so, conceded the principle of progressive taxation and is now arguing about how many brackets to have. That is a legitimate argument. It is just not the argument they started.

The second observation is where this part is heading, so I will flag it and deliberately not resolve it here. Notice what the R1 million exemption is *reaching for*. It would mean that almost every South African building wealth from a salary — the nurse, the engineer, the mid-career manager, the first-generation professional climbing without inherited assets behind them — pays no income tax at all, while the system leans entirely on the rands earned above a million. There is a real intuition in that: that a tax system might treat the *building* of wealth from earnings more gently than it currently does. We will come back to it properly in [What Tax Is For](/articles/eg-3-3-what-tax-is-for), where it turns out to point at something the income-tax rate cannot fix — the gap between how heavily South Africa taxes the *flow* of new earnings and how lightly it taxes the *stock* of accumulated assets. For now, just file the observation. The R1 million variant is not a flat tax, and the thing it is groping toward is not really a question about the income-tax rate at all.

## The assumption underneath everything

Step back and notice what every calculation in this article has quietly assumed. At each turn we have said: the state must collect R563 billion, so if you take some away here it has to come back there. Revenue is treated as a fixed sum the government is obliged to raise, and every change has to be "paid for" by another change. That assumption is so natural it is almost invisible — it is how households work, how businesses work, how your own budget works. It is also the assumption the rest of this part is going to interrogate directly.

Hold the question lightly for now, because it genuinely changes the shape of the board. If the state is *not* revenue-constrained in the way a household is — if, for a country that issues its own currency, "how will you pay for it?" is a more complicated question than the household metaphor admits — then "compensating" for an income-tax cut with a VAT rise is a *choice* about who bears the burden, not an arithmetic necessity. That is the argument of [The Household Fallacy](/articles/eg-3-6-the-household-fallacy), and it is contested economics that we will handle with real care. And even the distributional question this article keeps bumping into — who gets to build wealth, who merely holds it — will turn out, by the end of this part, to depend far less on the income-tax rate than on something almost nobody debates: where bank credit actually flows. That is where [The Real Lever](/articles/eg-3-8-the-real-lever) lands.

But none of that rescues the flat tax. Even if you become convinced that the revenue constraint is softer than it looks, the flat tax remains a large, regressive transfer from the bottom of the income distribution to the top, dressed in the language of simplicity and fairness. The arithmetic in this article holds whatever you decide about the assumption beneath it. What changes, later, is the *menu of better questions* you can ask — and "flat or progressive?" turns out not to be on the top of it.

Before we get there, the next article does the empirical test this one only modelled. We have shown what the flat tax does to South Africa's numbers on paper. [The Flat-Tax Experiments](/articles/eg-3-2-the-flat-tax-experiments) asks what actually happened in the real countries that adopted flat income taxes — and the ones that levy no income tax at all — and whether any of them offers South Africa a model it could copy and expect the same result.

## Practical Framework: The Tax Proposal Stress Test

:::takeaway
A flat income tax is a large tax cut for the small number of high earners who currently pay most of the income tax, and in South Africa the lost revenue is recovered through VAT — a regressive tax that falls hardest on the poor. The revenue-neutral flat rate is around 20–21%, roughly double the 10% that makes the idea attractive. The honest test of any tax proposal is not whether it sounds simple, but whether it says where the compensating revenue comes from. If it doesn't, the proposal is incomplete.
:::

The real value of working through the flat tax is not the verdict — it is the method. You now have a procedure you can apply to *any* tax change a politician, party, or commentator puts in front of you, and every step has a publicly checkable data source. The next time someone proposes a tax reform, run it through these five questions before you decide what you think.

:::framework
**The Tax Proposal Stress Test**

Apply these five questions to any proposed tax change. Each has a publicly available data source, so you can check the claims yourself rather than taking them on trust.

**1. What does it actually change — the rate, the base, the threshold, or the structure?**

Be precise about the mechanism. Cutting a *rate* is different from broadening the *base* (what gets taxed), raising a *threshold* (the point at which a tax starts), or changing the *structure* (flat versus progressive). Many proposals blur these deliberately. A "flat tax with a R1 million exemption", for instance, is not a change of structure at all — it is a two-bracket progressive tax. Source: the proposal's own detail, checked against SARS's published rates and thresholds.

**2. Who pays more and who pays less — in rands and as a share of income?**

A change that looks neutral in rands is often sharply unequal as a share of income, which is what actually matters for households. Ask specifically how the change lands across the income distribution. Source: SARS *Tax Statistics* (income-band tables) and Stats SA household-expenditure data.

**3. If it reduces one tax, where does the compensating revenue come from — and is that source named or hidden?**

This is the decisive question. A revenue loss does not disappear; it is either recovered from another tax, borrowed, or matched by spending cuts. If the proposal does not say which, it is incomplete. In South Africa, an income-tax shortfall is most often closed with VAT — so ask whether VAT is the unstated other half of the plan. Source: National Treasury *Budget Review*; the rule of thumb that one VAT point ≈ R30 billion.

**4. What does it assume about the revenue constraint?**

Most proposals assume the state must raise a fixed sum, so every cut must be "paid for". That assumption is worth making explicit, because it is the hinge of the whole debate — and, as later articles argue, more contestable than it appears. Source: the *Budget Review*'s revenue and expenditure framework.

**5. What behaviour is it trying to encourage or discourage?**

Every tax is a signal about what a society wants more or less of — a point developed in the next-but-one article. Ask what a proposed change is steering people toward or away from, intentionally or not. Source: the policy rationale stated in the *Budget Review*'s tax-proposals chapter, tested against the incentive it actually creates.

A proposal that survives all five questions is worth debating on the merits. One that fails the third — that cuts a tax without naming where the money comes from — is not a tax policy. It is a tax cut with the bill hidden.
:::
