Start with a claim that sounds like common sense and turns out to be half right, which is the most dangerous kind. Every tax is a disincentive. Tax a person's wages and you have made working slightly less rewarding than it was. Tax a company's profit and you have made the risk of starting and growing it slightly less worth taking. Tax a sale and you have made the transaction slightly less likely to happen at all. A tax is a price the state attaches to an activity, and prices change behaviour — that is the entire reason prices exist. So a tax system is never just a way of collecting money. It is a vast, mostly invisible set of signals about what a society wants more of and less of, and in South Africa, a country with more than seven million people who want work and cannot find it, the signals we are sending about *working, hiring and building* are about the most important thing the fiscus does. This article takes seriously the argument that we have those signals wrong — that we tax effort and enterprise too heavily, that this holds back the growth which is the only durable source of jobs, and that we could tax less, or tax differently, and end up with more. Then it does the thing that argument almost never does to itself: it runs the numbers.

This is the first article in Part 3 of this pillar, The Fiscal Levers. Parts 1 and 2 spent a long time on what South Africa should build — the growth path, the infrastructure, the institutions. The unavoidable next question is how to pay for it, and whether the tax system we have is even pointed in the right direction. The most provocative answer on offer is that the system is actively in the way: that the goal should not be to extract more from a stagnant economy but to extract *less* and unleash a growing one, on the theory that a smaller slice of a much larger pie feeds more people. It is a serious argument, made by serious people, and it deserves to be stated at full strength before it is tested. So that is the order we will follow. We give the case its best version, we correct the parts that are overstated, and then we take the single most popular version of it — cut the tax on earnings, make up the difference on consumption — and finish the calculation that its advocates usually leave hanging.

## What a tax actually is — a price on doing something

Economists have a precise way of describing the "disincentive" intuition, and it is worth borrowing because it is more useful than the slogan. When you tax a transaction, you drive a wedge between what the buyer pays and what the seller receives. The employer pays R130 for an hour of labour; the worker, after tax, keeps R100; the missing R30 is the wedge. Most of that wedge is simply transferred to the state, which is the point of the exercise. But some of it corresponds to transactions that *now never happen at all* — the extra hour not worked, the marginal worker not hired, the small job not worth doing once the wedge is added. That lost activity is not collected by anybody. It is value that the tax did not redistribute but destroyed, and economists call it deadweight loss. It is the part of a tax that has no offsetting benefit to the Treasury, and it is the hard kernel of truth inside "every tax is a disincentive". Taxes do not merely move money around. At the margin, they shrink the thing being taxed.

How much they shrink it is not a matter of opinion; it is something you can measure, and for South Africa it has been measured. The relevant number is the *elasticity of taxable income* — how much the income people declare changes when their tax rate changes. Research using South African Revenue Service records, including work associated with the Southern Africa – Towards Inclusive Economic Development (SA-TIED) programme, puts that elasticity at roughly 0.4, with the response concentrated almost entirely among high earners and the self-employed — exactly the people with the most room to adjust how much they work, how they structure their affairs, and where they declare their income. That is a real behavioural response, and it is large enough to take seriously. Raise the rate on the people who already carry most of the load and a meaningful chunk of the income simply stops appearing on the return.

And the most mobile of them do not just rearrange their affairs; they leave. South Africa's personal income tax leans on an unusually thin layer of high earners, and that layer has feet. SARS and National Treasury have flagged a steady outflow of taxpayers ceasing South African tax residency — tens of thousands over recent years, with one widely reported estimate of around 38,000 emigrating taxpayers in a single recent year — each one a slice of the base that does not come back. A tax system that depends this heavily on a small, internationally mobile group is exposed in a way that a broader base would not be, and that exposure is itself an argument for keeping the rates on that group competitive rather than punitive.

## Why this matters most in a country that needs jobs

Here is where the disincentive argument stops being a technical point about elasticities and becomes a moral one, which is why it has such force. South Africa's central failure is not that the rich are insufficiently taxed. It is that roughly a third of the people who want to work cannot, and that the cost of that failure — measured in poverty, in dependence, in lives spent waiting — outweighs almost everything else the fiscus does. In a country like this, growth is not one policy goal among many. It is the precondition for the rest of them. A larger economy is the only thing that has ever reliably turned millions of people into wage earners with meaningful livelihoods, and it does so far more durably than any grant the same money could fund.

That reframes the whole tax question. If the binding constraint on the country is too few jobs, then the most important test of a tax is not "is it fair?" but "what does it do to the activity that creates work?" A tax that raises a few billion rand while suppressing the investment that would have created tens of thousands of jobs has, on this view, made the country poorer even as it filled the till. The advocate of a lighter touch is asking a genuinely important question: are we, in our anxiety to fund the state and to be seen to tax the well-off, quietly taxing away the very enterprise that would do more for the poor than the revenue ever could? You do not have to agree with where that argument ends up to see that the question is the right one. We will hold onto it for the rest of the part, because even where the specific proposals fail, the goal behind them — growth that produces livelihoods — is the correct goal.

## How South Africa actually taxes income right now

Before judging any reform, you need to see clearly what is being reformed. South Africa runs a *progressive* personal income tax: the rate rises in steps as taxable income rises. For the 2026/27 tax year, the first R99,000 of income (for taxpayers under 65) is taxed at nothing at all — that is the tax threshold, the point below which you are registered but pay no income tax. Above it, income is sliced into brackets, and each successive slice is taxed at a higher *marginal* rate: 18% on the lowest band, then 26%, 31%, 36%, 39%, 41%, and finally 45% on taxable income above R1,878,600. The crucial thing most people misread is that the top rate applies only to the rand *above* each threshold, not to your whole income. Someone earning R1 million does not pay 45% of a million; they pay nothing on the first R99,000, 18% on the next slice, and so on up — an *effective* rate well below the bracket they top out in.

Now the numbers that make the rest of this article behave the way it does. According to the South African Revenue Service's *Tax Statistics 2025* — the eighteenth annual edition, jointly released with National Treasury — roughly 26 million people are registered for personal income tax, but only a fraction, on the order of nine million, actually submit returns and are formally assessed in a given year. Those assessed taxpayers reported an aggregated taxable income of about R2.7 trillion for the 2024 tax year, on which their assessed tax liability came to R563.3 billion. Step back to the level of the whole fiscus and personal income tax raised about R733 billion in 2024/25 — close to 40% of all the tax the state collected, making it by a clear margin the single largest source of revenue in the country.

Here is the fact that does much of the work below. That tax is paid by very few people. SARS's own distribution data for the 2024 tax year shows that almost half of assessed taxpayers earned below R95,750 and accounted for under 9% of total taxable income. At the other end, the 1.2% of assessed taxpayers earning more than R1.5 million a year — barely one in eighty — earned 15% of the taxable income but paid just over 30% of all the personal income tax. A small number of high earners carry a very large share of the load. That is not an accident of the system; it is the *point* of a progressive one. It is also, as the disincentive argument correctly notes, a concentration of risk: the more the revenue rests on a few mobile shoulders, the more it moves when those shoulders shrug.

:::expert
The Davis Tax Committee, the independent body chaired by Judge Dennis Davis that reviewed South Africa's tax system through the 2010s, repeatedly stressed how narrow and concentrated the personal income tax base is. Judge Davis, who teaches tax law at the University of Cape Town, has made the point in public testimony that a system leaning this heavily on a thin layer of high earners is both a strength and a vulnerability: it raises a great deal from comparatively few people, which is efficient, but it also means the revenue is exposed to the fortunes — and the mobility — of that small group. That double-edged quality is exactly why both sides of the tax debate can cite the same fact. The reformer sees a fragile, over-taxed base that should be relieved; the defender sees a progressive system doing precisely what it was designed to do. Both are reading the same table.
:::

## The case that we tax work too heavily

Put the argument together at its strongest, because a stress test is only honest if it tests the real thing. The case runs like this. South Africa's top marginal rate of 45% arrives relatively early by the standards of a developing economy that needs to retain and attract skilled, productive people. The base is thin and mobile, and the behavioural evidence says it responds. Layered on top of the income tax are a value-added tax of 15%, a corporate rate of 27%, a dividends tax of 20%, and capital gains tax — so that a rand of profit earned, retained, and eventually distributed is taxed several times over on its way from effort to spending. The cumulative message the system sends to anyone deciding whether to work an extra shift, expand a business, or move capital into the country is: a large and rising share of what you create will be taken. For an economy that needs investment and enterprise more than almost anything, that is close to the worst signal it could send.

And the prize for getting it right, the argument continues, is large — because the gains compound. A tax change that lifts the growth rate by even half a percentage point a year does not add up; it multiplies, year after year, into a materially larger economy and a materially larger tax base a decade out. On this logic, the country has been optimising the wrong variable. It has been arguing about how to divide a stagnant pie more fairly while the real opportunity — a faster-growing pie that would employ millions more people and, not incidentally, generate *more* tax revenue at *lower* rates — goes unattempted. The goal, the reformer says, should be to design the tax system backwards from growth and jobs, not forwards from this year's spending bill.

## The countries that bet on a lighter touch

The argument has exhibits, and they are genuinely striking. Ireland cut and then standardised its corporate tax to 12.5%, far below the European norm, and over the following decades became a hub for technology and pharmaceutical investment, its income per head overtaking much of Western Europe. Singapore pairs a low personal income tax topping out around the low-to-mid twenties with no tax on most capital gains, and converted itself within a generation from a port with no resources into one of the richest places on earth. Estonia introduced a 0% corporate tax on *reinvested* profit — you are taxed only when you take money out, not when you plough it back — and built one of the most dynamic digital economies in Europe. Three small countries, three deliberate bets that a lighter, simpler, more investment-friendly tax system would attract capital and talent, and three apparent vindications.

These cases are why the supply-side argument refuses to die, and they should not be waved away. But they have to be read with two cautions, both of which matter for South Africa specifically. The first is that tax was never the only variable. Ireland also had unrestricted access to the European single market, an English-speaking workforce, a favourable demographic moment, and decades of education investment; disentangling the 12.5% rate from all of that is genuinely hard, and serious economists disagree about how much credit the rate deserves. Singapore married low taxes to ferocious public investment in housing, ports and skills, and a state that was anything but small in its ambitions. The second caution is scale and structure: a small, open, trade-dependent city-state can win by becoming a magnet for *other* countries' capital, a strategy that does not generalise to every economy at once and sits awkwardly with South Africa's size, its inequality, and the sheer breadth of what its state is asked to fund. The exhibits prove that competitive tax design can be *part* of a growth story. They do not prove that cutting rates is *sufficient*, and they certainly do not tell you how to fill the revenue hole in the meantime. That last question is where the South African version of the argument has to be tested, and where it usually goes quiet.

## The correction: not every tax distorts equally

Before we run that test, one premise in the opening claim has to be corrected, because getting it wrong is how a good argument loses a careful reader. "Every tax is a disincentive" is true as far as it goes, but "every tax distorts the economy *equally*" — the slide the slogan invites — is false, and the difference is the most useful thing in this whole field. Taxes differ enormously in how much damage they do per rand raised, and the ranking is well established.

The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development, synthesising a large body of cross-country research, has set out a now-standard hierarchy of how harmful different taxes are to long-run growth. Recurrent taxes on immovable property do the least damage, because you cannot hide or relocate land and taxing it barely changes how much of it exists. Consumption taxes such as VAT come next: they are broad and hard to avoid, and they fall on spending rather than on the act of earning. Personal income taxes are more harmful, because they bite directly on the decision to work and save. And corporate income taxes are, on this evidence, the *most* harmful of the major taxes to growth, because capital is the most mobile thing of all. The lesson is not "tax bad". It is "*composition* matters" — that a country can raise the same revenue with very different consequences for growth depending on *what* it leans on.

Some taxes are even better than neutral. A tax on something society wants less of — fuel, carbon, alcohol, tobacco — does not create a distortion so much as *correct* one, charging an activity for a cost it would otherwise impose on everyone else; economists call these Pigouvian taxes, and they can improve efficiency while raising money. A well-designed tax on the unimproved value of land is the textbook case of a tax that raises real revenue while discouraging almost nothing useful. So the honest version of the reformer's premise is not "all tax is a drag to be minimised". It is the sharper and more defensible claim that *South Africa may be leaning on the more growth-harmful taxes and under-using the less harmful ones* — and that is a claim we can actually test, rather than a slogan we have to accept or reject whole.

## The proposal, taken seriously: cut PAYE, lean on VAT

The most popular concrete version of "tax work less" in South African debate follows directly from that composition logic, and it sounds almost unanswerable. Income tax is the growth-harmful one that bites on earning; VAT is the broader, less harmful one that bites on spending. So shift the weight from the first to the second. In its cleanest form: eliminate PAYE — the income tax deducted from pay packets — for everyone earning below some chosen level, putting cash directly back into workers' hands and reducing the tax on employment, and recover the lost revenue by raising VAT, which everyone pays. Cut the tax on the paycheck; make it up at the till. It promises a lighter burden on work, a simpler system, and — because VAT is so hard to avoid — no real loss to the fiscus. What is not to like?

To test it we need to be precise about the mechanism, because the appeal and the arithmetic live in different places. The proposal does two things at once: it removes income-tax revenue, and it raises consumption-tax revenue to replace it. The claim that the second "more than offsets" the first is, in a narrow sense, trivially true — you can always raise VAT high enough to cover any hole. The real questions are *how high*, and *who pays the difference once you have moved it*. Those are answerable, with public data, on the back of an envelope. So let us answer them.

## Running the numbers

Begin with how little the lower end actually contributes, because it determines how much a PAYE cut for that group really costs — and the answer cuts both ways. Recall that almost half of assessed taxpayers earn below about R96,000 and together account for under 9% of taxable income, and that anyone below the R99,000 threshold already pays no income tax at all. So if "below a certain level" means a genuinely modest level, eliminating PAYE there removes surprisingly little revenue — but it also delivers surprisingly little to the workers it is meant to help, because most of them were barely paying income tax to begin with. To put real money back in real pockets, the threshold has to be set high enough to reach the middle of the distribution, and the moment it does, the revenue forgone climbs steeply, because that is where the income — and the tax — actually sits.

Take the more ambitious version that advocates usually have in mind, and model the structurally identical proposal that makes the maths cleanest: drop the tax on the bulk of earners and apply one low rate. Apply a flat 10% to the whole R2.7 trillion base. Ten percent of R2.7 trillion is about R270 billion. Allow for credits to protect those at the bottom and Treasury-style modelling of exactly this exercise lands at roughly R274 billion of collections — call it a little over R270 billion. Now compare that to what the current schedule raises from the same base: R563.3 billion. The light-touch version collects less than half. The shortfall is on the order of R290 billion — larger than the entire annual budget of most government departments, opening up in a single stroke. The state's obligations have not changed: the grants still have to be paid, the salaries still fall due, the debt still has to be serviced. So R290 billion does not vanish. It has to reappear somewhere.

The reason the gap is so large is the concentration we have already seen. When a small group of high earners pays 30% of the income tax at marginal rates reaching 45%, lightening their load removes most of what they were contributing, and the modest amounts collected from everyone else do not make it up. This is the uncomfortable arithmetic at the heart of every "cut the tax on work" plan in a country this unequal: the people who would benefit most in rand terms from a lower income tax are, necessarily, the people who pay most of it now. The cut is real. It is just not aimed where the pitch implies.

## The VAT consequence, and who actually pays

So where does the R290 billion come from? In South African practice, a personal-income-tax shortfall of this size is closed with one instrument far more readily than the others: VAT. It is the second-largest tax after personal income tax, it raised about R477 billion in 2024/25, and it is the instrument the fiscus reaches for when it needs a lot of money reliably and quickly, because it is broad, hard to avoid, and collected continuously rather than assessed once a year. Corporate tax is volatile and internationally mobile; a wealth tax raises comparatively little and is administratively hard; fuel and sin taxes are already stretched. VAT is the workhorse — exactly as the composition argument recommends. When the sums have to balance, VAT is where the country looks.

How much VAT? The standard rate is 15%, and Treasury's own estimates put the yield of each percentage point at roughly R30 billion. To raise R290 billion you would need something close to ten additional points, pushing VAT from 15% into the mid-twenties. Modelling with slightly different inputs lands around 23%; on the most recent revenue numbers it comes out a little higher, in the 24–25% region. Distrust anyone who quotes it to the decimal, but the range is the message: to keep a low flat income tax revenue-neutral, VAT has to roughly *double* into the low-to-mid twenties. The composition argument said move from income to consumption; this is what moving the *whole shortfall* actually requires.

And here the proposal collides with its own justification, because that move is regressive — and this is the part that should give every reader pause regardless of their politics. VAT takes the same percentage from the rand spent by a domestic worker as from the rand spent by an executive. But the worker spends almost all of her income to live, so almost all of it is exposed to VAT, while the executive saves and invests a large share of his, sheltering it. As a proportion of income, a consumption tax therefore falls hardest on the poor. South Africa softens this with a zero-rated basket — maize meal, brown bread, rice, vegetables, paraffin and other essentials carry no VAT — but zero-rating only blunts the effect; it does not reverse it.

Make it concrete with the two households the proposal claims to be choosing between. A worker earning R8,000 a month spends essentially everything she earns. She already pays no income tax — she is below the threshold — so eliminating PAYE for "lower earners" changes nothing for her on the income side; there was nothing to eliminate. But the VAT on most of what she buys, once the zero-rated food is set aside, climbs from 15% toward the low-to-mid twenties. Her cost of living rises measurably and she receives no income-tax cut to offset it. Now take an executive on R250,000 a month. Under the current schedule he pays a large income-tax bill at marginal rates up to 45%; under the lighter regime that bill collapses. He spends a far smaller fraction of his income — much goes to savings, investments, a bond — so the higher VAT touches a smaller slice of his money. He is a large net winner; she is a net loser. The swap that was sold as relief for workers has, in distributional terms, run the transfer in reverse — and because of the shape of South African income, the households left worse off are disproportionately poor and disproportionately Black. "More than offset through VAT" is true at the level of the Treasury's books. It is false at the level of the very household the cut was meant to help.

:::expert
Professor Ingrid Woolard, an economist at the University of Cape Town, a research associate of the Southern Africa Labour and Development Research Unit, and a member of the Davis Tax Committee, has spent much of her career measuring exactly who bears which taxes. When the VAT rate was last seriously in play she chaired the independent panel that reviewed the list of zero-rated items, work grounded in detailed household-expenditure data. The consistent finding of that tradition of research is that VAT, even with a generous zero-rated basket, is mildly regressive — it takes a larger share of income from poorer households than richer ones — whereas the personal income tax is strongly progressive. Swapping a progressive tax for a larger dose of a regressive one is, in distributional terms, a transfer from the bottom of the income distribution to the top, whatever the intention behind it. That is not an argument that VAT is a bad tax; it is broad and efficient, which is why it is the workhorse. It is an argument that *who bears the swap* has to be stated honestly, not assumed away.
:::

## The break-even rate, and the 20% you already pay

There is a cleaner way to see the trap, and it connects directly to a figure often cited in favour of cutting the tax on earners. The complaint that high earners already face an effective rate above 20% is, broadly, correct — and the arithmetic of why is the same arithmetic that sinks the cheap version of the cut. Take the whole assessed base: R563.3 billion of tax on R2.7 trillion of taxable income is an average effective rate of about 20.8% across *all* assessed taxpayers. Because the system is progressive, the top fifth of earners sit above that mean and the bottom half well below it, so it is true that the upper end of the distribution carries an effective burden north of 20%. That is the system doing what a progressive system is built to do.

Now turn the same number around and it becomes the question every flat-or-low-rate proposal has to answer. Forget keeping the rate low — what *single* rate, applied to that same R2.7 trillion base, would raise the R563 billion the current system raises, so that no other tax has to move? It is the same division: about 20.8%. So the revenue-neutral rate — the rate at which a flat or simplified income tax pays for itself without raising VAT or anything else — is around 20 to 21%. Sit with that, because it is the whole argument compressed into one number. The light-touch income tax is attractive at 10%. To actually fund the country it has to be about 21% — which is roughly the effective rate the upper end of the distribution already pays. The gap between the rate that makes the proposal appealing and the rate that makes it solvent is about the size of the proposal itself. Every "low income tax" pitch you will hear lives in the space between 10% and 21%, and the honest question to put to its author is simply: which number are you actually proposing — and if it is the low one, who pays the difference? We have just seen that in South Africa the answer, by default, is the poor.

## The threshold variant: progressivity wearing a disguise

The more sophisticated reformers know all this, and the fix they reach for is precisely the "eliminate PAYE below a certain level" move, in its most generous form: exempt income below a high threshold entirely — say the first R1 million a year — and apply a single rate only above it. Now the low-paid and the middle class pay no income tax at all; only genuine high earners contribute. Doesn't that answer the regressivity objection?

It is worth taking seriously, so model it. The South African income distribution is brutally top-heavy, which cuts both ways here. Only 1.2% of assessed taxpayers earn above R1.5 million, and the cohort above R1 million, while larger, is still a thin slice — a few hundred thousand people. Exempt everything below R1 million and the entire income-tax base collapses to that slice, and within it you can only tax the rands *above* R1 million, since the first million is now exempt for everyone. To raise anything close to the R563 billion the system currently needs from that narrow band, the rate on it would have to be punishingly high — far above the "low, pro-growth" rate that was the whole selling point. The alternative is to accept that you will raise dramatically less and run a much smaller state. There is no third option hiding in the arithmetic.

Two honest observations follow. The first is almost definitional: a schedule of "0% up to R1 million, then a single rate above it" *is not a flat tax, and it is not really a cut in the tax on work*. It is a two-bracket progressive tax with a very high entry point. The high exemption *is* progressivity — coarser than the current seven-bracket schedule, but the same idea, taxing higher incomes at a higher average rate. The reformer who reaches for the high threshold to fix the regressivity problem has, without quite saying so, conceded the principle of progressive taxation and is now arguing about how many brackets to have. That is a legitimate argument. It is just not the argument they started.

The second observation is where this part is heading, so I will flag it and deliberately not resolve it here. Notice what the high exemption is *reaching for*. It would mean that almost every South African building wealth from a salary — the nurse, the engineer, the mid-career manager, the first-generation professional climbing without inherited assets behind them — is taxed gently or not at all on the *building*, while the rands above the threshold carry the load. There is a real and sympathetic intuition in that: that a tax system might treat the *earning* of wealth more kindly than it currently does. We come back to it properly in [What Tax Is For](/articles/eg-3-3-what-tax-is-for), where it turns out to point at something the income-tax rate cannot fix — the gap between how heavily South Africa taxes the *flow* of new earnings and how lightly it taxes the *stock* of accumulated assets. For now, just file it. The threshold variant is not a tax cut for workers, and the thing it is groping toward is not really a question about the income-tax rate at all.

## The assumption underneath everything

Step back and notice what every calculation here has quietly assumed. At each turn we have said: the state must collect R563 billion, so if you take some away here it has to come back there. Revenue is treated as a fixed sum the government is obliged to raise, and every change has to be "paid for" by another change. That assumption is so natural it is almost invisible — it is how households work, how businesses work, how your own budget works. It is also the assumption the rest of this part interrogates directly.

Hold the question lightly for now, because it genuinely changes the shape of the board. If the state is *not* revenue-constrained in the way a household is — if, for a country that issues its own currency, "how will you pay for it?" is a more complicated question than the household metaphor admits — then "compensating" for an income-tax cut with a VAT rise is a *choice* about who bears the burden, not an arithmetic necessity. That is the argument of [The Household Fallacy](/articles/eg-3-6-the-household-fallacy), and it is contested economics we will handle with care. And even the growth question this article keeps circling — whether a lighter tax would buy the jobs the country needs — turns out, by the end of the part, to depend far less on the income-tax rate than on something almost nobody debates: where bank credit actually flows. That is where [The Real Lever](/articles/eg-3-8-the-real-lever) lands.

But none of that rescues the cheap version of the cut. Even if you become convinced that the revenue constraint is softer than it looks, "eliminate the tax on work and recover it through VAT" remains, in its affordable form, a large and regressive transfer from the bottom of the income distribution to the top, dressed in the language of relief for workers. The disincentive intuition that opened this article is real: taxes do shrink the things they touch, the goal of growth and jobs is the right goal, and composition genuinely matters. What the arithmetic shows is that *this particular swap* does not buy the growth — its behavioural payoff is modest, with the elasticity evidence implying that even an optimally set top rate would land near the current 45% rather than far below it — while its distributional cost is large and lands on exactly the wrong people. The honest conclusion is not "taxes don't matter to growth". It is that the income-tax *rate* is a low-order lever, and the country has been arguing most fiercely about the knob that moves the least. The better levers are further down the part.

Before we get there, the next article does the empirical test this one only modelled. We have shown what a light-touch income tax does to South Africa's numbers on paper. [The Flat-Tax Experiments](/articles/eg-3-2-the-flat-tax-experiments) asks what actually happened in the real countries that adopted flat income taxes — and the ones that levy no income tax at all — and whether any of them offers South Africa a model it could copy and expect the same result.

## Practical Framework: The Tax Proposal Stress Test

:::takeaway
Every tax is a disincentive, but not every tax distorts equally, and the goal that matters in South Africa — growth that creates jobs — is the right one to design around. The popular fix of cutting the tax on earnings and recovering it through VAT, however, fails its own test: in its affordable form it is a large tax cut for high earners funded by pushing VAT into the low-to-mid twenties, a regressive burden that lands on the poor. The revenue-neutral income-tax rate is about 20–21% — roughly what the top of the distribution already pays — so the "low rate" only looks low while someone else absorbs the gap. The honest test of any tax proposal is not whether it sounds pro-growth, but whether it says where the compensating revenue comes from and who bears it.
:::

The real value of working through this is not the verdict — it is the method. You now have a procedure you can apply to *any* tax change a politician, party, or commentator puts in front of you, and every step has a publicly checkable data source. The next time someone proposes a reform, run it through these five questions before you decide what you think.

:::framework
**The Tax Proposal Stress Test**

Apply these five questions to any proposed tax change. Each has a publicly available data source, so you can check the claims yourself rather than taking them on trust.

**1. What does it actually change — the rate, the base, the threshold, or the structure?**

Be precise about the mechanism. Cutting a *rate* is different from broadening the *base* (what gets taxed), raising a *threshold* (the point at which a tax starts), or changing the *structure* (flat versus progressive). Many proposals blur these deliberately. "Eliminate PAYE below R1 million, then a single rate above it" is not a cut in the tax on work — it is a two-bracket progressive tax. Source: the proposal's own detail, checked against SARS's published rates and thresholds.

**2. Who pays more and who pays less — in rands and as a share of income?**

A change that looks neutral in rands is often sharply unequal as a share of income, which is what actually matters for households. Ask specifically how it lands across the income distribution, and watch for the case where the people meant to benefit were barely paying the tax being cut. Source: SARS *Tax Statistics* (income-band tables) and Stats SA household-expenditure data.

**3. If it reduces one tax, where does the compensating revenue come from — and is that source named or hidden?**

This is the decisive question. A revenue loss does not disappear; it is recovered from another tax, borrowed, or matched by spending cuts. In South Africa an income-tax shortfall is most often closed with VAT — so ask whether VAT is the unstated other half of the plan, and at what rate. Source: National Treasury *Budget Review*; the rule of thumb that one VAT point ≈ R30 billion.

**4. What does it assume about the revenue constraint?**

Most proposals assume the state must raise a fixed sum, so every cut must be "paid for". That assumption is worth making explicit, because it is the hinge of the whole debate — and, as later articles argue, more contestable than it appears. Source: the *Budget Review*'s revenue and expenditure framework.

**5. What behaviour is it trying to encourage — and is the growth it promises actually likely?**

Every tax is a signal about what a society wants more or less of, so ask what a change steers people toward or away from. Then ask the harder question the supply-side case turns on: is the behavioural response large enough to deliver the promised growth? For South Africa the measured elasticity of taxable income is real but moderate, which means rate cuts move behaviour at the margin without paying for themselves. Source: the policy rationale in the *Budget Review*'s tax-proposals chapter, tested against the elasticity evidence (SA-TIED / SARS administrative-data research).

A proposal that survives all five questions is worth debating on the merits. One that fails the third — that cuts a tax without naming where the money comes from — is not a tax policy. It is a tax cut with the bill hidden.
:::
