## One Score for Every Municipality — Built From Public Data

South Africa has 257 municipalities, and there is no single, publicly available index that tells you how well each one is performing across the dimensions that matter most for economic growth and service delivery. This article builds one. The Municipal Performance Index (MPI) is a quantitative framework that scores every municipality on four pillars — Economic Development, Service Delivery, Governance and Finance, and Infrastructure and Sustainability — using publicly available data from Statistics South Africa, National Treasury, the Auditor-General, and the Department of Water and Sanitation. Each pillar contains between three and six measurable indicators. Each indicator is normalised to a 0-to-100 scale so that different units (percentages, rand values, institutional ratings) can be compared on equal terms. The pillar scores are combined using a weighted geometric mean — a method that rewards balanced performance and penalises municipalities that score well on one dimension while collapsing on another. The result is a single overall score, from 0 to 100, for every municipality in the country, classified into four performance tiers: High Performance (70 and above), Moderate (55 to 69), Needs Improvement (40 to 54), and Critical (below 40).

If you have been following this series, you already know why this matters. [Watching the Watchers](/articles/eg-3-3-watching-the-watchers) showed you that South Africa has invested heavily in the infrastructure of measurement but underinvested in the infrastructure of consequence. The Auditor-General audits every municipality annually. National Treasury publishes quarterly financial data through its Section 71 reports. Stats SA conducts the Census, the General Household Survey, and the Quarterly Labour Force Survey. The Department of Water and Sanitation publishes Blue Drop and Green Drop scores for water and wastewater quality. The data exists. What does not exist is a framework that synthesises all of it into a single, comparable, transparent score that allows you — an ordinary citizen, a journalist, a councillor, a researcher — to answer the question: is my municipality getting better or worse, and how does it compare to similar municipalities?

The MPI is designed to answer that question. It is not designed to replace the detailed analyses that institutions like the Auditor-General and National Treasury produce. It is designed to make those analyses accessible and actionable. A municipality's audit outcome matters, but it matters more when you can see it in context — alongside that municipality's service delivery record, its economic development performance, and its infrastructure investment execution. The MPI provides that context. And because the methodology is transparent and the data sources are public, you can verify every score, challenge every ranking, and hold every municipal council accountable for its performance against objective benchmarks.

This article gives you the complete methodology. By the end, you will understand exactly how the MPI works, why each design choice was made, and how to calculate a score yourself. If you want to see the results — the actual rankings — you will find them in [The Metro Rankings](/articles/eg-4-2-the-metro-rankings). If you want to understand the municipal governance structures that produce these outcomes, start with [Municipal Councils](/articles/3-3-municipal-councils).

## Why a Municipal Performance Index Matters

Economic growth does not happen at national level. It happens in specific places. A factory opens in a specific municipality. A small business registers in a specific town. A young person finds or fails to find a job in a specific local economy. The national GDP growth rate is an average that conceals enormous variation between places. In any given year, some South African municipalities are growing employment, attracting investment, and delivering services effectively, while others are losing businesses, shedding jobs, and allowing infrastructure to collapse. The national statistics tell you about the average. The MPI tells you about the distribution.

This matters for several reasons. The first is accountability. South Africa holds local government elections every five years. Voters are asked to choose between parties and candidates who promise to improve their municipality. But on what basis do you evaluate whether the previous administration succeeded or failed? Anecdotes and lived experience are valuable but incomplete. A pothole on your street tells you something about road maintenance, but it tells you nothing about the municipality's debt collection rate, its capital expenditure execution, or its performance on water quality standards. The MPI gives you a structured basis for evaluation — not a substitute for your own experience, but a complement to it.

The second reason is comparison. South Africa's 257 municipalities are not all the same. They range from the City of Johannesburg, with a population of over five million and an annual budget of tens of billions of rands, to tiny rural municipalities in the Eastern Cape or Limpopo with populations under fifty thousand and budgets measured in hundreds of millions. Comparing Johannesburg to a rural municipality in the former Transkei is not useful without adjusting for the fundamentally different contexts, resources, and challenges these municipalities face. The MPI addresses this through differentiated weighting — the relative importance of each pillar shifts depending on whether you are assessing a metropolitan municipality (Category A), a secondary city (Category B1), a medium-sized town (Category B2 or B3), or a rural municipality (Category B4). This means you are always comparing like with like, or at least as closely as the data permits.

The third reason is diagnosis. A single overall score tells you whether a municipality is performing well or poorly, but the pillar scores tell you why. A municipality might have a strong economic development score but a weak governance and finance score, which suggests that economic activity is happening despite rather than because of the municipal administration. Another municipality might have excellent governance and finance scores — clean audits, high debt collection, timely creditor payments — but weak economic development indicators, which suggests that good administration alone is not enough to drive growth in the absence of economic fundamentals. The four-pillar structure allows you to identify the specific areas where a municipality is succeeding and the specific areas where it is failing, which is far more useful for policy and advocacy than a single headline number.

The fourth reason is that South Africa needs this and does not currently have it. There is no shortage of municipal data in South Africa. What there is a shortage of is municipal data that has been synthesised, normalised, and made comparable. National Treasury publishes the Municipal Finance Data on municipaldata.treasury.gov.za and the companion site municipalmoney.gov.za. The Auditor-General publishes detailed audit reports for every municipality every year at agsa.co.za. Stats SA publishes demographic and labour market data at statssa.gov.za. But these datasets exist in silos. The financial data does not talk to the service delivery data, which does not talk to the audit data, which does not talk to the economic data. The MPI is an attempt to break those silos and provide a unified picture.

## The Four Pillars

The MPI rests on four pillars, each capturing a distinct dimension of municipal performance. The pillars are not weighted equally, and the weights vary depending on the category of municipality being assessed. This section explains each pillar and the rationale for its weighting.

### Pillar One: Economic Development

Economic Development is the pillar that most directly measures whether a municipality is creating the conditions for growth. It carries the highest weight for metropolitan municipalities (Category A) at 35 per cent and for secondary cities (Category B1) also at 35 per cent, reflecting the fact that these are the municipalities where the bulk of South Africa's economic activity is concentrated. For medium-sized towns (Category B2 and B3) and rural municipalities (Category B4), the weight drops to 30 per cent — still the joint-highest pillar — because while economic development matters everywhere, rural municipalities have less direct control over local economic conditions and face structural constraints that no municipal intervention can fully overcome.

The rationale for weighting Economic Development most heavily is straightforward. Municipalities exist to serve their residents, and the most fundamental service a local economy can provide is employment. A municipality that collects rates efficiently, maintains clean audits, and delivers water reliably but presides over an economy where forty per cent of working-age adults cannot find a job is not succeeding. The economic pillar captures whether the local economy is creating opportunities — and it measures this through indicators that are within the partial influence of the municipality (like business registrations and LED budget execution) as well as indicators that reflect the broader economic environment (like unemployment rates and employment growth).

### Pillar Two: Service Delivery

Service Delivery measures the municipality's core function: providing basic services to residents. It carries a weight of 25 per cent for metros, secondary cities, and rural municipalities, and 30 per cent for medium-sized towns (Category B2 and B3). The elevated weight for medium-sized towns reflects the reality that in these municipalities, service delivery is often the primary point of contact between the state and the citizen. Medium-sized towns are large enough to have significant service delivery obligations but small enough that failures in service delivery are immediately and acutely felt by the population.

This pillar measures whether people actually have access to water, sanitation, electricity, and refuse removal — the four foundational municipal services specified in the Constitution. But it goes beyond access to measure quality, through the Blue Drop score (which rates the quality of drinking water treatment and management) and the Green Drop score (which rates the quality of wastewater treatment). A municipality might report that ninety-five per cent of households have access to piped water, but if the Blue Drop score reveals that the water treatment works is failing to meet quality standards, the access figure masks a serious public health risk. The combination of access and quality indicators gives a more complete picture of whether people are actually receiving the services they are constitutionally entitled to.

### Pillar Three: Governance and Finance

Governance and Finance measures the institutional health of the municipality — whether it manages public money properly, pays its creditors on time, collects the revenue it is owed, fills the senior positions it needs, and executes its budget as planned. This pillar carries a weight of 25 per cent for metros, secondary cities, and medium-sized towns, and 30 per cent for rural municipalities (Category B4). The elevated weight for rural municipalities reflects a hard empirical reality: governance and financial management failures are the leading cause of municipal collapse in rural South Africa. When a rural municipality receives a disclaimer of opinion from the Auditor-General, when its debt collection rate falls below fifty per cent, when senior management positions sit vacant for years, the municipality is not merely underperforming — it is ceasing to function as an institution. For rural municipalities, governance capacity is not a secondary consideration. It is the binding constraint.

The indicators in this pillar draw heavily on the Auditor-General's annual MFMA audit outcomes and on National Treasury's quarterly Section 71 financial reports. These are the most reliable and consistently published datasets on municipal financial management in South Africa. The audit outcome is coded on a 0-to-100 scale (which this article explains in detail below) and provides a summary measure of financial management quality. The other indicators — unauthorised, irregular, fruitless and wasteful (UIFW) expenditure as a percentage of budget, debt collection rates, budget execution rates, creditor payment timeliness, and senior post vacancy rates — provide the granular detail that the audit outcome summarises.

### Pillar Four: Infrastructure and Sustainability

Infrastructure and Sustainability measures whether the municipality is investing in its own future. It carries a weight of 15 per cent across all municipal categories. This is the lowest-weighted pillar, not because infrastructure is unimportant — it is critically important — but because infrastructure investment is a longer-term indicator that takes years to translate into visible outcomes. A municipality that executes its capital budget effectively this year will see the benefits in improved infrastructure condition three, five, or ten years from now. The MPI captures this forward-looking dimension at a lower weight to ensure that current performance (economic development, service delivery, governance) is not overshadowed by capital spending figures that may not yet have translated into results.

The indicators in this pillar include capital expenditure execution (what percentage of the municipality's approved capital budget was actually spent), non-revenue water (what percentage of water entering the distribution system is lost to leaks, theft, or unbilled consumption), and public transport coverage. Non-revenue water is particularly telling because it is a direct measure of infrastructure condition. A municipality that loses forty per cent of its water before it reaches consumers has a distribution network that is physically deteriorating — pipes are leaking, meters are broken, connections are unauthorised. No amount of new water infrastructure investment will solve the problem if the existing infrastructure is losing nearly half of what it carries.

### The Weighting Table

The pillar weights for each municipal category are as follows. For Category A metropolitan municipalities, Economic Development receives 35 per cent, Service Delivery 25 per cent, Governance and Finance 25 per cent, and Infrastructure and Sustainability 15 per cent. For Category B1 secondary cities, the weights are identical: 35, 25, 25, and 15 per cent. For Category B2 and B3 medium-sized towns, the weights shift: Economic Development 30 per cent, Service Delivery 30 per cent, Governance and Finance 25 per cent, and Infrastructure and Sustainability 15 per cent. For Category B4 rural municipalities, the weights are Economic Development 30 per cent, Service Delivery 25 per cent, Governance and Finance 30 per cent, and Infrastructure and Sustainability 15 per cent.

These weights are not arbitrary. They reflect the structural differences between municipal categories and the empirical evidence about what drives municipal success and failure at each level. The weights are fixed for the purposes of the index — they do not change from year to year — which means that year-on-year changes in a municipality's MPI score reflect genuine changes in performance rather than changes in methodology.

## The Indicators: What Gets Measured

Each pillar contains a set of indicators drawn from publicly available data sources. This section explains every indicator, what it measures, why it was chosen, and where the data comes from.

### Economic Development Indicators

The Economic Development pillar for Category A municipalities contains six indicators. The first is the unemployment rate, drawn from the Stats SA Quarterly Labour Force Survey (QLFS). This is the official narrow unemployment rate for the municipal area — the percentage of the economically active population (those who are either employed or actively seeking work) that is unemployed. The QLFS is published quarterly and provides metro-level breakdowns for all eight metropolitan municipalities. For smaller municipalities, the data is less granular, and the MPI relies on Census and General Household Survey estimates where QLFS data is not available at the municipal level.

The second indicator is youth unemployment, also drawn from the QLFS. This measures the unemployment rate among people aged 15 to 34. Youth unemployment is included as a separate indicator because it captures a dimension of economic failure that the overall unemployment rate partially conceals. A municipality can have a moderate overall unemployment rate while experiencing catastrophic youth unemployment, which means the local economy is sustaining existing jobs but failing to create new ones for labour market entrants. Youth unemployment rates in South Africa routinely exceed fifty per cent in some municipalities, and this indicator ensures that such failures are reflected in the economic development score.

The third indicator is employment growth, measured as the year-on-year percentage change in total employment in the municipal area, drawn from the QLFS. The unemployment rate is a snapshot; employment growth is a trend. A municipality with a high unemployment rate but positive employment growth is moving in the right direction. A municipality with a moderate unemployment rate but negative employment growth is deteriorating. Including both the level (unemployment rate) and the trend (employment growth) ensures that the MPI captures both the current state and the direction of change.

The fourth indicator is business registrations per ten thousand residents, drawn from the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC). New business registrations are an imperfect but useful proxy for entrepreneurial activity and economic dynamism. A municipality where forty-two new businesses register per ten thousand residents annually is experiencing more economic dynamism than one where twelve register. The CIPC publishes registration data monthly, and it can be geocoded to the municipal level based on the registered address of the business.

The fifth indicator is property rates growth, measured as the year-on-year percentage change in property rates revenue, drawn from National Treasury's Section 71 quarterly financial reports. Property rates are the single most important source of own-revenue for municipalities. Growth in property rates revenue reflects a combination of property value appreciation, new property development, and improved billing and collection. It is an indirect but telling measure of local economic health — when property values rise and new developments are completed, the economic base of the municipality is expanding.

The sixth indicator is LED budget execution, measuring the percentage of the municipality's budgeted local economic development expenditure that was actually spent during the financial year, also drawn from Treasury Section 71 reports. This indicator captures effort and capacity rather than outcome. A municipality that budgets R50 million for local economic development but spends only R20 million is either failing to implement its own economic plans or was unrealistic in its budgeting. Either way, a low LED budget execution rate signals a gap between stated economic development ambition and actual delivery.

### Service Delivery Indicators

The Service Delivery pillar contains six indicators. The first four measure access to basic services: the percentage of households with access to piped water (from Stats SA Census data), the percentage with access to adequate sanitation (Census), the percentage with access to electricity for lighting (Census), and the percentage with access to refuse removal at least weekly (Census). These are the four foundational municipal services and they are measured as simple percentages of the population served. Census data is comprehensive but infrequent — the most recent full census was conducted in 2022. Between censuses, the General Household Survey provides annual estimates, though at lower geographic granularity.

The fifth and sixth indicators are the Blue Drop and Green Drop scores, published by the Department of Water and Sanitation (DWS). The Blue Drop programme assesses the quality of drinking water treatment and management at every water treatment works in the country. Each works receives a score from 0 to 100 based on criteria including water quality compliance, process management, maintenance, skills, and monitoring. The municipal Blue Drop score is the weighted average across all water treatment works in the municipality. The Green Drop programme does the same for wastewater treatment works. A municipality's Green Drop score reflects the quality of its sewage treatment — a critical environmental and public health measure that is frequently neglected in public discourse.

The Blue Drop and Green Drop scores are particularly valuable because they measure quality rather than access. A municipality might report ninety-eight per cent water access, but if its Blue Drop score is thirty-five out of one hundred, that "access" is to water that may not meet safety standards. The combination of access percentages and quality scores provides the most complete picture of service delivery that publicly available data allows.

### Governance and Finance Indicators

The Governance and Finance pillar contains six indicators. The first is the audit outcome from the Auditor-General's annual MFMA audit, coded on a numerical scale. The coding is as follows: a Clean Audit (unqualified opinion with no findings on compliance or performance) receives a score of 100. An Unqualified opinion with findings receives 75. A Qualified opinion receives 50. An Adverse opinion receives 25. A Disclaimer of opinion receives 0. This coding converts the Auditor-General's categorical assessment into a continuous scale that can be averaged and compared. The intervals are not perfectly equidistant in terms of what they represent — the gap between Clean and Unqualified with findings is, in practice, smaller than the gap between Qualified and Adverse — but the five-point scale provides a reasonable approximation for index purposes.

The second indicator is UIFW expenditure as a percentage of the total municipal budget, drawn from the Auditor-General's MFMA reports. UIFW stands for unauthorised, irregular, and fruitless and wasteful expenditure — the three categories of problematic spending that the AGSA identifies and quantifies. Expressed as a percentage of the total budget, this indicator tells you how much of the municipality's spending was outside the rules. A municipality where two per cent of spending is flagged as UIFW is fundamentally different from one where twenty-five per cent is flagged.

The third indicator is the debt collection rate, drawn from Treasury Section 71 reports. This measures the percentage of billed revenue that the municipality actually collects. Municipal finances depend on the ability to bill residents and businesses for services (water, electricity, refuse removal, property rates) and to collect what is billed. A municipality with a debt collection rate of ninety per cent is financially sustainable. One with a rate of fifty-five per cent is heading toward insolvency, because it is delivering services it cannot pay for. Debt collection rates vary enormously across South African municipalities, from above ninety per cent in well-managed metros to below forty per cent in some dysfunctional rural municipalities.

The fourth indicator is budget execution, measured as the percentage of the municipality's adjusted operating budget that was actually spent, drawn from Treasury Section 71 reports. Underspending is a symptom of incapacity. A municipality that cannot spend the money it has been allocated — whether from own revenue or intergovernmental transfers — is failing to deliver the services and programmes that the budget was designed to fund. Chronic underspending often reflects vacancy in key positions, procurement delays, or simply a lack of project management capability.

The fifth indicator is creditor payment within thirty days, measuring the percentage of valid invoices paid within thirty days of receipt, drawn from Treasury Section 71 reports. This is a legal requirement under the MFMA and a practical indicator of financial management quality. When municipalities fail to pay their suppliers on time, the consequences cascade: suppliers refuse to do business with the municipality, or they inflate their prices to compensate for delayed payment, or they deliver lower-quality goods and services. Persistent late payment also undermines the local economy, because many municipal suppliers are small businesses that depend on timely payment for their own cash flow.

The sixth indicator is the percentage of senior management posts that are filled, drawn from the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA). This measures institutional capacity at the leadership level. When the municipal manager position is vacant, or when the chief financial officer position is filled by an acting appointment for years on end, or when three of five section heads are vacant, the municipality cannot function effectively regardless of its budget or its policies. Senior post vacancy rates are a leading indicator of institutional decline — they signal the exodus of skilled professionals from the municipality, often driven by political instability, poor working conditions, or interference by politicians in administrative appointments.

### Infrastructure and Sustainability Indicators

The Infrastructure and Sustainability pillar contains three indicators. The first is capital expenditure execution, measuring the percentage of the municipality's approved capital budget that was actually spent, drawn from Treasury Section 71 reports. Capital expenditure is the spending that builds and maintains infrastructure — roads, water pipes, electrical substations, community facilities. A municipality that budgets R200 million for capital projects but spends only R80 million is failing to maintain and expand its infrastructure, which means the current service delivery levels are being sustained on ageing and deteriorating assets. Capital underspending is one of the most persistent problems in South African local government, driven by procurement bottlenecks, project management capacity constraints, land acquisition delays, and politically motivated project changes.

The second indicator is non-revenue water, published by the Department of Water and Sanitation. This measures the percentage of water entering the municipality's distribution system that is not billed to consumers — lost to physical leaks, commercial losses (meter inaccuracies, theft, unbilled consumption), or administrative errors. Non-revenue water is one of the most telling indicators of infrastructure condition available. Well-managed water utilities globally target non-revenue water below fifteen per cent. Many South African municipalities have non-revenue water rates above forty per cent, meaning nearly half of the treated water they pump into the system disappears before it reaches a paying customer. This is simultaneously an infrastructure crisis (the pipes are literally falling apart), a financial crisis (the municipality is paying to treat water it cannot sell), and a sustainability crisis (the water resource is being wasted in a water-scarce country).

The third indicator is public transport coverage, measuring the percentage of the municipal population with access to formal public transport services. This indicator is most relevant for Category A and B1 municipalities, where public transport is a significant component of urban functionality. For smaller municipalities where formal public transport is largely absent, this indicator is less meaningful and may be replaced with alternative infrastructure measures as the MPI is refined over time.

## Normalisation: Making the Numbers Comparable

The raw indicators in the MPI are measured in different units. Unemployment is a percentage. Business registrations are a count per ten thousand people. The Blue Drop score is already on a 0-to-100 scale. The audit outcome has been coded to a 0-to-100 scale. To combine these into pillar scores and an overall score, every indicator must be converted to the same scale. This process is called normalisation.

The MPI uses min-max normalisation, which converts every indicator to a score between 0 and 100. The formulas are straightforward. For indicators where a higher value is better (like employment growth, debt collection rate, or Blue Drop score), the formula is: Score equals Actual minus Minimum, divided by Maximum minus Minimum, multiplied by 100. For indicators where a lower value is better (like unemployment rate or non-revenue water), the formula is reversed: Score equals Maximum minus Actual, divided by Maximum minus Minimum, multiplied by 100.

The Minimum and Maximum in these formulas are not the observed minimum and maximum in the data. They are pre-set goalposts — fixed benchmarks that define the range of performance the index considers. This is a deliberate design choice. If the normalisation used the observed minimum and maximum, the scores would shift every year as the best and worst performers changed, making year-on-year comparisons meaningless. By fixing the goalposts, the MPI ensures that a score of 70 means the same thing in 2024 as it does in 2029.

The key goalposts are as follows. For the unemployment rate, the goalposts are 15 per cent (best realistic case) and 40 per cent (worst realistic case). For youth unemployment, 25 per cent and 60 per cent. For employment growth, minus 5 per cent and plus 5 per cent. For business registrations per ten thousand residents, 0 and 50. For water access, 80 per cent and 100 per cent. For the Blue Drop score, 0 and 100. For the audit outcome (already coded), 0 and 100. For the debt collection rate, 50 per cent and 95 per cent. For capital expenditure execution, 40 per cent and 95 per cent. For non-revenue water, 15 per cent (best) and 50 per cent (worst).

### A Worked Example: Cape Town's Unemployment Score

To make this concrete, consider how the City of Cape Town's unemployment rate is normalised. Cape Town's unemployment rate is 23.4 per cent. Unemployment is a "lower is better" indicator. The goalposts are 15 per cent (Minimum, representing the best realistic case) and 40 per cent (Maximum, representing the worst realistic case).

The formula is: Score = ((Maximum - Actual) / (Maximum - Minimum)) x 100.

Substituting the values: Score = ((40 - 23.4) / (40 - 15)) x 100 = (16.6 / 25) x 100 = 66.4.

Cape Town receives an unemployment score of 66.4 out of 100. This means Cape Town's unemployment rate is closer to the best-case goalpost than to the worst-case goalpost, but there is still significant room for improvement. A municipality with an unemployment rate at the best-case goalpost of 15 per cent would score 100. A municipality at the worst-case goalpost of 40 per cent would score 0.

The same logic applies to every other indicator. Cape Town's youth unemployment rate of 38.0 per cent produces a score of 62.9. Its employment growth of plus 2.5 per cent produces a score of 75.0. Its business registrations of 42 per ten thousand produce a score of 84.0. Its property rates growth of plus 6.5 per cent produces a score of 76.7. Its LED budget execution of 92 per cent produces a score of 84.0. The Economic Development pillar score for Cape Town, calculated as the average of these six normalised indicator scores, is 74.8.

## The Weighted Geometric Mean: Why It Matters

Once each pillar has a score, the four pillar scores must be combined into a single overall MPI score. The MPI uses a weighted geometric mean rather than a simple weighted average. This is the single most important methodological choice in the entire framework, and it requires explanation.

A simple weighted average adds the pillar scores together, each multiplied by its weight. If Cape Town's four pillar scores were 74.8, 85.0, 90.0, and 65.0, and the weights were 0.35, 0.25, 0.25, and 0.15, the weighted average would be (74.8 x 0.35) + (85.0 x 0.25) + (90.0 x 0.25) + (65.0 x 0.15) = 26.18 + 21.25 + 22.50 + 9.75 = 79.68. This is a perfectly reasonable number, but it has a problem: it allows a municipality to compensate for catastrophic failure in one pillar with strong performance in another. A municipality that scores 95 on Governance and Finance but 15 on Service Delivery would still receive a respectable overall score under a simple average, even though a Service Delivery score of 15 means residents lack access to basic water, sanitation, and electricity — a condition that no amount of clean audits can justify.

The weighted geometric mean solves this problem. The formula is: Overall Score = (Pillar One to the power of weight one) multiplied by (Pillar Two to the power of weight two) multiplied by (Pillar Three to the power of weight three) multiplied by (Pillar Four to the power of weight four), where the four weights sum to one.

The mathematical property that makes this valuable is that the geometric mean is always less than or equal to the arithmetic mean, and the gap between the two grows as the spread between the inputs increases. In plain language: the geometric mean penalises imbalance. A municipality that scores 80 across all four pillars will get an overall score very close to 80. But a municipality that scores 95 on three pillars and 20 on one will get an overall score significantly lower than a simple average would suggest, because the geometric mean drags the score down toward the weakest pillar. This reflects a substantive judgement: a municipality that is excellent at financial management but cannot provide water to its residents is not performing adequately overall. The geometric mean enforces this judgement mathematically.

For Cape Town, with pillar scores that are relatively balanced and strong, the overall MPI score is 84.0, placing it firmly in the High Performance tier. For Johannesburg, where the scores across pillars are more uneven and include areas of notable weakness, the overall MPI score is 52.2, placing it in the Needs Improvement tier. The contrast is instructive. Both are Category A metropolitan municipalities. Both have large budgets, significant institutional capacity, and major economic bases. But the MPI reveals a fundamental difference in the consistency and quality of municipal performance across the four dimensions that matter.

:::expert
The Financial and Fiscal Commission (FFC), South Africa's constitutional advisory body on intergovernmental fiscal relations, has long advocated for a more systematic approach to measuring municipal performance. The FFC's annual submissions to Parliament have repeatedly noted that the intergovernmental fiscal system allocates grants to municipalities based primarily on formulae that measure need (population, poverty, backlogs) rather than performance (service delivery quality, financial management, economic outcomes). The MPI framework aligns with the FFC's recommendation that performance measurement should be transparent, multi-dimensional, and based on publicly available data. The FFC has cautioned, however, that any performance index must account for the vastly different capacities and endowments of municipalities — a concern the MPI addresses through its differentiated weighting system. The FFC's work on municipal fiscal health, available through the FFC's website and its annual Division of Revenue submissions, provides essential context for understanding why some municipalities perform well and others do not.
:::

## Performance Tiers

The MPI classifies every municipality into one of four performance tiers based on its overall score. These tiers are designed to be intuitive and actionable.

A municipality with a score of 70 or above falls into the High Performance tier. This does not mean the municipality is perfect — a score of 70 still implies significant room for improvement on several indicators. It means the municipality is delivering reasonably well across all four pillars, with no single dimension in critical failure. High-performing municipalities are candidates for benchmarking — other municipalities can study their practices to identify what is working.

A score between 55 and 69 places a municipality in the Moderate tier. These municipalities are functional and delivering on most fronts, but with notable weaknesses in one or more areas that prevent them from reaching the High Performance threshold. For a Moderate municipality, the pillar breakdown is essential: it tells you exactly which dimension needs the most attention.

A score between 40 and 54 places a municipality in the Needs Improvement tier. This is a warning zone. Municipalities in this tier are experiencing significant failures in at least one pillar, and the overall performance is below the level that citizens should reasonably expect. Johannesburg's score of 52.2 falls in this tier, which underscores that even South Africa's largest and wealthiest metropolitan municipality has serious performance gaps when measured across all four dimensions.

A score below 40 places a municipality in the Critical tier. These municipalities are in institutional distress. They are failing to deliver basic services, managing public finances poorly, losing institutional capacity, and presiding over economies that offer few opportunities to their residents. Many of the municipalities that fall into this tier are already the subject of Section 139 interventions (where the province steps in to assume municipal functions) or have been flagged for financial intervention by National Treasury. The MPI does not tell you anything new about these municipalities that the individual data sources have not already revealed — but it tells you about them in a single, comparable number that makes the scale of the crisis impossible to ignore.

## Data Sources and Where to Find Them

The MPI draws on data from a defined set of public sources. Transparency about data origins is essential because it allows you to verify any score the MPI produces and to challenge any ranking you believe is inaccurate.

Statistics South Africa (Stats SA) provides the economic and demographic data through several instruments. The Quarterly Labour Force Survey, published four times a year, provides unemployment and employment data at the metro level and for some secondary cities. The Census, most recently conducted in 2022, provides comprehensive municipal-level data on service access (water, sanitation, electricity, refuse removal), demographics, and economic activity. The General Household Survey, published annually, provides supplementary household-level data on service delivery and living conditions. All Stats SA data is available at statssa.gov.za.

National Treasury provides municipal financial data through the Section 71 quarterly reports, which every municipality is required to submit under the MFMA. These reports contain detailed revenue and expenditure data, including property rates revenue, debt collection performance, operating and capital budget execution, and creditor payment statistics. Treasury has made this data accessible through two online platforms: municipaldata.treasury.gov.za (the raw data interface) and municipalmoney.gov.za (a more user-friendly presentation aimed at citizens and journalists). Both are freely accessible and do not require registration.

The Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) provides the governance data through its annual MFMA audit reports. The general report on local government audit outcomes is published annually and covers all 257 municipalities plus their municipal entities. Individual audit reports for each municipality are also published. UIFW expenditure figures, audit outcomes, and material irregularity findings are all drawn from AGSA reports, available at agsa.co.za.

The Department of Water and Sanitation (DWS) provides the Blue Drop and Green Drop scores. These assessments rate every water and wastewater treatment works in the country. The DWS publishes the results periodically, though the publication schedule has been less consistent than the annual audit cycle. Non-revenue water data is also published by DWS through its regulatory reporting processes.

The Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC) provides business registration data. CIPC publishes monthly statistics on new company registrations, which can be geocoded to the municipal level based on registered addresses. The CIPC data is available through its website and through periodic statistical releases.

The Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA) provides data on senior management post vacancies and other institutional capacity indicators. COGTA's reporting is more ad hoc than the other sources — it does not follow a fixed annual publication cycle — which makes it the least reliable of the MPI's data inputs. Where COGTA data is unavailable or outdated, the MPI flags the affected indicator and applies the missing data rules described below.

:::expert
The South African Local Government Association (SALGA), as the representative body for all 257 municipalities, has access to institutional data that is not always captured in the formal reporting systems. SALGA's municipal barometer and its various capacity assessments provide supplementary information on municipal governance, staffing, and institutional functionality. While the MPI relies on publicly published data from official sources, SALGA's data could potentially be integrated into future iterations of the index to improve the coverage and accuracy of the governance indicators. SALGA has also been a vocal advocate for differentiated approaches to municipal performance assessment — recognising that the same standards cannot be applied uniformly to a metro with ten thousand employees and a rural municipality with fifty — which aligns with the MPI's differentiated weighting approach.
:::

## Data Limitations and Missing Data Rules

No index is better than its data, and the MPI's data has real limitations that must be acknowledged openly.

The first limitation is timeliness. Census data is comprehensive but infrequent — the 2022 Census was the first since 2011. Between censuses, service delivery access figures are based on estimates from the General Household Survey, which has smaller sample sizes and wider confidence intervals at the municipal level. QLFS data is quarterly but only provides metro-level estimates for the eight Category A municipalities. For smaller municipalities, labour market data relies on Census and GHS estimates that may be several years old.

The second limitation is coverage. Not all indicators are available for all 257 municipalities. The Blue Drop and Green Drop assessments do not cover every municipality in every reporting period. CIPC business registration data requires geocoding that may misallocate registrations to the wrong municipality in some cases. COGTA data on senior post vacancies is published irregularly and may not reflect current conditions.

The third limitation is accuracy. Municipal self-reported data — including some of the figures in Treasury Section 71 reports — is only as reliable as the municipality's own financial management systems. A municipality with a disclaimer of opinion from the Auditor-General is, by definition, a municipality whose financial records are unreliable. Using its self-reported debt collection rate or budget execution rate at face value is problematic because the very data systems that produce those numbers have been found to be untrustworthy.

The MPI addresses these limitations through two rules. First, if more than 30 per cent of a municipality's indicators have missing data, the municipality is excluded from the ranking entirely. It receives pillar scores where data is available, but no overall MPI score and no rank position. This prevents a municipality from being ranked on the basis of a partial and potentially misleading picture. Second, if less than 30 per cent of indicators have missing data, the missing values are imputed using the median value of the municipality's peer group (municipalities in the same category in the same province) and the imputed values are flagged in the published results. This allows the municipality to receive a score and a rank, but the flagging ensures that users of the data know which scores are based on actual data and which are based on peer imputation.

These rules are conservative by design. The MPI would rather exclude a municipality from the ranking than produce a misleading score. As data coverage improves over time — particularly if the Blue Drop and Green Drop assessments return to consistent annual publication, and if COGTA establishes a regular reporting cycle for institutional capacity data — the proportion of municipalities excluded for missing data should decline.

:::takeaway
The Municipal Performance Index is built entirely from publicly available data. Every indicator can be verified, every score can be recalculated, and every ranking can be challenged using information that is freely accessible online. The data sources are Stats SA (statssa.gov.za) for economic and demographic indicators, National Treasury (municipaldata.treasury.gov.za and municipalmoney.gov.za) for financial indicators, the Auditor-General (agsa.co.za) for governance indicators, and the Department of Water and Sanitation for service quality indicators. The transparency of the methodology is not a feature — it is the point. A performance index that citizens cannot verify is a performance index that can be manipulated.
:::

## Implementation Phases

Building the MPI from methodology to operational reality is a phased process. The phasing reflects practical constraints: data availability varies by municipal category, the technical infrastructure for calculating and publishing scores needs to be built incrementally, and the methodology itself should be tested and refined before being applied at national scale.

Phase 1, covering months one through six, is the metropolitan pilot. This phase calculates MPI scores for South Africa's eight metropolitan municipalities — Buffalo City, City of Cape Town, City of Johannesburg, City of Tshwane, eThekwini, Ekurhuleni, Mangaung, and Nelson Mandela Bay. The metros are the natural starting point because data availability is best for Category A municipalities. The QLFS provides quarterly labour market data at the metro level. Treasury Section 71 data is most complete and reliable for metros, which have the largest financial management teams. AGSA audit data covers all metros annually. Blue Drop and Green Drop assessments have historically covered metro water and wastewater works. The metro pilot allows the methodology to be tested, validated, and refined before extending to municipalities where data is less complete.

Phase 2, covering months seven through twelve, extends the MPI to Category B1 secondary cities and builds a public dashboard. South Africa has approximately twenty-one secondary cities (the exact number depends on the classification framework used), including municipalities like Msunduzi (Pietermaritzburg), Rustenburg, Polokwane, Mbombela (Nelspruit), and Emfuleni (Vereeniging/Vanderbijlpark). These municipalities are large enough to have reasonable data coverage but diverse enough to test the methodology under more varied conditions. The public dashboard — an online platform where citizens can look up their municipality's MPI score, pillar scores, and individual indicator values — is built during this phase, using the metro and secondary city data as the initial content.

Phase 3, covering months thirteen through eighteen, extends the MPI to all 257 municipalities and constructs a retrospective time series from 2019 to 2024. The retrospective calculation is important because it allows users to assess trends — is a municipality improving or declining over time? — rather than relying on a single point-in-time snapshot. The retrospective period is limited to 2019-2024 because earlier data is less consistent (pre-2019 Treasury reporting formats differ in some respects, and the 2022 Census replaced 2011 Census data for service delivery indicators). As annual data accumulates beyond the initial retrospective, the time series will extend automatically.

:::expert
National Treasury's Municipal Finance Improvement Programme (MFIP) and its successor initiatives have for years grappled with the challenge of translating municipal financial data into actionable performance information. Treasury's own internal assessments have noted that while the Section 71 reporting system generates substantial data, the data is not always used effectively for performance management — either by the municipalities themselves or by the national and provincial spheres of government that are supposed to provide oversight. The MPI's use of Treasury data for its Governance and Finance pillar aligns with Treasury's long-standing advocacy for data-driven municipal oversight. Treasury has also emphasised the importance of benchmarking — comparing municipalities to their peers rather than to an abstract standard — which the MPI facilitates through its category-specific weighting and its peer-group imputation rules for missing data.
:::

## How to Use the MPI

The MPI is a tool, not a verdict. It is designed to be used in several ways, and being explicit about intended use helps prevent misuse.

For citizens, the MPI provides a structured starting point for evaluating your municipality's performance. If your municipality scores 45 overall, you know it is in the Needs Improvement tier. The pillar breakdown tells you where the problem is concentrated — governance, service delivery, economic development, or infrastructure. You can take that information to council meetings, use it in public submissions, reference it when engaging with ward councillors, or use it as context when reading media coverage of your municipality.

For journalists, the MPI provides a framework for comparative reporting. Instead of covering individual audit findings or service delivery failures in isolation, the MPI allows you to report on patterns — which metros are improving and which are declining, which pillar is the weakest across a province, how the secondary cities compare to each other. The MPI does not replace investigative journalism. It provides a data-driven map that helps journalists know where to investigate.

For researchers, the MPI provides a dependent variable. If you want to study what causes some municipalities to perform better than others, you need a consistent, multi-dimensional measure of municipal performance. The MPI provides that. You can regress MPI scores against potential explanatory variables — political stability, administrative capacity, intergovernmental grant dependency, population density, economic structure — to identify the factors that most strongly predict municipal performance.

For provincial and national government, the MPI provides an early warning system. A municipality whose MPI score drops from 55 to 42 over three years is on a trajectory toward institutional failure. The pillar breakdown identifies which dimension is driving the decline, which allows the provincial government or National Treasury to target support and intervention more effectively than a one-size-fits-all approach permits.

For all users, the pillar and indicator detail matters more than the headline score. The overall MPI number is useful as a summary and for ranking purposes, but the real value lies in the disaggregated data. Two municipalities with the same overall score of 55 might have completely different profiles — one strong on governance but weak on economic development, the other the reverse. The policy response to each would be entirely different, and only the pillar-level data reveals this.

:::takeaway
The weighted geometric mean is the methodological heart of the MPI. Unlike a simple average, it penalises municipalities that perform well in some dimensions while failing in others. This means a municipality cannot achieve a high overall score by excelling at financial management while neglecting service delivery, or by presiding over a dynamic economy while allowing governance to collapse. The geometric mean enforces a simple principle: a well-performing municipality must perform adequately across all four pillars. Extreme weakness in any one area drags the overall score down disproportionately, which reflects the lived reality that citizens experience — a clean audit means nothing if the water is not running.
:::

## Practical Framework: MPI Methodology Guide

This section walks you through the complete calculation process from raw data to final score, step by step. You can follow this guide to calculate the MPI score for any municipality where sufficient data is available.

Step one is to gather the raw data. For each municipality, collect the most recent available value for every indicator in each pillar. Record the data source and the reporting period for each value. If an indicator value is unavailable, record it as missing. Count the number of missing indicators. If more than 30 per cent of all indicators are missing, stop here — the municipality cannot be scored in this cycle.

Step two is to normalise each indicator. For each available indicator, identify whether it is a "higher is better" or "lower is better" measure. Look up the goalposts for that indicator (the fixed Minimum and Maximum values). Apply the appropriate formula. For "higher is better" indicators: Score = ((Actual - Minimum) / (Maximum - Minimum)) x 100. For "lower is better" indicators: Score = ((Maximum - Actual) / (Maximum - Minimum)) x 100. If the calculated score falls below 0, set it to 0. If it exceeds 100, set it to 100. This capping ensures that extreme outliers do not distort the index.

Step three is to impute missing values where necessary. If fewer than 30 per cent of indicators are missing, identify the municipality's peer group (same category, same province). For each missing indicator, calculate the median value of that indicator across all peer municipalities that have data. Assign the peer median as the imputed value and flag the indicator as imputed.

Step four is to calculate pillar scores. For each pillar, take the simple average of all normalised indicator scores (including any imputed values) within that pillar. The result is the pillar score, on a 0-to-100 scale.

Step five is to identify the correct weights. Look up the municipality's category (A, B1, B2/B3, or B4) and apply the corresponding pillar weights from the weighting table.

Step six is to calculate the overall MPI score using the weighted geometric mean. The formula is: MPI = (Economic Development score raised to the power of its weight) multiplied by (Service Delivery score raised to the power of its weight) multiplied by (Governance and Finance score raised to the power of its weight) multiplied by (Infrastructure and Sustainability score raised to the power of its weight). Ensure the four weights sum to 1.00. The result is the overall MPI score.

Step seven is to assign the performance tier. If the MPI score is 70 or above, the municipality is High Performance. If 55 to 69, Moderate. If 40 to 54, Needs Improvement. If below 40, Critical.

Step eight is to document and publish. Record the overall score, pillar scores, individual indicator scores, data sources, reporting periods, any imputed values, and the performance tier. Transparency is not optional — it is what makes the index credible.

As a complete worked example, Cape Town's Economic Development pillar scores are: Unemployment Rate 66.4, Youth Unemployment 62.9, Employment Growth 75.0, Business Registrations per 10K 84.0, Property Rates Growth 76.7, LED Budget Execution 84.0. The pillar average is 74.8. When this pillar score is combined with Cape Town's Service Delivery, Governance and Finance, and Infrastructure and Sustainability pillar scores using the weighted geometric mean with Category A weights (0.35, 0.25, 0.25, 0.15), the overall MPI score is 84.0 — placing Cape Town in the High Performance tier. Compare this to Johannesburg, whose overall MPI score of 52.2 places it in the Needs Improvement tier, reflecting weaker and less balanced performance across the four pillars.

:::takeaway
You do not need to be a statistician to use the MPI. The methodology is deliberately transparent and the calculations require nothing more than basic arithmetic and a spreadsheet. If you can subtract, divide, and use the power function in a spreadsheet, you can calculate a municipality's MPI score from publicly available data. The value of this is not merely academic. When you can calculate the score yourself, you can verify the published rankings, challenge scores you believe are wrong, and hold both the index producers and the municipalities accountable. A performance index that citizens can reproduce is a performance index that cannot be quietly manipulated.
:::

:::framework
**The MPI Calculation Guide**

You can calculate a Municipal Performance Index score for any South African municipality using publicly available data. Follow these steps:

**Step 1: Identify your municipality's category and peer group.**

Determine whether your municipality is Category A (metro), B1 (secondary city), B2/B3 (medium town), or B4 (rural) using the Municipal Demarcation Board website (demarcation.org.za). Your municipality should only be compared to others in the same category.

**Step 2: Gather raw data from primary sources.**

Collect the most recent values for each indicator. Use National Treasury's Municipal Money portal (municipalmoney.gov.za) for financial data — revenue collection, expenditure, capital budgets, irregular expenditure, audit outcomes. Use Statistics South Africa (statssa.gov.za) for economic and demographic data. Use the Auditor-General's reports (agsa.co.za) for governance indicators. Use the Department of Water and Sanitation for Blue Drop and Green Drop water quality scores. If more than 30% of indicators are missing, the municipality cannot be reliably scored.

**Step 3: Normalise each indicator to a 0-100 scale.**

For "higher is better" indicators (debt collection rate, water access, employment growth): Score = ((Actual - Min) / (Max - Min)) x 100. For "lower is better" indicators (unemployment, non-revenue water): Score = ((Max - Actual) / (Max - Min)) x 100. Use the fixed goalposts: unemployment 15-40%, youth unemployment 25-60%, employment growth -5% to +5%, debt collection 50-95%, capital execution 40-95%, non-revenue water 15-50%. Cap scores at 0 and 100.

**Step 4: Calculate pillar scores and the overall MPI.**

Average the normalised scores within each pillar (Economic Development, Service Delivery, Governance and Finance, Infrastructure and Sustainability). Then apply the weighted geometric mean using your category's weights. For metros: 0.35, 0.25, 0.25, 0.15. For B4 rural: 0.30, 0.25, 0.30, 0.15. The formula is: MPI = (P1^w1) x (P2^w2) x (P3^w3) x (P4^w4).

**Step 5: Assign the performance tier.**

High Performance: 70 and above. Moderate: 55 to 69. Needs Improvement: 40 to 54. Critical: below 40. Compare your result to peer municipalities in the same category to understand where your municipality stands relative to structurally similar institutions.

With these five steps, you can verify any published MPI ranking, challenge scores you believe are wrong, and hold your municipal council accountable against objective, data-driven benchmarks.
:::

## Resources

The primary data sources for the MPI are all freely accessible online. Statistics South Africa publishes the Quarterly Labour Force Survey, Census results, and General Household Survey at statssa.gov.za, where you can download detailed tables broken down by metropolitan and municipal area. National Treasury's municipal finance data is available through two platforms: municipaldata.treasury.gov.za provides the raw Section 71 quarterly data in downloadable format, while municipalmoney.gov.za presents the same data in a more accessible interface designed for non-specialist users, including the ability to look up individual municipalities and compare them on key financial indicators. The Auditor-General publishes all MFMA audit reports, including the annual general report on local government and individual municipal audit reports, at agsa.co.za. The Department of Water and Sanitation publishes Blue Drop and Green Drop assessment results through its regulatory reporting systems, though the publication schedule has varied. The CIPC publishes company registration statistics that can be filtered by region. For those interested in the academic literature on composite index construction, the OECD-JRC Handbook on Constructing Composite Indicators provides a rigorous treatment of normalisation methods, weighting schemes, and aggregation approaches, including a detailed discussion of geometric versus arithmetic means. The Financial and Fiscal Commission's annual submissions to Parliament, available through the FFC website, provide essential context on the intergovernmental fiscal framework within which municipalities operate, and SALGA's municipal barometer offers supplementary institutional data. For citizens seeking to engage directly with their municipality's performance, [Your Economic Citizenship](/articles/eg-5-3-your-economic-citizenship) provides practical guidance on using public data for civic advocacy and accountability.
