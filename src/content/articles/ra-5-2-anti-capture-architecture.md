## Capture Is a Structural Condition, Not a Single Scandal

When most South Africans hear the word "capture," they think of a specific story — the Gupta family, their relationship with former President Jacob Zuma, and the systematic redirection of state resources documented by the Zondo Commission. That story is real and important. But if you think of capture only as that story, you will misunderstand both the problem and the solution. Capture is not a single scandal. It is a structural condition. And it exists in far more places than Saxonwold.

## What Capture Actually Means

Start with the distinction between corruption and capture, because the two are different problems requiring different solutions. Corruption is transactional. A municipal official takes a bribe to approve a building plan. A traffic officer accepts cash to overlook a violation. A procurement official steers a tender to a family member's company. These are individual acts of dishonesty, and they can be addressed through investigation, prosecution, and punishment. South Africa has a well-developed legal framework for fighting corruption — the Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act, the Public Finance Management Act, the Municipal Finance Management Act, and a network of institutions from the National Prosecuting Authority to the Special Investigating Unit. Corruption is a crime committed by individuals within an institution that is otherwise functioning according to its mandate.

Capture is something fundamentally different. Capture occurs when an institution's decisions systematically serve private interests rather than its public mandate — not because one person is corrupt, but because the institution's structure, incentives, and decision-making processes have been reorganised to produce that outcome. When an institution is captured, you do not need to bribe anyone. The normal functioning of the institution already delivers benefits to the capturing interest. The appointment system places loyal people in key positions. The procurement system channels resources to connected suppliers. The regulatory system protects incumbents rather than the public. The information system conceals problems rather than revealing them. Each of these mechanisms operates through formal, legal processes — appointments are made through selection panels, tenders are awarded through supply chain management, regulations are applied through published criteria. The machinery looks legitimate. The outcomes are not.

The Zondo Commission on State Capture, which sat from 2018 to 2022 under the chairmanship of Chief Justice Raymond Zondo, documented this distinction with devastating clarity. The Commission's reports, spanning thousands of pages across multiple volumes, showed how state-owned enterprises, government departments, and regulatory bodies were systematically reoriented to serve a network of private interests. The capture of Eskom, Transnet, Denel, and the South African Revenue Service was not achieved primarily through briefcases of cash. It was achieved through the capture of appointment processes (placing compliant executives in key positions), procurement systems (directing contracts through intermediaries to connected suppliers), regulatory oversight (weakening the capacity of oversight bodies to detect and respond to irregularities), and information flows (controlling what boards, ministers, and Parliament could see about institutional performance). Corruption was the method at points, but capture was the architecture.

What makes capture so dangerous is that it is self-reinforcing. Once an institution's appointment process has been compromised, the appointees protect the compromised procurement process. The compromised procurement process generates resources that fund the political network that controls the appointment process. The weakened oversight system cannot detect what is happening, and the controlled information system ensures that external oversight bodies receive sanitised reports. Each captured function reinforces the others. This is why capture is so difficult to reverse — removing one corrupt individual does not change the structure, and the structure will simply produce a replacement.

The RAARICLE™ framework, introduced in [The Eight Tests](/articles/ra-1-1-eight-tests), provides a diagnostic lens for institutional design. Applied to capture, the framework reveals that capture is not a failure of one governance element — it is the systematic compromise of multiple elements simultaneously. A fully captured institution has compromised accountability (oversight cannot see the real picture), compromised authority (decision-making power serves private ends), compromised information (data is filtered before reaching oversight), and compromised legitimacy (the institution no longer serves its public mandate, eroding public trust). Understanding capture through the RAARICLE™ lens allows you to identify specific structural vulnerabilities and design specific structural defences.

This article examines five vectors through which capture operates — political, commercial, labour, regulatory, and information — each of which corresponds to a distinct pathway through which private interests can redirect institutional decisions. For each vector, you will see how capture works in practice with South African evidence, the structural vulnerabilities that make it possible, and the structural defences that would make it architecturally difficult. The goal is not to name villains. The goal is to understand capture as an engineering problem and to design institutions that are resistant to it regardless of who occupies positions of power.

:::takeaway
Corruption is a crime committed by individuals within a functioning institution. Capture is a structural condition in which the institution itself has been reorganised to serve private interests. You can prosecute corruption with law enforcement. You can only prevent capture with institutional design. South Africa needs both — but its reform agenda has focused overwhelmingly on corruption while neglecting the structural conditions that make capture possible.
:::

## Vector 1: Political Capture

Political capture is the oldest and most pervasive form of institutional capture, and in South Africa it operates through three primary mechanisms: patronage appointments, cadre deployment, and political tariff manipulation. Each of these mechanisms exploits a structural vulnerability in the design of South Africa's governance system — the intersection of political authority with administrative and technical functions.

### Patronage and Cadre Deployment

The concept of cadre deployment has a specific history in South Africa. The African National Congress, drawing on Leninist organisational theory, established a formal cadre deployment committee tasked with identifying suitable ANC members and allies for appointment to positions across all spheres of government, state-owned enterprises, and key institutions. The Zondo Commission examined the ANC's deployment committee in considerable detail, obtaining minutes, correspondence, and testimony that documented how the committee discussed and influenced appointments to positions ranging from heads of department in national government to chief executive officers of state-owned enterprises to directors-general and municipal managers.

The deployment committee's activities, as documented by the Zondo Commission, reveal a fundamental structural problem. South Africa's Constitution and legislation establish merit-based appointment processes — the Public Service Act requires that appointments be based on ability, qualifications, and suitability. The Municipal Systems Act prescribes minimum competency levels for senior municipal managers. But these legal requirements operate within a political context where the governing party views the placement of loyal cadres in key positions as both a right of electoral victory and a necessary condition for implementing its policy programme. The tension between merit-based appointment law and party-directed deployment practice creates a structural vulnerability that any governing party — not just the ANC — could exploit.

The consequences are visible across the system. The Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs has documented that a significant proportion of municipal managers do not meet the minimum competency requirements prescribed by regulation, despite those requirements being law since 2007. The Auditor-General has repeatedly noted that instability in key positions — chief financial officers, heads of supply chain management, internal audit directors — correlates directly with poor audit outcomes. When these positions turn over every time political leadership changes, the institutional memory, technical expertise, and continuity necessary for effective governance are lost. The institution is then dependent on the capability of whoever the political principal has chosen — and if the criterion for selection was political loyalty rather than technical competence, the institution's capability is compromised at the highest level.

The Zondo Commission found that cadre deployment was a direct enabler of state capture. At Eskom, the deployment of compliant board members and executives created the conditions for the redirection of procurement worth billions of rands. At Transnet, the appointment of a particular chief financial officer — nominated through the deployment process — was followed by the restructuring of locomotive procurement in ways that channelled massive premiums to intermediaries connected to the Gupta network. At the South African Revenue Service, the deployment of a new commissioner was followed by the dismantling of the Large Business Centre and the investigative capacity that had been tracking illicit financial flows. In each case, the capture of the appointment process was the first domino. Everything else followed.

But political capture through appointments is not limited to national government or state-owned enterprises. At the municipal level, the appointment of municipal managers, chief financial officers, and heads of technical departments is often influenced by local political dynamics in ways that prioritise loyalty over competence. A municipal manager who owes their appointment to a particular political faction will find it difficult to resist that faction's demands regarding procurement, staffing, or service delivery priorities — even when those demands conflict with the municipality's public mandate. The structural vulnerability is the same at every level: when political authority over appointments is unchecked by independent verification of competence, the appointment process becomes the primary vector for institutional capture.

### Tariff Suppression and Electoral Manipulation

Political capture also operates through the manipulation of service delivery pricing. South African municipalities are constitutionally responsible for providing water, electricity, sanitation, and refuse removal services, and they fund these services primarily through user tariffs — the prices that households and businesses pay. The setting of tariffs is a council decision, which means it is a political decision made by elected councillors.

The structural vulnerability is straightforward. Raising tariffs is politically unpopular. Councillors who vote for tariff increases face the wrath of residents who are already struggling with the cost of living. The rational political calculation is to suppress tariff increases — to keep prices below the cost of providing the service, or to keep prices flat in nominal terms while input costs rise. The immediate political benefit is real: residents are less angry, and the governing party's electoral prospects in the ward or municipality improve.

The long-term consequences are catastrophic. When water tariffs do not cover the cost of water treatment, purification, and distribution, the water system decays. Pipes are not maintained. Treatment plants are not refurbished. Reservoirs are not repaired. The decay is invisible at first — it happens underground, inside treatment works, in the condition of infrastructure that residents cannot see. But after years of deferred maintenance funded by suppressed tariffs, the decay becomes visible and urgent: burst pipes, water outages, contaminated supply. The Department of Water and Sanitation's Blue Drop and Green Drop assessments have documented the progressive deterioration of water and wastewater quality across South African municipalities, with the number of systems achieving Blue Drop certification declining dramatically from 2012 to 2022. This deterioration is not primarily caused by corruption — it is caused by the structural incentive for political actors to suppress the tariffs that fund the infrastructure that delivers the service.

Electricity tariff suppression follows the same pattern, though the dynamics are complicated by the role of Eskom as the bulk supplier. Municipalities that distribute Eskom electricity earn a surplus on the difference between the Eskom bulk tariff and the municipal retail tariff — a surplus that cross-subsidises other municipal services. When municipalities suppress their electricity tariffs for political reasons, they erode not only the electricity distribution system but also the cross-subsidy that supports other services. When they fail to collect from non-paying consumers for political reasons — because disconnection is politically unpopular — the fiscal spiral accelerates.

:::expert
National Treasury's analysis of municipal financial health, published in the annual State of Local Government Finances reports, has consistently found that tariff suppression is one of the strongest predictors of municipal financial distress. Treasury's data shows that municipalities that set tariffs below cost-recovery levels are significantly more likely to experience cash flow crises, to accumulate debt to Eskom and water boards, and to produce qualified or adverse audit opinions. Treasury has recommended that tariff-setting be informed by mandatory cost-of-service studies — independent analyses of what it actually costs to deliver each service — with the results published so that councillors and residents can see the gap between what the service costs and what the tariff covers. Without this transparency, the political incentive to suppress tariffs operates in an information vacuum, and the long-term infrastructure consequences are hidden until they become crises.
:::

### Structural Defences Against Political Capture

Defending against political capture requires structural changes that separate political authority from technical appointment and pricing decisions — not to remove democratic accountability, but to ensure that technical functions are performed by technically competent people under conditions that protect their professional independence.

The first defence is professional appointment criteria with independent verification. This means that senior technical positions — municipal managers, chief financial officers, heads of infrastructure, heads of supply chain management — should be subject to prescribed minimum qualifications and experience requirements that are verified by an independent body before appointment. South Africa already has elements of this system. The Municipal Systems Act prescribes minimum competency levels for senior municipal managers. The problem is enforcement — the regulations exist, but appointments that violate them proceed anyway because no independent body has the authority and capacity to block non-compliant appointments before they take effect. A structural defence would require that no appointment to a prescribed position can be finalised until an independent professional body has certified that the candidate meets the prescribed requirements. This is not a novel concept — it is how the legal profession, the medical profession, and the engineering profession already operate.

The second defence is protected tenure for technical staff. Once appointed through a verified merit-based process, senior technical officials should serve fixed terms that do not coincide with political terms of office. Their removal should require documented performance grounds reviewed by an independent body — not merely a change in political leadership. This insulates technical functions from the political cycle without making officials unaccountable. They can still be removed for poor performance, but they cannot be removed simply because a new mayor or premier wants to install a loyalist.

The third defence is the separation of tariff-setting from the political cycle. Tariffs for water, electricity, sanitation, and refuse removal should be set through a transparent, evidence-based process that begins with an independent cost-of-service study and produces a recommended tariff level based on full cost recovery plus a contribution to infrastructure maintenance and replacement. The council retains the authority to set the final tariff, but if the council sets a tariff below the independently determined cost-recovery level, it must publicly disclose the gap and identify an alternative funding source. This does not remove democratic choice — it structures that choice so that the consequences of suppression are transparent.

The fourth defence is independent boards for municipal service entities. Where municipalities deliver services through dedicated entities — water utilities, electricity distributors, waste management companies — those entities should be governed by independent boards with prescribed professional qualifications, appointed through transparent processes with staggered terms. The board serves as a buffer between political authority and operational management, ensuring that service delivery decisions are made on technical and financial grounds even when political pressure pushes in a different direction.

The fifth defence addresses all five anti-capture vectors simultaneously: transparency. Every appointment, every tariff decision, every performance review should be published in accessible, machine-readable formats. When citizens and oversight bodies can see who was appointed, whether they meet the qualifications, what tariff level was recommended versus what was approved, and what the infrastructure condition is, the information asymmetry that enables political capture is reduced.

## Vector 2: Commercial Capture

Commercial capture is the redirection of public procurement — the purchasing power of government — to serve private interests rather than public purposes. In South Africa, government procurement at all levels amounts to hundreds of billions of rands annually. The scale of this spending, combined with structural vulnerabilities in procurement systems, makes commercial capture one of the most financially damaging forms of institutional capture.

### How Commercial Capture Works

Commercial capture operates through several interconnected mechanisms. The most visible is procurement fraud — the direct manipulation of tender processes to ensure that a particular supplier wins a contract. This can involve bid rigging (colluding with other bidders to submit artificially high prices), specification manipulation (writing tender specifications that only one supplier can meet), evaluation manipulation (scoring bids in ways that favour a predetermined winner), or outright bribery of evaluation committee members.

But the more sophisticated forms of commercial capture do not require anyone to break the rules. They work by shaping the rules themselves. A captured procurement system can channel resources to preferred suppliers through legitimate-looking mechanisms: emergency procurement provisions that bypass competitive bidding, extensions and variations to existing contracts that expand scope and value without new competition, prequalification requirements that exclude competitors, and payment terms that favour suppliers with existing relationships. Each of these mechanisms exists for legitimate purposes — emergencies do occur, contracts do need variation, prequalification can ensure quality, and payment terms do affect delivery. But each also creates discretionary space that can be exploited.

The Competition Commission's landmark case against construction firms provides one of the clearest South African examples of commercial capture through collusion. In 2013, the Commission reached settlement agreements with fifteen major construction companies that had been colluding on tenders for government infrastructure projects over a period of more than a decade. The companies — including Murray & Roberts, WBHO, Aveng/Grinaker-LTA, Basil Read, and Stefanutti Stocks — had operated a system of bid rotation and cover pricing on projects worth billions of rands, including stadiums built for the 2010 FIFA World Cup. The companies would agree among themselves which firm would win a particular tender, and the others would submit artificially inflated "cover bids" to create the appearance of competition. The system was sophisticated, long-running, and operated across hundreds of projects. The companies paid administrative penalties totalling approximately R1.46 billion — a fraction of the overcharging that the collusion had enabled.

What makes the construction collusion case instructive for understanding commercial capture is that the system operated within the formal procurement framework. Tenders were advertised. Bids were received. Evaluation committees scored the bids. Awards were made to the lowest qualifying bidder. Every step complied with the legal requirements. But the competition that the procurement system was designed to produce was illusory — the market itself had been captured by the colluding firms. The structural defence against this form of capture requires not just compliance with procurement procedures, but independent verification that the market is actually functioning competitively.

The Auditor-General's annual reports provide the most comprehensive quantitative picture of procurement irregularity in South Africa. For the 2022/23 financial year, the AG reported irregular expenditure of R232 billion across all spheres of government — a figure that represents expenditure incurred without complying with applicable procurement laws and regulations. Not all irregular expenditure involves commercial capture; some reflects administrative errors or capacity constraints. But the scale of the figure indicates the extent to which the procurement system is failing to function as designed. The AG has repeatedly identified the same root causes: inadequate supply chain management capacity, failure to follow competitive bidding processes, insufficient oversight of procurement decisions, and non-compliance with contract management requirements.

### The Beneficial Ownership Problem

One of the most significant structural vulnerabilities in South Africa's procurement system is the difficulty of identifying who ultimately owns and controls the companies that win government contracts. Beneficial ownership — the identity of the real people who own, control, and profit from a company — is often obscured through layers of corporate structures, nominee directors, shelf companies, and trusts. This opacity enables several forms of commercial capture. Companies owned by politically connected individuals can bid for contracts without the connection being apparent. A single beneficial owner can control multiple companies that appear to compete against each other in tender processes. Companies debarred from government procurement can reconstitute under new names with the same beneficial owners.

South Africa has made progress on beneficial ownership transparency. The Companies and Intellectual Property Commission maintains a register of company directors and members, and the General Laws (Anti-Money Laundering and Combating Terrorism Financing) Amendment Act of 2022 introduced requirements for companies to maintain records of their beneficial owners. But implementation has been slow, and the information is not yet integrated into procurement systems in a way that would allow automated checking of beneficial ownership at the point of tender evaluation.

:::takeaway
Commercial capture is not always illegal. The most damaging forms operate within the rules — through emergency procurement that bypasses competition, contract extensions that avoid new tenders, specifications written for a single supplier, and collusion that creates the appearance of a competitive market. Structural defences must focus not just on compliance with procurement rules, but on whether the procurement system is actually producing competitive outcomes. If the same suppliers win repeatedly, if prices consistently exceed benchmarks, or if emergency and deviation procurement is routine, the system may be captured even if every procedure has been followed.
:::

### Structural Defences Against Commercial Capture

The first structural defence is e-procurement with automated price benchmarking. South Africa's eTender portal publishes tender advertisements and awards, but it does not function as a transactional procurement platform that can automatically compare prices, flag deviations, and track patterns. A properly designed e-procurement system would maintain a database of historical prices for common goods and services, automatically flag bids that deviate significantly from historical benchmarks or from other bids in the same tender, and generate alerts when patterns suggest collusion (such as the same set of companies repeatedly bidding together with similar price structures). Countries like South Korea (KONEPS), Chile (ChileCompra), and Ukraine (ProZorro) have implemented transactional e-procurement platforms that have demonstrably reduced procurement fraud and improved competition.

The second defence is open contracting data standards. The Open Contracting Partnership has developed an international data standard (the Open Contracting Data Standard, or OCDS) that specifies how procurement data should be published so that it can be analysed by citizens, journalists, researchers, and oversight bodies. The standard covers the entire procurement cycle — from planning and tender through award and contract to implementation and payment. Publishing procurement data in this standard format allows automated analysis of patterns that would be invisible in individual tender documents — patterns like unusual pricing, recurring supplier relationships, and systematic use of non-competitive procurement methods.

The third defence is mandatory beneficial ownership disclosure at the point of tender submission. Every company bidding for government work should be required to disclose its full beneficial ownership chain — every individual who owns, directly or indirectly, more than a prescribed percentage of the company. This disclosure should be verified against the CIPC beneficial ownership register and cross-referenced against the database of restricted suppliers. The technology for this verification exists. What is lacking is the legal requirement and the integrated data infrastructure.

The fourth defence is automatic flagging of procurement anomalies. Using the data generated by e-procurement and open contracting systems, automated systems can flag procurement decisions that warrant closer scrutiny — contracts awarded to companies that were recently established, contracts where only one bid was received despite a competitive market, contracts awarded through emergency provisions where the emergency is not apparent, and contracts where prices exceed benchmark levels by more than a prescribed threshold. Flagging does not mean blocking — it means ensuring that anomalous procurement decisions receive additional oversight before being finalised.

The fifth defence is contract management and payment verification. Commercial capture does not end when the contract is awarded — it continues through the delivery and payment phases. Inflated invoices, payment for work not done, and variation orders that increase contract value are all mechanisms through which commercial capture extracts value after the initial award. Structural defences in the delivery phase include independent quantity surveying for construction contracts, automated three-way matching of purchase orders, delivery confirmations, and invoices, and mandatory publication of all contract variations and their justifications.

## Vector 3: Labour Capture

Labour capture is perhaps the most politically sensitive of the five vectors, because it involves the relationship between government as employer and organised labour as the representative of public sector workers. Discussing labour capture requires precision: the issue is not whether public sector workers deserve fair wages, reasonable working conditions, and protection from arbitrary management. They do. The issue is whether organised labour's institutional power has, in specific sectors and contexts, been redirected from protecting workers' legitimate interests to protecting institutional arrangements that serve the interests of union leadership and connected members at the expense of service delivery to the public.

### How Labour Capture Works

Labour capture operates through several mechanisms. The most significant is the resistance to performance management. In a well-functioning public service, employees who perform well are recognised and rewarded, and employees who consistently fail to perform are managed through a progressive process that may ultimately lead to dismissal. In a system subject to labour capture, the performance management system exists on paper but does not function in practice. Poor performance has no consequences. Disciplinary processes are so lengthy and procedurally complex that managers abandon them rather than endure years of hearings, appeals, and labour court challenges. The result is a system in which non-performing employees cannot be removed, and the burden of their non-performance falls on their colleagues (who must absorb extra work) and on the public (who receive degraded services).

The education sector provides the most extensively documented example of labour capture in South Africa. The South African Democratic Teachers' Union (SADTU), as the largest teachers' union, exercises significant influence over teacher appointments, promotions, and transfers in several provinces. The Ministerial Task Team appointed by the Minister of Basic Education in 2014 to investigate allegations of the "selling" of teaching posts found evidence that SADTU officials in certain provinces were involved in manipulating teacher appointment processes — recommending candidates for promotion based on union membership and loyalty rather than teaching competence, and in some cases allegedly demanding payments from teachers seeking promotion. The Task Team's report documented a system in which union officials sat on interview panels for teaching appointments and used their position to influence outcomes.

The consequences for the education system are severe. When teacher appointments are influenced by union affiliation rather than teaching competence, classrooms are staffed with teachers who may lack the subject knowledge and pedagogical skill necessary to deliver quality education. When principals cannot discipline teachers who arrive late, leave early, or fail to prepare lessons — because the union will contest any disciplinary action through processes that can take years — school management breaks down. When the Department of Education cannot transfer teachers from overstaffed schools to understaffed schools because union agreements restrict involuntary transfers, the maldistribution of teachers persists even when data clearly shows where the shortages are.

The healthcare sector presents a different but related form of labour capture. South Africa's community health worker programme — essential for primary healthcare delivery, HIV treatment adherence, tuberculosis detection, and chronic disease management — has been fragmented across multiple employment models. Some community health workers are employed by the Department of Health, some by NGOs contracted to the department, and some through the Expanded Public Works Programme. Organised labour has advocated for the absorption of all community health workers into the public service — a legitimate demand for employment security. But the fiscal and organisational implications of absorption are immense, and in some provinces the focus on the employment question has consumed institutional energy that might otherwise have been directed at the service delivery question: how to ensure that community health workers are actually reaching communities, conducting screenings, supporting treatment adherence, and referring patients who need facility-based care.

More broadly, labour capture manifests in the bloating of non-technical headcount while technical posts remain vacant. The Auditor-General and National Treasury have both documented the phenomenon of municipalities and departments that have large numbers of general administration and support staff — personal assistants, cleaners, drivers, security guards — while technical positions in engineering, finance, planning, and information technology remain unfilled for years. This staffing pattern does not serve the institution's public mandate. It serves the political function of providing employment to connected individuals in positions that do not require scarce technical skills. The wages paid to these employees consume the personnel budget, leaving insufficient funding to offer competitive salaries for the technical specialists the institution desperately needs.

:::expert
The Public Service Commission, in its annual reports on the state of the public service, has documented the persistent gap between funded technical posts and filled technical posts across all spheres of government. The Commission's analysis shows that the vacancy rate for specialised technical positions — engineers, town planners, environmental health practitioners, financial specialists — is consistently higher than the vacancy rate for general administrative positions, even though the technical positions are funded and advertised. The Commission has attributed this gap partly to uncompetitive public sector salaries for technical specialists (who can earn significantly more in the private sector) and partly to organisational cultures that do not value or support technical expertise. The Commission has recommended that government departments be required to maintain minimum ratios of technical to administrative staff, benchmarked against the services they are mandated to deliver, with deviations from these ratios requiring justification and approval from the relevant oversight body.
:::

### Structural Defences Against Labour Capture

Addressing labour capture is not about weakening workers' rights or dismantling unions. It is about designing employment systems that align the interests of public sector workers with the service delivery mandate of the institution. This alignment is possible, but it requires structural changes that the current system does not provide.

The first defence is outcome-based performance contracts for senior officials. Section 57 of the Municipal Systems Act already requires performance agreements for municipal managers and managers reporting directly to them. The problem is that these agreements are often pro forma documents that are not linked to measurable service delivery outcomes, not monitored regularly, and not enforced when targets are missed. A structural defence would require that performance agreements specify measurable outcomes linked to the institution's service delivery mandate (kilometres of road maintained, megalitres of water treated to standard, percentage of learners achieving grade-appropriate literacy), with quarterly reporting against these outcomes and consequences — including non-renewal of contract — for persistent failure to achieve them.

The second defence is right-sizing linked to service delivery benchmarks. Every institution should be required to maintain a staffing structure that is demonstrably linked to its service delivery mandate. This means establishing benchmarks for the minimum technical staffing required to deliver each service at an acceptable standard — how many engineers per kilometre of road network, how many water quality technicians per treatment plant, how many qualified teachers per learner. When an institution's actual staffing deviates significantly from these benchmarks — too few technical staff, too many non-technical staff — a restructuring process should be triggered. This process must respect labour law, including consultation requirements and alternatives to retrenchment. But it must be triggered automatically by the data, not left to the discretion of management that may itself be captured.

The third defence is the separation of national salary bargaining from operational conditions. Currently, public sector salaries are negotiated nationally through bargaining councils — the Public Service Coordinating Bargaining Council for national and provincial government, and the South African Local Government Bargaining Council for municipalities. These negotiations determine wage levels across the entire system. But operational conditions — staffing levels, performance management processes, working hours, redeployment mechanisms — are also negotiated in ways that constrain the ability of individual institutions to manage their workforce in response to local conditions. A structural reform would maintain national salary bargaining (ensuring equal pay for equal work across the system) while giving individual institutions greater operational flexibility to determine how many staff they need, in what roles, and with what performance requirements — subject to the service delivery benchmarks described above.

The fourth defence is transparent staffing data. Every institution should be required to publish its organogram, showing every funded position, whether it is filled or vacant, the qualifications of the incumbent, and the ratio of technical to administrative staff. When this data is public, citizens and oversight bodies can see whether the institution's staffing structure serves its public mandate or whether it has been captured to serve other interests.

The fifth defence is streamlined disciplinary processes. The current disciplinary framework for public sector employees involves multiple stages of hearings, appeals, and reviews that can take years to resolve. This procedural complexity is a structural enabler of labour capture — it makes the cost of managing poor performance so high that managers choose not to do it. Streamlining does not mean removing due process protections. It means establishing time limits for each stage of the disciplinary process, providing managers with professional support (rather than requiring them to conduct hearings themselves), and creating a specialised adjudication mechanism for public sector labour disputes that can process cases within defined timeframes.

## Vector 4: Regulatory Capture

Regulatory capture occurs when a body established to regulate an industry or function in the public interest instead operates in the interest of the entities it is supposed to regulate. This is not necessarily the result of corruption — it often results from structural dynamics that gradually align the regulator's perspective with the regulated industry's perspective, to the point where the regulator can no longer distinguish between the industry's interests and the public interest.

### How Regulatory Capture Works

Regulatory capture operates through several mechanisms, some subtle and some overt. The most commonly discussed is the revolving door — the movement of personnel between the regulator and the regulated industry. When a regulator's senior officials know that their next career move will likely be to a position in the industry they currently regulate, their enforcement decisions are shaped by the implicit understanding that aggressive regulation may close off future employment opportunities. Conversely, when industry executives move into regulatory positions, they bring with them the industry's worldview, priorities, and relationships. Over time, the regulator's culture comes to mirror the industry's culture rather than maintaining the independent perspective necessary for effective oversight.

A second mechanism is information asymmetry. Regulated industries typically have far more resources, data, and technical expertise than the regulators that oversee them. When a regulator must rely on the regulated industry for the information it needs to make decisions — because it lacks the capacity to generate that information independently — the industry effectively controls the basis on which regulatory decisions are made. The regulated entity presents its own data, framed from its own perspective, and the regulator lacks the capacity to challenge or independently verify that framing.

A third mechanism is political pressure. Regulators that depend on government for their budgets, their appointments, and their legal mandates are vulnerable to political pressure that may align with the interests of powerful regulated entities. When a regulated entity is politically connected — as state-owned enterprises inherently are — the regulator faces pressure from two directions: from the entity resisting regulation, and from the political principals who are aligned with the entity's leadership.

### South African Evidence

The National Energy Regulator of South Africa (NERSA) provides a compelling case study of regulatory capture. NERSA is responsible for regulating the electricity, piped-gas, and petroleum pipeline industries. Its most visible function is the determination of Eskom's electricity tariffs — the prices that Eskom is permitted to charge for the electricity it generates and transmits. NERSA's tariff determinations directly affect Eskom's financial viability, the cost of electricity to consumers, and the competitiveness of the South African economy.

The structural dynamics of the NERSA-Eskom relationship create conditions conducive to regulatory capture. Eskom is a state-owned enterprise reporting to the Minister of Public Enterprises (now the Minister of Electricity and Energy). NERSA's board members are appointed by the Minister of Energy. Both ministers serve in the same Cabinet. The political context in which NERSA operates is one in which Eskom's financial sustainability is a matter of national economic concern — and in which tariff increases are politically unpopular. NERSA has been subject to criticism from multiple directions: from Eskom and Treasury for not granting sufficient tariff increases to fund Eskom's operations and debt, and from consumers and industry for granting tariff increases that exceed inflation and erode affordability. In this cross-pressure, NERSA's decisions have sometimes appeared to prioritise political acceptability over regulatory rigour — approving tariff methodologies that deferred cost recovery, permitted inadequate maintenance expenditure, and accommodated Eskom's operational inefficiencies rather than compelling the utility to address them.

The Department of Water and Sanitation's failure to enforce the Blue Drop and Green Drop standards provides another example, though it operates through a different mechanism. The DWS established the Blue Drop certification programme (for drinking water quality) and the Green Drop certification programme (for wastewater treatment quality) as regulatory tools to monitor and incentivise municipal water and sanitation performance. The programmes were initially effective — municipal compliance with water quality standards improved during the period when Blue Drop assessments were conducted regularly and results were published. But from 2014 onwards, the DWS stopped publishing Blue Drop and Green Drop reports. Assessments were delayed, results were withheld, and the regulatory pressure that had been driving improvement evaporated.

The DWS's failure to enforce its own regulatory standards was not primarily caused by the revolving door or information asymmetry. It was caused by a combination of institutional collapse within the DWS itself (the department experienced severe governance failures during this period, including qualified audit opinions and leadership instability), political reluctance to publish data that would embarrass provincial and municipal governments controlled by the governing party, and resource constraints that reduced the department's capacity to conduct assessments. The result was the same as classic regulatory capture: the regulator ceased to regulate, and the entities it was supposed to oversee continued to degrade water and wastewater quality without consequence. When the DWS finally resumed publishing Blue and Green Drop reports in 2022, the results confirmed what communities had been experiencing — a dramatic deterioration in water and wastewater quality across much of the country.

:::takeaway
Regulatory capture does not always involve corruption or even conscious intent. It can result from structural conditions — information asymmetry, political pressure, resource constraints, revolving doors — that gradually align the regulator's behaviour with the regulated entity's interests. The most effective defence is not to rely on the regulator's integrity (though integrity matters) but to design structural safeguards that make capture architecturally difficult regardless of who occupies the regulatory positions.
:::

### Structural Defences Against Regulatory Capture

The first defence is automatic enforcement triggers. Instead of relying on the regulator's discretion to initiate enforcement action, the regulatory framework should specify automatic triggers — if a regulated entity's performance falls below a defined threshold on a defined metric for a defined period, enforcement action is initiated automatically. The regulator retains discretion over the specific enforcement response, but the initiation of enforcement is not discretionary. This removes the structural vulnerability that allows captured or resource-constrained regulators to simply not enforce. The [provincial intervention triggers](/articles/ra-3-3-when-provinces-fail) discussed elsewhere in this series apply the same principle to provincial government performance.

The second defence is cooling-off periods. Senior officials of a regulated entity should be prohibited from taking positions at the regulator (and vice versa) for a prescribed period — typically two to five years. This does not prevent knowledge transfer between the regulator and the industry (which can be valuable), but it reduces the incentive effects of the revolving door. The cooling-off period should be legally binding, with disclosure requirements and penalties for non-compliance.

The third defence is separate regulator funding. Regulators that depend on annual budget allocations from the very government departments that oversee the regulated entities are structurally compromised. An alternative model funds the regulator through levies on the regulated industry — as South Africa's financial regulators (the Prudential Authority and the Financial Sector Conduct Authority) are funded through levies on financial institutions. This does not eliminate the risk of capture (the regulated industry may attempt to influence the regulator through the levy structure), but it removes the political budget dependency that allows government to weaken regulators by cutting their funding.

The fourth defence is mandatory publication of all regulatory decisions, the data on which they were based, and the reasoning that supported them. When regulatory decisions are made transparently, with full disclosure of the information considered and the analysis performed, it is more difficult for captured decisions to pass without scrutiny. Civil society, media, and academic institutions can review the regulator's reasoning and challenge decisions that appear inconsistent with the public interest.

The fifth defence is independent technical capacity. Regulators must have the budget and mandate to generate their own data about the performance of regulated entities, rather than relying on the entities' self-reported data. This means employing technical specialists who can independently audit performance, inspect facilities, and analyse operational data. Where the regulator lacks internal capacity, it should have the budget to commission independent technical assessments from parties with no commercial relationship with the regulated entity.

## Vector 5: Information Capture

Information capture is the least visible but perhaps the most enabling of the five vectors. It operates by controlling what decision-makers, oversight bodies, and the public know about institutional performance. When information is captured, all other forms of capture become easier — because the people who could detect and respond to political, commercial, labour, or regulatory capture do not have the information they need to do so.

### How Information Capture Works

Information capture operates through several mechanisms. The most common is management filtering — the practice of senior management controlling what information reaches oversight bodies (municipal councils, provincial legislatures, parliamentary committees, boards of directors) by presenting curated reports that emphasise positive aspects and minimise or omit negative ones. This is not necessarily dishonest in the sense of fabricating data. It is selective — choosing which data to present, how to present it, and what context to provide. A quarterly report to council might highlight the number of new water connections installed while omitting the number of existing connections that are non-functional. A report to a parliamentary committee might present expenditure figures that show budget utilisation on track while omitting data on the quality of what was purchased.

A second mechanism is delayed reporting. When management controls the timing of information flow to oversight bodies, it can delay the release of unfavourable data until a moment when the oversight body is focused on other matters, or until the data can be presented alongside positive developments that offset it, or until the period to which the data relates is so far in the past that corrective action seems less urgent. Delayed reporting is particularly effective in environments where oversight bodies meet infrequently and have limited capacity to demand real-time data.

A third mechanism is narrative management — the practice of framing data in ways that serve management's interests rather than providing objective analysis. A decline in service delivery performance can be attributed to external factors (budget cuts, load shedding, drought) rather than internal management failures. A deterioration in audit outcomes can be presented as a technicality rather than a substantive governance concern. The narrative shapes how oversight bodies interpret the data, and when oversight bodies lack the technical capacity to challenge management's framing, the narrative becomes the reality on which decisions are based.

### South African Evidence

The gap between management reporting and Auditor-General findings provides the most systematic evidence of information capture in South Africa. The AG's annual audit process examines the same institutions whose management produces quarterly and annual reports to oversight bodies. In municipality after municipality, the AG finds material misstatements, irregular expenditure, and non-compliance with laws and regulations that were not disclosed in management's own reports to council. This gap is not merely an accounting discrepancy. It represents a systematic failure of internal reporting to provide oversight bodies with an accurate picture of institutional performance.

The AG's reports for local government have documented cases where municipal management reported to council that the municipality was in a sound financial position while the AG found that the municipality was technically insolvent — its liabilities exceeded its assets, its cash reserves were depleted, and it was unable to pay creditors within the required timeframes. Management reports that council members relied upon for their oversight function painted a picture that the AG's independent examination contradicted. This is information capture in its purest form — the people responsible for oversight were provided with filtered information that prevented them from exercising effective oversight.

The health sector provides equally striking examples. The Department of Health's Ideal Clinic programme establishes a set of standards that primary healthcare facilities are expected to meet — standards covering infrastructure, staffing, medicine availability, and service delivery. Provincial health departments report on their facilities' compliance with Ideal Clinic standards through the department's monitoring system. But independent monitoring initiatives — including the Stop Stock Outs Project, which tracks medicine availability at the facility level, and civil society organisations that conduct facility-level audits — have consistently found discrepancies between official compliance reports and the reality experienced by patients. Facilities reported as meeting medicine availability standards have been found to be experiencing stock-outs of essential medicines. Facilities reported as meeting staffing standards have been found to be operating with insufficient clinical staff to manage patient loads safely.

The Stop Stock Outs Project, managed by a coalition of civil society organisations, established an independent reporting mechanism — a hotline through which patients, healthcare workers, and community health workers could report medicine shortages at specific facilities. The data collected through this mechanism consistently showed higher rates of stock-outs than were reflected in official departmental reports. The discrepancy was not necessarily the result of deliberate falsification — it could reflect the timing of data collection (a facility that receives a delivery on the day it submits its report may report as "in stock" despite being out of stock for the preceding three weeks), the definition of "stock-out" (a facility may count a medicine as available if it has one box on the shelf, even if demand requires ten boxes), or the institutional pressure to report compliance. But regardless of the cause, the effect is the same: oversight bodies and the public received information that underestimated the severity of medicine shortages, which delayed the corrective action that would have been triggered by accurate data.

### Structural Defences Against Information Capture

The first and most important defence is automated data pipelines that bypass management. Instead of relying on management to compile and present data to oversight bodies, institutional information systems should be configured to transmit operational data automatically and directly to oversight platforms. A municipality's financial management system should automatically publish cash flow data, creditor payment data, and revenue collection data to a dashboard accessible to council members, the provincial treasury, and the National Treasury — without the data passing through a management review and filtering process. A healthcare facility's stock management system should automatically transmit medicine inventory data to a dashboard accessible to the district health office, the provincial department, and the national department — without facility management being able to edit the data before it is transmitted.

This is not a futuristic concept. The technology exists, and elements of it are already operational in South Africa. The Municipal Standard Chart of Accounts (mSCOA) initiative has standardised municipal financial reporting and requires municipalities to submit financial data through automated systems. The District Health Information System (DHIS) collects health facility data through standardised reporting tools. The challenge is not technology — it is the institutional willingness to implement systems that remove management's ability to filter the information that reaches oversight bodies. Management resists automated reporting for understandable reasons: it removes their ability to contextualise data, explain anomalies, and present a coherent narrative. But those are precisely the capabilities that enable information capture.

The second defence is mandatory real-time publication of key performance indicators. Every institution should be required to publish a defined set of operational indicators — updated at defined intervals (monthly, quarterly, or in real time for critical indicators) — on a public dashboard accessible to anyone. These indicators should cover financial health (cash position, creditor payment age, budget utilisation), service delivery (water quality, electricity availability, road condition, facility uptime), and governance (audit outcomes, vacancy rates, disciplinary case resolution times). When this data is publicly available, the information asymmetry between management and oversight bodies is eliminated, and the public becomes an additional oversight layer.

The third defence is direct data access for oversight bodies. Municipal council members, provincial legislature members, and parliamentary committee members should have direct access to institutional information systems — not filtered reports presented by management, but the underlying data from which those reports are drawn. This requires both technical infrastructure (secure access to information systems) and capacity support (training and analytical tools that enable oversight body members to interpret the data). The Auditor-General's real-time audit methodology, introduced in recent years, moves in this direction by providing audit findings to management and oversight bodies during the financial year rather than only after year-end. This approach should be extended to all oversight relationships.

The fourth defence is independent verification. In addition to management's own reporting and the automated data pipelines, regular independent verification of key data should be conducted by bodies with no reporting relationship to the institution's management. The Auditor-General performs this function annually for financial and compliance data. But service delivery data — the data that most directly affects whether citizens receive the services they are constitutionally entitled to — is not subject to the same independent verification. Extending independent verification to service delivery data, whether through the AG's expanded mandate, through specialised sector regulators, or through accredited civil society monitoring organisations, would close the gap that allows management to report compliance while citizens experience failure.

The fifth defence is whistleblower protection. Individuals within an institution who have access to information that contradicts management's official narrative are essential sources for detecting information capture. But they face enormous personal and professional risks if they disclose that information. South Africa's Protected Disclosures Act provides some legal protection for whistleblowers, but the practical reality is that whistleblowers in South Africa frequently face retaliation — dismissal, harassment, exclusion, and in some cases physical threats. Structural defence requires not just legal protection but institutional mechanisms that make it safe to report — anonymous reporting channels, independent investigation of disclosures, protection of the whistleblower's identity, and meaningful consequences for anyone who retaliates against a whistleblower.

:::expert
The Auditor-General of South Africa has increasingly emphasised the link between information quality and governance outcomes. In the 2022/23 audit reports, the AG introduced the concept of "preventive controls" — institutional mechanisms that prevent failures before they occur, rather than detecting them after the fact. The AG's analysis found that institutions with strong preventive controls, including automated financial reporting, real-time monitoring dashboards, and independent quality assurance of management reports, achieved significantly better audit outcomes than institutions that relied primarily on management's own reporting. The AG recommended that National Treasury prescribe minimum standards for information systems in all spheres of government, including mandatory automated reporting to oversight bodies and real-time publication of key financial indicators. The AG's vision is a governance system in which "information flows freely to those who need it for oversight, rather than being filtered through those who have an interest in controlling it."
:::

## Designing Capture-Resistant Institutions

The five vectors of capture — political, commercial, labour, regulatory, and information — do not operate in isolation. In a fully captured institution, they reinforce each other in ways that make the capture self-sustaining. Political capture of the appointment process places compliant officials in key positions. Those officials facilitate commercial capture of the procurement system. The bloated non-technical workforce created by labour capture provides a constituency that supports the political network. The weakened regulator created by regulatory capture cannot detect what is happening. And the filtered information produced by information capture ensures that oversight bodies cannot see the full picture.

Designing capture-resistant institutions requires addressing all five vectors simultaneously. An institution that has strong procurement controls but weak appointment protections will simply have its procurement controls dismantled by a captured appointee. An institution that has transparent financial reporting but no performance management for staff will publish data that nobody acts upon. An institution that has an independent regulator but no whistleblower protection will find that the regulator lacks the information it needs to intervene. The anti-capture architecture must be comprehensive — and it must be tested against all five vectors.

The RAARICLE™ framework provides the testing methodology. For any proposed institutional design or reform, ask: Does this design protect against political capture of appointments? Does it create transparent, competitive procurement that resists commercial capture? Does it align staffing structures with service delivery mandates in ways that resist labour capture? Does it establish independent oversight with automatic enforcement triggers that resist regulatory capture? And does it ensure that information flows directly to oversight bodies without management filtering, resisting information capture?

If the answer to any of these questions is no, the design has a vulnerability — and in South Africa's governance environment, vulnerabilities are exploited. The capture of South African institutions documented by the Zondo Commission was not the result of a uniquely corrupt set of individuals encountering an unlucky set of institutional weaknesses. It was the result of rational actors exploiting structural vulnerabilities that existed by design — vulnerabilities that would allow anyone with sufficient motivation and political access to achieve similar results. Preventing future capture requires eliminating those structural vulnerabilities, not merely hoping that future leaders will be more virtuous than their predecessors.

The framework below provides a systematic tool for conducting this assessment. Use it for any institution — a municipality, a provincial department, a national entity, a state-owned enterprise — and it will reveal where the structural defences are strong and where they are absent.

:::framework
**The Capture Vulnerability Assessment Framework**

Use this five-vector checklist to assess whether any government institution — a municipality, provincial department, or national entity — is structurally vulnerable to capture:

**Vector 1: Political capture — Are key appointments protected?**

Check whether senior technical and operational positions are filled through merit-based processes with prescribed minimum qualifications, or whether they are political appointments. Look for signs: high turnover of senior officials after elections, appointments without advertised processes, or technical posts filled by people with no relevant qualifications. If the institution's leadership changes every time the political principal changes, it is structurally vulnerable to political capture.

**Vector 2: Commercial capture — Is procurement transparent?**

Check whether the institution publishes its procurement data openly — tender awards, contract values, supplier details, and deviation reports. Look at the Auditor-General's findings on supply chain management. If procurement data is not published, if the same suppliers win repeatedly, or if deviations from competitive bidding are frequent, the institution is vulnerable to commercial capture. Advocate for mandatory publication on the eTender portal and open contracting standards.

**Vector 3: Labour capture — Are performance systems enforced?**

Check whether the institution has a functioning performance management system and whether poor performance has consequences. Look at the ratio of support staff to technical staff. If the institution has a bloated headcount in non-technical functions while technical posts are vacant, labour capture may be redirecting resources from service delivery to employment. This is not about attacking workers — it is about asking whether the staffing structure serves the public mandate.

**Vector 4: Regulatory capture — Does the overseer have teeth?**

Identify who regulates or oversees the institution and check whether enforcement actions have been taken in the last five years. If the regulator has never issued a compliance notice, withdrawn a licence, or imposed penalties despite poor performance, the enforcement mechanism exists on paper but not in practice. Advocate for automatic enforcement triggers tied to published performance data.

**Vector 5: Information capture — Can oversight bodies see the real data?**

Check whether the institution's management controls what information reaches oversight bodies (council, legislature, parliamentary committee). Look for gaps between management reports and Auditor-General findings — if management reports paint a positive picture while the AG finds material problems, information is being filtered. Advocate for automated data pipelines that send operational data directly to oversight bodies without passing through management.

With this five-vector assessment, you can diagnose whether an institution's problems are caused by individual misconduct or by structural vulnerabilities that would allow anyone in those positions to capture the institution. Structural problems require structural solutions.
:::

## South African Case Studies

The theory of the five capture vectors and their structural defences is grounded in South African experience. Three case studies — drawn from the Zondo Commission findings, the banking sector, and municipal governance — illustrate how capture operates through multiple vectors simultaneously and how structural defences would have prevented or limited the damage.

### Case Study 1: Eskom and the Gupta Network

The capture of Eskom, South Africa's electricity monopoly, is the most extensively documented case of institutional capture in the country's history. The Zondo Commission devoted multiple volumes of its report to Eskom, drawing on thousands of documents, witness testimony, and forensic analysis. The Eskom case is instructive because it demonstrates how all five capture vectors operated simultaneously and reinforced each other.

Political capture came first. Through the ANC's cadre deployment process and direct political intervention, individuals sympathetic to the Gupta network were placed on Eskom's board of directors and in key executive positions. The board was reconstituted in ways that removed independent directors and installed individuals who would not challenge the redirection of procurement. The appointment of specific chief executives and chief financial officers, documented by the Zondo Commission as influenced by the Gupta network through their political connections, created the management structure through which the subsequent capture was implemented. Had the structural defence of independent appointment verification been in place — requiring that board members and executives meet prescribed qualifications verified by an independent body, with appointment processes that could not be overridden by political principals — the first and most critical step in Eskom's capture would have been architecturally difficult.

Commercial capture followed rapidly. Once compliant executives were in place, Eskom's procurement was redirected. Coal supply contracts were channelled through intermediaries connected to the Gupta network. Consulting contracts were directed to firms that would share fees with connected parties. Major capital projects — including the Kusile and Medupi power station builds — experienced cost overruns that the Zondo Commission linked to corrupt procurement practices. The scale of the financial redirection was enormous — billions of rands in value were extracted from Eskom through procurement mechanisms that operated within the formal supply chain management framework but were directed by captured decision-makers. Structural defences including automated price benchmarking, mandatory beneficial ownership disclosure, and open contracting data publication would have created transparency that made these procurement decisions visible to oversight bodies and the public in real time, rather than years after the fact.

Regulatory capture compounded the problem. NERSA, as Eskom's tariff regulator, did not detect or respond to the governance failures unfolding within the utility. NERSA's tariff determinations continued to process Eskom's applications through normal procedures, even as the costs being submitted for tariff recovery included inflated procurement costs resulting from captured contracts. NERSA lacked the independent technical capacity to audit Eskom's cost claims at a level that would have detected the irregularities. The automatic enforcement triggers proposed as a structural defence — which would have triggered regulatory scrutiny when Eskom's costs deviated significantly from benchmarks or when governance indicators fell below thresholds — were absent.

Information capture ensured that the full picture remained hidden. Eskom's board — itself captured — received management reports prepared by captured executives. Parliament's portfolio committee received presentations prepared by the same management. The information that reached oversight bodies was filtered through the very people who were facilitating the capture. It was not until investigative journalists, civil society organisations, and ultimately the Zondo Commission obtained internal documents and testimony from whistleblowers that the full extent of the capture became apparent. Automated data pipelines that sent financial and procurement data directly from Eskom's systems to Treasury, the AG, and Parliament — without management being able to filter the data — would have made the anomalies visible far earlier.

The Eskom case demonstrates that the capture of a single institution costs far more than the direct financial extraction. The broader cost — load shedding, economic damage estimated at hundreds of billions of rands, infrastructure degradation, and the erosion of public confidence in state institutions — dwarfs the direct losses to corruption. Structural defences that prevented the capture would have been orders of magnitude cheaper than the consequences of allowing it.

### Case Study 2: VBS Mutual Bank

The collapse of VBS Mutual Bank in 2018 provides a case study of capture that operated primarily through commercial and information vectors, enabled by regulatory weakness and political connections. VBS was a small mutual bank that served primarily rural communities in Limpopo. Its collapse destroyed the savings of thousands of depositors, many of them among South Africa's poorest citizens.

The VBS case began with commercial capture — the bank's management, led by its chairman and key executives, orchestrated a scheme to inflate the bank's financial statements to conceal massive losses and create the appearance of a profitable, growing institution. This fictitious financial picture was then used to attract deposits from South African municipalities — which are required by regulation to deposit funds only with banks that meet prescribed prudential standards. At least fourteen municipalities, predominantly in Limpopo, deposited funds totalling approximately R1.5 billion with VBS, often in violation of municipal investment regulations that prohibited deposits with mutual banks. The municipal deposits were facilitated by kickbacks to municipal officials who authorised the deposits — connecting the commercial capture vector to political capture at the municipal level.

Information capture was central to the scheme's success. VBS's auditors — KPMG — issued unqualified audit opinions on financial statements that were later found to be fictionally misstated. The South African Reserve Bank's Prudential Authority, which was responsible for supervising VBS, relied on the bank's reported financial data and the audit opinions for its supervisory assessments. The information that reached both the auditors and the regulator was manipulated by the bank's captured management. When the Reserve Bank eventually conducted an on-site inspection and commissioned an independent forensic investigation (the "Great Bank Heist" report by Advocate Terry Motau SC), the investigation found that approximately R2 billion had been stolen or improperly paid out. The report named dozens of individuals who had benefited from improper payments, including political figures, municipal officials, and the bank's own management.

The structural defences that would have prevented or limited the VBS collapse span multiple vectors. Automated financial data reporting from the bank's systems directly to the Prudential Authority, bypassing management's ability to manipulate the reports, would have revealed the discrepancies between the bank's actual financial position and its reported position. Mandatory verification of municipal deposits against a real-time registry of approved deposit-taking institutions would have prevented the illegal deposits. Automatic enforcement triggers — requiring the Prudential Authority to investigate when a small bank's deposit growth exceeded statistical norms — would have flagged VBS's anomalous growth years before the collapse. And beneficial ownership and related-party transaction disclosure requirements, properly enforced, would have revealed the web of relationships through which funds were extracted.

### Case Study 3: Municipal Procurement Cartels

The third case study is not a single institution but a systemic pattern: the operation of procurement cartels at the municipal level. The SIU has investigated procurement fraud in dozens of municipalities, and the Competition Commission has received complaints and conducted investigations into collusion in municipal procurement across multiple sectors — construction, IT services, fleet management, and professional services.

Municipal procurement cartels operate by capturing the commercial vector through relationships with internal officials. A network of suppliers establishes relationships with supply chain management officials in a municipality. These relationships may involve direct corruption (payments to officials who steer tenders) or more subtle mechanisms (officials who establish companies through family members that bid for municipal work, officials who provide inside information on upcoming tenders to preferred suppliers, or officials who write specifications that favour particular suppliers). Over time, the cartel establishes a stable pattern of contract awards that operates within the formal procurement framework but produces non-competitive outcomes.

The information capture vector reinforces the commercial capture. The supply chain management officials who facilitate the cartel control the information that reaches the municipal manager and the council's oversight committees. Procurement reports present each award as a compliant, competitive process. Deviations from competitive bidding are justified with documented (but fabricated or exaggerated) reasons. The internal audit function, if it exists, may lack the capacity or the mandate to conduct procurement forensics. The AG's annual audit examines supply chain management compliance, but the audit scope and sampling methodology may not detect sophisticated collusion that complies with procedural requirements.

Labour capture provides additional insulation. Non-technical staff who are part of the patronage network that controls procurement have no incentive to report irregularities and every incentive to protect the system that provides their employment. Technical staff who might detect price anomalies or specification manipulation are either absent (their posts are vacant because the institution's staffing structure has been captured to prioritise non-technical employment) or marginalised (they are excluded from procurement decisions and lack the institutional support to challenge them).

The structural defences for municipal procurement cartels are the same as those outlined for commercial capture generally, but their application at the municipal level requires specific institutional infrastructure. A national e-procurement platform that all municipalities are required to use for all procurement above a defined threshold would generate the data necessary to detect patterns of collusion — the same suppliers winning in the same municipalities, prices that consistently exceed benchmarks, bidding patterns consistent with bid rotation. Mandatory publication of all procurement data in open contracting format would enable civil society organisations, journalists, and the Competition Commission to analyse procurement patterns and identify anomalies that individual municipalities cannot or will not detect internally. And automatic referral triggers — requiring the SIU or Competition Commission to investigate when procurement data shows patterns consistent with collusion — would ensure that detection leads to action.

These three case studies — Eskom, VBS, and municipal procurement cartels — represent different scales of capture but the same structural dynamics. In each case, the capture operated through multiple vectors simultaneously. In each case, the existing oversight mechanisms detected the capture only after enormous damage had been done. And in each case, structural defences that addressed all five vectors would have made the capture architecturally difficult — not by relying on the virtue of individuals, but by designing systems in which the information, transparency, and automatic triggers necessary to detect and respond to capture are built into the institutional architecture itself.
