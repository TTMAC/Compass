## The Bottom Line, Up Front

South Africa has built a sophisticated monitoring and evaluation framework on paper — but the gap between what gets measured and what gets acted on remains the system's critical weakness. The Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME) was established in 2010 with an ambitious mandate to monitor government performance across all three spheres. The Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) produces the most comprehensive public audit of government spending on the African continent. State-owned enterprises submit annual reports, corporate plans, and shareholder compacts to their oversight departments and to Parliament. The data exists. The reports are published. What does not reliably happen is the conversion of monitoring findings into policy correction, institutional reform, or individual accountability. The system measures prolifically and acts sporadically.

If you have read [The Execution Machine](/articles/eg-3-2-the-execution-machine), you understand the institutional architecture through which South Africa's economic strategies are supposed to be implemented — the coordination bodies, the sector agencies, the sequencing logic. This article examines the other side of that coin: the system that is supposed to tell you whether any of it is working. Monitoring and evaluation, commonly abbreviated to M&E, is the feedback loop. It is the mechanism by which a government learns from its own performance, identifies what is failing, and corrects course. Without effective M&E, policy-making becomes a series of announcements followed by hope. And if you have read [Reading the Auditor-General's Reports](/articles/5-1-reading-the-auditor-generals-reports) from the Government Structure pillar, you already have the constitutional context for oversight in South Africa. This article takes that framework and applies it specifically to the economic domain: how does South Africa monitor whether its economic growth strategies are actually delivering results?

The answer, as you will see, is that the country has invested heavily in the infrastructure of measurement — the departments, the frameworks, the assessment tools, the audit cycles — but has systematically underinvested in the infrastructure of consequence. Reports are written. Findings are tabled. Recommendations are made. And then, with disturbing regularity, nothing changes. This is not a story about the absence of monitoring. It is a story about the presence of monitoring and the absence of accountability. Understanding this distinction is essential for any citizen trying to evaluate whether South Africa's economic governance is improving or merely producing the appearance of improvement.

The stakes are not abstract. When monitoring identifies that an economic development programme is failing to create jobs, and no corrective action follows, the programme continues to consume public resources while delivering nothing. When the Auditor-General flags material irregularities in infrastructure spending, and the responsible officials face no consequences, the irregularities continue. When a state-owned enterprise's corporate plan reveals unsustainable financial trajectories, and the shareholder ministry does not intervene, the enterprise lurches toward the bailout that taxpayers will eventually fund. Every failure of the M&E system to trigger action is, ultimately, a failure that citizens pay for — in wasted taxes, in undelivered services, and in economic growth that never materialises.

## The Birth of Systematic M&E: DPME and the Presidency

South Africa's formal monitoring and evaluation system, as it exists today, dates to 2010. That year, President Jacob Zuma established the Department of Performance Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME) within the Presidency — initially as the Ministry of Performance Monitoring and Evaluation, later restructured and renamed the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation when it was merged with the National Planning Commission. The creation of DPME represented an institutional acknowledgement of a problem that had been growing since the early 2000s: the South African government had ambitious plans and policies, but no systematic way to track whether they were being implemented.

Before DPME, monitoring and evaluation in the South African government was fragmented and largely voluntary. Individual departments conducted their own evaluations — some rigorously, most superficially. National Treasury tracked expenditure through the budget process, but expenditure is an input measure, not an outcome measure. It tells you how much money was spent, not whether the spending achieved anything. The Public Service Commission conducted periodic assessments of public administration, but these were compliance-focused rather than performance-focused. There was no single entity with a mandate to look across all of government and ask: are the priority programmes working?

DPME was designed to fill that gap. Its founding mandate included several ambitious components. First, it was to develop and maintain a government-wide monitoring and evaluation framework — a common set of standards, tools, and practices that all departments would use to track their performance. Second, it was to monitor the delivery of government's priority outcomes, which were initially articulated as twelve outcome areas linked to the electoral mandate and later aligned to the National Development Plan. Third, it was to conduct evaluations of government programmes — not just monitoring whether activities were happening, but evaluating whether those activities were achieving their intended results. Fourth, it was to monitor the quality of frontline service delivery by physically visiting government facilities and assessing the experience of citizens.

The department developed several key instruments to fulfil this mandate. The Management Performance Assessment Tool, known as MPAT, was designed to assess the management practices of national and provincial government departments across four areas: strategic management, governance and accountability, human resource management, and financial management. MPAT worked through annual self-assessments by departments, followed by moderation and verification by DPME. Each department received scores on a four-point scale, and the results were published, creating a comparative picture of management quality across government. The logic was straightforward: departments with good management practices are more likely to deliver effectively than departments with poor management practices. By measuring management quality systematically, DPME could identify departments that needed support or intervention.

The National Evaluation Policy Framework (NEPF), adopted in 2011, established a structured approach to programme evaluation across government. Under the NEPF, government departments could propose programmes for evaluation, and a National Evaluation Plan was drawn up annually, identifying the evaluations that would be conducted. These evaluations ranged from design evaluations (assessing whether a programme's theory of change was sound) to implementation evaluations (assessing whether the programme was being delivered as intended) to impact evaluations (assessing whether the programme was achieving measurable outcomes). By the mid-2010s, DPME had commissioned and published dozens of evaluations covering major government programmes including the Expanded Public Works Programme, the Community Works Programme, early childhood development services, and business incentive schemes administered by the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition.

Frontline Service Delivery Monitoring (FSDM) took a different approach. Instead of reviewing documents and data, DPME officials physically visited government service points — clinics, schools, police stations, Home Affairs offices, social grant pay points — and assessed them against a set of basic standards. Were the facilities clean? Were staff present and working? Were citizens treated with dignity? Were waiting times reasonable? Were essential supplies available? The FSDM programme generated granular, facility-level data on the quality of service delivery that citizens actually experience, as opposed to the aggregate statistics that departments report. This was valuable precisely because it captured the gap between policy intention and ground-level reality — a gap that aggregate data routinely conceals.

Presidential Siyahlola visits, meaning "we are monitoring" in isiZulu, added a political dimension. The President and Cabinet ministers would conduct announced visits to communities to assess service delivery conditions firsthand. These visits generated media attention and political pressure, which sometimes produced rapid responses from provincial and local government. However, critics noted that Siyahlola visits tended to produce short-term fixes in the specific communities visited — a road repaired here, a clinic staffed there — rather than systemic improvements in the programmes responsible for those services. The visits were valuable as a signal of political priority, but limited as a mechanism for structural reform.

:::expert
The Public Affairs Research Institute (PARI), in its analyses of South Africa's monitoring and evaluation architecture, has drawn attention to a fundamental design tension within DPME. The department was created within the Presidency to give it political authority and proximity to executive decision-making. However, this same positioning created a dependence on presidential attention and support. When the political principals were actively engaged with M&E findings — as happened during some periods of the Zuma and Ramaphosa administrations — DPME had leverage. When political attention shifted to other priorities, DPME's findings could be acknowledged and shelved without consequence. PARI's research suggests that effective M&E systems require not just proximity to political power but institutional mechanisms that make responses to M&E findings mandatory rather than discretionary — a structural safeguard that South Africa's system lacks.
:::

What has DPME actually achieved? The honest assessment is: a great deal of measurement activity, some valuable individual evaluations, and insufficient systemic impact. MPAT generated useful comparative data on management quality, and some departments demonstrably improved their management practices in response to low scores. But MPAT had no binding consequences. A department could receive poor MPAT scores year after year without facing any formal penalty or compulsory intervention. The scores were published, which created some reputational pressure, but reputational pressure works only on officials and ministers who care about their reputation for competence — and in a political system where loyalty and factional allegiance often matter more than performance, that pressure was inconsistent.

The NEPF evaluations produced genuinely valuable knowledge about the effectiveness of government programmes. Several evaluations led to programme redesigns, and some led to programmes being discontinued or fundamentally restructured. But the pipeline from evaluation to policy change was slow, uncertain, and dependent on the willingness of the relevant department to accept and act on the findings. DPME could publish an evaluation showing that a programme was failing, but it could not compel the implementing department to change the programme. The department could thank DPME for the evaluation, promise to "consider the recommendations," and continue operating exactly as before. And because evaluations took months or years to complete, by the time findings were published, political priorities had often shifted, the responsible officials had moved on, and the institutional memory of why the evaluation was commissioned in the first place had faded.

## The Auditor-General: Africa's Most Sophisticated Public Audit

If DPME represents the executive's internal monitoring function, the Auditor-General of South Africa represents the external, constitutional accountability function. The AGSA is one of the Chapter 9 institutions — state institutions supporting constitutional democracy — established under Section 181 of the Constitution. The Auditor-General's mandate is to audit and report on the accounts, financial statements, and financial management of all national and provincial state departments and administrations, all municipalities, and any other institution or accounting entity required by national or provincial legislation to be audited.

In practical terms, the AGSA conducts annual audits of every government entity in South Africa. For national and provincial departments, the legal framework is the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) of 1999. For municipalities, it is the Municipal Finance Management Act (MFMA) of 2003. The AGSA's annual reports — the general report on the national and provincial audit outcomes, and the general report on local government audit outcomes — are the most comprehensive publicly available assessments of government financial management in South Africa. They cover hundreds of government entities and municipalities, and they are published with regularity and rigour that is exceptional by developing-country standards.

The audit outcomes are classified into four categories. A clean audit — technically, an unqualified audit opinion with no findings — means the entity's financial statements are free from material misstatement, it complied with applicable legislation, and it had effective internal controls. An unqualified opinion with findings means the financial statements were materially correct, but there were findings on compliance with legislation or on the quality of performance reporting. A qualified opinion means there were material misstatements in the financial statements that the entity could not or did not correct. A disclaimer of opinion — the worst outcome — means the entity's records were so incomplete or unreliable that the auditor could not form an opinion on the financial statements at all.

For economic governance, the AGSA's audit outcomes matter directly. Consider what the annual audit cycle reveals about the government's capacity to implement economic programmes. When the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition administers billions of rands in industrial incentives, the AGSA audits whether those funds were disbursed in accordance with the applicable legislation and whether the department can account for the outcomes. When the Department of Public Works manages infrastructure projects, the AGSA audits whether procurement followed proper processes and whether payments matched the work actually delivered. When municipalities administer local economic development programmes, the AGSA audits whether the expenditure was authorised, regular, and fruitful — a technical term meaning it achieved the intended purpose.

The AGSA's findings, accumulated over more than two decades of post-democratic auditing, paint a consistent picture. The proportion of government entities receiving clean audits has improved gradually at the national and provincial level, but remains stubbornly low at the municipal level. In the 2022/23 audit cycle, the AGSA reported that only a minority of municipalities achieved clean audits, while a significant proportion received qualified opinions or disclaimers. Irregular expenditure — spending that did not comply with applicable procurement legislation — continued to run into the tens of billions of rands annually across government. Unauthorised expenditure — spending that exceeded the approved budget or was not in accordance with the purpose for which it was approved — remained persistent. And fruitless and wasteful expenditure — spending that was made in vain and could have been avoided had reasonable care been exercised — continued to accumulate.

These are not technicalities. Irregular expenditure in an economic development programme means the procurement process was compromised — which means the state may not have received value for money, which means less economic development per rand spent. Fruitless and wasteful expenditure in an infrastructure programme means money was spent and nothing was built, or what was built is unusable. Unauthorised expenditure means the programme spent more than it was allocated, which means other programmes received less. Each of these audit findings represents a direct erosion of the state's capacity to drive economic growth through public spending.

In 2018, Parliament passed the Public Audit Amendment Act, which significantly expanded the AGSA's powers. Before the amendment, the Auditor-General could audit, report, and recommend — but had no enforcement power. If an accounting officer received an adverse audit finding and recommendations for corrective action, the Auditor-General could not compel compliance. The Amendment Act changed this in two important ways. First, it gave the AGSA the power to refer material irregularities to relevant public bodies — including the Hawks, the National Prosecuting Authority, and the Special Investigating Unit — for investigation. Second, it gave the AGSA the power to issue a certificate of debt, making an accounting officer personally liable for financial losses caused by their failure to implement the Auditor-General's recommendations. This was a significant shift from reporting power to remedial power — from a system that documented problems to one that could, in theory, trigger consequences.

:::expert
The AGSA's own published assessments have repeatedly identified what it calls the "accountability ecosystem failure." In its general reports, the AGSA has explained that audit findings, by themselves, cannot drive improvement if the broader accountability ecosystem is not functioning. That ecosystem includes internal audit units within departments, audit committees that are supposed to review and act on audit findings, accounting officers who are legally responsible for financial management, executive authorities (ministers and MECs) who are supposed to hold accounting officers to account, parliamentary portfolio committees that are supposed to use audit reports to hold the executive to account, and law enforcement agencies that are supposed to investigate and prosecute financial misconduct. The AGSA has consistently found that weaknesses across multiple nodes of this ecosystem — not just in any single institution — explain why audit findings persist year after year. Internal audit units are understaffed or lack independence. Audit committees are not taken seriously by management. Accounting officers face no consequences for repeated adverse findings. Portfolio committees do not follow up on commitments made by departments in previous hearings. The system produces accountability reports without reliably producing accountability.
:::

The expanded powers under the 2018 Amendment Act have been tested, but the results are still emerging. The AGSA has referred material irregularities to investigative bodies and issued certificates of debt in some cases. However, the effectiveness of these new powers depends on the responsiveness of the institutions to which referrals are made. If the Hawks are under-resourced or compromised, referrals from the AGSA sit in a queue. If the NPA lacks capacity to prosecute complex financial crimes, the referral does not lead to prosecution. The AGSA can identify the problem and trigger the process, but it cannot control what happens downstream. This is a recurring pattern in South Africa's oversight architecture: each institution performs its function adequately in isolation, but the chain of accountability breaks at the links between institutions.

## SOE Evaluation: Monitoring Without Mastery

State-owned enterprises occupy a peculiar position in South Africa's M&E framework. They are not government departments, so they are not directly subject to MPAT or DPME's departmental monitoring. They are registered as public entities under the PFMA and are audited by the AGSA, but their governance structures — boards of directors, shareholder compacts, corporate plans — place them in a hybrid zone between public accountability and corporate autonomy. The result is an evaluation framework that generates enormous quantities of documentation while frequently failing to prevent catastrophic governance failures.

The primary monitoring instruments for SOEs are the shareholder compact and the corporate plan. Each major SOE is required to enter into a shareholder compact with its shareholder ministry — the government department responsible for exercising ownership oversight. The shareholder compact sets out the SOE's strategic objectives, key performance indicators, and financial targets for the year. The corporate plan — typically a three-to-five-year document — sets out the medium-term strategic direction. Both documents are submitted to the shareholder minister, reviewed by the relevant department, and in some cases scrutinised by National Treasury and the relevant parliamentary portfolio committee.

The Department of Public Enterprises (DPE) historically served as the shareholder ministry for the largest commercial SOEs, including Eskom, Transnet, Denel, South African Airways, and South African Express. The DPE was responsible for reviewing shareholder compacts, appointing board members (on the minister's recommendation), monitoring financial and operational performance, and reporting to Parliament and Cabinet on SOE performance. Under the restructuring announced by President Ramaphosa, the DPE was dissolved, and oversight of individual SOEs was transferred to the relevant sector departments — Eskom to the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy, Transnet to the Department of Transport, and so on. This restructuring was intended to bring SOE oversight closer to sectoral expertise, but it also fragmented oversight across multiple departments with varying levels of capacity.

Development finance institutions (DFIs) such as the Industrial Development Corporation (IDC) and the Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA) operate under a different oversight model. They report to their respective shareholder departments (the IDC to the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition, the DBSA to National Treasury), submit corporate plans and annual reports, and are audited by the AGSA. Their mandate is not commercial profit maximisation but development impact — which makes evaluation more complex, because their success or failure should be measured against developmental outcomes (jobs created, industries supported, infrastructure financed) rather than purely financial metrics.

The Presidential SOE Council, established by President Ramaphosa in 2020, was designed to provide strategic, centralised oversight of SOEs at the highest level. The council was mandated to develop a unified approach to SOE reform, rationalise the SOE landscape, and address chronic governance and financial challenges. It represented an acknowledgement that the fragmented, department-by-department approach to SOE oversight had failed and that a central coordinating mechanism was needed.

Now consider the track record. Eskom's descent into financial and operational crisis was documented in real time by its own annual reports, by the AGSA's audit findings, by parliamentary portfolio committee hearings, and by the DPE's monitoring reports. The utility's financial deterioration — its growing debt, its declining generation capacity, its inadequate maintenance of plant — was visible in the data years before load-shedding became a national crisis. The AGSA flagged material irregularities. The DPE received shareholder compacts showing unsustainable financial projections. Parliamentary committees questioned Eskom executives about declining performance. The monitoring system worked — in the sense that it identified the problem. What did not work was the response. Political protection of compromised board members and executives, factional battles over procurement, and the broader dynamics of state capture — as comprehensively documented by the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture (the Zondo Commission) — meant that monitoring findings were acknowledged, filed, and ignored.

Transnet tells a similar story. The Zondo Commission found extensive evidence of procurement corruption, manipulated contracts, and governance failures at Transnet, particularly in its locomotive procurement programme. These were not hidden activities that monitoring failed to detect. Many of the irregularities were visible in Transnet's own procurement records, in the AGSA's audit findings, and in reports submitted to the DPE. The problem was not that the watchers failed to watch. The problem was that the watchers' findings were overridden by political actors with the power to ignore, suppress, or retaliate against those who raised alarms.

South African Airways consumed over R30 billion in government bailouts before it was placed into business rescue in 2019. Its financial unsustainability was evident for years in its annual reports, in AGSA audit findings that highlighted accumulated losses and going concern risks, and in DPE monitoring reports. Multiple turnaround strategies were announced, corporate plans were submitted, and shareholder compacts were signed. The monitoring system documented the airline's decline with precision. The political system chose not to act on those findings until the enterprise was effectively bankrupt.

:::takeaway
South Africa's SOE monitoring system has never suffered from a lack of data. Eskom, Transnet, and SAA all submitted annual reports, corporate plans, and shareholder compacts that documented their deteriorating performance in real time. The Auditor-General flagged material irregularities. Parliamentary committees heard testimony. The monitoring worked. What failed was the conversion of monitoring into action — because political protection, factional interests, and the dynamics of state capture overrode the system's findings. The lesson is that monitoring without enforceable consequences is documentation, not accountability.
:::

## The Gap Between Measurement and Action

By now, a pattern should be emerging. South Africa does not lack for monitoring instruments, evaluation frameworks, audit reports, or performance data. The gap — the systemic, structural, recurring gap — is between measurement and action. Understanding why this gap persists requires examining five specific weaknesses in the system's design and operation.

The first weakness is the absence of automatic consequences for poor performance data. When a department receives a poor MPAT score, there is no defined, automatic response that is triggered. The score is published, the department is encouraged to develop an improvement plan, and DPME may offer technical support. But "encouraged" is the operative word. There is no provision in law or regulation that mandates a specific intervention — such as the appointment of an administrator, the withholding of budget allocations, or the initiation of disciplinary proceedings against the accounting officer — when management performance falls below a defined threshold. The consequence framework is discretionary, which means it is political. And in a political system characterised by patronage networks, factional alliances, and a governing party with a dominant electoral position, discretionary consequences tend not to be applied against politically connected officials.

Compare this to the financial management framework for municipalities, where Section 139 of the Constitution does provide for provincial intervention in municipalities that fail to fulfil their constitutional obligations — including the appointment of administrators. Even this mechanism, which has a constitutional basis and a defined legal process, has been applied inconsistently and has frequently been subverted by political considerations. But at least it exists. For national and provincial departments receiving poor M&E results, there is no equivalent mechanism. The monitoring produces data; the system relies on political will to produce consequences.

The second weakness is that AGSA findings, while published with admirable regularity and thoroughness, have historically generated minimal accountability. Before the 2018 Amendment Act, the Auditor-General had no power to compel action. Even after the Amendment Act, the effectiveness of the new remedial powers depends on other institutions — the Hawks, the NPA, the courts — that have their own capacity constraints and independence challenges. The result is that departments and entities can accumulate adverse audit findings over years without facing formal consequences. The AGSA's own reports document this phenomenon explicitly: the same findings appear year after year in the same departments, because the underlying causes — poor internal controls, inadequate skills, compromised procurement processes — are not addressed. The AGSA has coined the term "regression" to describe entities whose audit outcomes worsen over time, and "stagnation" to describe entities whose outcomes remain poor without improvement. Both categories represent a failure not of auditing but of the accountability ecosystem that auditing is supposed to feed into.

The third weakness is that SOE evaluations reveal known failures, but political protection prevents corrective action. This has been documented extensively above, but it bears restating in structural terms. The shareholder compact system is premised on a principal-agent relationship: the government (principal) sets targets, the SOE board and management (agents) implement them, and the monitoring system tracks performance against targets. This model works only if the principal is willing to act when the agent underperforms — by replacing board members, changing management, restructuring the entity, or withdrawing financial support. When the principal is compromised — when ministers have personal or factional interests in maintaining the status quo at an SOE — the principal-agent model collapses. The monitoring data becomes a record of failure that the principal has chosen to tolerate.

The fourth weakness is DPME's institutional position. DPME can recommend but cannot compel. It sits within the Presidency, which gives it proximity to executive power but not executive power itself. It cannot direct a department to change its programme design. It cannot instruct a ministry to replace an underperforming SOE board. It cannot withhold budget allocations from entities that ignore evaluation findings. Its authority is derived from its association with the Presidency, which means its effectiveness fluctuates with presidential attention and political priorities. When the President is actively using M&E data to drive performance — as reportedly happened during certain periods — DPME has leverage. When the President's attention is elsewhere, DPME's findings gather dust. This is an inherently fragile foundation for a monitoring system that is supposed to provide a consistent, reliable feedback loop on government performance.

The fifth weakness is Parliament's inconsistent use of M&E data. Parliamentary portfolio committees are constitutionally mandated to exercise oversight over government departments and public entities. They have the power to summon officials, demand information, and issue recommendations. In theory, they should be the primary consumers of M&E data — using DPME evaluations, AGSA audit reports, and SOE performance data to hold the executive to account. In practice, parliamentary oversight quality varies enormously. Some portfolio committees are chaired by knowledgeable, diligent members who use audit data effectively. Others are perfunctory, poorly attended, and more concerned with protecting the governing party from embarrassment than with holding the executive to account. The research capacity available to portfolio committees is limited — members often lack the time or technical support to digest complex evaluation reports and translate them into focused, evidence-based questions. And the party political dynamics of South Africa's proportional representation system, where party lists rather than constituencies determine who sits in Parliament, can create incentives for members to prioritise party loyalty over rigorous oversight.

:::takeaway
The five structural weaknesses of South Africa's M&E system share a common root: the system was designed to produce information, not to compel action. MPAT has no automatic consequences. AGSA findings depend on downstream institutions for enforcement. SOE evaluation is undermined when the shareholder ministry is politically compromised. DPME can recommend but not direct. Parliament uses M&E data inconsistently. The result is a monitoring apparatus that functions as a sophisticated record-keeping operation rather than as a genuine accountability mechanism. The data exists; the consequences do not.
:::

## What Would an Effective M&E System Look Like?

Diagnosing the problem is the easier part. The harder question is what would need to change for South Africa's monitoring and evaluation system to actually drive improved economic governance. Several reform opportunities have been identified by researchers, by the AGSA itself, and by international comparative analysis.

The first and most fundamental reform would be to give DPME directive power — the legal authority to compel departments and entities to respond to evaluation findings within defined timeframes and with defined actions. This does not mean turning DPME into a super-ministry that micro-manages every department. It means establishing a legal framework in which evaluation findings trigger mandatory response processes. When a DPME evaluation finds that a government programme is failing to achieve its objectives, the relevant department should be required by law to produce a formal response plan within a specified period, setting out what changes it will make and by when. DPME should have the power to monitor the implementation of that response plan and to escalate non-compliance to Cabinet or to Parliament. This would transform DPME from an advisory body into an accountability body — a shift that would require legislative change, political will, and careful design to avoid creating a bureaucratic bottleneck.

The second reform would be to strengthen the link between AGSA findings and automatic remedial processes. The 2018 Amendment Act was a step in this direction, but it did not go far enough. One approach, modelled on practices in some OECD countries, would be to establish a statutory obligation on accounting officers to implement AGSA recommendations within a defined period, with automatic escalation to the Public Service Commission or to the relevant executive authority if recommendations are not implemented. The certificate of debt mechanism introduced by the Amendment Act is a powerful tool, but its application has been cautious and limited. Expanding its use — and ensuring that accounting officers understand that personal financial liability is a real risk, not a theoretical one — would significantly change the incentive structure.

The third reform would be to make SOE shareholder compacts fully public and auditable. Currently, shareholder compacts are submitted to the shareholder ministry and are sometimes tabled in Parliament, but they are not consistently available to the public in a format that allows meaningful scrutiny. Making every shareholder compact a public document — with clearly defined performance targets, financial benchmarks, and consequence clauses — would allow citizens, journalists, researchers, and opposition parties to hold SOEs and their shareholder ministries to account. If Eskom's shareholder compact had been publicly auditable in the early 2010s, the gap between the utility's stated targets and its actual performance would have been visible to the public years earlier than it was. Transparency does not guarantee accountability, but it is a prerequisite for it.

The fourth reform would be to build parliamentary capacity for M&E data use. This means investing in the research support available to portfolio committees — hiring analysts with the technical skills to digest complex audit and evaluation reports and brief committee members effectively. It means establishing a regular calendar of oversight activities tied to the annual M&E cycle — so that portfolio committees systematically review DPME evaluations and AGSA reports for every entity in their portfolio, rather than responding ad hoc to media coverage or political crises. And it means strengthening the independence incentives for members of portfolio committees — which is ultimately a question of electoral system design and party internal democracy that goes beyond M&E reform but fundamentally shapes the quality of parliamentary oversight.

The fifth reform — and in many ways the most important from a citizen perspective — would be to create a public M&E dashboard that makes government performance data accessible to ordinary citizens. The data that DPME, the AGSA, and SOE oversight bodies produce is technically public, but it is scattered across dozens of websites, published in dense technical reports, and presented in formats that require specialist knowledge to interpret. A unified, publicly accessible, regularly updated dashboard — showing key performance indicators for every government department, every major programme, every SOE, and every municipality — would democratise M&E data. Citizens should be able to look up their municipality, their provincial health department, their local police station, and see the most recent performance data in plain language. This is not a utopian proposal. Countries like Estonia, South Korea, and New Zealand have built public-facing government performance dashboards that present complex data in accessible formats. The technology exists. What is needed is the political commitment to transparency and the institutional capacity to maintain the platform.

:::expert
The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), in its reviews of South Africa's public governance, has consistently recommended strengthening the "results chain" in the M&E system — the explicit link between monitoring data, evaluation findings, budget decisions, and institutional consequences. The OECD's comparative analysis of M&E systems across its member countries identifies several features that distinguish high-performing systems from underperforming ones: mandatory government responses to evaluations within defined timeframes, direct links between evaluation findings and budget allocation decisions, public reporting on the implementation status of evaluation recommendations, and independent evaluation capacity that is insulated from political interference. South Africa's system, the OECD has noted, has many of the formal components of a high-performing M&E system but lacks the binding mechanisms that make those components produce results. The OECD's prescription is not more monitoring — South Africa already monitors extensively — but better integration of monitoring findings into decision-making processes that have real resource and personnel consequences.
:::

There is a deeper question here that goes beyond institutional design, and it connects directly to the themes explored in [From Strategy to Reality](/articles/eg-5-2-from-strategy-to-reality). The effectiveness of an M&E system ultimately depends on the quality of the political system it serves. In a political environment where elections are competitive, where citizens have access to performance data, where media is free and capable, and where political leaders face credible electoral consequences for poor governance — M&E data becomes a powerful tool for driving improvement. Politicians who know that their performance will be measured, published, and used by voters to make electoral decisions have strong incentives to pay attention to M&E findings and act on them. In a political environment where elections are less competitive, where patronage networks determine political survival more than performance, and where citizens lack access to or engagement with governance data — M&E becomes a bureaucratic exercise that generates reports but not results.

This is not an argument for despair. It is an argument for understanding that M&E reform cannot be separated from broader governance reform. Strengthening DPME, expanding AGSA powers, reforming SOE oversight — these are all necessary and valuable. But they will produce their full potential impact only in a governance environment where there are credible, enforceable consequences for failure. Building that environment is the long-term project of South African democracy, and monitoring and evaluation is both a tool for that project and a measure of its progress.

## How This Connects to Economic Growth

You might reasonably ask: what does all of this have to do with economic growth? The connection is direct and consequential. Every major economic growth strategy that South Africa has adopted — from the Reconstruction and Development Programme in 1994 to the Growth, Employment and Redistribution strategy in 1996, from the Accelerated and Shared Growth Initiative in 2006 to the National Development Plan in 2012, from the Economic Reconstruction and Recovery Plan in 2020 to the most recent iteration of industrial policy — has depended on government institutions to implement it. The quality of implementation determines whether strategies produce results or merely produce documents.

M&E is the mechanism by which implementation quality is tracked. When the M&E system works — when it identifies implementation failures, triggers corrective action, and feeds learning back into policy design — strategies have a chance of adapting and improving over time. When the M&E system fails — when implementation failures are documented but not addressed — strategies stagnate, resources are wasted, and the gap between policy ambition and economic reality widens.

Consider a concrete example. The Industrial Policy Action Plans (IPAPs), produced annually by the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition from 2007 onwards, set out detailed targets for industrial development — jobs created, investments attracted, exports increased, sectors developed. Each IPAP was monitored, and subsequent IPAPs reported on progress against previous targets. The monitoring data showed, with reasonable transparency, that many targets were not being met. Export diversification targets were missed. Job creation targets fell short. Sector development milestones were delayed. This information was available to DPME, to the AGSA (through the audit of the department's performance information), and to the parliamentary portfolio committee. The monitoring worked. What did not happen — or did not happen consistently — was a rigorous, evidence-based process of interrogating why targets were missed, identifying the structural barriers to achievement, redesigning the policy interventions accordingly, and holding responsible officials to account for persistent underperformance.

The result is a pattern that will be familiar to anyone who follows South African economic policy: new strategies are announced with fanfare, implementation is uneven, monitoring reveals shortfalls, and then a new strategy is announced to replace the old one — without a rigorous evaluation of why the previous strategy failed. This cycle — announcement, underperformance, replacement — is the signature of a governance system that monitors without learning. And it is one of the most important explanations for why South Africa has produced many economic strategies and little economic growth.

As you will see in [The Municipal Performance Index](/articles/eg-4-1-the-municipal-performance-index), the challenge of monitoring and evaluation is even more acute at the local government level, where capacity is weakest, data quality is poorest, and the distance between monitoring findings and corrective action is greatest. Municipalities are where economic policy meets the ground — where infrastructure is built, where business licences are issued, where land use is managed — and municipal M&E failures have direct, visible consequences for the local economies that citizens experience every day.

## Practical Framework: The M&E Effectiveness Test

The analysis above gives you the institutional detail. What follows is a simple framework you can apply to any monitoring and evaluation process you encounter — whether it concerns a national government programme, a provincial service, a municipal project, or a state-owned enterprise. The framework consists of five questions, and the pattern of answers tells you whether the monitoring is substantive or performative.

The first question is: are the findings publicly available? Effective M&E produces information that is accessible to citizens, journalists, researchers, and civil society organisations. If monitoring findings are classified, restricted, or published in formats so technical that only specialists can interpret them, the monitoring serves the bureaucracy rather than the public. Check whether the M&E reports for the programme or entity you are interested in are available on a government website, in a parliamentary committee record, or in the AGSA's published reports. If you cannot find the findings, that absence is itself a finding.

The second question is: is there a defined consequence for poor performance? Look for the consequence clause. When the M&E framework identifies that a programme is failing, a department is underperforming, or an SOE is missing its targets, what is supposed to happen? Is there a defined process — budget reduction, management change, programme redesign, administrative intervention — that is triggered by poor M&E results? Or does the framework rely on discretionary political will to produce consequences? If you cannot identify a specific, defined consequence for poor performance, the monitoring framework is advisory rather than binding.

The third question is: has the consequence ever been applied? A consequence that exists on paper but has never been applied is not a consequence — it is a decoration. Look for evidence that the defined consequences have actually been triggered. Has a department ever had its budget reduced because of poor evaluation findings? Has an accounting officer ever faced a certificate of debt from the AGSA? Has an SOE board ever been replaced because shareholder compact targets were missed? If the consequence has never been applied, you should treat it as hypothetical rather than real.

The fourth question is: has monitoring led to changed behaviour or improved outcomes? This is the ultimate test. Monitoring is not an end in itself — it is a means to improved performance. Look at the trend data. Have the programmes, departments, or entities that are subject to monitoring shown measurable improvement over time? If a department has been receiving M&E reports for a decade and its performance indicators are flat or declining, the monitoring is not producing its intended effect, regardless of how comprehensive the reports are.

The fifth question is: can citizens access and use the data? This question goes beyond publication to usability. Is the data presented in a format that an ordinary citizen — not an accountant, not a policy analyst, not a governance researcher — can understand and use? Can a voter in Limpopo look up how their province's economic development department is performing and compare it to Gauteng's? Can a resident of Emfuleni Local Municipality find out whether the municipality's infrastructure spending is producing results? If the data exists but is accessible only to specialists, the democratic purpose of M&E is not being served.

If your honest answers to most of these five questions are "no," then the monitoring system you are examining is performative. It produces the appearance of accountability without the substance. It generates reports that go into filing cabinets — physical or digital — without generating the pressure, the consequences, or the learning that drive improved performance. And in a country where economic growth has stagnated for a generation, performative monitoring is not a minor bureaucratic failing. It is a structural barrier to the improved governance that growth requires.

:::takeaway
Apply the M&E Effectiveness Test to any government programme: (1) Are findings public? (2) Is there a defined consequence for failure? (3) Has that consequence been applied? (4) Has monitoring improved outcomes? (5) Can citizens access the data? If most answers are "no," the monitoring is performative — it documents failure without driving change. This test works at national, provincial, and municipal level, and it works for departments, programmes, and SOEs alike. It is the simplest tool available to distinguish real accountability from accountability theatre.
:::

## Resources

South Africa's monitoring and evaluation landscape is extensively documented, and the primary sources are freely available online. The Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation publishes its evaluation reports, MPAT results, and annual performance plans on its website, and many of these documents are also available through the Government Technical Advisory Centre (GTAC). The AGSA's annual general reports on national and provincial audit outcomes and on local government audit outcomes are published on the Auditor-General's website and are essential reading for anyone who wants to understand the state of financial management across government — the reports include detailed analyses by department and municipality, as well as trend data showing improvement or regression over time.

The Zondo Commission's reports, officially the reports of the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture, are available online and provide the most comprehensive account of how SOE governance was compromised during the state capture period. The Public Affairs Research Institute (PARI) at the University of the Witwatersrand has published extensively on public administration reform, including analyses of DPME's role and effectiveness. The South African Monitoring and Evaluation Association (SAMEA) publishes research and policy briefs on M&E practice in South Africa, and its conference proceedings provide a window into the professional community's assessment of what is working and what is not.

For international comparative perspective, the OECD's Government at a Glance series and its specific country reviews of South Africa provide benchmarking against international best practice. The World Bank's Independent Evaluation Group publishes methodology and case studies on M&E systems in developing countries, and its work on results-based management offers frameworks that are directly applicable to South Africa's challenges. The Human Sciences Research Council (HSRC) has conducted research on government performance monitoring and on the use of evidence in policy-making in South Africa, and its publications are freely available through its online repository.
