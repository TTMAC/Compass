## Report a Crime, Almost Certainly Get No Justice

If you report a crime in South Africa today, the probability of that crime resulting in a conviction is catastrophically low.

The exact figure depends on the crime category, but the pattern is consistent across all of them: the criminal justice pipeline — from the moment a crime is reported through investigation, arrest, prosecution, trial, and sentencing — haemorrhages cases at every single stage. For every hundred serious crimes reported to SAPS, a fraction result in arrests. Of those arrests, a fraction result in prosecutions. Of those prosecutions, a fraction result in convictions. By the time you reach the end of the pipeline, the system has lost the overwhelming majority of cases that entered it.

This is not an abstract statistic. It means that if someone breaks into your home, assaults you on the street, robs you at gunpoint, or — in the most devastating cases — rapes you or murders someone you love, the system that is supposed to convert that crime into justice will, in all probability, fail. The perpetrator will, in all probability, face no consequences. And the message that sends — to perpetrators, to victims, to communities, to the country — is that crime in South Africa is a low-risk activity. The environment of impunity that [From Rainbow Nation to State Capture](/articles/ss-1-2-from-rainbow-nation-to-state-capture) documented as a product of state capture is not just a political phenomenon. It is a structural reality embedded in the performance data of every institution in the criminal justice pipeline.

## The Number You Need to Know

This article takes you through that pipeline stage by stage. At each stage, you will see where cases are lost, why they are lost, and what the data tells us about the scale of the attrition. By the end, you will have a mental model — a Pipeline Diagram — that you can use to trace any crime through the system and identify the specific points where the system failed. Understanding exactly where and why cases are lost is the first step toward demanding a system that actually converts reported crime into justice.

## The Pipeline: A Mental Model

Before we walk through the data, you need the mental model. Think of the criminal justice system as a pipeline with distinct stages, and at each stage there is a valve that either passes the case forward or lets it drain away.

The pipeline runs like this: a crime occurs in the real world. That crime may or may not be reported to the police — and the gap between crimes committed and crimes reported is the first and largest point of attrition. If the crime is reported, SAPS opens a case and assigns it a case number in the Crime Administration System (CAS). The case is then assigned to a detective for investigation. The investigation may or may not produce sufficient evidence for an arrest. If an arrest is made, the case docket is referred to the NPA, which decides whether to prosecute, decline to prosecute, or refer the case back to SAPS for further investigation. If the NPA decides to prosecute, the case enters the court system — first a bail hearing, then a series of remand appearances, then eventually (sometimes years later) a trial. The trial may result in a conviction or an acquittal. If there is a conviction, the court imposes a sentence.

At every one of these stages, cases fall out of the pipeline. Some fall out for legitimate reasons — the evidence genuinely does not support prosecution, or the suspect cannot be identified. But the vast majority fall out because of systemic failures: crimes that are never reported because victims do not trust the police; cases that are opened but never properly investigated because detectives are overwhelmed; dockets that are literally lost in the system; arrests that are never made because investigators lack the resources or skills to identify suspects; cases that are declined by the NPA because the investigation was too poor to prosecute; cases that collapse in court because witnesses have disappeared, evidence has degraded, or the case has been postponed so many times that it is no longer viable.

:::takeaway
Think of South Africa's criminal justice system as a pipeline with valves at each stage. Crime occurs → reported (or not) → case opened → investigated → arrest made → referred to NPA → prosecuted (or not) → trial → verdict → sentence. At every valve, cases drain away. The system's performance is not measured by how many cases enter the pipeline but by how many emerge as convictions at the other end. By that measure, the system fails catastrophically.
:::

## Stage One: The Reporting Gap — Crimes That Never Enter the System

The pipeline's first and most significant point of attrition occurs before the system even becomes aware that a crime has happened. The gap between crimes committed and crimes reported — what criminologists call the "dark figure of crime" — is enormous in South Africa.

SAPS annual crime statistics, which dominate public discourse about crime, reflect only reported crime. They tell you how many cases were opened at police stations. They do not tell you how many crimes actually occurred. For that, you need victim surveys — and the most comprehensive South African source is the Statistics South Africa Victims of Crime Survey (VOCS), conducted periodically since 1998.

The VOCS data consistently shows that a substantial proportion of crime victims do not report crimes to the police. The reporting rates vary by crime category: household burglary and vehicle theft tend to have higher reporting rates (often because insurance claims require a police case number), while assault, sexual violence, and robbery have significantly lower reporting rates. For sexual offences — the crime category where reporting is most critical for both justice and prevention — victim surveys suggest that fewer than one in three incidents are reported to police, and some estimates place the figure far lower.

Why do victims not report? The reasons are consistent across surveys and research: distrust of the police (a direct legacy of the apartheid-era relationship documented in [Order Through Oppression](/articles/ss-1-1-order-through-oppression)); fear of secondary victimisation at the police station, particularly for sexual offences; the belief that the police will not or cannot do anything; the physical distance to the nearest police station in rural areas; fear of retaliation from the perpetrator; and, for some crimes, a perception that the matter is private or can be handled within the community.

The reporting gap means that SAPS crime statistics systematically undercount the actual crime burden. When politicians or police officials cite declining crime statistics as evidence of improved policing, the first question you should ask is whether reporting rates have changed. A decline in reported crime could reflect an actual decline in crime — or it could reflect a declining willingness to report, which would be an indicator of deteriorating police-community trust. The Stats SA Victims of Crime Survey provides the data you need to distinguish between these two possibilities.

:::expert
The Institute for Security Studies (ISS) has conducted extensive analysis comparing SAPS crime statistics with victim survey data, consistently finding that official statistics significantly undercount the true crime burden. Their research has shown that the reporting gap is not uniform across the country — it tends to be largest in precisely the communities that experience the most crime: informal settlements, rural areas, and townships where police legitimacy is lowest and physical access to police stations is most limited. The ISS has also documented how the reporting gap creates a self-reinforcing cycle: when victims do not report because they do not trust the police, the police lack the data to allocate resources to the most affected areas, which in turn means those areas receive less policing, which further erodes trust, which further reduces reporting. Breaking this cycle requires not just improving police-community relations but fundamentally changing how the police measure and respond to crime — moving from a reliance on reported crime statistics to an intelligence-led model that uses victim surveys, community data, and other sources to build a more accurate picture of the crime environment.
:::

## Stage Two: Case Registration — The Station-Level Filter

When a crime is reported, the station-level process determines whether it is registered as a case in the SAPS Crime Administration System (CAS). This sounds like a mechanical step — victim arrives, case is opened, case number is assigned — but in practice, the station is itself a filter that can prevent cases from entering the pipeline.

There is well-documented evidence that some SAPS stations discourage victims from opening cases. This can take various forms: officers telling victims that the matter is "not serious enough" for a case; advising victims to resolve the matter privately; requiring victims to return at a different time or with additional documentation; or simply refusing to open a case. For domestic violence matters, despite the clear obligations imposed by the Domestic Violence Act of 1998, studies have consistently found that some station-level officers fail to open cases, fail to serve protection orders, or actively discourage complainants from pursuing their cases.

Even when cases are opened, the quality of the initial registration matters. The CAS system — SAPS's primary case management technology — has been widely criticised as outdated and inadequate. Case dockets are still largely paper-based, and the handoff between the initial case registration at the charge office and the detective assigned to investigate is a point where information is lost, dockets are mislaid, and cases begin their journey toward oblivion. The physical loss of case dockets — an almost unbelievable failure in a modern police service — remains a documented problem. When a docket is lost, the case effectively ceases to exist within the system.

## Stage Three: Investigation — Where Most Cases Go to Die

Investigation is the stage where the largest proportion of cases are effectively lost — not through a formal decision to close or withdraw, but through the slow death of neglect. A case is assigned to a detective. That detective, in a typical SAPS station, is carrying anywhere from 80 to over 150 active cases simultaneously. The detective must manage all of these cases — visiting crime scenes, interviewing witnesses, collecting evidence, liaising with forensic services, tracking suspects, and preparing dockets for prosecution. With caseloads of this magnitude, meaningful investigation of all but the most serious cases is physically impossible.

The detective-to-case ratio is one of the most important metrics in the criminal justice pipeline, and in South Africa it is catastrophically strained. International benchmarks suggest that a detective carrying more than 30 to 40 active cases cannot provide adequate investigation quality. South African detectives routinely carry two to four times that number. The result is triage by default: detectives focus on the cases they are most likely to close — often cases where the suspect is already known or where political or media pressure demands action — and the remaining cases receive minimal investigative attention.

The quality of crime scene management compounds the problem. First responders — the uniformed officers who arrive at a crime scene before detectives — are responsible for securing the scene and preserving evidence. In practice, crime scene contamination is widespread, particularly at scenes in high-crime areas where resources are stretched and response times are long. By the time a detective arrives, evidence may have been compromised, witnesses may have dispersed, and the window for effective forensic collection may have closed.

Forensic services represent another critical bottleneck. SAPS forensic laboratories process DNA samples, ballistics evidence, fingerprints, and other physical evidence. These laboratories have historically operated with massive backlogs — at various points, the DNA backlog alone has exceeded 100,000 samples, with turnaround times stretching to months or years. When forensic evidence is delayed, investigations stall. Witnesses relocate, memories fade, and cases that might have been prosecutable become unprosecutable. The forensic backlog is not a peripheral problem — for many crime categories, particularly sexual offences and violent crimes, forensic evidence is the difference between a prosecutable case and an unsolvable one.

:::expert
The Wits Justice Project, based at the University of the Witwatersrand, has documented the human consequences of investigation failure through extensive research into wrongful detentions, case collapses, and the experiences of both victims and accused persons caught in the system's dysfunction. Their investigations have revealed cases in which victims waited years for their cases to be investigated, only to be told that the docket had been lost; cases in which accused persons spent years in remand detention because investigation was so slow that their cases never reached trial; and cases in which convictions were overturned because the original investigation was so poorly conducted that the evidence could not withstand judicial scrutiny. The Wits Justice Project's work illustrates a critical point about the pipeline: investigation failure does not just mean that guilty people escape justice — it also means that innocent people are detained without resolution, that victims are denied closure, and that the system's legitimacy erodes with every case it fails to process competently.
:::

### The SAPS-NPA Coordination Failure

Perhaps the most structurally significant investigation failure is the chronic disconnect between SAPS investigators and NPA prosecutors. In a well-functioning criminal justice system, investigators and prosecutors work closely together — the prosecutor provides legal guidance on what evidence is needed to secure a conviction, and the investigator collects that evidence. In South Africa, the handoff between SAPS and the NPA is one of the pipeline's most destructive failure points.

The typical pattern works like this: a detective investigates a case with minimal prosecutorial input. When the detective believes the investigation is complete, the docket is sent to the NPA. A prosecutor reviews the docket and, in a substantial proportion of cases, finds it inadequate for prosecution — missing evidence, incomplete witness statements, broken chain of custody, insufficient forensic analysis. The prosecutor either declines to prosecute or refers the docket back to SAPS for "further investigation." The docket may bounce between SAPS and the NPA multiple times, with each cycle adding months or years to the case timeline. Meanwhile, witnesses relocate or become uncooperative, evidence degrades, and the case becomes progressively less prosecutable.

This coordination failure is not a management problem — it is a structural problem. SAPS and the NPA are separate institutions with separate mandates, separate budgets, separate reporting lines, and separate institutional cultures. There is no mechanism that compels them to collaborate effectively on individual cases, and the institutional incentives often work against collaboration. SAPS measures its performance partly by the number of cases "referred" to the NPA — which incentivises detectives to send dockets to prosecutors as quickly as possible, regardless of quality. The NPA measures its performance partly by conviction rates — which incentivises prosecutors to decline weak cases rather than invest resources in strengthening them.

The Scorpions, as [From Rainbow Nation to State Capture](/articles/ss-1-2-from-rainbow-nation-to-state-capture) documented, had solved this problem by integrating investigation and prosecution within a single unit. Their 93 per cent conviction rate demonstrated what was possible when the structural disconnect was eliminated. The destruction of the Scorpions and their replacement by the Hawks — housed within SAPS rather than the NPA — restored the structural disconnect and the case attrition that flows from it.

## Stage Four: The Prosecution Gateway

The NPA is the gateway between investigation and trial. When a case docket arrives from SAPS, a prosecutor must make one of three decisions: prosecute, decline to prosecute, or refer back for further investigation. This decision point is one of the pipeline's most significant filters.

The NPA's annual reports consistently show that a substantial proportion of cases referred by SAPS are either declined for prosecution or referred back for further investigation. The reasons for declining are instructive: insufficient evidence is the most common, followed by the complainant being uncooperative or unavailable, the suspect being unidentifiable, and technical deficiencies in the investigation. Each of these reasons points back to investigation quality as the upstream problem.

The NPA itself operates under severe capacity constraints. Prosecutors carry heavy caseloads, particularly in district courts (which handle less serious matters) and regional courts (which handle more serious matters including sexual offences). The specialised prosecution units — the Sexual Offences and Community Affairs (SOCA) unit for GBV cases, and the Asset Forfeiture Unit for proceeds of crime — are small relative to the caseload they face. As [Following the Money](/articles/2-1-following-the-money) documents, the NPA's budget allocation within the justice cluster has historically been insufficient relative to its mandate.

The NPA also makes prosecutorial decisions within a system where its own capacity was severely degraded during the state capture era. The decade of leadership instability documented in the previous article resulted in the departure of experienced prosecutors, the erosion of institutional memory, and the politicisation of case selection. The current NPA leadership has been working to rebuild institutional capacity and credibility, but the base from which they are rebuilding was deeply damaged.

:::takeaway
The NPA is the gateway between investigation and trial, and a substantial proportion of cases are lost at this gateway — not because prosecutors are unwilling to prosecute, but because investigation quality is so poor that the evidence does not support prosecution. The SAPS-NPA coordination failure — two separate institutions with separate incentives failing to collaborate on individual cases — is one of the most destructive structural flaws in the entire pipeline. Fixing this disconnect is a precondition for improving conviction rates.
:::

## Stage Five: The Court System — Justice Delayed Indefinitely

For the cases that survive the investigation and prosecution filters — already a small fraction of the cases that entered the pipeline — the court system presents its own set of attrition points.

The first is the bail hearing. Within 48 hours of arrest (as required by Section 35 of the Constitution), the accused must appear before a magistrate for a bail decision. The bail system is supposed to balance the accused person's right to freedom (they have not been convicted of anything) against the risk of flight, the risk of interfering with the investigation, and the seriousness of the offence. In practice, as [Justice Delayed, Rights Denied](/articles/ss-2-3-justice-delayed-rights-denied) will examine in detail, the bail system produces outcomes that are shaped more by the accused person's ability to afford bail than by a rational assessment of risk. Poor accused persons who cannot afford even modest bail amounts are detained in remand, while those with resources secure release.

After the bail hearing, the case enters the remand cycle — a series of court appearances at which the case is progressively moved toward trial. In theory, these appearances are milestones in a structured process. In practice, they are often brief, procedural events at which the case is postponed to a future date because the investigation is not yet complete, the prosecutor is not ready, the accused's legal representative is unavailable, a witness has not been served, an interpreter is not present, or the court roll is simply too full to accommodate the matter.

The postponement culture is one of the court system's most corrosive features. Cases are routinely postponed five, ten, fifteen, or more times before reaching trial. Each postponement adds weeks or months to the case timeline. Each postponement increases the probability that witnesses will become unavailable, that evidence will degrade, and that the accused (if in remand detention) will spend additional months in overcrowded correctional facilities awaiting a trial that may never happen.

The court infrastructure itself is part of the problem. Many magistrates' courts — which handle approximately 95 per cent of all criminal cases — operate in buildings that are physically deteriorating, with inadequate technology, insufficient courtrooms, and chronic shortages of support staff. The shortage of magistrates and prosecutors means that court rolls are overloaded, and the time available for each case is insufficient for thorough proceedings.

:::expert
Research published by the Institute for Security Studies and analysis from the Department of Justice's own annual reports has documented the scale of court delays and their consequences for the criminal justice pipeline. Their data shows that the average time from arrest to finalisation for cases in regional courts — which handle serious offences including murder, robbery, and sexual offences — frequently exceeds two years, and in many jurisdictions extends to three or four years. For complex cases involving multiple accused or multiple charges, timelines of five years or more are not uncommon. These delays have cascading effects throughout the pipeline: they swell the remand detention population (examined in the next article in this series), they demoralise victims and witnesses, they consume prosecutorial and judicial resources on procedural appearances rather than substantive trials, and they ultimately reduce conviction rates because the passage of time makes cases progressively harder to prove. The ISS has argued that court delay is not merely a symptom of under-resourcing — it is a systemic feature that requires structural reform, including the case-flow triage system and community courts proposed in Part 3 of this series.
:::

## Stage Six: Trial and Verdict — The Survivors

For the small fraction of cases that reach trial — having survived the reporting gap, the investigation, the prosecution gateway, and the court delays — the trial itself is the system's final filter. Trials can result in convictions, acquittals, or cases being struck from the roll (effectively dismissed because the state's case has collapsed).

The conviction rate — measured as a proportion of cases that reach trial — is actually relatively respectable in South Africa, typically in the range of 85 to 90 per cent for cases that the NPA brings to trial. This sounds encouraging until you realise what it actually means: the NPA, knowing that its resources are limited and that weak cases waste court time, selects only the strongest cases for prosecution. The high trial conviction rate reflects this selection effect — it does not reflect the system's overall ability to convert reported crimes into convictions. When you measure from the other end of the pipeline — what proportion of reported crimes result in convictions — the figure is devastating.

The overall case-to-conviction ratio varies by crime category. For contact crimes (murder, assault, robbery, sexual offences), the ratio of reported crimes to convictions is typically in the single digits — meaning that for every hundred reported contact crimes, fewer than ten result in someone being convicted and sentenced. For property crimes, the ratio is even lower. For sexual offences, the ratio is catastrophically low — a fact that will be examined in detail when this series reaches [The System's Most Catastrophic Failure](/articles/ss-2-4-the-systems-most-catastrophic-failure).

## Stage Seven: Sentencing — The Output Quality

For the small number of cases that result in convictions, sentencing is the pipeline's final stage. The sentencing framework in South Africa operates under the Criminal Law Amendment Act of 1997 (Act 105), which imposes minimum sentences for specified serious offences — including life imprisonment for premeditated murder and certain sexual offences. Outside of the minimum sentencing framework, magistrates and judges exercise discretion within broad parameters.

The sentencing stage has its own set of problems, which this series will address in detail in [A Sentencing Commission and Structured Guidelines](/articles/ss-3-4-sentencing-commission-and-structured-guidelines). The core issues are inconsistency — similar offences receive dramatically different sentences in different courts and provinces — and the blunt instrument of minimum sentencing, which removes judicial discretion without addressing the root causes of sentencing variation. But for the purposes of understanding the pipeline, the key point is this: sentencing is the output of the entire system, and if the upstream stages are failing — if cases are not being reported, not being investigated, not being prosecuted, and not reaching trial — then sentencing reform, however important, cannot compensate for the pipeline's overall failure to convert crime into consequences.

## The Pipeline's Cumulative Effect

Let us put the pipeline together with illustrative numbers. These are approximations drawn from multiple data sources — SAPS annual crime statistics, the NPA annual report, Stats SA Victims of Crime Survey data, and Department of Justice statistics — and the exact figures vary by crime category and year. But the pattern is consistent.

Imagine 1,000 serious crimes are committed. Of those 1,000, perhaps 400 to 600 are reported to the police, depending on the crime category — the reporting gap eliminates 40 to 60 per cent of crimes from the pipeline before the system is even aware of them. Of the 400 to 600 reported crimes, cases are opened and assigned to detectives. Of those cases, perhaps 150 to 250 result in arrests — the investigation stage eliminates roughly half to two-thirds of reported cases. Of the arrests, perhaps 100 to 150 cases are referred to the NPA with dockets that the NPA considers prosecutable. Of those, perhaps 60 to 100 are actually prosecuted — the NPA declines or refers back the remainder. Of the prosecuted cases, perhaps 50 to 85 result in convictions at trial. And of those convicted, some receive sentences that are adequate to the crime, and some do not.

So from 1,000 crimes committed, you end up with somewhere between 50 and 85 convictions — a system-wide conversion rate of roughly 5 to 8.5 per cent. For every hundred crimes committed in South Africa, between five and nine result in anyone being held accountable. The remaining 91 to 95 per cent of crimes produce no consequence for the perpetrator.

:::takeaway
The criminal justice pipeline's cumulative attrition means that for every hundred serious crimes committed in South Africa, fewer than ten result in a conviction. The attrition occurs at every stage — reporting, investigation, prosecution, trial — and at every stage the losses are driven by structural failures: distrust of police, inadequate detective capacity, poor forensic services, the SAPS-NPA coordination failure, and chronic court delays. This is not a system that is slightly underperforming. It is a system that is structurally incapable of delivering its core function.
:::

## Why This Matters Beyond the Numbers

The pipeline's failure has consequences that extend far beyond the individual cases that are lost. It shapes the behaviour of every actor in the system — and every actor outside it.

For potential perpetrators, the pipeline's failure is a rational signal that crime is low-risk. If the probability of facing consequences for committing a crime is less than 10 per cent, the deterrence function of the criminal justice system — Outcome 1 in the systems theory framework — is fundamentally compromised. This does not mean that people commit crimes solely because they calculate the odds of being caught. But it does mean that the environmental conditions favour offending: the perceived risk of apprehension and punishment is low, and this perception is accurate.

For victims, the pipeline's failure means that reporting a crime is an act of faith rather than a reasonable expectation of justice. Many victims, particularly repeat victims, have personal experience of reporting crimes that were never investigated, of cases that disappeared into the system, of being told to come back later or resolve the matter privately. This experience drives the reporting gap, which in turn drives the pipeline's first and largest point of attrition.

For police officers, prosecutors, and magistrates working within the system, the pipeline's failure creates moral injury and learned helplessness. Detectives who carry 150 cases know they cannot investigate them all properly. Prosecutors who receive inadequate dockets know that many cases cannot be won. Magistrates who see the same cases postponed repeatedly know that the system is not delivering justice. The individuals within the system are, in many cases, doing their best within impossible constraints — but the structural design of the system ensures that their best is not good enough.

For the country, the pipeline's failure drives the private security phenomenon examined later in this series, erodes social cohesion, deters investment, and undermines the legitimacy of the state. When citizens conclude that the state cannot perform its most basic function — protecting them from crime and delivering justice when crimes occur — their relationship with the state changes fundamentally. They purchase private protection if they can afford it. They take justice into their own hands if they cannot. They disengage from the formal system and build alternative mechanisms — some legitimate, some not — for managing safety in their communities. The pipeline's failure is not just a criminal justice problem. It is a governance problem, an economic problem, and a constitutional problem.

## The Pipeline Diagram: Your Tool for Tracing Any Crime Through the System

Now that you understand the pipeline's structure and its attrition points, you can use this framework to trace any crime — whether it is one you have experienced personally, one you read about in the news, or one you encounter in the data — through the system. At each stage, ask the diagnostic question. The answers will tell you where the system failed and which institution is responsible.

:::framework
**The Criminal Justice Pipeline Diagram**

**Stage 1 — Crime Occurrence → Reporting**
Diagnostic question: Was the crime reported? If not, why not?
Responsible institution: SAPS (community trust, station accessibility)
Data source: Stats SA Victims of Crime Survey (reporting rates by crime type)

**Stage 2 — Reporting → Case Registration**
Diagnostic question: Was a case opened and a case number assigned? Was the victim turned away or discouraged?
Responsible institution: SAPS (station-level management, CAS system)
Data source: SAPS Annual Report (cases opened by category)

**Stage 3 — Case Registration → Investigation**
Diagnostic question: Was the case actively investigated? Was a detective assigned? Was the crime scene processed? Was forensic evidence collected and analysed?
Responsible institution: SAPS (detective services, forensic services)
Data source: SAPS Annual Report (detection rates, cases referred to NPA)

**Stage 4 — Investigation → Arrest**
Diagnostic question: Was an arrest made? If not, why not — was the suspect unidentifiable, was the investigation inadequate, or did the case receive insufficient attention?
Responsible institution: SAPS (detective capacity, investigative resources)
Data source: SAPS Annual Report (arrest-to-case ratios by crime type)

**Stage 5 — Arrest → Prosecution Decision**
Diagnostic question: Did the NPA decide to prosecute? If the case was declined, what was the reason — insufficient evidence, uncooperative complainant, or technical deficiency?
Responsible institution: NPA (prosecution gateway)
Data source: NPA Annual Report (cases received, prosecuted, declined, and referred back)

**Stage 6 — Prosecution → Trial**
Diagnostic question: How long between the first court appearance and the start of trial? How many postponements? Was the accused in remand detention during this period?
Responsible institution: NPA and Department of Justice (court scheduling, case readiness)
Data source: Department of Justice annual statistics, court roll data

**Stage 7 — Trial → Verdict**
Diagnostic question: Was the accused convicted or acquitted? If acquitted, was it due to inadequate evidence (investigation failure) or procedural issues?
Responsible institution: NPA (prosecution quality), SAPS (investigation quality), courts (judicial process)
Data source: NPA Annual Report (conviction rates by court tier and crime type)

**Stage 8 — Conviction → Sentencing**
Diagnostic question: Was the sentence proportionate to the crime? Was it consistent with sentences for similar offences?
Responsible institution: Courts (sentencing)
Data source: Sentencing data (available through the Department of Justice, though not easily accessible)

**How to Find the Data:**
SAPS Annual Report: Published annually, available on the SAPS website. Contains crime statistics, detection rates, and case referral data by province and crime category.
NPA Annual Report: Published annually, available on the NPA website. Contains prosecution statistics, conviction rates, and case disposition data.
Stats SA Victims of Crime Survey: Published periodically, available on the Stats SA website. Provides victim-reported crime data that reveals the gap between actual crime and reported crime.
Department of Justice Annual Report: Contains court performance data including case finalisation rates and time-to-trial metrics.

Use these data sources to assess pipeline performance in your area. If your local police station has a low detection rate, the problem is at Stage 3. If the NPA in your province has a high rate of declining cases, the problem is at the Stage 3-to-5 handoff. If court delays are extreme, the problem is at Stage 6. The pipeline diagram helps you direct your questions — and your demands for accountability — to the right institution at the right stage.
:::
