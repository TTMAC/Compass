## The Reform Blueprint

<!-- Section: Single-entity ownership model; resource ring-fencing; automatic enforcement for quality and title deeds -->

:::framework
**The Housing Delivery Chain Audit**

Use this framework to trace a housing project from approval to occupation and identify exactly where the governance chain breaks — whether you are a beneficiary waiting for a house, a community leader advocating for delivery, or a citizen trying to understand why housing targets are consistently missed.

**Step 1: Identify every entity involved in your housing project.**

For a typical subsidised housing project, list the entities: the national Department of Human Settlements (subsidy framework and conditional grant), the provincial department (project approval, developer appointment, grant management), the municipality (land identification, zoning, bulk infrastructure — water, sewerage, roads — and building plan approval), the developer or contractor (construction), the National Home Builders Registration Council (construction quality assurance), and the Deeds Registry (title deed registration). That is at least six entities across three spheres. Write them down and note which RAARICLE element each one holds.

**Step 2: Ask who owns the outcome end-to-end.**

For the project you have identified, ask: if the beneficiary moves into a house with cracking walls, no water connection, and no title deed, which single entity is answerable? If the answer is "it depends on which part failed," you have confirmed the fragmentation problem. No entity owns the outcome from project approval to a satisfied, title-holding occupant.

**Step 3: Trace the title deed.**

South Africa has a backlog of hundreds of thousands of unregistered title deeds for subsidised housing. For any completed housing project in your area, check whether beneficiaries have received their title deeds. If they have not, identify the bottleneck: is it the municipality (which must provide a compliant township register), the provincial department (which must process the subsidy and conveyancing), or the Deeds Registry (which must register the transfer)? Title deed failure is the clearest indicator of fragmentation — it requires multiple entities to complete a sequential process, and a failure at any point blocks the entire chain.

**Step 4: Check construction quality.**

Has the NHBRC inspected the houses? Were defects identified? If defects were found, what enforcement action followed — was the developer required to rectify at their own cost, or did the defects persist with no consequence? If developers can deliver substandard houses and still receive payment for the next project, the enforcement element has failed.

**Step 5: Follow the housing money.**

Identify whether the Human Settlements Development Grant (the main conditional grant for subsidised housing) was fully spent in your province in the most recent financial year. If it was underspent, identify why: was it a land availability problem (municipal responsibility), a procurement delay (provincial responsibility), or a developer capacity issue? Underspending on housing grants while the waiting list grows is a direct indicator of authority and capability failures in the delivery chain.

**Step 6: Assess bulk infrastructure coordination.**

A house without water, sewerage, and road access is not a habitable dwelling. Check whether the municipality's bulk infrastructure programme is aligned with the provincial housing delivery schedule. If houses are built before bulk infrastructure is in place — or if bulk infrastructure is installed but no houses follow — you have identified a coordination failure between the municipal and provincial programmes. This is one of the most common causes of housing project delays and the clearest example of what happens when resources and authority are split across spheres.

This audit gives you the evidence base to engage your ward councillor, your provincial MEC for human settlements, or organisations like the Housing Development Agency — with a precise map of where the delivery chain is broken, not just a complaint that houses are not being built.
:::
