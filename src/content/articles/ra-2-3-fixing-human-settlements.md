## The Most Fragmented Function in South African Governance

If you want to understand what maximum institutional fragmentation looks like — what happens when a government function is scattered across so many entities that nobody owns the outcome — human settlements is the case study. No other function in South African governance involves as many institutions, as many handoff points, and as complete an absence of end-to-end ownership as the delivery of a subsidised house from project conception to a beneficiary holding a title deed.

## The Human Settlements Value Chain

In [Why South Africa's Institutions Keep Failing](/articles/ra-1-2-why-institutions-fail), fragmentation was identified as the first of five structural failure patterns. Human settlements is where that pattern reaches its most extreme expression. The education value chain is fragmented across two spheres. The healthcare value chain involves multiple entities but at least has a single facility as the point of delivery. The housing value chain involves at least six distinct entities across all three spheres of government, plus private sector developers, a regulatory body, and a deeds registration system — and the output is not a recurring service but a once-off physical product that must be planned, financed, built, inspected, and legally transferred. Every step requires a different entity. Every handoff creates a gap. And the beneficiary at the end of the chain — the person who has been on a waiting list for years or decades — bears the full cost of every gap.

Trace the chain from start to finish. The national Department of Human Settlements sets the policy framework for subsidised housing. The DHS determines the subsidy quantum — the amount allocated per housing unit under the various subsidy programmes (currently ranging from approximately R160,000 to R220,000 depending on the programme and location). The DHS publishes the National Housing Code, which prescribes the norms and standards for subsidised housing — minimum house size (40 square metres), construction specifications, and environmental standards. The DHS administers the two main conditional grants: the Human Settlements Development Grant (HSDG), which funds housing delivery in all provinces and non-metropolitan municipalities, and the Urban Settlements Development Grant (USDG), which provides integrated infrastructure funding to metropolitan municipalities.

Move to the provincial level. Each provincial department of human settlements manages the housing pipeline within its province. The provincial department receives the HSDG allocation, approves housing project applications from municipalities or developers, appoints or approves the developers who will construct the houses, manages the disbursement of subsidy funding to developers against construction milestones, and is supposed to inspect completed houses before approving final payment. The provincial MEC for Human Settlements is politically accountable for housing delivery targets in the province.

Move to the municipality. The municipal role is foundational but often underappreciated. The municipality identifies and makes available suitable land for housing development. This requires rezoning (if the land is not already zoned for residential use), environmental impact assessment, geotechnical assessment (to confirm the land is suitable for construction), and township establishment (the legal process of creating a new township with registered stands). The municipality is responsible for providing bulk infrastructure — water connections, sewerage networks, stormwater drainage, road access, and electricity reticulation — to the housing development site. Without bulk infrastructure, houses cannot be occupied. The municipality also approves building plans and issues occupancy certificates once construction is complete.

Now add the developer or contractor. The developer is the entity that physically constructs the houses. Developers are appointed through the provincial department (in non-metropolitan areas) or directly by the municipality (in metros with Level 2 accreditation). The developer procures materials, manages the construction workforce, and builds the houses according to the specifications in the National Housing Code. The developer is paid in tranches — typically against milestones such as foundation completion, wall plate, roof, and practical completion — with each tranche requiring inspection and sign-off.

Add the National Home Builders Registration Council. The NHBRC is mandated to protect housing consumers by registering home builders, enrolling new housing projects, and inspecting construction quality. Every subsidised housing project must be enrolled with the NHBRC, and every completed house must be inspected against prescribed construction standards before it is approved for occupation. The NHBRC issues a warranty on enrolled houses — in theory protecting beneficiaries against structural defects for five years.

Finally, add the Deeds Registry. Once the house is completed and the beneficiary takes occupation, the title deed must be registered in the beneficiary's name at the Deeds Registry. Title deed registration requires a compliant township register from the municipality, a transfer instruction from the provincial department, conveyancing by an appointed attorney, and processing by the Deeds Registry. The title deed is not a bureaucratic formality — it is the legal instrument that gives the beneficiary ownership, the right to sell or bequeath the property, and the ability to use it as collateral.

Count the entities: DHS (national), provincial human settlements department, municipality, developer, NHBRC, Deeds Registry — and we have not yet mentioned the Housing Development Agency (a national entity created to acquire and develop land), the provincial department of public works (which may be involved in infrastructure provision), or the Surveyor-General's office (which must approve cadastral plans before township establishment can be completed). At minimum, six entities across three spheres must execute their respective functions in sequence for a single house to move from project approval to a beneficiary holding a title deed. If any entity in the chain fails, stalls, or underperforms, the entire project is delayed — and the beneficiary waits.

:::takeaway
The human settlements value chain is the most fragmented in South African governance. A single subsidised housing unit requires sequential action by at least six entities across three spheres of government. No single entity owns the outcome from project approval to title deed registration. This is not a coordination problem that better communication can solve — it is a structural design failure that guarantees accountability gaps, delivery delays, and the persistent housing backlog that defines South Africa's urban and peri-urban landscape.
:::

## RAARICLE™ Diagnosis

### Responsibility — Rating: 1 (Critical Failure)

Who is responsible for delivering a subsidised house? Nobody owns it end-to-end. The DHS sets the framework but does not build houses. The province approves projects and appoints developers but does not provide land or bulk infrastructure. The municipality provides land and infrastructure but does not manage the construction process (except in metros with accreditation). The developer builds the house but is not responsible for infrastructure, land suitability, or title deed registration. The NHBRC inspects quality but does not manage the project. The Deeds Registry registers title but has no role in ensuring the house was properly built or that the infrastructure was connected.

When a housing project produces defects or service gaps — structural cracks, inadequate sewerage, undelivered title deeds years after occupation — each entity in the fragmented delivery chain can point to its piece and claim it fulfilled its function. The province approved the project. The municipality made land available. The developer built the structure. The NHBRC enrolled the project. Each entity completed its task. And yet the beneficiary lives in a defective house without legal ownership or adequate services. No single entity bears accountability for the final outcome: a house that meets standards, has full service connectivity, and confers legal ownership.

The responsibility element earns the lowest possible rating — 1, critical failure — because **end-to-end responsibility does not merely fail in practice; it does not exist by design.** The institutional architecture distributes responsibility across so many entities that *the concept of a single responsible party is structurally impossible.*

The partial exception is metropolitan municipalities with Level 2 accreditation. The Housing Act allows municipalities to apply for accreditation to administer national housing programmes directly, bypassing the provincial department. Level 2 accreditation gives the municipality the authority to manage the full project cycle — from project identification through developer appointment, construction management, and subsidy disbursement. Several metropolitan municipalities, including the City of Johannesburg, eThekwini, and the City of Cape Town, have achieved Level 2 accreditation and manage significant portions of their housing delivery directly. Their performance — while not without problems — demonstrates the value of consolidating responsibility in a single entity.

### Accountability — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

The national Minister of Human Settlements reports to Parliament on national housing delivery targets. Provincial MECs report to their legislatures. Municipal managers are accountable to their councils for infrastructure provision. But the beneficiary who has been on a waiting list for fifteen years — or who has received a defective house with no title deed — has no single accountability point.

The Auditor-General's audits of the DHS and provincial human settlements departments have consistently identified accountability gaps. The AG's 2022-23 report noted that the national department had underspent its budget by R1.97 billion, primarily on the HSDG. Several provinces showed persistent patterns of underspending on housing conditional grants while their housing backlogs continued to grow. The AG identified "inadequate oversight of contractors" and "failure to enforce contractual conditions" as recurring findings — symptoms of an accountability system where no single entity is responsible for the end-to-end delivery outcome.

:::expert
The Auditor-General's consolidated reports on human settlements have documented what the AG describes as an "accountability vacuum" in the housing value chain. The AG has found that "the separation of functions across spheres — with national setting policy and providing funding, provinces managing projects, and municipalities providing land and infrastructure — creates a structural condition where no single entity bears unambiguous accountability for the end-to-end delivery of a housing unit to a qualifying beneficiary." The AG has recommended that the DHS, provincial departments, and municipalities develop binding joint accountability frameworks, but implementation of this recommendation has been limited.
:::

### Authority — Rating: 1 (Critical Failure)

The 30-day authority test fails comprehensively in human settlements. Consider the sequence of decisions required to deliver a single housing project.

The municipality identifies suitable land. But the land may require rezoning — a process that can take months or years, involving public participation, environmental assessment, and municipal council approval. Once the land is zoned and available, the municipality must establish the township — another process requiring surveyor-general approval, registration with the deeds office, and compliance with planning legislation. The municipality then needs to provide bulk infrastructure — water, sewerage, roads — which requires its own procurement process, its own funding (from the municipal infrastructure budget or the USDG), and its own construction timeline.

Meanwhile, the province must approve the housing project — a process that depends on the municipality having completed the land and infrastructure preparation. The province then appoints a developer — through a procurement process governed by the PFMA and supply chain management regulations. The developer must be registered with the NHBRC. Construction begins only after all of these sequential steps are completed.

No single entity can accelerate this sequence using its own authority alone. The province cannot force the municipality to rezone land faster. The municipality cannot force the province to approve a project faster. Neither can control the Deeds Registry's processing timelines. **The 30-day test does not just fail — it is structurally impossible.** The housing delivery timeline is measured in years, not days, because *the sequential dependencies between entities create a critical path that no single entity controls.*

### Resources — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

Housing funding is fragmented across multiple instruments with different conditions, different reporting requirements, and different accountability channels.

The HSDG is the primary funding instrument for subsidised housing delivery outside metropolitan areas. In the 2024/25 financial year, the HSDG was approximately R24.8 billion nationally. The grant is conditional — provinces must spend it on approved housing programmes, comply with the National Housing Code, report against prescribed indicators, and manage developer payments against verified milestones.

The USDG provides metropolitan municipalities with integrated urban development funding — approximately R14.5 billion in 2024/25. Unlike the HSDG, the USDG is more flexibly structured, allowing metros to allocate funds across housing, infrastructure, and urban management priorities. This flexibility gives metros more operational discretion but also makes it harder to track how much USDG funding is actually spent on housing delivery versus other urban priorities.

Bulk infrastructure funding comes from a separate source — the Municipal Infrastructure Grant or the municipality's own capital budget. This means that the top structure (the house itself) and the bulk infrastructure (the water, sewerage, and roads that make the house habitable) are funded through different instruments, managed by different entities, on different timelines. When the infrastructure is not ready when the houses are built — or when the houses are not built when the infrastructure is complete — the funding fragmentation is the structural cause.

The subsidy quantum itself is a resource constraint. **At approximately R160,000 to R220,000 per unit, the subsidy must cover land acquisition, infrastructure, professional fees, and construction.** In high-cost areas — particularly metropolitan regions where land is expensive — the subsidy quantum is widely regarded as insufficient to produce a house that meets the prescribed quality standards. This creates pressure on developers to cut costs, which manifests as the construction quality failures that are documented in province after province.

### Information — Rating: 1 (Critical Failure)

The information deficit in human settlements is arguably the most severe of any government function. South Africa does not have a single, accurate, national database of housing demand. Municipal waiting lists — the primary mechanism for identifying qualifying beneficiaries — are managed by individual municipalities with no standardised format, no common data architecture, and no systematic verification process. The Housing Subsidy System (HSS), the national database that records subsidy approvals and disbursements, has documented data quality problems. Duplicate entries, deceased beneficiaries who remain on lists, and beneficiaries who have received subsidies but are not recorded — all have been identified in audits and investigations.

The absence of reliable demand data means that planning is based on estimates rather than verified needs. A province cannot accurately determine how many qualifying beneficiaries are in each municipality because the municipal waiting lists are unreliable. A municipality cannot accurately verify that its waiting list reflects actual demand because it lacks the administrative capacity to conduct regular verification exercises. The DHS cannot produce a definitive national figure for the housing backlog because the underlying data — municipal waiting lists — is not standardised or verified.

Project-level information is equally fragmented. There is no single platform that tracks every housing project from approval through construction to title deed registration. The provincial department tracks projects it has approved. The municipality tracks bulk infrastructure provision. The NHBRC tracks enrolled projects and inspections. The Deeds Registry tracks title registrations. But these systems are not integrated. Nobody has a single view of where every project stands at every stage of the delivery chain. When a project stalls, identifying exactly where and why it stalled requires manual investigation across multiple institutional systems.

### Capability — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

The capability crisis in human settlements operates at every level of the value chain.

At the municipal level, many non-metropolitan municipalities lack qualified town planners, land surveyors, and project managers. Township establishment — the legal process required before housing construction can begin — is a technically complex process that requires specialised professional capacity. Municipalities that lack this capacity must outsource to consultants, adding cost and time. Bulk infrastructure provision requires engineering capacity that many smaller municipalities do not have. The result is that the municipal functions in the housing chain — land preparation, planning, infrastructure provision — become the bottleneck that delays the entire project.

At the provincial level, project management capacity varies enormously. Provinces must manage dozens or hundreds of housing projects simultaneously, each at a different stage of the delivery chain. This requires procurement specialists, contract managers, construction supervisors, and quality inspectors — skills that are scarce in the public sector. The Auditor-General has identified "lack of project management capacity" as a contributing factor to HSDG underspending in multiple provinces.

At the developer level, the quality of construction is inconsistent. The subsidised housing sector includes a mix of established construction companies and emerging developers. Emerging developers, while important for economic transformation, may lack the technical capacity and financial resources to deliver at the quality and scale required. The NHBRC's capacity to inspect every house in every project is limited — its inspection coverage is incomplete, and developers who deliver substandard work may not face consequences until well after the beneficiary has taken occupation.

:::expert
Research published by the Centre for Affordable Housing Finance in Africa has documented that South Africa's housing delivery model suffers from what the researchers term "capability mismatch at every node." Municipal planning departments lack the capacity to prepare land at the pace required by provincial delivery targets. Provincial project management units lack the capacity to manage the volume of projects in their pipelines. Developers lack the financial depth to absorb cost overruns when the subsidy quantum proves insufficient. And the NHBRC lacks the inspection capacity to ensure quality across the full volume of construction. The researchers found that the housing delivery model assumes institutional capabilities that do not exist at any level of the chain — making housing delivery failure a structural inevitability rather than a management aberration.
:::

### Legitimacy — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

Section 26 of the Constitution establishes the right of access to adequate housing. The Constitutional Court's landmark 2000 Grootboom judgment reinforced this right, holding that the government must devise and implement a comprehensive programme to progressively realise the right to housing, including providing relief for those in desperate need. The constitutional mandate is powerful and judicially enforceable.

But the gap between the constitutional mandate and the lived experience of housing delivery has eroded the institutional legitimacy of the human settlements system in the eyes of many beneficiaries. Communities that have waited decades for houses — that have seen projects announced, started, stalled, and abandoned — have lost confidence that the system will deliver. This legitimacy deficit manifests in land invasions (where communities occupy land illegally because they have given up on the formal process), service delivery protests (where housing delivery failures are a central grievance), and widespread distrust of municipal waiting lists (which communities believe are manipulated for political purposes).

The legitimacy of the formal housing system is further undermined by documented instances of housing subsidy allocation being influenced by local political relationships — a pattern identified across municipalities led by different parties, not limited to any one governing bloc. This perception of politicised allocation erodes the social legitimacy of the housing delivery system and drives the informal settlement growth that the system is supposed to address.

### Enforcement — Rating: 1 (Critical Failure)

Enforcement failure in human settlements is pervasive and consequential.

Developers who deliver substandard houses face inadequate consequences. The NHBRC warranty system is supposed to protect beneficiaries against structural defects, but beneficiaries who discover defects often face a lengthy and bureaucratic process to secure rectification. The NHBRC's enforcement capacity is limited relative to the volume of subsidised housing construction. In some documented cases, developers who delivered defective houses in one project were appointed to subsequent projects by the same provincial department — demonstrating that past quality failures do not automatically disqualify a developer from future work.

The title deed backlog is perhaps the most damning evidence of enforcement failure. **An estimated 900,000 or more subsidised housing beneficiaries have not received their title deeds** — many of them occupying houses completed years ago. This backlog has accumulated across multiple provincial administrations and across parties with different governing records, **indicating that the fragmented institutional design — not the competence of any individual officials or any one governing party — is the structural cause.** There is no automatic trigger that compels resolution of the backlog. No entity faces mandatory consequences for failing to register title deeds within a prescribed timeframe. *The backlog grows because there is no enforcement mechanism that makes it shrink.*

:::takeaway
Human settlements scores 1 — critical failure — on both responsibility and enforcement, and 1 on information. This is the lowest-scoring function in the RAARICLE™ diagnostic across the entire Reform Agenda series. The fragmentation is so extreme that it reaches a point of structural irrecoverability within the current institutional architecture. Incremental improvements — better coordination, more frequent meetings, shared databases — will not fix a value chain that is fundamentally designed to distribute responsibility to the point where nobody owns the outcome.
:::

## The Root Cause

The RAARICLE™ diagnosis confirms what the value chain mapping suggested: human settlements is a case of **maximum fragmentation**. Every RAARICLE™ element sits in a different institution. Responsibility is scattered across six or more entities. Accountability is diffused across three spheres. Authority is split between the municipality (land), the province (project management), and national government (policy and funding). Resources flow through different channels for different components of the same project. Information is fragmented across incompatible systems. Capability is insufficient at every level. And enforcement — the element that should hold the chain together — is virtually absent.

**This is not a coordination problem.** Coordination assumes that the institutional architecture is sound and the entities simply need to communicate better. The RAARICLE™ diagnosis reveals that *the architecture itself is the problem*. The housing value chain was designed — through the Housing Act, the Division of Revenue, and the functional assignments in the Constitution — in a way that distributes every element of governance across different institutions. No amount of coordination meetings, intergovernmental forums, or shared monitoring frameworks can overcome a design that structurally prevents end-to-end ownership.

The Level 2 metropolitan accreditation model provides the evidence. Metros with Level 2 accreditation manage the full housing delivery process internally — from project identification and developer appointment through construction management and subsidy disbursement. They still depend on the Deeds Registry for title registration, but the core delivery chain is consolidated in a single entity. While metro housing delivery is far from perfect, the metros' ability to manage the process end-to-end eliminates the inter-sphere handoff problems that plague non-metropolitan delivery. The accreditation model is proof of concept that consolidation works.

## The Reform Blueprint

The structural prescription is clear: consolidate the housing delivery chain into a single institutional owner. The entity that identifies the land must also manage the construction, ensure the quality, and register the title deed. End-to-end ownership is not a luxury — it is the minimum design requirement for a function this complex.

### The Core Design: Municipal Housing Delivery Agencies

The reform proposes that municipalities — or, for municipalities that lack the capacity, regional housing delivery agencies serving clusters of municipalities — become the single point of end-to-end ownership for subsidised housing delivery. This extends the Level 2 metropolitan accreditation model to all municipalities, with institutional support for those that cannot manage the process alone.

For metropolitan municipalities and capable secondary cities, the model is straightforward: the municipality receives the housing conditional grant directly, manages the full project cycle (land, infrastructure, construction, quality assurance, title registration), and is held accountable for delivery outcomes through binding performance agreements.

For smaller municipalities that lack the institutional capacity to manage the full process, the reform creates Regional Housing Delivery Agencies — entities that serve clusters of adjacent municipalities. Each regional agency is governed by a board comprising the participating municipalities and is staffed with the professional capacity (town planners, project managers, engineers, conveyancers) needed to manage the delivery chain. The regional agency receives the conditional grant, manages projects on behalf of participating municipalities, and is accountable for delivery and title deed registration.

In both models, the provincial department of human settlements loses its project management function. The province retains a monitoring and oversight role — assessing municipal and regional agency performance, providing technical support, and escalating to national government when capacity is critically deficient. But the province no longer approves projects, appoints developers, or manages subsidy disbursement. These functions are consolidated at the municipal or regional level.

### Element-by-Element Design Rules

**Responsibility:** One entity — the municipal housing agency or regional delivery agency — owns the housing delivery chain from land identification through title deed registration. The agency identifies land, procures developers, manages construction, ensures NHBRC compliance, and manages the title deed registration process. The handoff points that currently exist between municipality, province, developer, and deeds registry are reduced to a single management relationship.

**Accountability:** The municipal manager (for metro agencies) or the regional agency CEO signs a binding performance agreement with specific, measurable delivery indicators: number of units completed, percentage of units meeting NHBRC standards, percentage of title deeds registered within 12 months of practical completion, and beneficiary satisfaction scores. The performance agreement includes defined consequences for non-achievement.

**Authority:** The delivery agency passes the 30-day authority test for all core functions. The agency can initiate a land acquisition process using delegated powers. The agency can appoint a developer through its own procurement process within prescribed timelines. The agency can terminate a developer contract for poor performance without requiring provincial approval. The agency can instruct a conveyancer to lodge title deeds without waiting for provincial authorisation. The critical path is under single-entity control.

**Resources:** The HSDG and USDG are consolidated into a single Human Settlements Delivery Grant, allocated directly to municipal housing agencies and regional delivery agencies on a formula basis that accounts for the housing backlog, available land, institutional capacity, and delivery track record. The grant covers the full project cost — land, infrastructure, top structure, professional fees, and title deed registration — eliminating the current fragmentation where top structures are funded through one instrument and bulk infrastructure through another. The agency manages an integrated project budget rather than navigating multiple funding streams with different rules.

**Information:** A national Housing Delivery Platform tracks every project from registration through to title deed completion. The platform is mandatory for all agencies receiving the consolidated grant. It captures project milestones in real time — land acquisition, planning approval, infrastructure completion, construction milestones, NHBRC inspection outcomes, and title deed registration status. The platform replaces the fragmented municipal waiting lists with a standardised, verified national housing demand register. Beneficiary eligibility is verified against the HSS and the population register to eliminate duplicates and deceased records. The platform is publicly accessible — any citizen can check the status of any housing project in their municipality.

**Capability:** Regional delivery agencies are staffed with prescribed minimum professional complements — registered town planners, professional engineers, project managers with prescribed qualifications, and conveyancing attorneys. A national Housing Delivery Academy provides training and professional development for agency staff. For municipal agencies, the capability requirements are the same but are met through the municipality's existing professional establishment supplemented by targeted recruitment. A capacity assessment must be completed before an agency can receive the consolidated grant — agencies that do not meet the minimum capability threshold receive transitional support from a national Housing Support Unit until their capability is built.

**Legitimacy:** Municipal housing agencies derive their mandate from the Housing Act and municipal council approval. Regional delivery agencies are established through inter-municipal agreements authorised by the relevant provincial legislation. Community participation is maintained through project-level beneficiary committees and the existing ward committee structures. The shift from provincial to municipal/regional management aligns housing delivery with the sphere of government closest to the beneficiary — strengthening the democratic accountability link.

**Enforcement:** Automatic triggers address the two most persistent enforcement failures in human settlements.

For construction quality: every house built under the consolidated grant must be inspected by the NHBRC before final payment to the developer. If a developer fails NHBRC inspection on more than 15 per cent of units in a project, the developer's NHBRC registration is automatically suspended pending rectification. No provincial or municipal official can override this trigger — it is built into the grant conditions and the NHBRC's regulatory framework.

For title deed registration: a title deed must be registered within 12 months of practical completion. If an agency's title deed registration rate falls below 80 per cent for a financial year, the agency receives mandatory support from the national Housing Support Unit to clear the backlog. If the rate remains below 80 per cent for two consecutive years, the agency's grant allocation is conditionally suspended — funds are held in escrow until the title deed backlog is resolved.

:::expert
The Centre for Affordable Housing Finance in Africa has analysed the performance differential between Level 2 accredited metropolitan municipalities and non-accredited municipalities in housing delivery. Their research shows that metros managing the full delivery chain internally achieve higher completion rates, faster delivery timelines, and better title deed registration outcomes than projects managed through the traditional province-to-municipality handoff model. The performance differential is not attributable to metro municipalities being inherently better managed — it reflects the structural advantage of consolidated ownership. When one entity controls the land, the infrastructure, the construction process, and the title registration, the coordination failures that plague the fragmented model are eliminated by design.
:::

### Anti-Capture Protections

Political capture is addressed through mandatory competitive procurement for developer appointment. The current system, where provincial MECs and municipal politicians can influence developer selection, is replaced by a transparent procurement process managed by the agency's professional staff, with prescribed evaluation criteria and independent evaluation panel participation. Beneficiary selection must follow the verified national housing demand register — eliminating the opportunity for political manipulation of waiting lists.

Commercial capture is addressed through the integrated procurement transparency system. All contracts above a defined threshold are published on the national Housing Delivery Platform. Developer performance — completion rates, quality inspection outcomes, cost overruns — is publicly visible. Developers with documented quality failures are automatically flagged in the system and cannot be appointed to new projects until rectification is complete.

Labour capture is managed through the agency's professional staffing requirements. Critical positions — CEO, chief town planner, project director — must be filled through merit-based processes with prescribed minimum qualifications specified in the agency's establishment framework. These requirements are binding, not advisory.

Regulatory capture is addressed through the NHBRC's structural independence from the delivery chain. The NHBRC inspects and certifies — it does not participate in project management. The agency cannot influence the NHBRC's inspection outcomes. The automatic enforcement trigger (suspension upon excessive inspection failures) ensures that the agency has no incentive to compromise quality.

Information capture is addressed through the public accessibility of the national Housing Delivery Platform. Project status, delivery timelines, quality inspection outcomes, and title deed registration rates are visible to all stakeholders — beneficiaries, communities, oversight bodies, media, and civil society. The agency cannot control the narrative about its performance because the data is publicly available in real time.

:::framework
**The Housing Delivery Chain Audit**

Use this framework to trace a housing project from approval to occupation and identify exactly where the governance chain breaks — whether you are a beneficiary waiting for a house, a community leader advocating for delivery, or a citizen trying to understand why housing targets are consistently missed.

**Step 1: Identify every entity involved in your housing project.**

For a typical subsidised housing project, list the entities: the national Department of Human Settlements (subsidy framework and conditional grant), the provincial department (project approval, developer appointment, grant management), the municipality (land identification, zoning, bulk infrastructure — water, sewerage, roads — and building plan approval), the developer or contractor (construction), the National Home Builders Registration Council (construction quality assurance), and the Deeds Registry (title deed registration). That is at least six entities across three spheres. Write them down and note which RAARICLE™ element each one holds.

**Step 2: Ask who owns the outcome end-to-end.**

For the project you have identified, ask: if the beneficiary moves into a house with cracking walls, no water connection, and no title deed, which single entity is answerable? If the answer is "it depends on which part failed," you have confirmed the fragmentation problem. No entity owns the outcome from project approval to a satisfied, title-holding occupant.

**Step 3: Trace the title deed.**

South Africa has a backlog of hundreds of thousands of unregistered title deeds for subsidised housing. For any completed housing project in your area, check whether beneficiaries have received their title deeds. If they have not, identify the bottleneck: is it the municipality (which must provide a compliant township register), the provincial department (which must process the subsidy and conveyancing), or the Deeds Registry (which must register the transfer)? Title deed failure is the clearest indicator of fragmentation — it requires multiple entities to complete a sequential process, and a failure at any point blocks the entire chain.

**Step 4: Check construction quality.**

Has the NHBRC inspected the houses? Were defects identified? If defects were found, what enforcement action followed — was the developer required to rectify at their own cost, or did the defects persist with no consequence? If developers can deliver substandard houses and still receive payment for the next project, the enforcement element has failed.

**Step 5: Follow the housing money.**

Identify whether the Human Settlements Development Grant (the main conditional grant for subsidised housing) was fully spent in your province in the most recent financial year. If it was underspent, identify why: was it a land availability problem (municipal responsibility), a procurement delay (provincial responsibility), or a developer capacity issue? Underspending on housing grants while the waiting list grows is a direct indicator of authority and capability failures in the delivery chain.

**Step 6: Assess bulk infrastructure coordination.**

A house without water, sewerage, and road access is not a habitable dwelling. Check whether the municipality's bulk infrastructure programme is aligned with the provincial housing delivery schedule. If houses are built before bulk infrastructure is in place — or if bulk infrastructure is installed but no houses follow — you have identified a coordination failure between the municipal and provincial programmes. This is one of the most common causes of housing project delays and the clearest example of what happens when resources and authority are split across spheres.

This audit gives you the evidence base to engage your ward councillor, your provincial MEC for human settlements, or organisations like the Housing Development Agency — with a precise map of where the delivery chain is broken, not just a complaint that houses are not being built.
:::

## Implementation Pathway

The housing delivery reform is constitutionally feasible but politically complex. Housing is listed in Schedule 4 as a concurrent function between national and provincial government, with local government having a role in municipal planning and building regulations (Schedule 4 Part B). The reform does not remove housing from the concurrent space — it shifts the operational delivery function from provinces to municipalities and regional agencies, which is achievable through legislative amendment to the Housing Act and the Division of Revenue framework.

The institutional complexity is real. Provincial departments of human settlements will face institutional incentives to retain the project management function, which currently carries significant administrative authority over developer appointments and subsidy disbursements. Any decentralisation shifts power and oversight to municipalities and regional agencies. Addressing this resistance requires clear legislative mandates, transparent performance thresholds, and transitional support for provincial staff moving into the new regional delivery structures.

### Phase 1: Legislative and Institutional Foundation (Year 1-2)

The Housing Act must be amended to establish the municipal delivery agency and regional delivery agency models as the primary institutional framework for subsidised housing delivery. The amendment must prescribe the delegation of authority from provinces to agencies, the governance structures for regional agencies, the minimum professional staffing requirements, and the conditions under which the consolidated Human Settlements Delivery Grant is allocated.

The Division of Revenue framework must be amended to consolidate the HSDG and USDG into a single grant allocated directly to delivery agencies. This requires National Treasury's cooperation and amendment of the annual Division of Revenue Act to reflect the new grant structure.

The NHBRC Act must be amended to introduce automatic enforcement triggers — mandatory suspension of developer registration upon excessive inspection failures, and mandatory rectification obligations with prescribed timelines and consequences.

A national Housing Delivery Platform must be commissioned and developed. The platform design should be informed by the existing HSS but built as a new system with real-time project tracking, verified beneficiary registration, and public accessibility. The platform is a prerequisite for the pilot phase — without it, the information element of the reform cannot function.

### Phase 2: Pilot Municipalities (Year 2-4)

The consolidated model should be piloted in six to eight municipalities: two or three metros (extending their existing Level 2 accreditation to the full model with integrated funding and automatic enforcement), two or three capable secondary cities (testing the municipal agency model), and two or three clusters of smaller municipalities (testing the regional delivery agency model).

The pilot period should be at least two years — enough time to take a project from inception through to title deed registration. Independent evaluation must assess whether the pilot agencies deliver faster, at higher quality, with better title deed registration rates, and at comparable or lower cost per unit than the traditional provincial-to-municipality model. The evaluation must also assess whether the anti-capture protections function as designed — whether developer appointment is genuinely competitive, whether beneficiary selection follows the verified register, and whether NHBRC enforcement triggers are activated when warranted.

### Phase 3: National Rollout (Year 4-7)

Based on pilot evaluation, the model is extended nationally. The rollout must be phased — metros and capable municipalities transition first, while regional delivery agencies are established and capacitated for smaller municipalities. The provincial department's role is formally redefined as monitoring, oversight, and technical support. Provincial staff with project management skills are offered positions in the new agencies — preventing skills loss and addressing provincial concerns about staff displacement.

The national rollout period is longer than for education or healthcare reform (five to seven years rather than three to five) because the institutional restructuring is more complex. Establishing regional delivery agencies, building the national platform, and capacitating municipal agencies requires sustained investment in institutional development. But **the housing backlog — growing by an estimated 178,000 units annually while delivery stagnates** — means that the cost of delay is measured in decades of unmet housing need. Every year the current fragmented model continues is a year in which the backlog grows and the beneficiaries on the waiting list wait longer.

:::takeaway
The housing reform requires legislative amendment to the Housing Act and the Division of Revenue, not a constitutional change. The core design — municipal or regional delivery agencies with end-to-end ownership, consolidated funding, and automatic enforcement — is constitutionally feasible but politically contested. The implementation timeline is five to seven years, reflecting the complexity of establishing new institutional entities. But the alternative — continuing with a fragmented system that has produced a backlog of over two million units and 900,000 unregistered title deeds — is worse.
:::

The three articles in Part 2 of this series have diagnosed the three most important concurrent national functions — education, healthcare, and human settlements — and proposed reform blueprints for each. The structural prescription is consistent across all three: co-locate authority with accountability at the level closest to the service user, ring-fence resources for the function, build real-time information systems, and replace discretionary enforcement with automatic triggers. In Part 3, we turn to provincial government itself — examining the structural crisis of the provincial sphere and asking whether the design of the nine provinces can be reformed to make concurrent functions work.
