## The Reform Blueprint

<!-- Section: Proposed institutional architecture for concurrent functions that passes all eight RAARICLE tests -->

:::framework
**The Concurrent Function Design Test**

Use this framework to evaluate any proposal to restructure a concurrent function — whether it comes from government, a political party, or civil society — by testing whether the proposed design co-locates the eight RAARICLE elements or merely rearranges the same fragmentation.

**Step 1: Identify the concurrent function and its current design.**

Name the Schedule 4 function (education, health, housing, environment, public transport, etc.) and describe its current institutional architecture. Which entity sets the policy? Which entity delivers the service? Which entity controls the budget? Which entity employs the staff? Which entity enforces compliance? Map the current distribution of all eight RAARICLE elements across spheres.

**Step 2: Apply the 30-day authority test to the current design.**

Pick a concrete operational decision — hiring a teacher, procuring medicine, approving a housing project, fixing a provincial road. Ask: can the entity responsible for delivery execute this decision within 30 days using only its own authority, budget, and staff? If not, identify every external dependency that creates delay: national salary negotiations, conditional grant conditions, procurement thresholds requiring Treasury approval, national frameworks that constrain provincial discretion. Each dependency is an authority leak.

**Step 3: Evaluate the proposed reform against all eight elements.**

For each proposed reform model, ask these eight questions: (1) Does one entity own the function end-to-end? (2) Is one person accountable for a measurable outcome? (3) Can that entity act within 30 days using its own powers? (4) Does revenue flow directly to the responsible entity? (5) Does the entity have real-time operational data? (6) Are critical posts filled by qualified, permanent staff? (7) Is the entity's mandate clearly recognised? (8) Are there automatic triggers and pre-defined consequences for failure? Score each question yes or no.

**Step 4: Test for unintended consequences.**

Ask whether the proposed reform creates new problems: Does full devolution to provinces risk widening the performance gap between well-resourced and poorly-resourced provinces? Does centralisation into a national agency remove democratic accountability at the provincial level? Does creating a new entity add another layer of fragmentation rather than reducing it? The best reform reduces the total number of entities and handoff points while preserving democratic accountability and regulatory independence.

**Step 5: Check constitutional feasibility.**

Determine whether the proposed reform can be achieved through ordinary legislation (an Act of Parliament, requiring a simple or special majority), through intergovernmental agreements under the Intergovernmental Relations Framework Act, or whether it requires a constitutional amendment (requiring a two-thirds majority in the National Assembly and support of six of nine provinces in the NCOP). Reforms that can be achieved legislatively are more feasible than those requiring constitutional change — but even legislative reform requires political will and stakeholder alignment.

This test ensures that reform proposals are evaluated on structural merit rather than political rhetoric — and that citizens can distinguish between genuine co-location reforms and cosmetic reorganisations that leave the underlying fragmentation intact.
:::
