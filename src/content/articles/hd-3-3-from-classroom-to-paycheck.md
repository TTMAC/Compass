## The Bottom Line, Up Front

South Africa's expanded unemployment rate exceeds 33 per cent. Among young people aged 15 to 34, it exceeds 45 per cent. These are among the highest unemployment rates in the world for a country not experiencing war or state collapse. The conventional explanation — that there are not enough jobs — is true but incomplete. The deeper explanation is that the economy cannot produce the kinds of jobs that would absorb the available labour force, because the education system does not produce the kinds of workers that a modern economy requires.

This is not a cyclical problem that will resolve when the business cycle turns. It is a structural problem embedded in the relationship between what schools produce and what the economy needs. The economy demands workers who can read technical manuals, operate sophisticated machinery, write software, manage supply chains, analyse data, and solve problems. The education system, as [The Schooling Paradox](/articles/hd-3-1-the-schooling-paradox) documented, produces a majority of learners who cannot read for meaning by Grade 4. The gap between the demand for skills and the supply of skills is not a gap that job creation programmes, wage subsidies, or expanded public works can close. It is a gap that only a fundamentally better education system can close.

The international evidence on this point is unambiguous. The economists Eric Hanushek and Ludger Woessmann, in research spanning two decades and covering virtually every country with available data, have demonstrated that cognitive skills — not years of schooling, not credentials, not spending — are the education variable that predicts economic growth. A one-standard-deviation improvement in a country's average test scores on international assessments is associated with approximately 2 percentage points of additional annual GDP growth. For South Africa, which has been growing at roughly 1 per cent per year in per capita terms, a 2-percentage-point increase would be transformational. But it requires an education system that actually produces cognitive skills — not one that merely produces years of seat-time.

This article traces the pipeline from classroom to paycheck. It documents the evidence on how education quality drives economic growth, explains why South Africa's skills mismatch produces structural unemployment, examines the concept of economic complexity and its dependence on human capital, compares South Africa's trajectory with countries that invested in education and then grew, and connects the education-growth link to the reinforcing cycle that this entire series tracks. The argument is not that education is the only driver of economic growth. It is that education is the driver without which the other drivers — investment, infrastructure, institutions, trade — cannot produce sustained, inclusive growth.

## The Education-Growth Evidence

The relationship between education and economic growth has been studied for over half a century, and the findings have evolved significantly. The early human capital theory, developed by Gary Becker, Theodore Schultz, and Jacob Mincer in the 1960s, treated education primarily as an investment in productive capacity — years of schooling increase a worker's productivity, which increases output, which drives growth. This framework generated the simple prediction that more years of schooling should produce more economic growth. The HDI's education component is built on this logic.

The problem is that the prediction does not hold. When Hanushek and Woessmann assembled data across countries and decades, they found that years of schooling — the variable that the HDI measures — has essentially no independent effect on economic growth once cognitive skills are controlled for. Countries with the same average years of schooling but different levels of cognitive skills (measured by international assessment scores) have dramatically different growth trajectories. Years of schooling without learning is, from a growth perspective, economically inert.

The variable that does predict growth is cognitive skills. Hanushek and Woessmann's research, published in a series of papers and summarised in their 2015 book "The Knowledge Capital of Nations," uses test score data from international assessments (PISA, TIMSS, PIRLS) as a proxy for the cognitive skills of a country's population. Their central finding: a one-standard-deviation increase in average test scores is associated with approximately 2 percentage points of additional annual GDP growth over the long term. This relationship holds after controlling for initial income levels, institutional quality, geographic factors, and other variables that might confound the analysis.

To put the 2-percentage-point figure in context: South Africa's GDP growth rate over the past decade has averaged approximately 1 per cent in per capita terms. If South Africa's education system produced cognitive skills at the level of, say, Turkey (which scores approximately one standard deviation higher on international assessments), the implied growth increment of 2 percentage points would more than triple the per capita growth rate. Over a generation — 25 years — the compound effect would be transformational: per capita income roughly 65 per cent higher than under the current trajectory.

This is not a magical thinking exercise. It is a direct implication of the empirical evidence, applied to South Africa's specific circumstances. And it highlights the extraordinary economic cost of the schooling paradox: every year that the education system fails to produce cognitive skills is a year of forgone economic growth that compounds into permanently lower income.

:::expert[Hanushek and Woessmann Research Programme]
The Hanushek-Woessmann research programme, spanning publications from 2007 to the present, has fundamentally reshaped the economics of education by shifting the focus from years of schooling to learning quality. Their methodological contribution was to assemble a cross-country database of cognitive skill measures (drawn from international assessments conducted since the 1960s) and link it to long-run economic growth data. The robustness of their central finding — that cognitive skills, not years of schooling, predict growth — has been tested against multiple alternative specifications, including controlling for institutional quality, trade openness, property rights, and other growth determinants. The finding survives all of these controls. Their interpretation is that cognitive skills capture the actual human capital of a population — the ability to acquire and apply knowledge productively — in a way that years of schooling do not. For South Africa, the implication is stark: the country's education spending, which is high by global standards, is generating a return on growth that is far below what the spending level would predict, because the spending is not being converted into cognitive skills.
:::

## Economic Complexity and Human Capital

The concept of economic complexity, developed by Ricardo Hausmann and César Hidalgo at Harvard's Center for International Development, provides a complementary lens on the education-growth relationship. The Economic Complexity Index (ECI) measures the diversity and sophistication of a country's export basket — the idea being that complex economies produce a wide variety of sophisticated products, which requires a wide variety of sophisticated productive capabilities, which in turn requires a well-educated, skilled workforce.

The empirical relationship between economic complexity and prosperity is strong. Countries with high ECI — Germany, Japan, South Korea, Switzerland — tend to be wealthy. Countries with low ECI — commodity exporters, countries with narrow export baskets — tend to be less wealthy. And critically, ECI predicts future growth: countries that are more complex than their current income level would suggest tend to grow faster, as if converging toward the income level that their productive sophistication implies.

South Africa's ECI ranking is instructive. Despite being classified as an upper-middle-income country, South Africa's economic complexity is relatively low — its export basket is dominated by minerals (gold, platinum, iron ore, coal), vehicles (largely assembled from imported components), and basic agricultural products. The country's ECI has been declining over the past two decades, meaning that its productive structure is becoming less complex over time, not more. This is the opposite of the trajectory followed by successful growth stories: South Korea's ECI increased dramatically between 1960 and 2000 as it moved from rice and textiles to semiconductors and automobiles; China's ECI has increased steadily since the 1980s as it moved from basic manufactures to electronics, machinery, and now green technology.

The connection to education is direct. Complex products require complex productive capabilities. Complex capabilities require educated, skilled workers. You cannot manufacture semiconductor chips, develop financial software, design pharmaceutical molecules, or engineer renewable energy systems without a workforce that has the mathematical, scientific, and technical knowledge to do these things. The productive capabilities that define economic complexity are, in their most fundamental form, the cognitive capabilities of the people who do the work.

South Africa's declining economic complexity is, in this framework, a downstream consequence of its education failure. The economy cannot diversify into complex products because it lacks the human capital to produce them. The automotive sector — South Africa's most successful manufacturing export — illustrates the point: the country can assemble vehicles because assembly requires moderate skills that can be developed through firm-level training, but it cannot manufacture the most sophisticated components (electronic control systems, advanced materials, precision instruments) because these require the kind of deep STEM knowledge that the education system does not produce at scale.

The Atlas of Economic Complexity, an interactive tool produced by Harvard's Growth Lab, maps the "product space" — the network of products and the capabilities required to produce them. Products that require similar capabilities cluster together, and countries can most easily diversify into products that are near their current capabilities. South Africa's position in the product space shows significant potential for diversification — the country has capabilities adjacent to products it does not currently export competitively — but actualising that potential requires filling the human capital gaps that prevent the leap from current production to adjacent-possible production.

:::takeaway
Economic complexity — the diversity and sophistication of what a country produces — is a powerful predictor of prosperity and growth. South Africa's economic complexity is low and declining. The constraint is human capital: the economy cannot produce complex goods and services because the education system does not produce workers with the requisite capabilities. Education reform is not just a social policy. It is an industrial policy.
:::

## The Skills Mismatch

The abstract connection between education and economic complexity becomes concrete in the labour market, where the mismatch between the skills that employers demand and the skills that the education system supplies produces one of the defining features of the South African economy: simultaneous mass unemployment and critical skills shortages.

South Africa has approximately 3.5 million people aged 15 to 24 who are not in employment, education, or training — the so-called NEET category. These are young people who have left the education system without the skills to find employment and who are not in any form of training that might develop those skills. The NEET rate among this age group exceeds 40 per cent, one of the highest in the world.

At the same time, South African employers report persistent shortages in specific skill categories. The Department of Home Affairs' Critical Skills List identifies occupations where demand exceeds domestic supply — and the list reads like a catalogue of the capabilities that a modern economy requires: engineers (civil, electrical, mechanical, chemical), ICT professionals (software developers, data scientists, cybersecurity specialists), healthcare professionals (doctors, pharmacists, specialist nurses), artisans (electricians, plumbers, welders, toolmakers), financial professionals (actuaries, quantitative analysts), and natural scientists (geologists, physicists, chemists).

The paradox of simultaneous unemployment and skills shortages is not actually a paradox. It is the predictable outcome of an education system that produces the wrong skills — or, more precisely, that fails to produce skills at all for the majority of learners and produces the wrong balance of skills among the minority who do learn. The economy needs 30,000 new artisans per year; the TVET system produces fewer than 15,000. The economy needs tens of thousands of STEM-literate graduates; the university system produces approximately 30,000 STEM graduates per year, many of whom emigrate. The economy needs a workforce that can read technical documentation, perform quantitative analysis, and solve novel problems; the education system produces a majority who cannot read for meaning.

The skills mismatch is not evenly distributed across the economy. It is concentrated in precisely the sectors that the growth strategy documented in [The Efficiency Transition](/articles/eg-2-3-the-efficiency-transition) and [Innovation-Driven Growth](/articles/eg-2-4-innovation-driven-growth) identifies as critical for South Africa's economic future: manufacturing, ICT, green energy, financial services, and healthcare. These are the sectors that create well-paying jobs, generate exports, and drive productivity growth. And these are the sectors where the education system most catastrophically fails to produce the workers the economy needs.

**The education-employability gradient.** Within South Africa's labour market, there is a stark gradient between education level and employment probability. For adults with a university degree, the unemployment rate is approximately 10 per cent — high by international standards but manageable. For adults with matric (Grade 12) only, the unemployment rate is approximately 35 per cent. For adults without matric, it exceeds 50 per cent. Each step down the education ladder roughly doubles the unemployment rate.

But this gradient overstates the protective effect of education, because it counts credentials rather than capabilities. A matric certificate from a well-functioning System A school represents a meaningfully different level of capability than a matric certificate from a dysfunctional System B school, even though both carry the same formal qualification. Employers know this, which is why many use the school attended — not just the qualification obtained — as a screening criterion. The credential has been devalued by the quality gap, and the labour market responds accordingly.

:::expert[Development Policy Research Unit (DPRU), University of Cape Town]
DPRU's research on structural unemployment in South Africa, using data from the Quarterly Labour Force Survey and the Post-Apartheid Labour Market Series, has demonstrated that the skills mismatch is not a temporary phenomenon but a structural feature of the economy that has persisted for over two decades. Their analysis shows that the sectors creating the most jobs are not the sectors experiencing the most growth — a pattern that indicates that the growing sectors cannot find workers with the right skills and therefore cannot expand employment as fast as their output growth would otherwise allow. DPRU's decomposition of unemployment by education level, field of study, and geographic location reveals that the mismatch is most severe for young people (aged 15-34), for people without post-secondary qualifications, and for people in former homeland areas — precisely the demographics most affected by the System B education crisis documented in the previous articles. Their policy recommendation is that addressing unemployment requires simultaneous action on both the demand side (economic growth, job creation) and the supply side (education reform, skills development) — because growth alone will create jobs that the current workforce cannot fill, and education reform alone will produce graduates into an economy that is not creating enough jobs.
:::

## Structural Unemployment: The Education Dimension

South Africa's unemployment is routinely described as "structural" rather than "cyclical." Understanding what that distinction means, and why education is at the centre of it, is essential for understanding why conventional economic stimuli — lower interest rates, government spending programmes, public works — have failed to make a significant dent in the unemployment rate.

Cyclical unemployment is unemployment that rises during economic downturns and falls during recoveries. It responds to macroeconomic policy — fiscal stimulus, monetary easing — because it is caused by temporary shortfalls in aggregate demand. When the economy recovers, the jobs come back, because the unemployed workers have the skills the economy needs.

Structural unemployment is unemployment that persists even when the economy is growing. It does not respond to macroeconomic policy, because it is not caused by insufficient demand. It is caused by a mismatch between the skills of the unemployed and the skills that the economy requires. The jobs exist — or would exist if firms could find workers to fill them — but the unemployed workers do not have the skills to fill them. No amount of macroeconomic stimulus can close a skills gap.

South Africa's unemployment has remained above 25 per cent (narrow definition) through economic booms and busts, through commodity super-cycles and commodity crashes, through fiscal expansion and fiscal consolidation. It did not fall below 20 per cent even during the commodity boom of 2003-2008, when the economy was growing at 4-5 per cent per year and the global economy was providing maximum tailwinds. This persistence through varying economic conditions is the signature of structural unemployment.

The education dimension of structural unemployment operates through several channels.

**The threshold effect.** For many jobs in the formal economy, there is a minimum skill threshold below which a worker is simply not employable — not because the employer is discriminating, but because the job genuinely requires skills the worker does not have. An electrician who cannot read a wiring diagram cannot wire a building. A bank teller who cannot perform basic arithmetic cannot handle transactions. A factory worker who cannot read safety instructions is a liability. The education system's failure to produce basic literacy and numeracy in the majority of learners pushes millions of people below this threshold, rendering them unemployable in the formal economy regardless of the state of labour demand.

**The technology channel.** The global economy is becoming more skill-intensive over time, as automation and digital technology replace routine tasks and increase demand for non-routine cognitive skills. This trend — well-documented in the labour economics literature and accelerating with developments in artificial intelligence — means that the minimum skill threshold for employment is rising, not falling. A worker who lacks digital literacy, quantitative reasoning, or the ability to learn new tasks is increasingly unemployable, not just in South Africa but globally. South Africa's education system is not preparing learners for the economy of 2024, let alone the economy of 2034.

**The geographic channel.** South Africa's spatial economy — a legacy of apartheid-era spatial planning — concentrates economic activity in a few urban centres while distributing the population across former homelands and township peripheries. Workers in these areas face not only skills barriers but physical barriers to employment: the distance from home to workplace, the cost of transport, and the time required for commuting all reduce the effective return to employment and increase the reservation wage (the minimum wage at which a person is willing to work). The education system compounds the geographic mismatch because the worst schools are in the areas furthest from economic opportunity — the former homelands and township peripheries where spatial disadvantage and educational disadvantage reinforce each other.

## The TVET Question

If the education-growth link depends on cognitive skills, and if the skills mismatch is the proximate cause of structural unemployment, then the obvious policy response is to fix the pipeline between education and employment. And the institution that is supposed to serve as that pipeline — the Technical and Vocational Education and Training (TVET) system — is, in South Africa, a case study in missed potential.

South Africa has 50 public TVET colleges, enrolling approximately 700,000 students. These colleges are supposed to produce the middle-skill workers that the economy most desperately needs: artisans, technicians, IT professionals, hospitality workers, office administrators. In the growth framework documented in [The Efficiency Transition](/articles/eg-2-3-the-efficiency-transition), TVET graduates are the workforce backbone of the manufacturing scale-up, the services expansion, and the infrastructure maintenance that Stage 2 growth requires.

The reality falls far short of this aspiration. TVET college throughput rates — the proportion of enrolled students who complete their qualifications — are dismally low. For the National Certificate (Vocational), the flagship TVET qualification, completion rates in many programmes are below 30 per cent. A majority of students who enrol in TVET programmes do not complete them. Of those who do complete, a significant proportion remain unemployed — the Post-School Education and Training monitoring data shows that TVET graduate employment rates, while better than for those with no post-school qualification, are far below what a well-functioning TVET system should produce.

The problems are structural. First, employer disconnect: TVET curricula are designed by education bureaucrats with limited input from employers. The qualifications that students earn do not map cleanly onto the skills that employers need. Second, inadequate practical training: TVET is supposed to combine classroom theory with practical workplace experience, but the workplace experience component is chronically underdeveloped. Many TVET students complete their theoretical modules but cannot find workplace placements to complete their qualifications. Third, quality of instruction: TVET college lecturers are often former teachers without industry experience, teaching theoretical content from textbooks rather than practical skills from workplaces. Fourth, stigma: TVET is widely perceived as a second-class option for learners who failed to gain university admission, rather than as a respected pathway to skilled employment. This perception discourages high-performing learners from choosing TVET and makes it harder for TVET graduates to compete in the labour market.

**The German comparator** is instructive because it shows what a well-functioning TVET system looks like. Germany's "dual system" (duales Ausbildungssystem) is the most successful vocational education model in the world. Approximately 500,000 young Germans enter the dual system each year. They split their time between classroom instruction at a vocational school (Berufsschule) and practical training at a company (Ausbildungsbetrieb). The curriculum is co-designed by employers and educational institutions, ensuring that graduates have the specific skills that the labour market demands. Companies bear a significant portion of the training cost — they pay apprentices a salary during training — because they view apprenticeships as an investment in their future workforce. The result: German youth unemployment is consistently below 7 per cent, and German manufacturing is among the most productive in the world.

**South Korea's transformation** provides a different but equally relevant comparator. In the 1960s, South Korea was poorer than many African countries. Its education system was weak, its workforce was unskilled, and its economy was dependent on rice and light textiles. Over the following four decades, South Korea implemented a systematic education strategy that prioritised STEM education at every level — primary, secondary, technical, and tertiary. The government invested heavily in teacher quality, particularly in mathematics and science. It expanded technical education through a network of polytechnics and technical high schools. And it linked educational investment directly to industrial policy, ensuring that the skills produced by the education system matched the skills required by the targeted industrial sectors. The result: South Korea's per capita income increased from approximately $1,000 in 1960 to over $30,000 today, one of the most dramatic economic transformations in human history.

:::expert[Human Resource Development Council of South Africa (HRDCSA)]
The HRDCSA, established to coordinate human resource development strategy across government departments, has produced detailed analyses of the TVET system's failures. Their research identifies the employer-education disconnect as the central problem: TVET qualifications are not designed in partnership with employers, practical training components are inadequate, and the signals that TVET qualifications send to the labour market are weak. The HRDCSA's comparative analysis of South Africa's TVET system with Germany's dual system, Australia's competency-based training, and South Korea's technical education model identifies common features of successful systems that South Africa lacks: mandatory employer co-design of curricula, structured workplace learning integrated into qualifications, quality assurance driven by employer assessment of graduate competence, and a funding model that incentivises employer participation. The HRDCSA has recommended a fundamental restructuring of the TVET system around these principles, but implementation has been slow, hampered by institutional fragmentation, insufficient employer engagement, and the sheer scale of the transformation required.
:::

## From Education to Productivity

The macroeconomic evidence — Hanushek and Woessmann's cross-country research, the economic complexity literature — establishes the connection between education quality and economic growth at the country level. But the connection also operates at the microeconomic level, within firms, in ways that are directly relevant to South Africa's growth prospects.

Total factor productivity (TFP) — the efficiency with which an economy converts inputs (labour, capital) into output — is the ultimate driver of long-run economic growth. Countries that sustain high TFP growth become wealthy. Countries that do not, do not. And human capital — the skills, knowledge, and capabilities of the workforce — is a primary determinant of TFP.

The mechanisms through which human capital drives TFP are well-understood. Educated workers are more productive in direct production — they make fewer errors, learn new tasks faster, and adapt more readily to technological change. Educated workers are better at adopting and implementing new technologies, which is a key channel through which TFP grows. Educated managers are better at organising production, allocating resources, and making strategic decisions. And educated entrepreneurs are more likely to identify opportunities, innovate, and create new firms that contribute to productivity growth.

The microeconomic evidence from South Africa specifically supports these mechanisms. Research using firm-level data from the World Bank Enterprise Surveys and the South African Revenue Service's tax records shows that firms with more educated workforces — holding capital, sector, and other factors constant — are more productive. The productivity premium for education is particularly large for firms in technology-intensive sectors, where the ability to adopt and adapt new technologies depends critically on workforce skills.

But the microeconomic evidence also reveals the dark side of the education-productivity relationship. Firms respond to the skills environment they face. When the education system does not produce skilled workers, firms adapt — by substituting capital for labour (automating tasks that would otherwise require skilled workers, which reduces employment), by importing skills (hiring foreign workers to fill positions that South African graduates cannot fill, which improves firm productivity but does not address domestic unemployment), or by choosing less productive, less complex production processes that can be executed with the available workforce (which reduces productivity and competitiveness).

This last adaptation is particularly insidious. A firm that cannot find workers capable of operating sophisticated machinery will not invest in sophisticated machinery. It will invest in simpler equipment that its available workforce can operate. This means lower productivity per worker, lower output quality, and lower competitiveness — which reduces the firm's ability to grow, export, and create well-paying jobs. The education deficit is not just a social problem for the unemployed. It is a productivity drag on the entire economy, including firms and workers who are fully employed.

## The Comparator Country Evidence

The most compelling evidence for the education-growth link comes not from cross-country regressions but from the stories of countries that invested in education and then grew. Three comparators are particularly instructive for South Africa.

**South Korea, 1960-2000.** In 1960, South Korea's per capita income was approximately $1,000 (in purchasing power parity terms). Its adult literacy rate was below 80 per cent. Its economy was agrarian. The Park Chung-hee government, which took power in 1961, implemented an education strategy that would become the template for the East Asian economic miracle. Universal primary education was achieved by the mid-1960s. Universal secondary education was achieved by the 1980s. Technical education was expanded dramatically through polytechnics and technical high schools. Teacher quality — particularly in mathematics and science — was made a national priority, with teacher salaries raised to attract top graduates into the profession and teacher training reformed to emphasise subject mastery.

The education investment preceded the economic takeoff. South Korea's growth acceleration began in the late 1960s and continued for four decades. By 2000, per capita income exceeded $15,000. By 2020, it exceeded $30,000. Today, South Korea has the world's most complex economy (by ECI) and one of the world's highest-performing education systems (by PISA and TIMSS). The causal sequence is clear: education investment → human capital accumulation → economic complexity → growth → more education investment. The virtuous cycle.

**Singapore, 1965-present.** Singapore's education-led growth strategy is even more dramatic than South Korea's, because Singapore started with even fewer natural advantages — no natural resources, no agricultural land, a tiny domestic market. Lee Kuan Yew's government bet everything on human capital. The education system was reformed to prioritise mathematics, science, and technical skills. English was adopted as the medium of instruction to integrate Singapore into the global economy. A tier of polytechnics was created to provide industry-relevant technical education. And the entire strategy was executed with a relentlessness and institutional discipline that few countries have matched.

The result: Singapore went from a per capita income of approximately $2,000 at independence in 1965 to over $60,000 today — the wealthiest country in Asia by per capita income. The education system is now among the world's highest performers on PISA, TIMSS, and other international assessments. Singapore's economic complexity — in financial services, biomedical sciences, electronics, and logistics — is the direct product of the education system's output.

**Vietnam, 2000-present.** Vietnam's PISA results, first published in 2012, shocked the education world. A country with a per capita income of approximately $2,000 — one of the poorest countries ever to participate in PISA — produced scores in mathematics and science that were comparable to Germany and above the OECD average. How?

The answers are instructive for South Africa. Vietnam invested heavily in teacher quality at the primary level, ensuring that primary school teachers had strong subject knowledge in mathematics and reading. It implemented a highly structured curriculum with detailed lesson plans and aligned textbooks — essentially the same approach that the South African EGRS trials found to be effective. It maintained high expectations and a culture of academic effort across all socioeconomic levels. And it spent far less per learner than South Africa — demonstrating that the relationship between spending and outcomes is mediated entirely by how the money is spent.

Vietnam's economic trajectory has tracked its educational achievement. Manufacturing FDI has poured into the country, attracted not just by low wages (wages are rising rapidly) but by the quality of the available workforce. Vietnam's economic complexity is increasing rapidly, with diversification into electronics, machinery, and higher-value manufactures. The education-growth pipeline is operating in real time.

:::takeaway
South Korea, Singapore, and Vietnam all invested in education before their economic takeoffs — not after. The common pattern: prioritise teacher quality, focus on STEM, align vocational education with employer needs, and sustain the investment across political cycles. South Africa spends more than any of them did at comparable income levels and gets worse outcomes. The problem is not spending. It is what the spending produces.
:::

## The Reinforcing Cycle: Education, Growth, and More Education

The comparator country evidence illustrates the reinforcing cycle that this entire Human Development series traces. When education improves, the human capital stock of the economy improves. When human capital improves, economic complexity rises — the economy can produce a wider variety of more sophisticated goods and services. When economic complexity rises, productivity grows, wages increase, and the economy generates more tax revenue. When tax revenue increases, the government can invest more in education — and in the safety, nutrition, and housing that [Learning in a War Zone](/articles/hd-3-2-learning-in-a-war-zone) identified as prerequisites for learning. And when education investment increases, education improves further. The cycle reinforces.

This is the virtuous version of the cycle. South Korea, Singapore, and Vietnam have been operating it for decades. The compounding returns are visible in their income trajectories, their productive structures, and their social indicators.

South Africa is operating the vicious version. The education system fails to produce cognitive skills. The economy cannot diversify because it lacks skilled workers. Growth stagnates. Tax revenue is constrained. Investment in education (and in the conditions that support education) is insufficient. And the education system continues to fail. The cycle degrades.

Breaking from the vicious version to the virtuous version requires an intervention that is large enough, sustained enough, and well-targeted enough to shift the equilibrium. The evidence reviewed in this article and the preceding two articles points to what that intervention looks like: structured learning programmes in the Foundation Phase to build foundational literacy and numeracy; teacher content knowledge development in mathematics and science; TVET reform built on employer co-design and workplace integration; safe routes to school and school-based psychosocial support; and enhanced nutrition programmes. These interventions, implemented simultaneously and sustained across political cycles, could begin to shift the equilibrium.

The cost of the intervention is significant but finite. The cost of not intervening is infinite — because the vicious cycle, left to its own dynamics, will continue to compound the deficit with each passing generation. Every year that a cohort of learners passes through a system that does not teach them is a year of human capital that the economy will never recover. The compound interest on educational failure is unforgiving.

:::expert[National Treasury Fiscal Policy Analysis]
National Treasury's analysis of the education-growth nexus, presented in the Medium-Term Budget Policy Statements and the annual Budget Review, has increasingly acknowledged the fiscal dimension of the education crisis. Their modelling shows that South Africa's long-run fiscal sustainability depends on achieving higher economic growth, which depends on higher productivity, which depends on improved human capital. The fiscal circularity is explicit in Treasury's own documents: low growth reduces revenue, which constrains spending on education and other human capital investments, which keeps growth low. Breaking this circularity requires what Treasury has termed "quality spending" — not just more expenditure on education but better expenditure, redirected toward the highest-impact interventions. Treasury has specifically highlighted the high fiscal returns to early-grade reading interventions, TVET reform, and teacher development as areas where marginal reallocation of existing education spending could yield the largest long-run growth dividends.
:::

## The Structural Transformation That Hasn't Happened

Every country that has moved from middle-income to high-income status has undergone a structural transformation in which the labour force moves from low-productivity activities (subsistence agriculture, informal trade) to high-productivity activities (manufacturing, services, technology). This transformation requires that the education system produce workers capable of performing high-productivity work. It is the bridge between the agricultural and informal economy that developing countries start with and the complex, diversified economy that developed countries have built.

South Africa's structural transformation has stalled. The manufacturing sector's share of GDP has declined from approximately 21 per cent in 1994 to approximately 12 per cent today. Employment in mining has declined steadily. The sectors that have grown — financial services, government, retail — require skills that the education system produces for some graduates but not at scale. And the informal economy, which absorbs many of those who cannot find formal employment, is characterised by low productivity, low incomes, and limited potential for the kind of sustained growth that raises living standards.

The stalled transformation is visible in the employment data. Of South Africa's approximately 16 million employed people, a disproportionate share work in low-productivity activities — informal trade, domestic work, subsistence agriculture, low-skill services. The high-productivity sectors — manufacturing, ICT, professional services, financial services — employ a relatively small share of the workforce, not because they could not employ more but because they cannot find workers with the skills they need.

The consequence is an economy that is simultaneously too small (insufficient aggregate demand) and too constrained on the supply side (insufficient skilled labour). Job creation programmes address the demand side but not the supply side. Skills development programmes address the supply side but only at the margins. The structural transformation that would solve both problems simultaneously — by moving workers from low-productivity to high-productivity activities — cannot happen without an education system that produces workers capable of performing high-productivity work.

## What This Means for You

The education-growth link is not an abstraction. It manifests in the lived experience of every South African.

If you are a parent, the quality of your child's school is not just an educational question. It is an economic question that will determine your child's lifetime earnings, employment prospects, and ability to participate in the economy as a productive citizen. The school governing body election is an economic policy decision. The choice between System A and System B — for those who have a choice — is a choice that echoes across a lifetime.

If you are an employer, the skills mismatch is not just a recruitment headache. It is a structural constraint on your firm's productivity, competitiveness, and growth potential. Investing in workplace training, engaging with the TVET system, and supporting educational improvement in your community are not corporate social responsibility — they are strategic imperatives.

If you are a voter, the education question is the economic growth question. A political party that promises economic growth without a credible plan for improving education quality is making a promise it cannot keep. The comparator country evidence is clear: no country has achieved sustained, inclusive growth with a failing education system. Demand better.

If you are a young person navigating the education system, the evidence in this article is both sobering and empowering. Sobering, because the system-level failures documented here are real and they constrain your opportunities in ways that are not your fault. Empowering, because understanding the system — understanding what it produces and what it fails to produce — gives you the information you need to make strategic decisions about your own education: which skills to develop, which pathways to pursue, which gaps to fill through self-directed learning.

The reinforcing cycle runs through every classroom in the country. When a child learns to read, she is accumulating human capital. When she acquires mathematical reasoning, she is building economic complexity. When she develops the ability to solve problems, she is contributing to productivity growth. And when the economy grows as a result, it generates the revenue to educate the next generation. The classroom is where the cycle begins. The paycheck is where it becomes visible. And the distance between the two — the distance from classroom to paycheck — is the distance that South Africa must close if it is to become the country that its Constitution promises.

:::takeaway
Education quality is not a separate policy domain from economic growth. It is the same policy domain. The evidence from international research, from economic complexity analysis, and from comparator country experience all converges on the same conclusion: cognitive skills — not years of schooling, not credentials, not spending — are the education variable that drives growth. South Africa's education system does not produce cognitive skills at the level the economy needs. Until it does, the growth that would create jobs, reduce poverty, and fund better services will remain out of reach. From classroom to paycheck, the pipeline is broken. Fixing it is the single highest-return investment South Africa can make.
:::
