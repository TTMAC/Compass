In the last article, [The Disincentive Question](/articles/eg-3-1-the-flat-tax-mirage), we established that every tax is a disincentive, that *what* you tax matters more than the headline rate, and that the income-tax rate is a surprisingly low-order lever — and we watched a flat income tax dissolve, on South Africa's own numbers, into a VAT increase. But arithmetic on a spreadsheet is one thing; the real world is another. The obvious next question is empirical. Forget the modelling — what actually happened in the countries that adopted flat income taxes, or that levy no income tax at all? Surely somewhere out there is a country that cut its rate, flattened its schedule, and watched growth take off, a living proof that the idea works.

There are countries that did exactly that, and we are going to look at them honestly. The tour delivers a sharper lesson than the cases are usually wheeled out to prove, and it is the thread that runs through the rest of this part: real states treat the shape of their income tax — flat or progressive, two brackets or seven — as a *dial they turn* in response to revenue, administration and politics, not as a sacred principle they defend on its own merits. They flatten, and then several of them re-steepen; they cut, and then they broaden. Watching them do it is the surest cure for the habit, common on both sides of the South African debate, of treating either the flat tax or the progressive schedule as a matter of first principle rather than of practical design. But the verdict on the flat tax specifically is more sobering than either side wants. The flat-tax economies that boomed were mostly small post-communist states catching up to Western Europe with an entire package of reforms at once, which makes the flat tax's independent contribution very hard to isolate — and several of them have since quietly walked the flat tax back. The no-income-tax economies are almost all either sitting on oil wealth per head that South Africa cannot dream of matching, or are tiny financial havens whose model does not scale to a country of 63 million people with deep public-service obligations. There are genuine lessons here, mostly about administration and the design of the tax base. But the honest finding is that no flat-tax or no-tax country offers South Africa a template it could lift and expect the same result. The evidence cautions against flat-tax hype and against reflexive dismissal in equal measure.

This is the second article in The Fiscal Levers, and it is written in the same spirit as Part 1's [The Comparator Countries](/articles/eg-1-3-the-comparator-countries) — the discipline of learning from other nations without fooling yourself. So before we tour the cases, a word about how to read them.

## How to read a comparison without fooling yourself

The single most common move in a tax debate is to point at a country. "Estonia cut taxes and boomed." "The Gulf has no income tax and look how rich it is." The move is powerful because it feels like evidence — a real place, real growth, a real policy. And it is almost always a trap, for one reason: countries change many things at once, and the human mind reaches for the change that fits the argument it already holds.

When Estonia adopted a flat tax in 1994, it also left the rouble zone, privatised state industry, opened to foreign investment, rebuilt its institutions from the rubble of Soviet occupation, and began a two-decade march toward European Union membership. Its economy grew strongly over the following years. Which of those changes produced the growth? You cannot tell from the growth rate alone, and anyone who attributes it confidently to the flat tax is choosing a conclusion, not deriving one. Economists call this the *identification problem*: when several causes move together, you cannot read off the effect of any one of them from the outcome. It is the reason a single fast-growing flat-tax economy proves almost nothing, and the reason cherry-picking — naming the one case that fits while ignoring the confounders and the counter-examples — is the characteristic vice of comparative tax argument.

So here is the test we will hold every case to. Did the cited growth coincide with other large reforms or external tailwinds? Has the country kept the policy, or repealed it? Is it structurally like South Africa? And is the real engine the tax design, or something else entirely — oil, accession, a one-off catch-up? Keep those four questions in mind as we go. They are the spine of the Practical Framework at the end, and they are how you turn a slogan back into a question.

## The flat-tax cluster: the post-communist wave

Start with the strongest cases the flat-tax case has, because a fair test gives the other side its best evidence first.

Estonia is the pioneer and the poster child. In 1994 it became the first country in Eastern Europe to adopt a flat personal income tax, replacing a progressive schedule of 16%, 24% and 33% with a single 26% rate, later cut to 21% by 2008 and to 20% more recently. Estonia subsequently became one of the great success stories of the post-Soviet transition — a digital state, a start-up hub far out of proportion to its 1.3 million people, a member of the EU and the eurozone. Its near neighbours followed: Lithuania flattened in 1994 and Latvia in 1995, both at relatively high rates close to their previous top rates. This first, Baltic wave set its flat rates near the *top* of the old schedule — a tidy-up of a progressive system rather than a dramatic rate cut.

Then came the second wave, and it was bolder. Russia introduced a 13% flat personal income tax in 2001, and tax compliance and revenue rose noticeably afterwards — a result often cited as proof that low flat rates pay for themselves through better compliance. Slovakia enacted a 19% flat tax on personal and corporate income in late 2003, effective from January 2004, as the centrepiece of a broader liberalisation that made it a magnet for foreign manufacturing investment. Bulgaria went furthest, introducing a 10% flat tax in 2008 — among the lowest rates in the European Union, and one it has kept. Romania, Georgia, Serbia, Ukraine, the Czech Republic and Hungary all adopted flat or single-rate systems in the 2000s and early 2010s. For a decade it genuinely looked like the future of taxation in the post-communist world.

Now turn the cases over and read the other side, because it is just as real.

First, the confounders. Almost every one of these reforms happened in the middle of a transformation. EU accession brought enormous foreign direct investment, structural funds, institutional reform and a catch-up growth dynamic as economies converged toward Western European productivity. Privatisation and macroeconomic stabilisation were happening simultaneously. And — this is the part flat-tax advocates rarely mention — many of the reforms paired the flat rate with substantial *base-broadening*, closing loopholes and exemptions so that the lower rate applied to a wider slice of income. A good deal of the revenue that survived the rate cut was recovered by broadening the base, not by the flat rate magically calling forth more activity. The IMF's careful study of these reforms concluded that the strong "Laffer" story — that the rate cuts paid for themselves through sheer growth — was not generally supported; where revenue held up, base-broadening and better compliance, not a growth surge, did most of the work.

Second, the repeals — and this is where the cases teach the lesson that matters most for South Africa. A policy that worked exactly as advertised would not get reversed by the countries that pioneered it, and yet several did. Slovakia abandoned its flat tax in 2013, reintroducing a second, higher rate. Latvia returned to a progressive system in 2018. The Czech Republic moved back toward progressivity in 2021. Russia, the compliance success story, reintroduced a higher 15% band on high incomes in 2021, breaking the flatness it had championed for two decades. The flat tax was not a one-way ratchet toward which every serious country was converging; it was a policy that a meaningful number of its adopters tried and then partly undid.

Read that pattern for what it actually says, because it cuts against a habit on *both* sides of the debate. These governments flattened their schedules when flattening suited the moment — to simplify a chaotic system, to signal openness to investors, to lift compliance — and then re-steepened them when revenue ran short or the politics of fairness shifted. They treated the progressivity of the income tax as a *dial*, to be turned up or down as circumstances changed, not as a sacred line of principle to be held at any cost. That is the realistic posture, and it is worth importing. In South African argument, the progressive schedule is often defended — and the flat tax often attacked — as though the *shape* of the rate curve were a moral commitment rather than an engineering choice about how to raise a given sum with the least damage and the broadest consent. The countries that have actually moved their schedules back and forth show that it is the latter. There is nothing sacred about a flat rate; there is nothing sacred about a progressive one either. Each is an instrument, justified or not by what it does, not by what it symbolises — a point the next article develops all the way down to its ethical foundations.

So what is the measured lesson? It is that the flat tax's best-demonstrated benefits are *administrative* — simplicity, lower compliance costs, and reduced evasion in environments where evasion was rife (Russia in 2001 is the clearest case, an economy where a complex schedule was widely dodged and a simple low rate was easier to collect). Its *growth* effects, by contrast, are confounded by everything else that was happening and contested even among specialists. That is a real finding, and it is neither the triumphant story the advocates tell nor the dismissive one their opponents prefer.

:::expert
The most authoritative assessment of these reforms is the International Monetary Fund's, set out in the working paper "The 'Flat Tax(es)': Principles and Evidence" by Michael Keen, Yong-Sung Kim and Ricardo Varsano (IMF, 2006). Keen, one of the world's leading public-finance economists, and his co-authors examined the wave of Eastern European flat-tax reforms and reached a deliberately deflationary conclusion: the economic effects had been widely overstated on both sides. They found little evidence that the flat taxes had spurred the strong supply-side growth their champions claimed, and little sign that revenue had collapsed as critics feared — largely because the reforms were accompanied by base-broadening and, in high-evasion economies, by improved compliance. Their verdict was that the flat tax was better understood as an administrative simplification than as a growth engine, and that its appeal owed as much to its political symbolism as to any demonstrated economic miracle.
:::

## The Russian compliance puzzle, and why it doesn't travel

Russia's 2001 reform deserves a closer look, because it is the single case flat-tax advocates cite most often, and because picking it apart shows you exactly how to read a "success". When Russia replaced its progressive schedule with a 13% flat rate, real personal-income-tax revenue rose by about 26% the following year. On its face, this is the dream: cut the top rate, collect *more*. If that is causal, it is the strongest evidence the flat tax has ever produced.

It is almost certainly not causal in the way the slogan needs. The definitive study — Anna Ivanova, Michael Keen and Alexander Klemm's analysis in the journal *Economic Policy* in 2005 — found that the revenue jump came overwhelmingly from improved *compliance*, which they estimated rose by roughly a third, rather than from people working or investing more because the rate had fallen. Crucially, they could not even establish that the rate cut caused the compliance improvement, because Russia simultaneously ramped up tax enforcement: more audits, tougher penalties, better administration. The flat rate and the crackdown arrived together, and the crackdown is the more plausible driver of people who had been evading suddenly paying. The authors drew an explicit warning for policymakers: do not count on automatic revenue benefits from a reform like this, because "the Laffer curve does not promise a fiscal free lunch".

So the most-cited flat-tax triumph turns out, on inspection, to be a *compliance and enforcement* story in an economy where evasion had been rampant — not a supply-side growth story, and not one that transfers to a country like South Africa where the high-earner tax base is already largely captured through PAYE and is collected, not evaded at Russian-1990s scale. South Africa's problem is not that its high earners are hiding their salaries from a chaotic tax administration. The Russian medicine does not fit the South African ailment.

## The no-income-tax cluster: oil and offshore

The flat-tax cases at least involve income taxes. The bolder fantasy is the country with *no* income tax at all, and here the examples are real: the Gulf hydrocarbon states — the United Arab Emirates, Qatar, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, Bahrain and Oman — along with Brunei, and a scattering of microstate havens such as Monaco, the Bahamas, the Cayman Islands and Bermuda. Citizens and residents of the UAE keep every dirham of their salaries; there is no personal income tax. The appeal is obvious, and it is regularly held up in South African debate as proof that a state can fund itself without taxing income.

It can — if it has one of two things South Africa does not. The Gulf states fund their governments not through income tax but through *hydrocarbon rents*: the state owns the oil and gas, sells it, and channels the proceeds through the national budget and through sovereign wealth funds that invest the surplus for future generations. Oil and gas revenue accounts for something like half of GDP across the GCC. This is not a tax-policy story at all; it is a *resource-revenue* story, the same phenomenon this pillar examined in [Resource Optimisation](/articles/eg-2-1-resource-optimisation). A country with enough oil per head can simply not tax its people, because it is selling a non-renewable inheritance instead. South Africa, with a large population and mineral wealth that is real but nothing like oil-per-capita on the Gulf scale, has no such option. Spread the country's resource rents across 63 million people and they fund a fraction of the state; spread Qatar's across fewer than three million citizens and they fund the whole of it, with change left over for a sovereign wealth fund.

It is worth being precise about how the Gulf model actually works, because the sovereign wealth funds are the part South Africans sometimes admire most. A state like the UAE or Qatar takes the rent from selling its oil and gas, funds the budget from part of it, and invests the surplus in a fund — the Abu Dhabi Investment Authority, the Qatar Investment Authority, the Kuwait Investment Authority — that buys assets around the world, so that the income from those assets can one day substitute for the oil when it runs low or loses its value. Norway, not a Gulf state but the model often held up as the prudent version, runs its Government Pension Fund Global on the same logic and has built it into the largest such fund on earth. This is genuinely impressive fiscal engineering, and this pillar's [Resource Optimisation](/articles/eg-2-1-resource-optimisation) argues South Africa should take the sovereign-wealth idea seriously for its own mineral rents. But notice what it is: a way of *converting a resource windfall into a permanent endowment*. It presupposes the windfall. Brunei, a tiny sultanate floating on oil and gas, can fund itself without income tax for the same reason — it has hydrocarbon rents per head that dwarf its population's needs. None of this is a tax-policy lesson that a populous, resource-modest economy can apply. You cannot run a sovereign wealth fund off rents you do not have.

The microstate havens work differently again, and just as un-repeatably. Monaco, the Cayman Islands, Bermuda and the Bahamas fund themselves through some combination of tourism, financial services, and historically a degree of secrecy that attracts capital seeking to avoid tax elsewhere. Their model depends precisely on being small — a jurisdiction of tens or hundreds of thousands of people can live off being a booking centre for the wealth of much larger economies. It does not scale to a populous, middle-income country that has to educate millions of children, run a public health system, and pay a social grant to nearly half its population. You cannot run South Africa as an offshore boutique.

And here is the detail that should settle the matter: even the no-tax model is eroding among the very states that pioneered it. The GCC introduced a value-added tax from 2018 — the UAE and Saudi Arabia leading at 5% in January of that year, with others following. In 2023 the UAE went further and introduced its first federal *corporate* income tax, at 9% on business profits above AED 375,000, for financial years beginning on or after 1 June. One analysis described it as the moment the UAE "formally ceased to be a tax-free haven". The reason is instructive: as oil's long-term future clouds and populations grow, even resource-rich states are broadening their tax bases toward consumption and corporate taxes — moving *toward* the kind of diversified tax system South Africa already has, not away from it. The no-tax model is not the future these states are walking toward. It is the past they are carefully walking away from.

## Bringing it home: what actually transfers

Lay the two clusters side by side and the lesson for South Africa comes into focus, and it is not the one either camp expects.

What the successful flat-tax states genuinely share is less the *flatness* of their rate than the *administrative simplicity and broad base* that came with it. A tax system that is simple to understand, cheap to comply with, and hard to dodge is a real asset — and those are virtues SARS could pursue without abandoning progressivity at all. You can have a simple, broad-based, well-administered tax system with three or four brackets just as easily as with one. The flat-tax reforms that worked best worked because they cleaned up administrative messes and closed loopholes, and you can capture most of that benefit while keeping the rate structure progressive. The flatness was the headline; the housekeeping was the substance.

This reframes what South Africa should actually take from the flat-tax story. Not the single rate — the housekeeping. SARS already does a great deal of the administrative work the flat-tax reformers prized: most personal income tax is collected at source through PAYE, e-filing is mature, and the high-earner base is largely captured rather than evaded. Where the country loses ground is elsewhere — in the complexity of allowances and structuring at the top, in the resources of the tax authority after years of institutional damage, in the long tail of the informal economy that sits outside the net entirely. Those are real problems, and every one of them can be tackled without flattening a single bracket. The lesson from Tallinn and Bratislava is "simplify and broaden", not "abandon progressivity" — and the two have been conflated for so long that the second rides in on the credibility of the first.

And there is a counter-case that demolishes any simple "lower, flatter, faster" claim outright: the Nordic countries. Denmark runs a tax-to-GDP ratio around 45%, with Sweden, Norway and Finland between 42% and 43% — well above the OECD average near 34% — on steeply progressive schedules. By the logic that high progressive taxes strangle enterprise, these should be stagnant, innovation-starved economies. They are the opposite. The 2025 Global Innovation Index placed Sweden second in the world, Finland seventh and Denmark ninth; Spotify, Just Eat and a string of other globally successful firms emerged from these high-tax states, which rank among the best places on earth to start a business. The Nordic model does not prove that high taxes *cause* prosperity any more than Estonia proves flat taxes do — the same identification problem cuts both ways. But it is decisive against the claim that progressive taxation is incompatible with growth and innovation. Plainly it is not, when paired with strong institutions, an educated workforce and efficient public spending.

Notice, though, exactly how much — and how little — that proves, because the symmetry is the point. The Nordics show that steep progressivity *can* coexist with a dynamic economy; they do not show that it is *required* for one, still less that it is morally obligatory. Plenty of prosperous countries run flatter, more consumption-weighted tax mixes and do perfectly well. What the high-tax success stories and the flat-tax success stories establish *together* is the deflationary truth this part keeps arriving at: the shape of the income-tax schedule is not the thing that makes or breaks an economy, and it carries no inherent moral charge in either direction. The Nordics did not choose progressivity because justice demanded it and prosperity followed as a reward; they chose a particular bargain — high taxes for an expansive welfare state, sustained by the trust and institutions to make it work — and progressivity is one instrument inside that bargain. Strip away the reverence in both directions and what is left is a design question: given what a country must fund, and the people and capital it must keep, which mix of taxes raises the money with the least harm and the broadest buy-in? That is an engineering question with a values component, not a commandment.

It is worth dwelling on the scale mismatch, because it is the thing the comparisons quietly ignore. Estonia made its flat tax work with 1.3 million people and no meaningful welfare obligations to a poor majority. Qatar funds its state for fewer than three million citizens. Monaco governs about 38,000 people. South Africa is a country of roughly 63 million, more than 18 million of whom receive a monthly social grant, with a public health system serving the large majority who have no private cover, and a constitutional commitment to progressively realising social and economic rights. The fiscal machine that has to be financed here is of a different order entirely. A tax model that balances the books for a digital city-state or a petro-monarchy tells you almost nothing about what will finance basic education, district hospitals and the social wage for tens of millions. The bigger and poorer and more unequal the population a tax system must serve, the more it has to lean on the people who can actually pay — which is to say, the more a graduated structure is forced on it by sheer arithmetic, not by principle. This is worth stating precisely, because it is easy to misread as a defence of progressivity on its merits, and it is not one. The point is narrower and more useful: in a country this large, poor and unequal, *some* concentration of the burden on high earners is hard to avoid if the books are to balance at all, simply because that is where the income is. That is a constraint, not a virtue. It says nothing about whether the schedule should top out at 45% or 40% or 35%, nothing about how many brackets there should be, and nothing about whether progressivity is *just*. Those remain open questions, to be argued on the merits rather than settled by reverence — which is exactly what the next article does.

Most important of all, none of these models — flat, zero, or Nordic — speaks to South Africa's actual wealth problem. That problem, as the next article argues in detail, is not the *rate* at which income is taxed. It is the gap between a lightly taxed asset *stock* and a heavily taxed income *flow* — and, deeper still, the question of who can get the credit to build assets in the first place. A flat tax does not touch that gap. Neither does abolishing income tax. The Gulf's oil does not exist here, the Baltics' EU accession cannot be repeated, and the havens' smallness cannot be manufactured. The international evidence is genuinely interesting, and genuinely useless as a template. Its real value is to inoculate you against the next person who waves a country at you. The flat tax travels well as a slogan and badly as a policy, and knowing the difference is most of what a citizen needs from the comparative evidence. The countries that get cited are real, their growth was real, and the lesson drawn from them is almost always wrong — because the lesson ignores everything that was happening around the tax change. That habit of looking past the confounders is the one this article most wants to leave you with.

:::expert
The Davis Tax Committee, in its macro-analysis of the South African tax system, took exactly this comparative approach — examining international experience not to find a model to copy but to understand what the country's tax mix could and could not achieve. The committee's consistent posture was sceptical of imported silver bullets: it treated the structure of South Africa's taxes as something to be reformed in light of the country's own inequality, administrative capacity and revenue needs, rather than benchmarked against small transition economies or resource-rich states with fundamentally different circumstances. That discipline — asking whether a cited country is actually comparable before drawing a lesson from it — is the single most useful habit a citizen can borrow from the professional tax-policy literature.
:::

## Where this leaves the argument

We have now done both halves of the flat-tax question. [The Disincentive Question](/articles/eg-3-1-the-flat-tax-mirage) showed what it does to South Africa's numbers on paper: a large, regressive shift of the burden onto VAT. This article has shown what it did in the real countries that tried it: an administrative tidy-up whose growth dividend is confounded and contested, walked back by several adopters, alongside a no-tax model that runs on oil or smallness and is eroding even where it exists. Both halves point the same way. The flat tax is not the door it appears to be.

That clears the ground for the better question, which the next article takes up. If the debate is not really "flat or progressive?", then what *is* a tax for — and does the progressive schedule that South Africa argues over actually have the moral standing its defenders assume? [What Tax Is For](/articles/eg-3-3-what-tax-is-for) goes back to first principles, ethical ones included — and begins to develop the argument that runs through the rest of this part: that South Africa taxes the flow of new earning far more heavily than the stock of old wealth, and that this, not the shape of the rate schedule, is the asymmetry worth arguing about. The deepest answer of all — that who gets to build wealth depends less on any tax than on where bank credit flows — waits in [The Real Lever](/articles/eg-3-8-the-real-lever).

## Practical Framework: The Comparator Reality Check

:::takeaway
The countries usually cited as proof that flat or zero income tax works do not survive scrutiny. The flat-tax booms coincided with EU accession, privatisation and base-broadening, and several adopters later repealed the policy; the no-income-tax states run on oil rents or offshore smallness that South Africa cannot replicate, and even they are now broadening their tax bases. Meanwhile the high-tax, progressive Nordic economies are among the most innovative on earth. The honest conclusion is that no foreign tax model transfers cleanly to South Africa — and that the useful skill is knowing how to discount a cherry-picked example.
:::

Whenever someone cites a country as proof that a tax idea works — in a column, a debate, a manifesto — you can test the claim in about a minute with four questions. Most cherry-picked examples fail at least one of them.

:::framework
**The Comparator Reality Check**

Apply these four questions to any country offered as evidence for a tax policy. If the example fails even one, treat the lesson as suggestive at best, not proof.

**1. Did the cited growth coincide with other large reforms or external tailwinds?**

Look for what *else* was happening — EU accession, a commodity boom, mass privatisation, the end of a war or a sanctions regime, a one-off catch-up from a low base. When several big changes move together, you cannot credit the growth to the tax change alone. Estonia's boom rode EU accession and a wholesale post-Soviet rebuild; the flat tax was one ingredient among many.

**2. Has the country kept the policy, or repealed it?**

A policy that delivered as promised tends to survive. Check whether the country still has it. Slovakia, Latvia and the Czech Republic abandoned their flat taxes; Russia reintroduced a higher band. Repeal by the country that pioneered the policy is strong evidence against it.

**3. Is the country structurally comparable to South Africa?**

Compare population size, resource dependence, development stage and inequality. A lesson from a 1.3-million-person digital state, or a three-million-person petro-economy, may not transfer to a 63-million-person middle-income country with deep inequality and large public-service obligations.

**4. Is the real funding source the tax design — or oil, offshore finance, or a one-off catch-up?**

Ask what actually pays for the state. If the answer is hydrocarbon rents (the Gulf), financial-secrecy services (the havens), or temporary convergence (the Baltics), then the "no income tax" or "low flat tax" is a *consequence* of the funding source, not a transferable cause of prosperity.

A country that passes all four — comparable in structure, funded genuinely by its tax design, still running the policy years later, with the growth not better explained by something else — is worth learning from. In tax debates, such examples are rarer than the confident citations suggest.
:::
