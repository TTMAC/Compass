## A Strategy Without Sequenced Milestones Is Just a Wish List

A strategy without a sequenced implementation plan, measurable milestones, and a course-correction mechanism is just a wish list. South Africa's history is defined by strategy documents that read beautifully and deliver nothing because they lack three things: a sequencing logic that specifies what must happen first because other things depend on it, measurable key performance indicators at each stage so you know whether you are on track, and a formal course-correction mechanism so you can adjust when reality diverges from the plan. The National Development Plan, the Industrial Policy Action Plans, the New Growth Path, the Economic Reconstruction and Recovery Plan — each was announced with conviction, endorsed by Cabinet, tabled in Parliament, and then allowed to drift into irrelevance because nobody built the operational infrastructure to convert strategic intention into tracked execution. This article builds that infrastructure.

If you have been following this series, you now have the full picture. You understand the three engines of growth — resource optimisation, efficiency-driven transition, and innovation-led development. You have seen the institutional architecture that is supposed to drive execution in [The Execution Machine](/articles/eg-3-2-the-execution-machine) and the monitoring system that is supposed to track whether any of it is working in [Watching the Watchers](/articles/eg-3-3-watching-the-watchers). You have the Municipal Performance Index from [The Municipal Performance Index](/articles/eg-4-1-the-municipal-performance-index) as your local-level scorecard. And you have the political economy analysis from [The Political Economy of Growth](/articles/eg-5-1-the-political-economy-of-growth) that explains why reform is always a negotiation among competing interests. What you do not yet have is the sequenced plan that puts all of these pieces in order, assigns measurable targets to each phase, and specifies what happens when targets are missed. That is what this article provides.

The approach draws heavily on Rwanda's Economic Development and Poverty Reduction Strategy (EDPRS) monitoring system — not because Rwanda's political context is transferable to South Africa, but because its implementation mechanics represent the most rigorous example of strategy-to-execution conversion on the African continent. Rwanda built a system where every strategic priority has a measurable indicator, every indicator has a responsible institution, every institution reports on a fixed schedule, and deviations from targets trigger a formal review process. South Africa has the institutional capacity to build something at least as good. The question has never been capability. It has been will.

This article gives you the sequencing logic, the stage-specific KPIs, the dashboard architecture, the MPI implementation phases, the course-correction mechanism, and the accountability architecture. By the end, you will be able to look at any government economic strategy and assess whether it has the operational machinery to actually deliver — or whether it is, once again, a wish list dressed up as a plan.

## Why Sequencing Matters More Than Strategy

Every economic strategy South Africa has produced since 1994 has contained good ideas. The Reconstruction and Development Programme identified the right priorities. The Growth, Employment and Redistribution strategy contained sound macroeconomic logic. The Accelerated and Shared Growth Initiative was analytically rigorous. The National Development Plan is, by any reasonable assessment, a well-researched document that correctly identifies infrastructure, education, and institutional capability as binding constraints. The problem has never been the quality of the analysis. The problem has been the assumption that a good strategy, once announced, will somehow implement itself.

Strategies do not implement themselves. They are implemented through a sequence of institutional actions, each of which depends on the successful completion of prior actions. This is the sequencing problem, and it is the single most underrated reason why South African economic strategies fail. Consider what happens when you try to attract foreign direct investment into manufacturing without first fixing the electricity supply. The investor conducts due diligence, discovers that load-shedding will interrupt production an average of four hours per day, factors in the cost of backup generation, and invests in Vietnam instead. The manufacturing strategy was sound. The sequencing was wrong. You cannot attract manufacturing investment until you have reliable electricity, and you cannot have reliable electricity until you have resolved Eskom's generation capacity crisis, and you cannot resolve Eskom's generation capacity crisis without both new generation procurement (the REIPPPP) and the restructuring of Eskom's transmission and distribution functions. Each step depends on the one before it. Skip a step, and the entire chain breaks.

This is not a theoretical observation. It is the empirical history of South Africa's economic policy since the mid-2000s. The Industrial Policy Action Plans identified beneficiation as a strategic priority — adding value to South Africa's mineral resources before export. Correct in principle. But beneficiation requires reliable and affordable electricity (which was failing), functioning rail and port infrastructure to move finished goods (which Transnet was not delivering), and a regulatory environment that made it cheaper to process minerals locally than to export them raw (which the fiscal and regulatory framework did not consistently provide). The strategy was announced. The prerequisites were not in place. The strategy produced marginal results. A new strategy was announced. The cycle repeated.

The sequencing logic for South Africa's economic growth trajectory follows a clear dependency chain. Infrastructure must come first — not because it is more important than education or institutional reform, but because everything else depends on it. You cannot run an efficient manufacturing operation on unreliable electricity. You cannot export goods through congested ports. You cannot attract private investment into areas without water, roads, or broadband connectivity. Infrastructure is the platform on which all other economic activity operates, and when the platform is broken, the activity cannot happen regardless of how well-designed the policies are.

Resource optimisation can begin in parallel with infrastructure repair. This involves extracting more value from South Africa's existing endowments — minerals, agriculture, tourism, the financial services sector — through better regulation, reduced red tape, improved logistics, and targeted beneficiation. Resource optimisation does not require the full infrastructure platform to be complete, but it does require that the most critical infrastructure bottlenecks are being addressed. You can begin optimising port operations while still building new rail capacity, but you cannot optimise port operations if the port equipment is not being maintained at all.

Efficiency-driven growth follows, because it requires both the infrastructure foundation and the institutional reforms — skills development, regulatory simplification, competition policy — that take longer to implement. This is the stage where South Africa moves from extracting more from what it already has to doing things genuinely better: higher productivity, more sophisticated manufacturing, competitive services exports. It requires a workforce with technical skills (which means the TVET system must have been reformed), a regulatory environment that enables rather than obstructs business formation (which means the regulatory reform process must have progressed), and trade agreements that open export markets (which means the AfCFTA and bilateral trade negotiations must have advanced).

Innovation-driven growth is the long-term destination. It requires all of the preceding stages to have succeeded: reliable infrastructure, an optimised resource base, efficient institutions, a technically skilled workforce, a functioning intellectual property regime, and a culture of entrepreneurship supported by venture capital and university-industry collaboration. No country has achieved innovation-driven growth without first passing through the preceding stages. South Korea did not go from rice paddies to Samsung in one step. It went from infrastructure investment (1960s) to efficiency-driven manufacturing (1970s-1980s) to innovation-driven technology leadership (1990s-2000s). Each stage took a decade or more and depended on the one before it.

## The Three-Stage Timeline: From $7,000 to $50,000

The implementation roadmap is structured around three stages, each with a distinct economic objective, a defined GDP per capita target range, and a set of stage-specific KPIs. The timeline is ambitious but not unrealistic — it reflects the growth trajectories achieved by countries that successfully implemented sequenced development strategies, adjusted for South Africa's specific constraints and advantages.

Stage 1 covers Years 1 through 7 and targets GDP per capita growth from approximately $7,000 to $15,000. This is the infrastructure and resource optimisation stage. The central objective is to fix the binding physical and institutional constraints that prevent the South African economy from operating at its existing potential. This is not about innovation or leapfrogging. It is about making what already exists work properly. Electricity must be reliable. Ports must function. Rail must move freight. Water systems must not lose forty per cent of their supply to leaks. Regulatory approvals must not take three years when they should take three months. The private sector is not asking for subsidies or special treatment. It is asking for a platform that works. Stage 1 builds that platform.

Stage 2 covers Years 8 through 15 and targets GDP per capita growth from $15,000 to $30,000. This is the efficiency-driven growth stage. With the infrastructure platform in place and resource optimisation generating improved returns, the economy can begin to move up the value chain. Manufacturing becomes more sophisticated. Services exports grow. South African firms begin competing in regional and continental markets under the AfCFTA. The TVET system begins producing graduates with the technical skills that employers need. The regulatory environment has been simplified to the point where starting and operating a business in South Africa is competitive with peer economies. Foreign direct investment flows increasingly into manufacturing and services rather than just mining, because the operating environment justifies the investment.

Stage 3 covers Years 16 through 25 and targets GDP per capita growth from $30,000 to $50,000. This is the innovation-driven growth stage. South Africa becomes a significant producer of intellectual property, not just a consumer of it. R&D spending as a percentage of GDP approaches the levels seen in upper-middle-income countries transitioning to high-income status. Universities produce research that is commercialised through spin-offs and licensing. The venture capital ecosystem funds domestic technology startups. Patent applications rise. High-technology exports constitute a growing share of total exports. This stage is the furthest from South Africa's current reality, but it is not a fantasy. It is the trajectory that every country currently classified as high-income has followed, adapted to South Africa's specific circumstances.

## Stage-Specific KPIs: How You Know Whether It Is Working

The difference between a strategy and a plan is measurability. A strategy says "we will improve infrastructure." A plan says "we will increase the Transnet port container throughput at Durban from X TEUs per annum to Y TEUs per annum by the end of Year 3, measured quarterly using Transnet's published operational data." The former is an aspiration. The latter is a commitment that can be tracked, evaluated, and — crucially — used to hold someone accountable if it is not met.

Each stage of the implementation roadmap has two categories of KPIs: leading indicators and lagging indicators. This distinction is critical and frequently misunderstood. A leading indicator is an early signal — it tells you whether the activities that should produce results are actually happening. A lagging indicator is an outcome measure — it tells you whether the results have actually materialised. You need both. If you only track lagging indicators, you will discover that you have failed only after it is too late to correct course. If you only track leading indicators, you might be doing all the right activities and still failing to produce results, because your theory of how activities translate into outcomes was wrong. The combination gives you both early warning and outcome verification.

This distinction between leading and lagging indicators draws on a broader framework known as the Logic Model, first developed and popularised by the W.K. Kellogg Foundation in the late 1990s. The Kellogg Logic Model maps the causal chain from **inputs** (resources invested) to **activities** (what you do with those resources) to **outputs** (the direct products of those activities) to **outcomes** (the changes that result) to **impact** (the long-term systemic effect). Leading indicators typically correspond to the left side of this chain — inputs, activities, and outputs — because these are the things you can observe early and adjust in real time. Lagging indicators correspond to the right side — outcomes and impact — because these take longer to materialise and are harder to influence directly. The power of the Logic Model is that it makes the theory of change explicit. It forces you to articulate *why* you believe that a particular set of activities will produce a particular set of outcomes. When leading indicators are on track but lagging indicators are not, the Logic Model tells you where to look: the problem is not in the execution of activities but in the assumptions linking those activities to results. When leading indicators themselves are off track, the problem is upstream — in resource allocation, institutional capacity, or political will. South Africa's National Evaluation System, housed in the DPME, has adopted the Logic Model framework for programme evaluation, but its application to real-time KPI tracking during policy implementation remains inconsistent.

### Stage 1 KPIs (Years 1-7): Infrastructure and Resource Optimisation

The leading indicators for Stage 1 focus on whether the infrastructure repair and resource optimisation activities are actually happening. The infrastructure project delivery rate measures the percentage of approved infrastructure projects that are completed on time and within budget. South Africa's historical record on this measure is dismal — the 2019 Infrastructure Fund concept struggled to move from announcement to implementation, and the DPME's own evaluations have repeatedly found that infrastructure project delivery rates across government hover well below acceptable levels. A meaningful target would set the delivery rate above 75 per cent by Year 3 and above 85 per cent by Year 7, with quarterly reporting by the Infrastructure Fund and the relevant implementing agencies.

REIPPPP capacity additions measure the megawatts of renewable energy capacity connected to the grid through the Renewable Energy Independent Power Producer Procurement Programme. This is one of South Africa's genuine policy success stories — when properly managed, the REIPPPP has delivered new generation capacity on time, on budget, and at declining costs. The leading indicator tracks whether the procurement rounds are happening on schedule, the bid windows are opening as planned, and the preferred bidders are reaching financial close. The target is cumulative gigawatts of new capacity, with quarterly reporting on the procurement pipeline.

Port turnaround improvements measure the average time a container vessel spends in a South African port from arrival to departure. Transnet's port operations have been a significant drag on economic competitiveness, with turnaround times at Durban and other ports substantially exceeding international benchmarks. The target is to reduce average turnaround times to levels competitive with peer ports — an achievable goal if the port equipment is maintained, the labour force is properly managed, and the Transnet National Ports Authority reforms are implemented.

Beneficiation project pipeline value measures the total rand value of beneficiation projects (adding value to raw materials before export) that have progressed past the feasibility stage and are under construction or in operation. This tracks whether the resource optimisation strategy is translating into actual investment in value-adding activities, or whether minerals continue to leave the country in raw form.

The lagging indicators for Stage 1 measure whether all of this activity is producing macroeconomic results. GDP per capita trajectory tracks whether the economy is growing at the rate required to move from $7,000 to $15,000 over seven years — which implies sustained real GDP growth above five per cent per annum, a rate South Africa has rarely achieved but which is well within the range of countries at similar income levels that have implemented similar reforms. Manufacturing as a percentage of GDP measures whether the economy is beginning to diversify away from primary commodity exports and financial services toward productive manufacturing — a structural shift that is essential for sustained employment growth. The export diversification index, as measured by UNCTAD, tracks whether South Africa's export basket is becoming more varied or remaining concentrated in a few primary commodities. Employment growth, measured through Stats SA's Quarterly Labour Force Survey, tracks the ultimate test: are more people working?

### Stage 2 KPIs (Years 8-15): Efficiency-Driven Growth

The leading indicators for Stage 2 reflect the shift from infrastructure repair to economic upgrading. FDI inflows to manufacturing measures not just total foreign direct investment but specifically investment directed at manufacturing operations — the kind of investment that creates factory jobs, transfers technology, and builds supply chains. The distinction matters because South Africa has historically attracted FDI into mining and financial services, which are capital-intensive rather than labour-intensive. The target is a sustained increase in manufacturing-directed FDI as a share of total FDI inflows.

TVET graduate employment rates measure whether the technical and vocational education system is producing graduates who actually find jobs in their field of training. South Africa's TVET system has struggled with quality, relevance, and employer recognition. A meaningful leading indicator tracks not just graduation numbers (an input measure) but employment within six months of graduation (an outcome measure) and employment in the field of training (a relevance measure). This indicator requires the Department of Higher Education and Training to conduct systematic tracer studies — tracking graduates after they leave the system — which is itself a measure of institutional capability.

Regulatory compliance cost reductions measure whether the administrative burden on businesses is actually decreasing. This is measured through the World Bank's ease-of-doing-business indicators (or their successors), through the annual compliance cost surveys conducted by the Small Business Institute, and through the time-and-cost data collected by the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission for business registration. The target is specific: reduce the average time to register a business, obtain a construction permit, and connect to the electricity grid by defined percentages within defined timeframes.

AfCFTA trade volumes measure South Africa's trade with other African countries under the African Continental Free Trade Area agreement. The AfCFTA represents the largest potential export market expansion available to South African firms, particularly for manufactured goods and services. The leading indicator tracks the actual trade volumes — not the agreements signed or the tariff schedules negotiated, but the goods and services actually moving across borders under AfCFTA provisions.

The lagging indicators for Stage 2 are GDP per capita (on trajectory from $15,000 to $30,000), the economic complexity index ranking (as calculated by the Harvard Growth Lab, measuring the sophistication and diversity of a country's export basket), formal sector employment growth (measuring whether jobs are being created in the formal economy rather than the survivalist informal sector), and services export revenue (measuring whether South Africa is developing exportable service capabilities in areas like financial services, ICT, professional services, and tourism).

### Stage 3 KPIs (Years 16-25): Innovation-Driven Growth

The leading indicators for Stage 3 reflect the shift to innovation as the primary growth driver. R&D spending as a percentage of GDP is the most widely used proxy for a country's investment in innovation capacity. South Africa's gross expenditure on research and development has historically been around 0.8 per cent of GDP — well below the 2 to 3 per cent range typical of innovation-driven economies. The target is to increase this to at least 1.5 per cent by Year 20 and 2 per cent by Year 25, with annual reporting by the Department of Science and Innovation through the national R&D survey.

Venture capital deals measure the number and value of venture capital investments in South African startups. A functioning innovation ecosystem requires risk capital — investors willing to fund early-stage companies with unproven technologies and business models. South Africa has a nascent venture capital sector, concentrated in Cape Town and Johannesburg, but it remains small relative to the size of the economy and heavily dependent on international investors. The target is a sustained increase in the number of domestic venture capital funds, the total capital under management, and the number of deals completed annually.

University spin-offs measure the number of companies created to commercialise research produced at South African universities. This is a direct measure of whether the university research system is producing knowledge with commercial potential and whether the institutional infrastructure exists to transfer that knowledge to the private sector. South Africa's universities produce research that is internationally competitive in several fields — particularly in mining technology, biotechnology, and astronomy — but the translation of that research into commercial enterprises has been limited.

Patent applications, measured through both the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (for South African patents) and the World Intellectual Property Organisation (for international patent applications originating from South Africa), track whether the country is producing protectable innovations. Patent counts are an imperfect measure of innovation — not all innovations are patented, and not all patents represent significant innovations — but they are a widely used and internationally comparable indicator.

The lagging indicators for Stage 3 are GDP per capita (on trajectory from $30,000 to $50,000), high-technology exports as a percentage of total exports (measuring whether South Africa is exporting sophisticated manufactured goods rather than primary commodities), and innovation-driven firms as a percentage of the economy (measuring the structural shift from resource-dependent and efficiency-dependent firms to firms whose competitive advantage derives from proprietary technology, processes, or business models).

## The Transparent Dashboard: Learning from Rwanda's EDPRS

Having the right KPIs is necessary but not sufficient. The KPIs must be publicly visible, regularly updated, and presented in a format that allows citizens, journalists, researchers, and opposition politicians to track progress without needing a statistics degree. This is where Rwanda's EDPRS monitoring system provides a genuinely instructive model.

Rwanda's Economic Development and Poverty Reduction Strategy — now in its third iteration as the National Strategy for Transformation (NST1) — is accompanied by one of the most rigorous implementation monitoring systems in Africa. The system works because it has four features that South Africa's monitoring architecture has historically lacked.

First, every strategic priority in the EDPRS has a measurable indicator with a baseline value, an annual target, and an end-of-strategy target. These are not aspirational statements. They are specific numbers. "Reduce the time to register a business from X days to Y days by 2024." "Increase electricity access from X per cent to Y per cent by 2024." "Reduce stunting in children under five from X per cent to Y per cent by 2024." The specificity is the point. You cannot track progress against a goal that says "improve the business environment." You can track progress against a goal that says "reduce business registration time from 43 days to 4 days."

Second, every indicator has a responsible institution — a specific ministry, agency, or district that is accountable for delivering against that target. This sounds obvious, but it is precisely what South Africa's strategies have repeatedly failed to do. The National Development Plan identifies dozens of priorities but assigns clear institutional responsibility for only a fraction of them. The result is that when a target is missed, nobody is specifically accountable. Everyone can point to someone else. Rwanda's system eliminates this ambiguity. If the business registration target is missed, the responsible institution is identified by name, and the question "why was this target missed?" has a specific addressee.

Third, reporting happens on a fixed, non-negotiable schedule. District-level reports are submitted quarterly. National-level progress reviews happen semi-annually. The annual Joint Sector Review brings together government, development partners, civil society, and the private sector to assess progress across all sectors. These are not optional meetings that can be postponed when the findings are uncomfortable. They are institutionalised events on the government's calendar, and they happen whether the news is good or bad.

Fourth, the data is publicly accessible. Rwanda's monitoring dashboard publishes indicator data that any citizen with internet access can review. This creates a transparency mechanism that does not depend on the goodwill of political leaders. If the government claims that electricity access has increased by ten percentage points, the dashboard data either confirms or contradicts that claim. If a district claims that school enrollment has risen, the data is there to verify.

:::expert[World Bank and CPIA Assessments of Rwanda's Implementation Discipline]
The World Bank's Country Policy and Institutional Assessment (CPIA) has consistently ranked Rwanda at the top of sub-Saharan Africa, with a score of 4.2 — the highest on the continent. The Bank's Country Program Evaluation for Rwanda (covering FY09–17) found that 88 per cent of Bank-supported operations in Rwanda were rated moderately satisfactory or better, compared to a sub-Saharan African average of 64.9 per cent. Development economists who have worked across both countries observe that the difference between South Africa and Rwanda is not one of analytical capacity — South Africa has more economists, more statisticians, and more sophisticated data systems. The difference is one of institutional commitment to using data for decision-making rather than for reporting. Rwanda's Imihigo system — performance contracts signed between the President and district mayors, with annual evaluations and public rankings — creates a direct link between measured performance and career consequences. In Rwanda, a missed target triggers a mandatory review process. In South Africa, a missed target more commonly triggers a new strategy document. The lesson is not that Rwanda's political system should be emulated — it should not — but that the mechanical discipline of assigning targets, tracking progress on a fixed schedule, and creating consequences for persistent failure is transferable to any institutional context that has the political will to sustain it.
:::

A South African implementation dashboard would need to be adapted to South Africa's institutional context, but the core principles are directly transferable. The dashboard would be hosted by the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME), which already has the institutional mandate and the data infrastructure. It would be publicly accessible — a website where any citizen can see, for every indicator in the implementation roadmap, the baseline value, the target for the current period, and the actual achieved value. The dashboard would use a simple traffic-light system: green means the indicator is on or above target, amber means the indicator is within a defined tolerance of the target (say, within 10 per cent), and red means the indicator has missed the target by more than the tolerance threshold.

The dashboard would be updated quarterly for leading indicators and annually for lagging indicators. Quarterly updates for leading indicators matter because these are the early warning signals — if the infrastructure project delivery rate drops from green to red in Quarter 2, you have three quarters to investigate and correct before the annual lagging indicator (GDP growth) confirms that you have a problem. Annual updates for lagging indicators are appropriate because macroeconomic outcomes do not move on a quarterly basis in ways that are meaningful for policy correction.

Each indicator on the dashboard would link to a detailed methodology page explaining how the indicator is calculated, where the data comes from, and who is responsible for providing the data. This methodological transparency is essential for credibility. If civil society organisations or academic researchers question the data, they need to be able to see exactly how the numbers were produced. If they identify errors, those errors can be corrected. A dashboard that publishes numbers without showing its working is a propaganda tool. A dashboard that publishes numbers with full methodological transparency is an accountability tool.

The dashboard would also include a "lessons learned" section for each indicator that moves from green to red or remains red for two consecutive reporting periods. This section would require the responsible institution to publish a brief explanation of why the target was missed and what corrective actions are being taken. This is not a punishment mechanism — it is a learning mechanism. Some targets will be missed for legitimate reasons: a global commodity price shock, a pandemic, a drought. The point is not that every target must be hit. The point is that every miss must be explained and responded to.

## The MPI Implementation Roadmap: From Eight Metros to 257 Municipalities

The Municipal Performance Index — described in full in [The Municipal Performance Index](/articles/eg-4-1-the-municipal-performance-index) — is the local-level component of the implementation dashboard. It provides a standardised, transparent, publicly available score for every municipality in South Africa, enabling citizens, councillors, and national government to track local economic and governance performance on a comparable basis. But an index is only useful if it is actually produced, published, and used. This section sets out the phased implementation plan for rolling the MPI out across all 257 municipalities.

The phased approach is not a luxury. It is a necessity. South Africa's municipalities vary enormously in their data availability, institutional capacity, and administrative sophistication. The eight metropolitan municipalities — Johannesburg, Cape Town, eThekwini, Ekurhuleni, Tshwane, Nelson Mandela Bay, Mangaung, and Buffalo City — have dedicated research units, published economic data, and the staff to engage with a performance index. A Category B4 rural municipality in the Eastern Cape may have a single finance officer, no research capacity, and data that exists only in handwritten ledgers that have not been digitised. Rolling out a sophisticated performance index simultaneously to all 257 municipalities would guarantee that the rural municipalities are scored on incomplete data and then unfairly penalised for data gaps that reflect capacity constraints rather than performance failures.

### Phase 1: Metro Pilot (Months 1-6)

The first phase covers the six months from the formal launch of the MPI to the publication of the first metro rankings. It focuses exclusively on the eight metropolitan municipalities (Category A). These municipalities are chosen for the pilot because they have the most complete and readily available data across all four MPI pillars (Economic Development, Service Delivery, Governance and Finance, Infrastructure and Sustainability), the strongest institutional capacity to engage with the methodology, and the most significant economic impact — the eight metros account for more than half of South Africa's GDP and a substantial proportion of its formal employment.

During Phase 1, the implementation team — housed within DPME with technical support from Statistics South Africa and National Treasury — conducts a data audit for each metropolitan municipality. The audit identifies, for each of the MPI indicators, the most recent available data point, the data source, any gaps or inconsistencies in the data, and the lag between the reference period and data availability. This data audit is the foundation of the entire index. If the data is unreliable, the scores are unreliable, and the index loses credibility immediately. The data audit is not a desktop exercise. It requires engagement with each metropolitan municipality's finance department, economic development unit, and technical services department to verify that the published data matches the municipality's own records.

Phase 1 also includes a stakeholder consultation process. The methodology — pillar weights, indicator definitions, normalisation procedures, geometric mean aggregation — is presented to each metropolitan municipality's senior management for comment before the first scores are published. This is not an invitation to negotiate favourable scores. It is a transparency measure that ensures the municipalities understand how they are being assessed and have the opportunity to identify factual errors in the data before publication. If eThekwini's finance department can demonstrate that the published debt collection rate used in the MPI calculation is based on outdated data and that the correct figure is significantly different, that correction must be made before publication. The goal is accuracy, not gotcha journalism.

The Phase 1 output is the first MPI publication: the metro rankings, showing each metropolitan municipality's overall score, pillar scores, and indicator scores, with a full methodological appendix. This publication is timed for September — coinciding with the Auditor-General's MFMA audit cycle, which provides the most recent governance and finance data — to ensure that the scores reflect the most current available information.

### Phase 2: Secondary Cities (Months 7-12)

The second phase extends the MPI to the approximately 27 Category B1 municipalities — South Africa's secondary cities. These include municipalities like Msunduzi (Pietermaritzburg), Emfuleni (Vereeniging/Vanderbijlpark), Polokwane, Mbombela (Nelspruit), Rustenburg, and others that function as regional economic centres. They are smaller than the metros but substantially larger than the rural municipalities, and they typically have reasonable data availability and at least some institutional capacity to engage with the index.

Phase 2 begins while Phase 1 is being published, creating a continuous pipeline. The data audit process for B1 municipalities is more intensive than for the metros because the data is less consistently available. Some B1 municipalities publish annual reports and economic data that is comparable in quality to the metros. Others have significant data gaps, particularly in economic development indicators, where QLFS data may not be available at the municipal level and the MPI must rely on proxy indicators from the Census or General Household Survey.

Phase 2 also involves the first calibration exercise. Once the B1 scores are calculated, the implementation team compares the B1 rankings with the metro rankings to ensure that the differentiated weighting system is working as intended. If a B1 municipality with manifestly weaker economic fundamentals than a metro scores higher on the Economic Development pillar because the proxy indicators used for B1 municipalities are systematically more favourable than the QLFS data used for metros, the indicator definitions or normalisation procedures need to be adjusted. This cross-category calibration is a technical exercise, but it matters for the credibility of the index as a whole.

The Phase 2 output is the expanded MPI publication, now covering all Category A and B1 municipalities — approximately 35 municipalities representing the overwhelming majority of South Africa's urban economic activity.

### Phase 3: All 257 Municipalities (Months 13-18)

The third phase is the most challenging. It extends the MPI to all remaining municipalities — the roughly 220 Category B2, B3, and B4 municipalities that include medium-sized towns, small towns, and deeply rural municipalities. Data availability in these municipalities varies from adequate to virtually non-existent. Some medium-sized towns have functional finance departments that produce reliable data. Some rural municipalities in the former homeland areas have data systems that are, to be frank, dysfunctional.

Phase 3 addresses this challenge through a minimum-data-threshold approach. For each indicator, the implementation team defines a minimum data standard. If a municipality's data for a given indicator does not meet that standard — because the data is too old, too incomplete, or too unreliable — the indicator is flagged as "data not available" rather than scored. The municipality's overall score is then calculated using only the indicators for which reliable data exists, with the pillar weights adjusted accordingly. This is not ideal. It means that some municipalities are scored on fewer indicators than others, which introduces comparability issues. But it is far better than the alternative, which is either excluding those municipalities entirely (which defeats the purpose of a universal index) or scoring them on unreliable data (which discredits the index).

Phase 3 also includes a data improvement component. For each municipality where indicators are flagged as "data not available," the implementation team produces a data improvement plan specifying what data systems the municipality needs to develop, what technical support is available (from DPME, National Treasury, Stats SA, and SALGA — the South African Local Government Association), and what the timeline is for achieving data readiness. The MPI thus serves a dual purpose: it scores performance where data exists and identifies data gaps where it does not, creating a roadmap for improving the data infrastructure itself.

The Phase 3 output is the complete MPI publication covering all 257 municipalities, published in September of the second year. This becomes the baseline against which all future progress is measured.

### Ongoing: The Annual Cycle and Biennial Review

After the initial 18-month rollout, the MPI enters a steady-state annual cycle. Every September, updated scores are published for all 257 municipalities using the most recent available data. The September timing is not arbitrary — it is designed to incorporate the Auditor-General's MFMA audit outcomes, which are typically finalised in the first half of the year, and Stats SA's most recent survey data. The September publication also positions the MPI results to inform the municipal budgeting cycle, which begins in earnest in October and November.

Every two years, the methodology undergoes a formal review. The biennial methodology review examines whether the indicators remain relevant, whether the data sources remain reliable, whether the pillar weights should be adjusted, and whether new indicators should be added or existing indicators retired. The review is conducted by an independent panel convened by DPME, including representatives from Stats SA, National Treasury, the AGSA, SALGA, and at least two academic institutions with expertise in local government research. The review panel publishes its recommendations, the final methodology changes are gazetted for public comment, and the updated methodology is applied starting with the next annual publication. This ensures that the MPI evolves with the data environment rather than becoming a static instrument that loses relevance over time.

SALGA capacity building runs in parallel throughout. SALGA, as the body representing all municipalities, plays a critical role in ensuring that municipalities understand the MPI, can engage with their scores, and can use the results to identify areas for improvement. SALGA's capacity-building programme includes workshops for municipal managers and chief financial officers explaining the MPI methodology, peer-learning exchanges where high-performing municipalities share practices with lower-performing ones, and technical assistance for municipalities seeking to improve their data systems. The MPI is not designed as a naming-and-shaming exercise. It is designed as a performance improvement tool, and SALGA's role is to ensure that it functions as such.

:::expert[DPME and the Gap Between Monitoring and Consequence]
South Africa's Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation (DPME), established in 2010 under Minister Collins Chabane, built one of the most comprehensive government-wide monitoring and evaluation frameworks in Africa. The Management Performance Assessment Tool (MPAT), the National Evaluation System, and the Frontline Service Delivery Monitoring programme together generate enormous quantities of performance data across national, provincial, and municipal government. Yet the persistent challenge — documented in DPME's own annual reports, in the Auditor-General's commentary, and in academic assessments from institutions including the Wits School of Governance and the Centre for Learning on Evaluation and Results (CLEAR) — is that this monitoring data is insufficiently used for decision-making. Government departments and municipalities receive audit findings, performance assessments, and evaluation reports, but the institutional mechanisms for converting those findings into changed behaviour remain weak. The challenge is not measurement but response. What South Africa's M&E architecture lacks is what might be called consequential monitoring — systems where poor performance on measured indicators triggers mandatory institutional responses, rather than being noted, filed, and forgotten. The MPI implementation plan incorporates this principle by linking municipal MPI scores to both SALGA capacity-building interventions (for municipalities that score poorly) and to the intergovernmental fiscal framework (for municipalities whose scores indicate systemic institutional failure).
:::

## The Course-Correction Mechanism: When to Stay and When to Pivot

No plan survives contact with reality. This is not a criticism of planning. It is a description of the world. Economic conditions change. Global commodity prices shift. Pandemics disrupt supply chains. New technologies create opportunities that did not exist when the strategy was written. Political events — elections, reshuffles, coalition realignments — alter the institutional landscape. A rigid plan that cannot adapt to these changes is not disciplined. It is brittle. It will break under pressure and deliver nothing.

But the opposite extreme — constant revision, perpetual pivoting, the strategy du jour — is equally destructive. If the strategy changes every time a new minister is appointed, no policy is in place long enough to produce results. If targets are revised downward every time they are missed, the monitoring system becomes a machine for manufacturing the appearance of success. If course corrections are made in response to political pressure rather than empirical evidence, the strategy degenerates into a series of tactical adjustments with no strategic coherence.

The course-correction mechanism must thread the needle between rigidity and drift. It must be stable enough to provide continuity and flexible enough to respond to genuine changes in circumstances. This requires a formal decision framework — a set of rules that specify when to stay the course, when to adjust the approach while maintaining the objective, and when to change the objective itself.

The framework operates on three levels. Level 1 is operational adjustment. This is triggered when a leading indicator moves from green to amber on the dashboard — the indicator is within 10 per cent of its target but trending in the wrong direction. Operational adjustment does not change the target or the strategy. It changes the implementation approach. If the infrastructure project delivery rate is slipping because of procurement delays, the operational adjustment might involve streamlining the procurement process, deploying additional project management capacity, or escalating bottlenecks to the relevant minister. Operational adjustments are the responsibility of the implementing department and are reported in the quarterly dashboard update.

Level 2 is strategic adjustment. This is triggered when a leading indicator moves from amber to red — the indicator has missed its target by more than 10 per cent — or when a lagging indicator misses its target in two consecutive reporting periods. Strategic adjustment may involve revising the target, changing the implementation vehicle, reallocating resources between programmes, or escalating the issue to a higher level of government. Strategic adjustments require approval from the relevant Cabinet committee (in the case of national-level indicators) or the relevant MEC (in the case of provincial-level indicators) and are published in the semi-annual progress review.

Level 3 is strategic pivot. This is the most serious level of course correction and is triggered when fundamental assumptions underlying the strategy have been invalidated. A strategic pivot is not a response to a missed target. It is a response to a changed world. If a global technology shift renders an entire industry obsolete (as the shift to renewable energy is doing to coal), a strategic pivot may be required to redirect resources from the declining industry to emerging alternatives. If a sustained commodity price collapse undermines the revenue assumptions of the resource optimisation stage, a strategic pivot may require accelerating the transition to the efficiency-driven stage. Strategic pivots require Cabinet approval, are preceded by a formal review (discussed below), and are published with a full explanation of the evidence that justified the change.

The key principle is that the burden of proof escalates with the magnitude of the correction. An operational adjustment can be made by a departmental director-general based on quarterly data. A strategic adjustment requires ministerial or Cabinet committee approval based on semi-annual data. A strategic pivot requires full Cabinet approval based on a formal review. This graduated structure prevents two failure modes: the paralysis that comes from requiring Cabinet approval for every minor adjustment, and the chaos that comes from allowing individual departments to unilaterally change strategic direction.

## The Annual Strategy Review: South Africa's Missing Institution

The course-correction mechanism requires a regular forum where progress is assessed, evidence is reviewed, and decisions about adjustments are made. This is the annual strategy review, and it is the single most important institutional gap in South Africa's economic governance architecture.

Rwanda conducts its Joint Sector Reviews annually. Singapore's Economic Development Board conducts quarterly strategy sessions with private sector leaders. South Korea's Blue House (now the Yongsan Presidential Office) held monthly export promotion meetings during its high-growth decades — meetings chaired by the president, attended by the heads of major conglomerates, and focused on identifying and removing obstacles to export performance. These were not ceremonial events. They were working sessions where data was presented, problems were identified, and solutions were agreed upon with specific deadlines and accountable officials.

South Africa has no equivalent. The closest approximation was the Presidential Council on the Economy, established during the Ramaphosa administration, but its meetings were infrequent, its agenda was broad, and its proceedings were largely opaque. The Cabinet lekgotla (planning retreat) happens twice a year but covers the entire government programme, not just economic strategy. Parliamentary portfolio committees hold hearings on individual departments but do not conduct cross-cutting reviews of the economic strategy as a whole.

The proposed annual strategy review would work as follows. It would be convened in March of each year — after the September MPI and dashboard publications have been digested and before the budget cycle for the following year begins in earnest. It would be chaired by the Minister of Finance (not the President, because the review is technical rather than ceremonial, and the Finance Minister has the fiscal authority to reallocate resources in response to findings). Participants would include the Director-General of DPME (presenting the dashboard data), the Director-General of National Treasury (presenting the fiscal implications), the relevant departmental directors-general (presenting their sector-specific progress), representatives of the private sector (through Business Unity South Africa and the Black Business Council), organised labour (through COSATU, FEDUSA, and SAFTU), civil society (through nominated organisations), and academic experts (nominated by the National Planning Commission).

The review would follow a structured agenda. First, DPME presents the dashboard update: which indicators are green, which are amber, which are red. Second, for each red indicator, the responsible department presents an explanation and a proposed corrective action. Third, the private sector and labour representatives respond with their perspective on whether the proposed corrections are likely to work. Fourth, the review makes formal recommendations: operational adjustments (which the relevant department implements without further approval), strategic adjustments (which are referred to the relevant Cabinet committee for approval), or strategic pivots (which are referred to Cabinet). Fifth, the recommendations are published in full within 30 days of the review, creating a public record against which next year's review can assess whether the corrections were implemented.

This review process is not revolutionary. It is standard practice in well-governed countries and in well-managed corporations. The fact that South Africa does not currently have a structured, data-driven, publicly accountable annual economic strategy review is itself a measure of the gap between the country's institutional capacity and its institutional practice.

## The Accountability Architecture: Who Answers When Targets Are Missed?

The most carefully designed dashboard, the most rigorous KPIs, and the most sophisticated course-correction mechanism are all worthless if nobody is accountable for the results. Accountability in this context means two things: somebody is specifically responsible for delivering against each target, and there are defined consequences — positive and negative — for performance against those targets.

The accountability architecture operates at four levels. At the national level, each indicator in the implementation dashboard is assigned to a specific departmental director-general. Not a department. A person. This is essential because institutional accountability — "the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition is responsible for export diversification" — creates diffusion of responsibility. Nobody personally owns the target. When the target is missed, the department issues a media statement blaming external factors and moves on. Personal accountability — "the Director-General of the DTIC is responsible for achieving X on the export diversification index by Year Y" — creates a direct link between an individual's professional reputation and a measurable outcome.

At the ministerial level, each Cabinet member who oversees a department with indicator responsibilities reports on those indicators at the annual strategy review. This report is public. The minister cannot hide behind the department's annual report, which is typically dense, technical, and designed to emphasise achievements while minimising failures. The dashboard forces transparency. The indicator is either green or it is not. The target was either met or it was not. The explanation for a miss must be specific and evidence-based, not a paragraph of bureaucratic hedging.

At the parliamentary level, the relevant portfolio committees receive the dashboard data and the annual strategy review recommendations. Portfolio committees already have the constitutional mandate to hold the executive accountable for the performance of government departments. What they typically lack is structured, comparable, time-series data that allows them to track performance over multiple years and identify patterns of persistent failure. The dashboard provides this data. A portfolio committee can see that a specific indicator has been red for three consecutive years, that the responsible department has provided three different explanations, and that none of the promised corrective actions has produced results. That pattern tells the committee something important about institutional capability — or institutional dishonesty — that no single annual report can reveal.

At the municipal level, the MPI scores create accountability within the intergovernmental system. Municipalities whose MPI scores fall into the "Critical" tier (below 40) for two consecutive years trigger mandatory intervention processes under Section 139 of the Constitution. This is not a new power — Section 139 already provides for provincial and national government to intervene in failing municipalities. What the MPI adds is an objective, transparent, data-driven trigger for intervention. Currently, Section 139 interventions are often delayed because the decision to intervene is political — it depends on the provincial government's willingness to act, which is influenced by factional and party-political considerations. An MPI-triggered intervention process would not eliminate political considerations, but it would make it significantly harder for a provincial government to ignore a municipality that has objectively and measurably failed.

:::expert[The Accountability Gap in South Africa's Public Service]
Research on public sector accountability in South Africa — including work by governance scholars at the Wits School of Governance, the Public Affairs Research Institute, and the HSRC — has consistently identified a structural weakness that might be described as upward accountability without downward consequence. Government departments are accountable upward to their ministers, their portfolio committees, and the Presidency through annual reports, strategic plans, and performance agreements. But this accountability rarely translates downward into consequences for non-performance at the operational level. A director-general who presides over persistent failure in programme delivery may eventually be replaced, but the replacement process is slow, politically mediated, and often results in a lateral transfer rather than a genuine consequence. The Public Service Commission's own reports have documented that disciplinary processes for senior managers are protracted and frequently inconclusive. The research consensus identifies three elements that effective accountability systems require in combination: clear performance expectations (which KPIs provide), regular and transparent reporting (which a dashboard provides), and credible consequences for non-performance (which South Africa's public service framework does not reliably deliver). Without the third element, the first two become exercises in documentation rather than instruments of change.
:::

## What South Africa Can and Cannot Learn from Rwanda

Rwanda's EDPRS monitoring system is the reference model for this implementation plan, and it is important to be honest about both what is transferable and what is not. The transferable elements are mechanical: the practice of assigning every strategic priority a measurable indicator, the discipline of fixed-schedule reporting, the transparency of public dashboards, and the formal link between missed targets and mandatory review processes. These are institutional technologies — ways of organising monitoring and decision-making — that do not depend on Rwanda's specific political context.

What is not transferable is the political concentration that makes Rwanda's system work. Rwanda's monitoring system is effective in part because the political leadership has the authority and the will to enforce consequences for non-performance. This is facilitated by a political system with a dominant executive, limited political competition, and a public service culture that prioritises compliance with presidential directives. South Africa's political system is fundamentally different: multi-party democracy, constitutional separation of powers, a federal structure with three autonomous spheres of government, strong trade unions with collective bargaining rights, a judiciary that actively reviews executive action, and a political culture that values debate and contestation. These features are strengths, not weaknesses. But they mean that the enforcement mechanism for South Africa's monitoring system must be institutional rather than executive — built into the legal and administrative framework rather than dependent on the personal authority of the president.

This is why the dashboard, the KPIs, the course-correction mechanism, and the accountability architecture described in this article are designed as institutional systems, not presidential programmes. The dashboard is hosted by DPME, a permanent department, not by the office of a particular president. The KPIs are embedded in departmental performance agreements that survive changes of minister. The course-correction mechanism operates through Cabinet committees and portfolio committees — institutional bodies with constitutional mandates — not through ad hoc presidential task teams. The accountability architecture links MPI scores to intergovernmental intervention provisions that are already in the Constitution. Each element is designed to be durable — to survive changes of government, reshuffles, coalition realignments, and the inevitable political turbulence that accompanies reform in a democracy.

This institutional durability is the hardest thing to build and the most important. South Africa's history is littered with monitoring systems that worked while a particular minister or director-general championed them and collapsed when that person left. The Government-wide Monitoring and Evaluation System was championed by Minister Collins Chabane and Director-General Sean Phillips. The Operation Phakisa programme was championed by the Presidency. The War on Leaks programme was championed by the Department of Water and Sanitation. In each case, the programme's effectiveness was closely tied to the personal engagement of its champion rather than to the institutional systems that were supposed to sustain it. A monitoring system that depends on the enthusiasm of its champion is a hobby, not an institution. The goal of this implementation plan is to build an institution.

## The Test of Ministerial Survival

There is a simple test for whether an implementation plan is institutionally embedded or merely politically sponsored: can it survive a change of minister? In South Africa's post-1994 history, the average tenure of a Cabinet minister in an economic portfolio has been approximately three to four years. Ministers are reshuffled, redeployed, or replaced following elections, party conferences, or coalitional adjustments. Every such change creates a period of institutional uncertainty during which the new minister reviews the previous minister's priorities, consults with new advisors, and frequently announces a "refreshed" or "revised" strategic direction that is, in many cases, merely a repackaging of the previous strategy with a new acronym.

The implementation plan must be designed to withstand this cycle. The way to do this is to embed the plan in legal and administrative instruments that do not change when ministers change. The KPIs should be incorporated into the Medium-Term Strategic Framework (MTSF) — the five-year government programme that is approved by Cabinet and binds all departments. The dashboard should be established through a DPME policy directive that creates an institutional obligation to publish, not a ministerial preference to monitor. The annual strategy review should be mandated through a Cabinet resolution that specifies its timing, participants, and outputs. The MPI publication schedule should be incorporated into DPME's annual performance plan, making it a measurable output against which the department is assessed by Parliament.

None of these institutional anchors makes the plan immune to political interference. A determined minister can undermine any system. But institutional anchors raise the cost of abandonment. It is easy to let a ministerial pet project lapse when the minister leaves. It is much harder to discontinue a programme that is embedded in the MTSF, mandated by Cabinet resolution, and tracked by Parliament. The anchoring strategy does not guarantee permanence, but it guarantees that discontinuation requires a deliberate, visible, politically costly decision rather than a quiet administrative fadeout.

## Connecting the Dashboard to the Citizen

If you have been reading this article thinking "this is all very well for technocrats, but what does it mean for me?" — that is exactly the right question, and it is the question that [Your Economic Citizenship](/articles/eg-5-3-your-economic-citizenship) addresses in full. But the short answer is this: the dashboard and the MPI are designed to give you, as a citizen, the tools to hold your government accountable for economic outcomes. Not for promises. Not for announcements. Not for strategy documents. For outcomes.

When a politician tells you that the economy is improving, you will be able to check the dashboard and see whether the leading and lagging indicators support that claim. When a municipal councillor tells you that your municipality is attracting investment, you will be able to check the MPI and see whether the economic development indicators are actually trending upward. When a minister tells Parliament that the implementation plan is on track, the portfolio committee — and you, through the committee's public proceedings — will be able to see whether the indicators are green, amber, or red.

This is what [Watching the Watchers](/articles/eg-3-3-watching-the-watchers) called the democratisation of data. Not data for its own sake, but data as a tool for democratic accountability. The government collects enormous quantities of data through Stats SA, National Treasury, the AGSA, DPME, and dozens of other institutions. Most of that data is technically public, published in formats that are accessible to specialists but incomprehensible to ordinary citizens. The dashboard and the MPI translate that data into a format that any citizen can understand: is this indicator getting better or worse? Is my municipality performing above or below average? Is the government's own plan on track or off track?

The answer to those questions will not always be comfortable for the government of the day. That is the point. An implementation monitoring system that only delivers good news is not a monitoring system. It is a public relations operation. The value of the system lies precisely in its willingness to show red indicators — to tell the truth about where the plan is failing, who is responsible, and what needs to change. A government that is genuinely committed to economic transformation should welcome such a system, because it provides the information needed to correct failures before they become catastrophes. A government that resists such a system is telling you something important about its relationship with accountability.

:::framework
**The Strategy-to-Execution Audit**

When government announces an economic strategy — national, provincial, or municipal — use this six-step audit to determine in under thirty minutes whether the strategy has the operational machinery to deliver results or whether it is a wish list dressed up as a plan.

**Step 1: Check for sequencing.**

Does the strategy specify what must happen first, second, and third — and why? Look for explicit dependency statements: "We cannot begin X until Y is in place." A strategy that presents twenty priorities simultaneously without sequencing them has not done the implementation thinking. South Africa's binding constraints must be addressed in order: infrastructure first, then resource optimisation, then efficiency-driven growth, then innovation. If the strategy skips stages, it will fail at the prerequisites it ignored.

**Step 2: Check for measurable KPIs with baselines and deadlines.**

For every objective, look for a specific indicator with a current baseline value, a target value, and a date. "Improve port efficiency" is not a KPI. "Reduce average ship turnaround time at Durban from 95 hours to 45 hours by March 2030, measured quarterly using TNPA operational data" is a KPI. Also check whether the strategy distinguishes between leading indicators (early warning signals) and lagging indicators (outcome measures). A strategy that only tracks outcomes will discover failure too late to correct course.

**Step 3: Check for a public dashboard.**

Are the KPIs published where citizens can see them? Look for a specific commitment to a public reporting platform, a reporting frequency (quarterly for leading indicators, annually for lagging indicators), and full methodological transparency. A strategy that measures its own performance internally but does not publish results publicly is accountable only to itself.

**Step 4: Check for a course-correction mechanism.**

What happens when targets are missed? Look for a graduated response framework: operational adjustments for minor deviations, strategic adjustments for significant misses, and strategic pivots for fundamental changes in circumstances. If the strategy says "we will review and adjust" without specifying who decides, what triggers the decision, and what approval process applies, there is no real mechanism — just a platitude.

**Step 5: Check for personal accountability.**

Is each target assigned to a specific institution and, ideally, a specific person? If the strategy says "the Department will improve export diversification" without specifying which official owns the target, what reporting obligations they have, and what happens if the target is missed for three consecutive years, then nobody is actually accountable. Look for performance agreements, reporting obligations, and consequence management provisions.

**Step 6: Check for institutional anchoring.**

Is the strategy embedded in instruments that survive changes of minister — the Medium Term Strategic Framework, Cabinet resolutions, parliamentary tracking mechanisms, departmental annual performance plans? A strategy that lives only in a minister's presentation slides will disappear when the minister is reshuffled. A strategy embedded in the MTSF and tracked by portfolio committees has institutional durability.

By applying this audit, you can quickly separate the plans that have a realistic chance of delivering from the wish lists that will generate press conferences and achieve nothing.
:::

## Practical Framework: The Implementation Readiness Checklist

Every strategy document that crosses your desk — whether it is a national economic strategy, a provincial growth plan, a municipal integrated development plan, or a sectoral master plan — can be evaluated against six questions. If the strategy can answer all six convincingly, it has the operational machinery to deliver. If it cannot, it is a wish list, regardless of how sophisticated its analysis or how eloquent its prose.

The first question is: is there a sequenced plan with clear phase gates? A strategy that tries to do everything simultaneously will achieve nothing. Look for a clear statement of what happens first, what happens second, and what happens third — and why that order was chosen. Look for explicit dependencies: "we cannot begin X until Y is in place." Look for phase gates: defined criteria that must be met before the next phase begins. A strategy that presents a list of priorities without sequencing them has not done the implementation thinking. It has produced an analytical document, not an operational plan.

The second question is: are there measurable KPIs at each phase? For every objective in the strategy, there should be a specific, numeric indicator with a baseline value, a target value, and a deadline. "Improve the business environment" is not a KPI. "Reduce the average time to register a business from 40 days to 7 days by March 2028, as measured by the CIPC registration data" is a KPI. Look for the distinction between leading and lagging indicators. A strategy that only measures outcomes (lagging indicators) will discover failure too late. A strategy that includes leading indicators gives itself the chance to correct course before outcomes deteriorate.

The third question is: is there a public dashboard tracking progress? The KPIs must be visible to the public, not buried in departmental reports that nobody reads. Look for a commitment to regular public reporting — quarterly for leading indicators, annually for lagging indicators. Look for a specific platform (a website, a published report) where the data will be available. A strategy that measures its own performance but does not publish the results is accountable only to itself, which is no accountability at all.

The fourth question is: is there a formal course-correction mechanism? The strategy must specify what happens when targets are missed. Not "we will review and adjust" — that is a platitude. Look for a graduated response framework: operational adjustments for minor deviations, strategic adjustments for significant misses, and strategic pivots for fundamental changes in circumstances. Look for specific decision-making authority: who decides to adjust, what evidence triggers the decision, and what approval process applies. A strategy without a course-correction mechanism is betting that everything will go according to plan. Nothing ever goes entirely according to plan.

The fifth question is: who is accountable for delivery, and what happens if they fail? Every target in the strategy should be assigned to a specific institution and, ideally, a specific person. Look for accountability provisions: performance agreements, reporting obligations, consequence management. If the strategy says "the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition will improve export diversification" but does not specify which official owns that target, what reporting obligations they have, and what happens if the target is missed for three consecutive years, then nobody is actually accountable. Accountability without consequences is just surveillance.

The sixth question is: has the strategy survived at least one change of minister? This is the toughest test. If a strategy was adopted under one minister and continues to be implemented — with the same KPIs, the same dashboard, the same accountability mechanisms — under a successor minister, it has achieved institutional embedding. If it was quietly shelved when the new minister arrived and replaced with a "refreshed" strategy that carries a new name but has no continuity with the previous plan, it was a ministerial project, not a national strategy. You cannot always answer this question in advance, but you can look for the institutional anchoring mechanisms described earlier: embedding in the MTSF, Cabinet mandates, parliamentary tracking. Strategies that lack these anchors are vulnerable to the reshuffle cycle. Strategies that have them are not invulnerable, but they are significantly more durable.

These six questions are your diagnostic tool. Apply them to any economic strategy you encounter — national, provincial, or local — and you will quickly distinguish the plans that have a realistic chance of delivering from the wish lists that will generate headlines and achieve nothing. In [Your Economic Citizenship](/articles/eg-5-3-your-economic-citizenship), we explore how you can use this diagnostic tool, along with the dashboard and the MPI, to exercise your rights and responsibilities as an economic citizen of South Africa. The strategy exists. The scorecard exists. The implementation plan exists. What happens next depends, in part, on whether citizens demand that it be followed.
