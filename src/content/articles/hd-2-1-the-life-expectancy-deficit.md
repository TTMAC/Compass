## The Bottom Line, Up Front

South Africa should have a life expectancy at birth of approximately seventy-three years. That is the number the Preston Curve — the well-established relationship between national income and population health — predicts for a country at South Africa's income level. Instead, the country's life expectancy sits at approximately sixty-three years. That ten-year deficit is not a statistical curiosity. It represents roughly five hundred thousand premature deaths per decade, millions of years of productive life lost, and a direct, measurable drag on the Human Development Index. The overwhelming explanation for this gap is the convergence of two infectious diseases: HIV/AIDS and tuberculosis. South Africa has the world's largest HIV epidemic — approximately 7.7 million people living with HIV — and one of the world's highest tuberculosis burdens, with the two diseases feeding on each other in a syndemic that has reshaped the country's demographic profile more dramatically than any other force since colonialism.

The good news — and there is genuine good news in this story — is that this deficit is not destiny. South Africa's HIV treatment programme, the world's largest antiretroviral therapy programme, has already clawed life expectancy back from a nadir of approximately fifty-two years in 2005. The UNAIDS treatment cascade — the sequence from diagnosis to treatment initiation to viral suppression — shows South Africa performing well on two of three targets: ninety-five percent of people living with HIV know their status, and ninety-three percent of those on treatment are virally suppressed. The bottleneck is the second target: only seventy-nine percent of those who know their status have initiated treatment. That sixteen-percentage-point gap between the first and second ninety-five represents approximately 1.2 million people who know they are HIV-positive but are not on antiretroviral therapy. Closing that gap is the single most cost-effective intervention available for improving South Africa's HDI score. It is, in the language of this series, the highest "points per rand" opportunity in the entire human development portfolio.

This article maps the terrain. It explains why South Africa's life expectancy is where it is, what specific failures and successes have brought it to this point, and what it would take — in concrete, measurable terms — to close the deficit. If you have read [South Africa's Report Card](/articles/hd-1-2-south-africas-report-card), you understand how life expectancy feeds into the HDI calculation. This article shows you where the years are being lost and where they can be recovered.

## The Preston Curve and South Africa's Outlier Position

In 1975, the demographer Samuel Preston published a paper that would become one of the most cited in public health scholarship. Using data from nations across income levels, Preston demonstrated that the relationship between national income and life expectancy follows a distinctive curve: life expectancy rises steeply with income at low levels — moving from $1,000 to $5,000 per capita produces dramatic health gains — then flattens at higher incomes, as the basic infrastructure of public health (clean water, sanitation, vaccination, nutrition) is established and further gains require more targeted and expensive interventions. The curve that bears his name has been updated repeatedly with contemporary data, and the fundamental shape holds: income buys health, but with diminishing returns.

What makes the Preston Curve analytically powerful is not the average relationship but the deviations from it. Countries that sit above the curve — that achieve life expectancies higher than their income would predict — are doing something right with their health systems, their public health infrastructure, or their social determinants of health. Countries that sit below the curve are failing in one or more of these domains. The distance from the curve provides a rough but useful measure of health system performance, independent of wealth.

South Africa is one of the most dramatic outliers below the Preston Curve in the contemporary world. At a GDP per capita of approximately $7,000 in current US dollar terms — placing it in the upper-middle-income category — the curve predicts a life expectancy of roughly seventy-two to seventy-four years. The actual figure, as of the most recent data from Statistics South Africa's mid-year population estimates, is approximately sixty-three years. That ten-year gap is among the largest negative deviations of any country in the world. To find comparable deviations, you would need to look at countries experiencing active conflict, famine, or state collapse.

Consider the comparators. Brazil, at a similar income level, achieves a life expectancy of approximately seventy-five years. Thailand, at a slightly lower income level, achieves approximately seventy-seven years. Mexico achieves approximately seventy-five years. Colombia achieves approximately seventy-three years. Even countries with significantly lower incomes than South Africa outperform it on this measure: Vietnam, at roughly half South Africa's GDP per capita, has a life expectancy of approximately seventy-five years. Bangladesh, at roughly one-third South Africa's income, achieves approximately seventy-three years. These are not countries with vastly superior health systems or unlimited public resources. They are countries that have not had their demographic profiles reshaped by an HIV epidemic that, at its peak, was killing approximately a thousand people per day.

The Preston Curve deviation tells you something that no amount of political rhetoric can obscure: South Africa's health outcomes are not consistent with its wealth. The country is rich enough — on paper — to keep its people alive for seventy-three years on average. It is failing to do so by a full decade. Understanding why requires confronting the specific disease burdens that drive the gap.

:::expert
The Preston Curve relationship has been critiqued and refined over the decades since Preston's original paper. Researchers have noted that the curve has shifted upward over time — at any given income level, life expectancy is higher today than it was in 1975, reflecting improvements in medical technology, public health knowledge, and international health cooperation that benefit countries at all income levels. This "upward shift" means that South Africa's deviation is even more striking than it would have been in Preston's original data: the curve has moved up, but South Africa has, for extended periods, moved in the wrong direction. The curve has also been critiqued for its inability to capture within-country inequality. South Africa's private healthcare sector delivers life expectancies that are fully consistent with the Preston Curve — indeed, the wealthiest South Africans enjoy life expectancies comparable to those in high-income countries. It is the public-sector-dependent majority — approximately eighty-four percent of the population — whose life expectancies drag the national average far below the curve. This within-country Preston Curve deviation mirrors, at the national level, the broader global pattern: inequality kills, and it kills in measurable, predictable ways.
:::

## The HIV Catastrophe: A Timeline

To understand where South Africa's life expectancy stands today, you must understand how it got here. The story of HIV/AIDS in South Africa is, in compressed form, the story of the greatest public health catastrophe in the country's modern history — one that was amplified immeasurably by political failure.

The first cases of AIDS in South Africa were identified in 1982, among gay men in Johannesburg and Cape Town. Through the 1980s, the epidemic remained relatively contained within specific populations, and the apartheid government — preoccupied with maintaining white minority rule against mounting internal and external pressure — paid it little systematic attention. Epidemiological surveillance was limited, and what data existed suggested a much smaller epidemic than was developing in East and Central Africa. This was misleading. HIV was establishing itself in the general heterosexual population, driven by the same structural factors that would later fuel its explosive growth: labour migration, urban-rural mobility, gender inequality, and poverty.

The transition to democracy in 1994 coincided with the acceleration of the epidemic. Nelson Mandela's government inherited an HIV prevalence rate that was climbing sharply — antenatal clinic surveillance data, the primary tracking mechanism, showed prevalence among pregnant women rising from approximately 0.7 percent in 1990 to approximately 7.6 percent in 1994. The new government recognised the threat in principle — the Mandela administration launched the National AIDS Plan — but the response was fragmented, underfunded, and quickly overshadowed by the overwhelming demands of building a new democratic state. The window for aggressive early intervention, the window that Uganda and Thailand exploited in the late 1980s and early 1990s to contain their epidemics before they became generalised, was closing rapidly.

Then came the catastrophe within the catastrophe. President Thabo Mbeki, who succeeded Mandela in 1999, publicly questioned the scientific consensus that HIV causes AIDS. Drawing on the discredited claims of a small group of dissident scientists — most prominently Peter Duesberg of the University of California, Berkeley — Mbeki argued that AIDS in Africa was caused by poverty, malnutrition, and other conditions rather than by a virus. His health minister, Manto Tshabalala-Msimang, became internationally notorious for promoting garlic, beetroot, and lemon juice as alternatives to antiretroviral therapy. The government delayed the rollout of antiretrovirals in the public health system, challenged court orders requiring the provision of nevirapine to prevent mother-to-child transmission, and created an environment of confusion and stigma around treatment.

The human cost was staggering. A study published in the Journal of Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndromes in 2008 by researchers at the Harvard School of Public Health estimated that the Mbeki government's delay in rolling out antiretroviral therapy resulted in approximately 330,000 preventable deaths between 2000 and 2005. Other estimates have placed the figure higher. During this period, life expectancy in South Africa fell from approximately sixty-two years in 1994 to approximately fifty-two years by 2005 — a decline of ten years in a single decade, a peacetime demographic collapse without precedent in modern history outside of famine or genocide.

The reversal began in 2004, when the government — under sustained pressure from civil society, particularly the Treatment Action Campaign, from the courts, and from international partners — finally authorised the public-sector rollout of antiretroviral therapy. The programme expanded rapidly, particularly after Mbeki's departure from office in 2008. Under the health ministries of Barbara Hogan and subsequently Aaron Motsoaledi, South Africa's antiretroviral therapy programme became the largest in the world. By 2023, approximately 5.7 million people were receiving antiretroviral therapy through the public health system.

The impact on life expectancy has been dramatic. From the nadir of approximately fifty-two years in 2005, life expectancy recovered to approximately sixty-two years by 2015 and to approximately sixty-three to sixty-four years by 2023. This is an extraordinary public health achievement — a gain of more than ten years of life expectancy in less than two decades, driven primarily by a single intervention: providing antiretroviral drugs to people who would otherwise have died. But it is also a recovery to roughly where South Africa was in 1994, before the epidemic's peak impact. Three decades after the transition to democracy, the country has fought its way back to its starting point on life expectancy. It has not yet moved beyond it.

:::takeaway
South Africa's life expectancy fell from approximately sixty-two years in 1994 to fifty-two years in 2005 — a decade of lost life driven overwhelmingly by HIV/AIDS and amplified by political denial. The subsequent antiretroviral rollout, the world's largest, has recovered life expectancy to approximately sixty-three years. This represents an extraordinary achievement in absolute terms but a devastating indictment in relative terms: three decades of democracy have produced a net gain of roughly one year of life expectancy, while comparable countries have added five to ten years over the same period.
:::

## The Treatment Cascade: 95-79-93

The UNAIDS 95-95-95 targets provide the most precise available framework for understanding where South Africa's HIV response is succeeding and where it is failing. The targets, first articulated by UNAIDS in 2014, specify that by 2025, ninety-five percent of people living with HIV should know their status, ninety-five percent of those who know their status should be on sustained antiretroviral therapy, and ninety-five percent of those on treatment should be virally suppressed. Achieving all three targets simultaneously means that approximately eighty-six percent of all people living with HIV would be virally suppressed — a level that epidemiological models suggest would be sufficient to end the epidemic as a public health threat through the "treatment as prevention" effect, whereby viral suppression dramatically reduces onward transmission.

South Africa's treatment cascade, as of the most recent data compiled by UNAIDS and the National Department of Health, stands at approximately 95-79-93. Let us unpack each number.

The first ninety-five: ninety-five percent of people living with HIV know their status. This is a genuine success. South Africa has invested heavily in HIV testing and counselling, including provider-initiated testing in healthcare facilities, community-based testing campaigns, and the availability of HIV self-testing kits. The massive investment in testing infrastructure means that the vast majority of the estimated 7.7 million people living with HIV in South Africa have been tested and know their status. This is not a trivial achievement. It required overcoming deep-seated stigma, building testing infrastructure in rural and peri-urban areas, and creating a policy environment that normalised testing as a routine part of healthcare.

The second ninety-five — or rather, the seventy-nine percent that should be ninety-five — is where the cascade breaks down. Of those who know their status, only approximately seventy-nine percent have initiated and are currently on antiretroviral therapy. That means approximately twenty-one percent of people who know they are HIV-positive — roughly 1.2 million individuals — are not on treatment. They know they need treatment. They have been tested and counselled. But somewhere between the testing room and the treatment programme, they have fallen through the cracks.

Why? The reasons are multiple, overlapping, and deeply rooted in the structural failures of the health system and the social environment.

First, access. Despite the massive expansion of antiretroviral therapy sites — from a handful in 2004 to thousands of clinics today — geographic access remains a barrier, particularly in rural KwaZulu-Natal, the Eastern Cape, Limpopo, and Mpumalanga. Patients in rural areas may need to travel significant distances to reach a clinic, take time off work (in an economy where precarious employment means a day of missed work can mean a day without food), and wait in queues that can stretch for hours. The cumulative friction of these access barriers is sufficient to deter a significant proportion of people from initiating treatment, particularly young men who may feel healthy and see no immediate reason to endure the inconvenience.

Second, stigma. Despite decades of public education campaigns, HIV stigma remains a potent force in South African society. Disclosure of HIV status — to employers, to family members, to sexual partners — carries real social and economic risks. Fear of stigma drives people away from treatment facilities, particularly in smaller communities where attending an HIV clinic may be tantamount to public disclosure. Young men, in particular, have been identified in multiple studies as the demographic group least likely to initiate treatment after testing positive, driven by a toxic combination of stigma, denial, and norms of masculinity that equate health-seeking behaviour with weakness.

Third, health system failures. South Africa's primary healthcare system — the system that delivers the vast majority of antiretroviral therapy — operates under enormous strain. Clinics are understaffed, with vacancy rates for professional nurses exceeding thirty percent in some provinces. Drug stockouts, while less frequent than in the early years of the rollout, still occur. The administrative burden of initiating treatment — registration, baseline blood tests, adherence counselling, follow-up scheduling — creates friction that can be particularly deterring for patients who are mobile, employed informally, or managing multiple responsibilities. The shift to the "Universal Test and Treat" policy in 2016, which eliminated CD4 count thresholds for treatment initiation and made anyone with a positive HIV test eligible for immediate treatment, was clinically correct but placed additional strain on an already stretched system.

Fourth, treatment interruption. Not all of the twenty-one percent who are "not on treatment" have never started. A significant proportion initiated treatment and then stopped — a phenomenon known as treatment interruption or, in older terminology, "defaulting." Reasons for treatment interruption include side effects of medication, feeling healthy and seeing no reason to continue, relocation (particularly labour migrants who move between provinces and lose continuity of care), mental health conditions including depression and substance abuse, and, again, stigma. The consequences of treatment interruption are severe: viral rebound, progression to AIDS, development of drug resistance, and resumed transmission risk.

:::expert
The treatment cascade gap at the second ninety-five has been the subject of intensive research and programmatic innovation. South Africa's National Strategic Plan for HIV, TB, and STIs 2023-2028 identifies the linkage-to-care gap as a primary strategic priority and targets specific interventions: differentiated service delivery models that reduce clinic visits for stable patients (including multi-month dispensing, community-based adherence groups, and external pickup points for medication), welcome-back campaigns that reduce barriers for patients who have interrupted treatment, and targeted programmes for "hard to reach" populations including young men, mobile workers, and people who use drugs. The evidence base for these interventions is robust — differentiated service delivery, in particular, has been shown in multiple randomised controlled trials and implementation studies in sub-Saharan Africa to improve retention in care without compromising clinical outcomes. The challenge is not knowing what to do but scaling what works across a health system that serves nearly eight million people living with HIV. The Thembisa model — the leading mathematical model of HIV in South Africa, developed by researchers at the University of Cape Town — projects that achieving the 95-95-95 targets would reduce new HIV infections from approximately 210,000 per year to fewer than 100,000 and would add approximately three to five years to national life expectancy over the following decade.
:::

## Tuberculosis: The Silent Partner

If HIV is the headline story of South Africa's life expectancy deficit, tuberculosis is the quietly devastating subtext. South Africa has one of the highest tuberculosis burdens in the world — the World Health Organization estimates an incidence rate of approximately 468 per 100,000 population, placing South Africa among the top ten highest-burden countries globally. Approximately 58,000 South Africans die of tuberculosis each year — a number that, in a country fixated on its murder statistics, receives astonishingly little public attention.

The relationship between TB and HIV in South Africa is synergistic and devastating. HIV weakens the immune system, making HIV-positive individuals dramatically more susceptible to developing active TB — estimates suggest that people living with HIV are fifteen to twenty-two times more likely to develop active TB than HIV-negative individuals. Conversely, active TB accelerates the progression of HIV disease. In South Africa, approximately sixty percent of TB cases occur in people who are also living with HIV. This co-infection drives mortality: TB is the leading cause of death among people living with HIV globally, and South Africa, with its enormous HIV-positive population, bears a disproportionate share of this burden.

South Africa's tuberculosis crisis has deep historical roots that long predate the HIV epidemic. The mining industry — the foundation of the industrial economy for over a century — created the conditions for TB transmission on a massive scale. Migrant labourers, drawn from across southern Africa, were housed in crowded compounds, worked in dusty underground conditions that damaged their lungs (silicosis is a powerful predisposing factor for TB), and received inadequate healthcare. When they became ill, they were frequently sent back to their rural communities — in South Africa, Mozambique, Lesotho, Swaziland, and beyond — carrying the TB bacillus with them. The mining industry, in this very literal sense, seeded tuberculosis across the subcontinent. The legacy persists: former mining communities in the Eastern Cape, KwaZulu-Natal, and the Free State continue to have among the highest TB incidence rates in the country.

Drug-resistant tuberculosis adds another layer of crisis. South Africa has one of the highest burdens of multidrug-resistant TB (MDR-TB) and extensively drug-resistant TB (XDR-TB) in the world. MDR-TB requires treatment regimens that are longer (nine to twenty months versus six months for drug-susceptible TB), more expensive (by a factor of ten or more), more toxic (with more severe side effects), and less effective (cure rates of approximately seventy to seventy-five percent versus ninety percent or higher for drug-susceptible TB). XDR-TB, resistant to even more drugs, was first identified as a distinct category in KwaZulu-Natal in 2006, in a cluster of cases at a Church of Scotland Hospital in Tugela Ferry where virtually all patients died within weeks of diagnosis. That outbreak — fifty-two of fifty-three patients dead, most of them HIV-positive — made international headlines and exposed the terrifying potential of drug-resistant TB in the context of HIV co-infection.

The TB response in South Africa has improved significantly, but from a very low base. The introduction of GeneXpert molecular diagnostics — which can identify TB and rifampicin resistance within two hours, compared to the weeks required for traditional culture-based methods — has transformed diagnostic speed and accuracy. South Africa was the first country in the world to deploy GeneXpert at scale, and the technology is now available in most primary healthcare facilities. New treatment regimens for drug-resistant TB — including the shorter, more effective bedaquiline-based regimens — have improved outcomes. The TB mortality rate has declined substantially from its peak.

But the TB programme still operates in the shadow of the HIV programme. TB receives less funding, less political attention, less media coverage, and less civil society advocacy than HIV. The Treatment Action Campaign, which was so effective in pressuring the government on antiretroviral therapy, has no TB equivalent with comparable political influence. TB is a disease of poverty, overcrowding, and malnutrition — and addressing those underlying drivers requires structural interventions that go far beyond the health system.

:::takeaway
Tuberculosis kills approximately 58,000 South Africans per year and is deeply intertwined with the HIV epidemic — sixty percent of TB cases occur in people living with HIV. South Africa's mining history seeded TB across the subcontinent, and drug-resistant strains add a layer of crisis. Despite diagnostic improvements like GeneXpert, TB receives far less political attention and funding than HIV relative to its mortality burden. Any serious strategy to close the life expectancy deficit must treat TB and HIV as a single integrated challenge, not as separate diseases with separate programmes.
:::

## Maternal and Child Mortality

Life expectancy is an aggregate measure, and its components reveal important patterns about where and how years of life are being lost. Maternal and child mortality — deaths during pregnancy, childbirth, and early childhood — contribute significantly to South Africa's life expectancy deficit, and the pattern of progress (and the remaining gaps) tells a story about the health system's strengths and weaknesses.

South Africa's under-five mortality rate — the probability of a child dying before their fifth birthday — has declined substantially, from approximately eighty per thousand live births in the early 2000s (at the height of the AIDS epidemic, when mother-to-child transmission was unchecked) to approximately thirty-three per thousand by the most recent estimates. This decline is primarily attributable to the prevention of mother-to-child transmission (PMTCT) programme, which has been one of South Africa's most unambiguous public health successes. The PMTCT programme has reduced the rate of HIV transmission from mother to child during pregnancy and breastfeeding from approximately thirty percent in the pre-treatment era to approximately one percent today. Given that paediatric HIV infection was, at the epidemic's peak, the leading cause of under-five mortality, the impact of this programme on child survival has been enormous.

But an under-five mortality rate of thirty-three per thousand is still high by upper-middle-income standards. Brazil's is approximately fourteen. Thailand's is approximately eight. Malaysia's is approximately seven. The gap is partially explained by residual HIV-related deaths but increasingly by other causes: neonatal complications (prematurity, birth asphyxia, neonatal sepsis), diarrhoeal disease, pneumonia, and malnutrition. These are, overwhelmingly, deaths that functioning primary healthcare systems prevent. They point to the quality-of-care challenges at facility level — the gap between having a clinic and having a clinic that functions well enough to identify danger signs, manage complications, and refer effectively.

Neonatal mortality — deaths in the first twenty-eight days of life — has been more resistant to improvement than post-neonatal mortality. South Africa's neonatal mortality rate of approximately eleven to twelve per thousand live births accounts for more than a third of all under-five deaths. Reducing neonatal mortality requires a different set of interventions than reducing post-neonatal mortality: skilled birth attendance, emergency obstetric care, neonatal resuscitation capability, and kangaroo mother care for premature infants. These are facility-based, skill-intensive interventions that depend on the availability and competence of midwives and neonatal nurses — precisely the healthcare workers in shortest supply.

Maternal mortality — deaths during pregnancy, childbirth, or within forty-two days of delivery — presents a mixed picture. The maternal mortality ratio has declined from catastrophically high levels in the mid-2000s (when AIDS-related deaths during pregnancy drove the ratio above five hundred per hundred thousand live births in some provinces) to approximately one hundred and twenty to one hundred and fifty per hundred thousand live births. Again, the decline is primarily attributable to antiretroviral therapy, which has transformed HIV from a death sentence during pregnancy to a manageable chronic condition. But a maternal mortality ratio of one hundred and twenty or more is still unacceptable for a country at South Africa's income level — Malaysia's is approximately twenty-one, and Thailand's is approximately twenty-nine — and the non-HIV causes of maternal death (hypertensive disorders, obstetric haemorrhage, and complications of caesarean section) point to health system quality challenges that antiretroviral therapy alone cannot resolve.

The district-level variation in maternal and child mortality is stark. The best-performing districts — typically in the Western Cape and Gauteng — achieve under-five mortality rates and maternal mortality ratios comparable to upper-middle-income averages. The worst-performing districts — typically in the Eastern Cape, KwaZulu-Natal, and Limpopo — have rates two to three times higher. This variation within a single country, under a single national health policy, with funding flowing through the same equitable share formula, reveals the extent to which outcomes depend on provincial and district-level implementation capacity — the unglamorous work of staffing clinics, maintaining supply chains, supervising quality, and managing referral systems.

## Non-Communicable Diseases: The Emerging Burden

Even as South Africa continues to fight the infectious disease epidemics that have dominated its health profile for three decades, a new challenge is mounting. Non-communicable diseases — diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, cancer, chronic respiratory diseases — are rising rapidly and are increasingly contributing to the overall mortality burden. South Africa is experiencing what epidemiologists call the "double burden of disease" or, more precisely, a colliding epidemiological transition: the unfinished business of infectious disease meeting the rising tide of lifestyle-related chronic conditions.

The numbers are arresting. Approximately one in three South African adults has hypertension — and the majority are either undiagnosed or inadequately treated. Diabetes prevalence has been rising steadily and is estimated at approximately twelve percent of the adult population, placing South Africa among the highest-prevalence countries in sub-Saharan Africa. Cardiovascular disease is now the second leading cause of death in South Africa after HIV/TB. Cancer incidence is increasing, particularly breast cancer (which is often diagnosed at advanced stages due to inadequate screening), cervical cancer (closely linked to HPV and HIV co-infection), and prostate cancer.

The drivers of the NCD epidemic are familiar from the global literature but take distinctly South African forms. Urbanisation has transformed dietary patterns: the "nutrition transition" has shifted millions of South Africans from traditional diets rich in unprocessed foods to diets heavy in refined carbohydrates, sugar, processed meats, and ultra-processed foods. The sugar-sweetened beverage levy introduced in 2018 was a significant policy innovation — South Africa was among the first countries in Africa to implement such a levy — but its impact has been modest relative to the scale of the dietary transformation underway. Physical inactivity is rising, particularly in urban areas where walkability is poor, public spaces are perceived as unsafe, and the built environment is designed around automobile transport. Tobacco use, while declining, remains prevalent, and alcohol consumption — South Africa is among the heaviest-drinking countries in Africa — contributes to liver disease, cardiovascular disease, and cancer.

The health system implications are severe. Non-communicable diseases require long-term management — lifelong medication, regular monitoring, lifestyle modification support — that places fundamentally different demands on a health system than acute care. South Africa's primary healthcare system was designed in the post-apartheid period primarily around the infectious disease model: episodic visits, acute treatment, and vertical disease programmes (HIV, TB, maternal and child health). It was not designed for the chronic disease management model that NCDs require: regular scheduled visits, medication titration, patient self-management support, and integrated care across conditions. The Ideal Clinic programme — the government's quality improvement initiative for primary healthcare — has begun to incorporate NCD management indicators, but the integration of NCD care into a system already strained by HIV and TB is a formidable organisational challenge.

:::expert
The convergence of infectious and non-communicable diseases creates clinical complexities that are poorly addressed by vertical programme structures. A patient living with HIV who is on antiretroviral therapy, has developed diabetes (antiretroviral-associated metabolic syndrome is a recognised complication of long-term treatment), has hypertension, and has a history of TB may need to attend multiple clinic queues, see multiple providers, and manage multiple medication regimens — a burden that undermines adherence to all conditions simultaneously. The concept of "integrated chronic disease management" — treating HIV, TB, diabetes, and hypertension through a single clinical platform rather than separate vertical programmes — has been a policy aspiration in South Africa since the mid-2010s, but implementation has been uneven. The research evidence, including implementation studies conducted in South Africa by researchers at the University of Cape Town, the University of the Witwatersrand, and the Africa Health Research Institute, suggests that integrated care models can improve outcomes for all conditions, but require significant reorganisation of clinic workflows, retraining of staff, and restructuring of supply chains. The NCD burden is projected to increase substantially over the next two decades as South Africa's population ages and as the first generation to survive long-term on antiretroviral therapy develops the metabolic complications of prolonged treatment. The health system will need to manage this transition while simultaneously maintaining the HIV and TB programmes that are responsible for the life expectancy gains of the past fifteen years.
:::

## The Health System: District Health Barometer Data

Understanding South Africa's life expectancy deficit requires looking not just at disease burdens but at the health system through which care is delivered. The District Health Barometer — published annually by the Health Systems Trust, an independent research organisation — provides the most comprehensive available picture of primary healthcare performance across South Africa's fifty-two health districts and eight metropolitan municipalities.

The data consistently reveal a system of extreme internal variation. On almost every indicator — facility utilisation rates, immunisation coverage, antenatal care visits, cervical cancer screening, treatment adherence — the gap between the best-performing and worst-performing districts is a factor of two to three. The Western Cape's Cape Winelands district and the City of Cape Town consistently rank among the top performers. The Eastern Cape's OR Tambo and Alfred Nzo districts, KwaZulu-Natal's Umkhanyakude and Zululand districts, and Limpopo's Vhembe district consistently rank among the lowest performers.

What drives this variation? The District Health Barometer data, combined with qualitative research and health systems analysis, point to several factors.

Human resources are the most critical. South Africa has approximately 0.9 doctors per thousand population — the WHO recommends a minimum of one per thousand for basic healthcare delivery. But this national average masks extreme maldistribution. The private sector employs approximately seventy percent of South Africa's specialist doctors and approximately fifty percent of general practitioners, while serving approximately sixteen percent of the population. Within the public sector, urban facilities are better staffed than rural ones, and the Western Cape and Gauteng are better staffed than other provinces. Some rural clinics operate with a single professional nurse serving a catchment population of twenty thousand or more. Vacancy rates for critical cadres — professional nurses, medical officers, pharmacists — exceed thirty percent in some districts and fifty percent for some categories of specialist in rural areas.

Infrastructure quality matters. The Ideal Clinic programme — launched in 2013 with the goal of ensuring that every primary healthcare clinic meets a set of defined quality standards — has improved the physical infrastructure and basic equipment availability of many facilities. But "ideal clinic" status measures inputs and processes, not outcomes. A clinic can achieve ideal clinic status by having the right equipment, the right medicine stocks, and the right administrative procedures in place, while still delivering poor clinical care because its staff are poorly supervised, demoralised, or undertrained. The gap between structural quality (is the equipment there?) and process quality (is care being delivered correctly?) remains significant.

Supply chain reliability has improved but remains a vulnerability. The availability of essential medicines — antiretrovirals, TB treatment, contraceptives, chronic disease medication — has improved through centralised procurement by the National Department of Health and distribution through provincial depots. But "last mile" logistics — getting medicine from the provincial depot to the district clinic and keeping it in stock — remain prone to disruption, particularly in remote areas with poor road infrastructure.

The District Health Barometer data, read carefully, tell a story about the unfinished decentralisation of South Africa's health system. The Constitution assigns health as a concurrent national-provincial function, and the National Health Act structures the system around district-based primary healthcare. But the capacity to manage a health district effectively — to recruit and retain staff, maintain infrastructure, manage supply chains, supervise clinical quality, and engage communities — varies enormously across provinces and districts. The strongest districts function as reasonably competent healthcare delivery systems. The weakest districts function more like administrative shells within which individual facilities operate autonomously, with little coordination, quality control, or accountability.

:::takeaway
South Africa's health system is characterised by extreme internal variation: the best-performing districts achieve upper-middle-income health outcomes while the worst-performing districts achieve outcomes comparable to low-income countries. Human resource maldistribution — with the private sector employing seventy percent of specialists while serving sixteen percent of the population — is the single most important driver of this variation. Supply chain reliability, infrastructure quality, and management capacity all contribute to the gap between South Africa's health policy aspirations and the reality of healthcare delivery at the facility level.
:::

## The HDI Points-Per-Rand Calculation

This article has mapped the terrain of South Africa's life expectancy deficit — the diseases, the health system failures, the structural challenges. Now let us turn to the question that frames this entire series: where do you get the most human development improvement per rand of public investment?

The Human Development Index calculates its health dimension using life expectancy at birth, with a minimum value of twenty years and a maximum value of eighty-five years. South Africa's current life expectancy of approximately sixty-three years translates to a health index score of approximately 0.662. If life expectancy increased to sixty-eight years — a plausible target if the treatment cascade gap were closed and TB outcomes improved — the health index would rise to approximately 0.738. If life expectancy reached seventy-one years — achievable with treatment cascade closure, TB improvements, and meaningful NCD prevention — the health index would reach approximately 0.785.

These are not small numbers in HDI terms. An increase from 0.662 to 0.738 in the health dimension alone would increase South Africa's overall HDI by approximately 0.025 — equivalent to the total HDI improvement that many countries achieve in a decade. An increase to 0.785 would add approximately 0.041 to the overall HDI. For context, the total difference between South Africa's current HDI of approximately 0.713 and the "high human development" threshold of 0.800 is 0.087. Closing the treatment cascade gap and improving TB outcomes would get South Africa roughly halfway to that threshold through health improvements alone.

Now consider the cost. The per-patient cost of antiretroviral therapy in South Africa's public health system has declined dramatically over the past two decades, from approximately $10,000 per patient per year in the early days of the rollout to approximately $150 to $200 per patient per year today, thanks to the use of generic drugs, fixed-dose combinations, and bulk procurement. Initiating treatment for the approximately 1.2 million people who know their status but are not on treatment would cost, in direct drug costs, approximately R3.5 billion to R5 billion per year — significant, but a fraction of the approximately R260 billion annual public health budget. The additional costs — staffing, laboratory testing, facility maintenance — are harder to estimate precisely but are marginal rather than transformative, given that the treatment infrastructure already exists and the challenge is utilisation rather than construction.

Compare this cost to other potential HDI-improving investments. Improving the education index by a comparable amount would require increasing mean years of schooling from approximately ten to approximately twelve and improving expected years of schooling from approximately fourteen to approximately fifteen — changes that would take a generation to achieve and would require sustained investment in teacher quality, school infrastructure, and curriculum reform across twenty-five thousand schools. Improving the income index by a comparable amount would require increasing GDP per capita from approximately $7,000 to approximately $9,000 — economic growth of approximately thirty percent, which, at South Africa's recent growth rates of one to two percent per year, would take fifteen to twenty years.

The arithmetic is unambiguous. Closing the HIV treatment cascade gap is the single most cost-effective intervention available for improving South Africa's HDI. The infrastructure is largely in place. The drugs are affordable. The clinical protocols are well established. The barriers are implementation challenges — access, stigma, health system capacity — not technological or scientific unknowns. In the language of this series, health is where the points-per-rand ratio is highest.

:::expert
The "points per rand" framing is a simplification that serves analytical clarity but should be interpreted with appropriate caveats. First, HDI improvements across dimensions are not perfectly substitutable in terms of their welfare impact — a year of additional life expectancy is not equivalent, in terms of human wellbeing, to an additional year of schooling, even if they produce similar changes in the HDI number. Second, the health investments described here have broader returns beyond their direct HDI impact. People who are alive and healthy work, earn income, raise children, pay taxes, and contribute to economic growth. The economic return on health investment — the GDP growth attributable to improved population health — has been estimated by researchers at the Lancet Commission on Investing in Health at approximately nine to twenty times the initial investment in low- and middle-income countries. Third, the interactions between HDI dimensions mean that health investments can amplify education and income gains: healthy children learn better, healthy adults work more productively, and healthy parents invest more in their children's education. The case for prioritising health investment is therefore stronger than the HDI arithmetic alone suggests — but the HDI arithmetic alone is already compelling.
:::

## What Closing the Gap Would Take: A Policy Roadmap

Understanding the problem is necessary but not sufficient. What would it actually take to close the treatment cascade gap and begin addressing the broader health drivers of South Africa's life expectancy deficit?

The first priority is closing the second ninety-five. This requires a package of interventions that are individually well-evidenced and collectively achievable within existing fiscal constraints. Differentiated service delivery — tailoring the frequency and modality of clinic visits to patients' clinical stability — has been shown to reduce the burden on patients and facilities simultaneously. Stable patients on antiretroviral therapy do not need to visit a clinic every month; they can collect medication from community pickup points, pharmacy dispensing units, or adherence group meetings. This frees clinic capacity for new initiations and complex patients while reducing the friction that deters people from starting and staying on treatment. Multi-month dispensing — providing three or six months of medication at once rather than one month at a time — reduces clinic visits further and has been shown in South African implementation studies to improve retention without compromising outcomes.

Welcome-back programmes are needed for the significant number of people who have interrupted treatment. Traditional health system approaches to treatment interruption have often been punitive — patients who miss appointments are labelled "defaulters" and may face judgmental attitudes from healthcare workers when they attempt to re-engage. Evidence-based approaches treat treatment interruption as a systems failure rather than a patient failure, and design re-engagement pathways that are welcoming, non-judgmental, and clinically appropriate (including resistance testing before restarting therapy, to ensure the regimen remains effective).

Targeted interventions for young men are essential. Men aged fifteen to thirty-four consistently have the lowest rates of HIV testing, treatment initiation, and treatment retention of any demographic group. The reasons are well documented in the qualitative literature: norms of masculinity that stigmatise health-seeking behaviour, working conditions that make clinic attendance difficult, and health system designs that are implicitly feminised (clinics that are primarily staffed by women, during hours that suit women's schedules, in spaces that feel unwelcoming to men). Male-friendly clinic hours, workplace-based testing and treatment, and male-targeted health communication have all shown promise in pilot studies but have not been scaled systematically.

On tuberculosis, the priorities are active case-finding, infection prevention, and treatment completion. Active case-finding — going out into communities and workplaces to identify people with TB symptoms rather than waiting for them to present at facilities — has been shown to increase diagnosis rates and reduce the infectious period. Infection prevention in congregate settings — including the mining sector, prisons, and crowded informal settlements — requires investments in ventilation, UV germicidal irradiation, and workplace health programmes. Treatment completion requires the same differentiated, patient-centred approaches that have worked for HIV: community-based support, reduced clinic visit burdens, and culturally sensitive adherence counselling.

On non-communicable diseases, the priorities are primary prevention and integrated chronic disease management. South Africa's sugar-sweetened beverage levy should be strengthened and expanded. Tobacco taxation, which has been one of the country's genuine public health success stories (smoking prevalence has declined substantially since the introduction of aggressive tobacco taxation in the late 1990s), should continue. Population-level salt reduction — South Africa was a global pioneer in mandating sodium limits in processed foods in 2016 — should be expanded and enforced. And the primary healthcare system must be progressively re-engineered to manage chronic diseases alongside infectious diseases, through integrated clinical platforms, electronic health records, and community health worker programmes that support both infectious and chronic disease patients.

None of this is revolutionary. None of it requires technological breakthroughs or vast new fiscal resources. What it requires is sustained, competent execution — the unglamorous work of health system strengthening that produces no headlines but saves lives by the tens of thousands. This is the central paradox of South Africa's life expectancy deficit: the country knows what to do, it can afford what to do, and it has the institutional infrastructure to do it. The gap between knowledge and execution is where the lost years reside.

:::takeaway
Closing South Africa's life expectancy deficit requires no technological breakthroughs. The treatment cascade gap can be narrowed through differentiated service delivery, multi-month dispensing, welcome-back programmes, and targeted interventions for young men. TB requires active case-finding and infection prevention. NCDs require population-level prevention and integrated chronic disease management. The total additional cost is a fraction of the existing health budget. The barrier is implementation capacity, not knowledge or resources — and the HDI return on investment is the highest available across any dimension of human development.
:::

## The Road From Sixty-Three to Seventy-Three

South Africa's life expectancy of sixty-three years is not a number etched in stone. It is the output of specific, identifiable, addressable failures — a treatment cascade that leaks at the second target, a TB programme that operates in the shadow of HIV, a primary healthcare system of extreme internal variation, and an emerging NCD burden that the system is not yet configured to manage.

The Preston Curve says this country should be at seventy-three. Getting there is not a matter of a single intervention or a single budget cycle. It is a generational project that requires sustained political commitment, competent health system management, and the willingness to do the boring, difficult, unglamorous work of closing gaps rather than launching new programmes.

But the arithmetic is clear, and the pathway is mapped. Every percentage point added to the second ninety-five saves thousands of lives. Every TB case found and cured prevents onward transmission. Every hypertensive patient diagnosed and treated prevents a stroke or heart attack. And every year added to life expectancy moves South Africa closer to the human development level its income has already earned — the level where it should be, but is not yet.
