## The Bottom Line, Up Front

South Africa holds approximately 50,000 people in remand detention on any given day.

These are not convicted criminals. They are accused persons — people who have been arrested and charged but who have not yet had their day in court. Under the Constitution, they are presumed innocent. Under the law, they are entitled to a trial that begins and concludes without unreasonable delay. Under the reality of South Africa's criminal justice system, they sit in overcrowded correctional facilities for months, years, and in some cases the better part of a decade, waiting for a system that cannot process them.

This is not a footnote in the criminal justice system. The remand population constitutes roughly a third of all people held in correctional facilities in South Africa. Many of these individuals are detained not because a court has determined that they are dangerous or likely to flee, but because they cannot afford bail amounts that would be trivial for a middle-class accused person. They are, in the starkest terms, imprisoned for being poor. And while they wait — in facilities operating at 150 per cent or more of designed capacity, where tuberculosis transmission is rampant, where gang violence is a daily reality, and where the conditions of detention are themselves a violation of constitutional rights — their cases crawl through the system at a pace that makes a mockery of the constitutional guarantee of a speedy trial.

Section 35(3)(d) of the Constitution guarantees every accused person the right "to have their trial begin and conclude without unreasonable delay." For the 50,000 people in remand detention, this right exists on paper only. The remand detention crisis is a constitutional emergency hiding in plain sight — visible to anyone who visits a correctional facility, documented in every report by the Judicial Inspectorate for Correctional Services, acknowledged in multiple court judgments, and yet persisting year after year because the structural causes are embedded in the same pipeline failures that [The Broken Pipeline](/articles/ss-2-1-the-broken-pipeline) documented.

## Fifty Thousand People Who Have Not Been Convicted of Anything

## The Scale of the Crisis

To understand the remand detention crisis, you need to start with the numbers — and the numbers are damning.

South Africa's correctional facilities were designed to hold approximately 120,000 people. The actual population consistently exceeds 150,000, with some facilities operating at 200 per cent or more of their designed capacity. Of this total population, roughly 50,000 — approximately one in three — are unsentenced remand detainees. The proportion has fluctuated over the years, dropping slightly during periods of judicial attention and rising again as the underlying structural causes reassert themselves, but it has remained stubbornly high for over two decades.

The average time spent in remand detention varies by court tier and crime category, but the figures are consistently alarming. For cases in district courts — which handle less serious offences — the average time from arrest to case finalisation can exceed six months. For cases in regional courts — which handle serious offences including murder, robbery, and sexual offences — the average stretches to two years or more. For complex cases with multiple accused or multiple charges, remand periods of three to five years are not uncommon. Individual cases of remand detention exceeding seven or eight years have been documented.

These are not exceptional cases. They are the product of a system that processes cases at a pace fundamentally mismatched to the volume it handles. The Department of Correctional Services (DCS) annual reports document the remand population, the Judicial Inspectorate for Correctional Services monitors detention conditions, and the Department of Justice tracks court performance metrics. All three data sources tell the same story: the system detains more people than it can process, and the gap between intake and throughput produces a remand population that grows like water behind a dam.

:::takeaway
South Africa's remand detention population of approximately 50,000 people represents roughly one-third of all persons held in correctional facilities. These are legally innocent people — not convicted of any crime — who are detained because the system cannot process their cases within a reasonable timeframe and because the bail system fails to release those who pose no flight or safety risk. The average remand period for serious cases frequently exceeds two years, and individual cases of five or more years are documented.
:::

## How People End Up in Remand: The Bail System's Structural Bias

To understand why so many people are in remand detention, you need to understand the bail system — because bail is the mechanism that determines whether an accused person waits for trial in the community or in a correctional facility. In theory, bail is supposed to balance the accused person's right to freedom against the risk that they will flee, interfere with the investigation, or pose a danger to the public. In practice, the bail system operates with a structural bias toward detention — a bias that falls disproportionately on the poor.

The Criminal Procedure Act of 1977 (as amended) establishes the framework for bail decisions. For most offences, the accused has the right to apply for bail, and the state bears the onus of showing that release would be against the interests of justice. But for offences listed in Schedule 5 of the Act — which include serious violent offences — the onus is shifted: the accused must show that the interests of justice permit their release. For offences listed in Schedule 6 — which include murder, rape, and certain robberies — the onus is shifted even further: the accused must demonstrate that "exceptional circumstances" justify release. This reversed onus, intended to address public concern about serious crime, means that for the most serious offence categories, the system's default position is detention.

The bail amount itself is a second filter that discriminates by wealth. A magistrate who sets bail at R3,000 for a shoplifting case may consider this a modest amount — and for a middle-class accused person, it is. But for an unemployed person from an informal settlement, R3,000 is an impossibility. The accused person cannot pay, remains in remand detention, and joins the 50,000. The offence for which they were arrested may ultimately result in a suspended sentence or a fine — but they will have spent months in a correctional facility before reaching that outcome. In effect, they serve a harsher punishment awaiting trial than they would have received upon conviction.

Research consistently shows that a significant proportion of remand detainees are held for relatively minor offences — theft, assault, trespassing, drug possession — where the likely sentence upon conviction would be less than the time already spent in remand. This is not just unjust. It is irrational from a systems perspective: the state spends approximately R350 to R400 per day to detain each prisoner, meaning that holding a petty theft suspect in remand for six months costs the state over R60,000 — far more than any sentence the court would impose, and far more than the value of whatever was allegedly stolen.

:::expert
The Wits Justice Project, based at the University of the Witwatersrand, has conducted extensive research into remand detention in South Africa, documenting individual cases that illustrate the systemic nature of the crisis. Their investigations have revealed accused persons who spent years in remand for offences that were ultimately dismissed or resulted in acquittals; individuals who were detained because they could not afford bail amounts of R500 or R1,000; and juveniles who entered the remand system as children and emerged as adults, having spent their formative years in facilities designed for sentenced prisoners. The Wits Justice Project's work has consistently highlighted that the remand crisis is not primarily driven by the severity of the offences charged — it is driven by the interaction between the bail system's structural bias toward detention and the court system's inability to process cases within reasonable timeframes. Their analysis has shown that the most reliable predictor of whether an accused person ends up in remand is not the seriousness of the alleged offence but their socio-economic status: poor accused persons are detained; those with resources are released. The bail system, in practice, operates as a wealth filter rather than a risk filter.
:::

## Conditions Inside: What Remand Detention Actually Looks Like

If the bail system is the mechanism that puts people into remand detention, the conditions inside are what makes remand detention a constitutional crisis rather than merely an administrative inconvenience. The conditions in South Africa's remand facilities are, by any reasonable standard, a violation of the constitutional right to conditions of detention consistent with human dignity (Section 35(2)(e)).

Overcrowding is the foundational problem from which most other conditions issues flow. When a facility designed for 2,000 people holds 3,500, the consequences are physical, medical, and social. Cells designed for single or double occupancy hold four, six, or eight people. Sleeping space is rationed — in the most overcrowded facilities, detainees sleep in shifts, sharing mattresses, or sleeping on the floor. Ablution facilities designed for a fraction of the actual population are overwhelmed, creating sanitation conditions that are degrading and health-threatening.

The health consequences of overcrowding are severe. Tuberculosis — a disease that thrives in crowded, poorly ventilated environments — is endemic in South African correctional facilities. The TB infection rate among prisoners is estimated to be several times higher than in the general population. HIV prevalence is also significantly elevated. For remand detainees — who are legally innocent and whose detention may ultimately prove to have been unnecessary — contracting TB or HIV in a correctional facility represents a life-altering harm inflicted by a system that was supposed to be holding them temporarily.

Violence is a pervasive feature of life in correctional facilities, and remand detainees are particularly vulnerable. The Numbers gangs — the 26s, 27s, and 28s — operate as parallel governance systems within South African prisons, controlling territory, enforcing codes of conduct, and inflicting violence on those who resist or transgress. New remand detainees, particularly those who are young, small in stature, or unfamiliar with prison culture, are at acute risk of physical and sexual violence. The DCS has neither the staffing levels nor the institutional capacity to protect vulnerable detainees from gang violence — the staff-to-prisoner ratio in many facilities makes meaningful supervision impossible.

Awaiting-trial juveniles face particular vulnerabilities. Despite legal requirements that juvenile detainees be separated from adults, overcrowding and infrastructure limitations mean that separation is not always achieved in practice. Juveniles in the remand system are exposed to adult inmates, gang recruitment, violence, and conditions that are developmentally destructive. Many juvenile remand detainees have been arrested for relatively minor offences — theft, drug possession, malicious damage to property — that should be diverted through the child justice system rather than resulting in detention.

:::expert
The Judicial Inspectorate for Correctional Services (JICS), the independent body mandated to inspect correctional facilities and report on conditions of detention, has documented the remand detention crisis in its annual reports with a consistency that borders on despair. Year after year, the Inspectorate's reports describe the same conditions: overcrowding that violates constitutional standards, inadequate medical care, insufficient nutrition, gang violence that the DCS cannot control, and a remand population that grows despite repeated recommendations for reform. The Inspectorate's reports have noted that the conditions in many remand sections are worse than those in sections housing sentenced prisoners — because remand detainees are not in the system long enough to be integrated into the (limited) programmes and routines that provide some structure to sentenced prisoners' daily lives, and because the constant turnover of remand detainees creates instability within the facility. The Inspectorate has repeatedly recommended reforms to the bail system, the expansion of non-custodial alternatives to remand detention, and the establishment of dedicated remand facilities with conditions appropriate for people who have not been convicted of any crime. These recommendations have been largely unimplemented.
:::

## The Court Delays That Perpetuate the Crisis

The remand detention crisis is perpetuated by the same court delays documented in [The Broken Pipeline](/articles/ss-2-1-the-broken-pipeline) — but from the perspective of the person trapped inside the system, those delays take on a different and more devastating character.

Every postponement of a remand detainee's case means additional weeks or months in detention. Every missing witness, every unavailable interpreter, every incomplete investigation, every overloaded court roll translates directly into continued incarceration of a legally innocent person. The postponement culture that defines South Africa's lower courts — cases routinely postponed five, ten, fifteen times before reaching trial — is not merely an administrative inefficiency when viewed from the remand detainee's perspective. It is a progressive violation of constitutional rights, compounding with each additional court date that produces no progress.

The causes of delay mirror the pipeline failures: SAPS investigations that remain incomplete months or years after arrest, because the detective is carrying 150 other cases; forensic results that have not been returned from backlogged laboratories; NPA prosecutors who are not ready to proceed because they received an inadequate docket; defence attorneys from Legal Aid South Africa who are themselves carrying enormous caseloads and cannot prepare for trial in the available time; and court infrastructure that simply does not have enough courtrooms, magistrates, or support staff to process the volume of cases before it.

The result is a system in which the most rational strategy for all institutional actors — SAPS, the NPA, Legal Aid, the courts — is to postpone. The detective needs more time to investigate. The prosecutor needs more time to prepare. The defence attorney needs more time to consult. The court needs to accommodate other matters on the roll. Each postponement is individually rational. Collectively, they produce a system in which remand detainees serve de facto sentences that may exceed the sentences they would receive upon conviction.

:::takeaway
The remand detention crisis is perpetuated by the same court delays that the pipeline analysis documented — postponements driven by incomplete investigations, slow forensics, overloaded prosecutors and defence attorneys, and insufficient court capacity. But from the remand detainee's perspective, each postponement is not an administrative inconvenience — it is an additional period of imprisonment for a person who has not been convicted of any crime. The system's inefficiency is, for remand detainees, functionally indistinguishable from punishment.
:::

## The Constitutional Rights That Exist on Paper

The Constitution provides a comprehensive set of rights for arrested, detained, and accused persons in Section 35. These rights were drafted with the apartheid experience in mind — the detention without trial, the torture in custody, the denial of legal representation that characterised the apartheid security state. They represent the constitutional framers' determination that the new democracy would treat detained persons with dignity and provide them with meaningful protections. Understanding these rights is essential because the remand detention crisis is, at its core, a systematic violation of constitutional guarantees.

Section 35(1) provides rights for arrested persons: the right to remain silent, to be informed of the right to remain silent and of the consequences of not remaining silent, to not be compelled to make a confession, to be brought before a court as soon as reasonably possible (but not later than 48 hours after the arrest), and to be released if the interests of justice permit (subject to reasonable conditions).

Section 35(2) provides rights for detained persons: the right to be informed of the reason for detention, to choose and consult with a legal practitioner, to have a legal practitioner assigned by the state at state expense if substantial injustice would otherwise result, to challenge the lawfulness of the detention before a court, and to conditions of detention consistent with human dignity (including adequate accommodation, nutrition, reading material, and medical treatment at state expense).

Section 35(3) provides rights for accused persons at trial: the right to a fair trial, which includes the right to be informed of the charge with sufficient detail to answer it, to have adequate time and facilities to prepare a defence, to a public trial before an ordinary court, to have the trial begin and conclude without unreasonable delay, and to be presumed innocent.

Each of these rights is routinely violated for remand detainees. The 48-hour first appearance requirement is generally met — but the substance of that first appearance is often a brief bail hearing followed by a remand to a date weeks later, at which the case is again postponed. The right to legal representation is formally honoured through Legal Aid South Africa, but Legal Aid's caseloads are so heavy that the quality of representation is often inadequate. The right to conditions of detention consistent with human dignity is violated by the overcrowding, violence, and health conditions described above. And the right to a trial without unreasonable delay is the most systematically violated right of all — unreasonable delay is the norm, not the exception, for remand detainees.

The Constitutional Court has addressed aspects of the remand crisis in several judgments. In *S v Dlamini; S v Dladla and Others; S v Joubert; S v Schietekat* (1999), the Court confirmed that pre-trial detention is a serious infringement of liberty that must be justified by the state. In subsequent matters, courts have ordered the release of specific detainees whose remand periods had become unreasonable. But these judicial interventions, while important for the individuals involved, have not addressed the structural causes of the crisis. The courts can order individual releases, but they cannot reform the bail system, fund more courtrooms, or increase detective capacity. The structural solutions lie in the legislative and executive domains — and they require the kind of systemic reform that Part 3 of this series will propose.

## The Human Cost Beyond the Numbers

Behind the statistics — 50,000 remand detainees, facilities at 150 per cent capacity, average remand periods exceeding two years — are individual human beings whose lives are being destroyed by a system that is supposed to be holding them temporarily while their cases are resolved.

Consider what remand detention means for the accused person's family. When a breadwinner is detained in remand, the family loses income. When a parent is detained, children lose a caregiver. The economic consequences cascade: rent goes unpaid, children drop out of school because fees cannot be met, dependants turn to extended family or informal support networks that may already be strained. The social consequences are equally severe: the stigma of having a family member in prison (even when they have not been convicted), the disruption of family relationships, the psychological impact on children who visit their parent in a correctional facility.

Consider what remand detention means for the accused person themselves, if they are ultimately acquitted or have their charges withdrawn. They have lost months or years of their life. They may have lost their employment (few employers hold positions for employees in remand), their housing, and their social connections. They may have contracted TB, HIV, or other illnesses. They may have been physically or sexually assaulted. They may have been recruited into gang structures that will follow them after release. And they have no recourse — the state does not compensate remand detainees who are acquitted, unless they can prove that their detention was unlawful (a high burden of proof). The system inflicted irreversible harm on a person who was, by the system's own ultimate determination, innocent.

:::expert
Legal Aid South Africa, which provides state-funded legal representation to accused persons who cannot afford private attorneys, has documented the systemic challenges that its practitioners face in representing remand detainees. Their annual reports describe caseloads that make meaningful attorney-client consultation impossible — legal aid attorneys may be responsible for dozens or hundreds of remand cases simultaneously, with insufficient time to prepare for each matter, insufficient access to clients in correctional facilities, and insufficient institutional support for the investigative work that effective defence requires. Legal Aid has noted that the remand crisis is partly a product of inadequate defence capacity: when accused persons lack effective legal representation, they are less likely to obtain bail, less likely to challenge unreasonable delays, and less likely to have their cases resolved quickly. The organisation has argued for systemic reforms including expanded legal aid funding, earlier access to legal representation for arrested persons, and the establishment of dedicated remand courts with accelerated case processing — reforms that would address the demand side of the crisis rather than merely the supply of detention capacity.
:::

## The Structural Solutions

The remand detention crisis will not be solved by building more prisons, increasing correctional services budgets, or making incremental improvements to court scheduling. It is a systemic problem that requires structural solutions — solutions that address the upstream causes rather than managing the downstream symptoms.

The case-flow triage system proposed in Part 3 of this series would address the court delay dimension. Under this model, every criminal matter entering the system would be classified within 72 hours into one of four tracks, each with defined time limits. Summary offences that currently clog the magistrates' courts — and that result in unnecessary remand detention for minor offences — would be diverted to community courts with accelerated processing. The triage system would constitutionalise the right to a speedy trial by imposing enforceable time limits on each track.

Bail reform would address the detention-by-poverty dimension. A risk-based bail system — in which bail decisions are determined by the accused person's flight risk and danger to the community rather than by their ability to pay — would release thousands of remand detainees who pose no risk but cannot afford their bail. Electronic monitoring, reporting conditions, and community-based supervision could replace custodial remand for the majority of cases where detention is unnecessary.

The investigating magistracy proposed in [An Independent Investigating Magistracy](/articles/ss-3-2-independent-investigating-magistracy) would address the investigation-delay dimension. When cases are investigated more efficiently and dockets are prepared to prosecution standard before referral to the NPA, the cycle of postponements driven by incomplete investigations would be broken.

And the sentencing commission proposed in [A Sentencing Commission and Structured Guidelines](/articles/ss-3-4-sentencing-commission-and-structured-guidelines) would address the sentencing-predictability dimension. When accused persons and their attorneys can predict the likely sentence range for their offence — because sentencing guidelines are structured and transparent — plea negotiations can proceed more efficiently, reducing the number of cases that require full trials and thereby reducing court delays.

These reforms are examined in detail in Part 3. For now, the critical point is that the remand detention crisis is not an isolated problem. It is a downstream consequence of every upstream failure in the pipeline — investigation failure, prosecution failure, court failure, bail system failure. Solving it requires fixing the pipeline, not just expanding the detention capacity.

## The Rights Awareness Guide: What You Need to Know

If you or someone you know is arrested, understanding your Section 35 rights is not an academic exercise — it is a practical necessity. The system will not volunteer this information. You need to know it before you need it.

:::framework
**Your Section 35 Rights — A Practical Guide**

**At Arrest:**
You have the right to remain silent. You do not have to answer questions from the police beyond providing your name and address. Anything you say can be used against you. You have the right to be informed of these rights in a language you understand. You have the right to consult with a legal practitioner of your choice — or to have one assigned at state expense if you cannot afford one. You have the right to be brought before a court within 48 hours of arrest. If you are not brought before a court within 48 hours, your detention becomes unlawful and you can challenge it.

**At the Bail Hearing:**
You have the right to apply for bail. For most offences, the state must show why you should not be released. For Schedule 5 offences (serious violent offences), you must show that the interests of justice permit your release. For Schedule 6 offences (murder, rape, certain robberies), you must show "exceptional circumstances." If bail is set at an amount you cannot afford, your attorney can ask the court to reduce it — bail should be proportionate to the accused person's means. If bail is denied, you have the right to appeal the bail decision to a higher court.

**During Remand Detention:**
You have the right to conditions of detention consistent with human dignity — adequate accommodation, nutrition, reading material, and medical treatment at state expense. You have the right to communicate with and be visited by your legal practitioner, spouse or partner, and next of kin. You have the right to challenge the lawfulness of your detention at any time. If your case is not progressing and remand is becoming unreasonable, your attorney can bring an application for your release on the grounds of unreasonable delay.

**How to Get Legal Aid:**
If you cannot afford a private attorney, contact Legal Aid South Africa. They provide free legal representation to qualifying accused persons. Applications can be made at any magistrate's court, at Legal Aid offices (located in every major centre), or through the Legal Aid toll-free line. Legal Aid attorneys are assigned to courts and correctional facilities and should make contact with you within a reasonable period after your arrest.

**How to Report Detention Conditions Violations:**
If conditions in the facility violate your rights — overcrowding, inadequate food, lack of medical treatment, violence — report them to the Judicial Inspectorate for Correctional Services (JICS). JICS has Independent Correctional Centre Visitors (ICCVs) assigned to every correctional facility. You can also report violations to your legal representative, who can bring the matter before the court. Organisations that monitor detention conditions and can assist include the Wits Justice Project, the Socio-Economic Rights Institute (SERI), Sonke Gender Justice, and the South African Human Rights Commission.

**The Key Principle:**
You are presumed innocent until proven guilty. Remand detention is not punishment — it is a temporary measure to ensure that you appear for trial. If your detention has become unreasonable in duration, if the conditions violate your dignity, or if you are being detained because you cannot afford bail rather than because you pose a genuine risk, you have constitutional grounds to challenge your continued detention. Exercise your rights — the system will not exercise them for you.
:::
