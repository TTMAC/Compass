## South Africa Would Not Be Inventing This

The previous article described the proposed model: a universal crucible of basic training, then a choice between a military and a civilian track. To many South African readers the idea will sound novel, even radical. It is worth knowing, then, that national service is one of the more common institutions in the modern world, that several of the most cohesive and successful societies on earth run it as a normal part of growing up, and that South Africa would not be inventing the concept so much as adapting a well-tested one to its own circumstances.

That last phrase — *adapting to its own circumstances* — carries the whole weight of this article. There is a lazy way to use international examples, which is to point at a country you admire, say "they do it and it works," and imply South Africa should simply copy them. This article tries to do the harder and more honest thing. It looks at what several national-service systems actually do, extracts the *design principles* that travel across very different contexts, and is equally clear about what does **not** transfer — the features of Singapore or Israel or South Korea that depend on conditions South Africa does not share. A model borrowed without that second step is a model destined to fail in translation. The goal here is to borrow with our eyes open.

It also helps to know that national service is not a relic of the past but a live and growing international conversation. Sweden reinstated conscription in recent years after having suspended it; Norway extended it to all genders; a number of countries have introduced or actively debated new national civic-service schemes, motivated less by the old logic of mass armies than by exactly the concerns this series is about — social cohesion in fragmenting societies, the civic formation of young people, and the desire to give a generation a shared stake in the nation. South Africa, in other words, would not be reaching backward to a discarded idea. It would be joining a contemporary global rethinking of how diverse societies bind their young people to one another and to the common good. That context matters, because it means the design questions South Africa faces — universal or selective, military or civilian, how to fund it, how to keep it fair — are questions other serious countries are actively working through too, and there is a living body of experience to learn from rather than a museum piece to admire.

Four cases are worth examining in depth: Singapore, the primary inspiration; Israel, which runs the clearest real-world version of a dual military-and-civilian system; South Korea, which teaches hard lessons about fairness; and Germany alongside the Nordic countries, which together illuminate the civilian alternative and the question of who, exactly, should serve.

## Singapore: Service as the Maker of a Nation

Singapore is the primary inspiration for this series, and the reason is precise. When Singapore became independent in 1965, it was a small, vulnerable, ethnically divided city-state — Chinese, Malay, and Indian populations with different languages and religions, no shared national history, and no obvious reason to feel like one people. Its leaders concluded that a nation would have to be *built*, deliberately, and that National Service — introduced in 1967, very early in the country's life — would be one of the central instruments for building it.

What Singapore did is instructive on several fronts. National Service is **universal** for male citizens: it is not something the poor do while the rich buy their way out, but a shared obligation that the son of a cabinet minister and the son of a hawker both fulfil. It is **substantial** — a period of full-time service followed by years of reservist obligations — long enough to be genuinely formative rather than a token. It is **prestigious**: completing National Service is a normal, expected, respected part of becoming a Singaporean man, woven into the culture rather than stigmatised. And its rationale was always explicitly about **cohesion as much as defence**. The official logic has long held that putting young men from every background through the same demanding experience, depending on one another, builds the common identity and mutual trust that a diverse society needs to function — the precise mechanism this series built its model around.

The results South Africa should care about are not mainly military. They are that a deliberately divided society used a shared service institution, sustained over generations, to help manufacture a genuine sense of common nationhood, and that the institution carried enough prestige and universality to be accepted as legitimate rather than resented. That is the transferable lesson: national service can be a nation-builder, but only if it is universal, substantial, and prestigious. Strip away any of those three and the Singaporean magic does not happen.

It is worth understanding the *mechanism* a little more closely, because Singapore did not simply train young men once and hope. National Service created a lifelong thread: full-time service followed by years of recurring reservist obligations, so that the shared experience was not a single episode in late adolescence but a recurring point of contact with men from across society throughout adult life. The unit a Singaporean trains and serves with becomes a durable cross-cutting network — people from different backgrounds, schools, and income levels who know one another through shared effort rather than through the sorting machinery of class. Multiply that across a whole society over decades and you get something a constitution cannot legislate: a population that has, in fact, mixed, depended on one another, and built the muscle memory of common membership. This is the concrete face of "cohesion," and it is built, not declared.

There is also a hard-edged honesty in how Singapore framed the bargain, which South Africa should note. The state did not pretend National Service was costless or purely a privilege; it asked something real of young men and, in return, embedded the service in a wider social compact in which the state delivered visibly — housing, education, security, rising living standards — so that the demand to serve sat inside a relationship of mutual obligation rather than one-way extraction. A government that demands service while delivering nothing has no moral standing to make the demand. Singapore's authority to ask flowed partly from its record of delivering. That is a sobering lesson for any South African version: the legitimacy to require service has to be earned by a state that visibly holds up its own end of the bargain.

But the honest reading must also note what made Singapore's version work and what South Africa does not share. Singapore is a compact city-state with a small cohort, formidable state capacity, and the fiscal resources of a high-income economy. Training and absorbing its young people is a logistically contained problem. South Africa is a large, geographically dispersed, middle-income country with a youth cohort many times larger, weaker state capacity in important places, and far tighter fiscal room. The *principle* — universal, prestigious, cohesion-building service — transfers. The *ease* of delivering it does not. That gap is exactly what Part 3 of this series has to close, and pretending Singapore's conditions are South Africa's would be the fastest way to design a failure.

## Israel: The Clearest Dual-Track Precedent

If Singapore supplies the cohesion rationale, Israel supplies the closest real-world example of the *dual-track* structure this series proposes — a system in which military service and a recognised civilian national service exist side by side.

Israel runs mandatory military service for most young citizens, men and women, and the institution sits close to the centre of national life. But the feature most relevant here is what exists alongside it. For those who are exempt from or unsuited to military service — including, notably, many religious young women and others — Israel developed **Sherut Leumi**, a national *civilian* service in which participants work in hospitals, schools, welfare organisations, and community settings. Sherut Leumi is the living proof that a serious country can run a structured civilian national-service track in parallel with a military one, with real placements doing real social work, as a recognised and respected way of discharging the duty to serve.

For South Africa's proposal, Israel offers two specific lessons. The first is that the **civilian track is not a theoretical nicety**; it can be a substantial, functioning institution that delivers genuine services and provides a route for those who cannot or should not take the military path — including, crucially, a route consistent with freedom of conscience. The second is that a country can hold both tracks within one national idea of service, so that doing one's duty in a hospital and doing it in uniform are both understood as serving the nation. That is precisely the equal-status principle the previous article insisted on.

The conscience dimension deserves emphasis, because it answers one of the strongest objections to any compulsory military element. A person may have sincere religious, ethical, or political objections to military training or service. A system that forced such a person into uniform regardless would violate freedom of conscience — a freedom South Africa's Constitution explicitly protects. Sherut Leumi shows the way through: a recognised, respected civilian service that lets the objector discharge the duty to serve fully and honourably *without* the military component. In the South African model, the civilian track plays exactly this role. It is not only a deployment option for those who simply prefer it; it is the constitutional pressure valve that allows a near-universal service to coexist with freedom of conscience, by guaranteeing that no one is forced to bear arms against their convictions while still asking everyone to serve the nation in some form. The existence of a substantial, dignified civilian alternative is part of what would make a South African service lawful as well as legitimate — and Israel demonstrates that such an alternative can be real rather than nominal.

There is also a lesson in what Israel gets *wrong*, or at least what remains contested within it: exemptions granted to particular communities have been a long-running source of social friction, with recurring public anger over the perception that some groups carry the burden of service while others are excused. This rhymes with the South Korean warning below, and it reinforces the same point: the design of who is exempt, and why, is not a technical footnote. It is one of the most politically charged choices in the whole system, and getting it wrong turns an instrument of unity into a fresh source of division.

Here too, though, the limits of transfer must be stated plainly, and they are sharper for Israel than for any other case. Israel's system is shaped by a security situation, a history, and a set of internal political dynamics that are specific to it and in important respects troubled; the militarisation of its society is bound up with an ongoing conflict that South Africa neither shares nor should wish to emulate. The lesson South Africa can responsibly draw is narrow and structural: *a parallel civilian national service can work, at scale, alongside a military one.* The broader Israeli context is not a model to import, and this series does not suggest it is. Extract the dual-track design principle; leave the rest.

## South Korea: A Warning About Fairness

South Korea runs mandatory military service for its young men, lasting around a year and a half to two years, and it is a deeply embedded national institution. But South Korea earns its place in this article less as an inspiration than as a *warning* — a case study in the single thing most likely to destroy the legitimacy of any national service: the perception that the burden is not shared fairly.

Over the years, South Korea's system has been repeatedly shaken by controversy over exemptions and evasion — debates over whether celebrities, elite athletes, the wealthy, or the well-connected find ways to avoid, shorten, or soften their service while ordinary young men serve in full. Each such controversy strikes at the heart of the institution, because the entire moral basis of compulsory service is that *everyone* carries it. The moment a society comes to believe that the powerful escape the obligation they impose on the powerless, the service stops being a shared rite and becomes one more arena in which privilege buys its way out — and the resentment that follows is corrosive precisely because the promise was equality of sacrifice.

The lesson for South Africa is direct and severe. In a country already defined by deep inequality and by well-founded public suspicion that elites are insulated from the rules that bind everyone else, a national service perceived as something the poor do while the rich evade would not build cohesion — it would inflame the exact grievance it was meant to heal. Universality cannot be a slogan; it has to be real, visible, and protected against the thousand quiet mechanisms by which the advantaged exempt themselves. If South Africa cannot guarantee that the service genuinely binds every young person, including the children of the powerful, it would be better not to attempt it. South Korea shows what is at stake in getting that one thing right.

What would it take, concretely, to protect fairness? The South Korean experience points to several design requirements that a South African system would have to build in from the start rather than bolt on later. Exemptions would have to be **narrow, published, and rule-based** — granted only for genuine medical or disability reasons, assessed transparently, with the criteria and the numbers open to public scrutiny, so that "medically unfit" cannot quietly become the rich young person's escape hatch. There would have to be **no purchasable alternatives** — no provision by which a payment, a connection, or a well-placed parent substitutes for service. The placements of the privileged would have to be as real and as demanding as everyone else's, not a soft posting arranged by influence. And the whole system would need **visible accountability**: published data on who serves, who is exempted and why, and independent oversight able to investigate the inevitable attempts to game it. None of this is easy in a country where the powerful have repeatedly shown they can bend systems to their advantage — which is precisely why the Reform Agenda pillar of this series treats anti-capture design as central, and why the costing and implementation articles return to it. A national service that cannot be protected from elite evasion should not be built, because a visibly unfair shared sacrifice is worse than no shared sacrifice at all. The fairness problem is not a reason to abandon the idea; it is a non-negotiable specification the idea must meet.

## Germany and the Nordics: The Civilian Alternative and the Question of Who Serves

The final cluster of cases illuminates two design questions the others leave open: how to handle conscientious objection, and whether *everyone* must serve or only a selected portion.

**Germany** ran conscription for decades, and alongside it a civilian alternative — **Zivildienst** — for conscientious objectors, who served instead in hospitals, elder care, ambulance services, and social institutions. Over time, Zivildienst became quietly essential: a large share of Germany's care and social sector came to rely on the steady supply of young people doing their civilian service. When Germany suspended conscription in 2011, it also ended Zivildienst — and the resulting gap in the care sector was significant enough that the country created new voluntary-service schemes to help fill it. There are two lessons here. The positive one is that a civilian service track can deliver social value so real that an entire sector comes to depend on it. The cautionary one is the mirror image: build a service that delivers genuine value and you create a genuine dependency, so that ending or weakening it has real costs. A national service is not a programme to switch on and off lightly; once it is doing real work, the work matters.

**The Nordic countries** — Norway and Sweden in particular — answer the "who serves" question differently from Singapore or Israel, and in a way South Africa should weigh carefully. Both run conscription, but **selectively**: only a portion of each cohort is actually called up, chosen from a much larger pool. Norway extended conscription to women, making it gender-neutral; Sweden, having suspended and then reinstated conscription, also applies it to all genders and selects only those it needs. The striking result is that, because selection is competitive and the service is respected, being chosen is widely regarded as an *honour* rather than a burden. This points to a design option distinct from full universality: a *selective* model in which not everyone serves, but selection is prestigious and the burden is distributed fairly through transparent criteria.

For South Africa, the Nordic example raises a genuine and important design fork, which Part 3 must confront. Full universality — everyone serves — is the strongest cohesion-builder but the most expensive and logistically demanding, especially given the size of South Africa's youth cohort. A selective model is cheaper and more feasible but dilutes the universality that gives the crucible its nation-building power, and it raises hard questions about how selection is done fairly. The proposal in this series leans toward universality as the destination precisely because cohesion is a central goal, but the Nordic model is an honest alternative that a serious costing exercise might push toward — and the later articles treat that tension openly rather than pretending it away.

It is worth sitting with this fork, because it is the single most consequential design choice after the crucible itself, and the comparators genuinely disagree on it. Singapore and Israel point toward universality: everyone (within the relevant group) serves, and that very universality is what makes the institution a nation-builder. The Nordics point the other way: select only as many as the system needs and can do well, make selection transparent and competitive, and let the prestige of being chosen carry the legitimacy that universality carries elsewhere. Each path solves a different problem and creates a different one. Universality maximises cohesion and fairness-by-inclusion but collides hardest with cost and capacity — and South Africa's youth cohort is so large that universal service would be a generational undertaking of enormous scale. Selection eases the cost and capacity problem dramatically but reopens the fairness question in a new form: if only some serve, *how they are chosen* becomes the whole ballgame, and in a low-trust society any selection mechanism will be suspected of bias unless it is visibly, demonstrably fair. There is also a middle path the later articles consider: universal *basic training* as the shared cohesion crucible, with the longer deployment phase scaled to what the country can absorb — preserving the universal shared experience that does the nation-building while managing the cost of the lengthy service tail. The point here is not to resolve the fork — that is Part 3's job, and it cannot be resolved without the numbers — but to show that the world's national-service systems have taken different, defensible answers to it, and that South Africa's answer must be chosen deliberately, with its specific scale, budget, and trust deficit in full view, rather than assumed.

## What Travels, and What Does Not

Pull the four cases together and the transferable principles are clear, and they are exactly the design features the previous article built into the model:

- **Universality or fair selection.** Whether everyone serves (Singapore) or a fairly-selected portion does (the Nordics), the burden must be visibly equitable. South Korea shows what happens when it is not.
- **Prestige.** Service must be respected and aspirational, not stigmatised, or it cannot pull in the whole cohort or build cohesion. Singapore and the Nordics make service an honour; that is not an accident, it is engineered.
- **A credible civilian track.** Israel and Germany show that a substantial civilian national service can deliver real social value and provide a conscience-respecting alternative to the military path.
- **A genuine social contract.** In every successful case, service is tied to a clear set of mutual obligations and benefits between the young person and the nation — the subject of this series' next article.
- **Sustained commitment.** These institutions work because they are maintained over generations, not switched on and off. Germany shows the cost of ending one abruptly.

And the features that do **not** transfer are just as important to name, because ignoring them is how borrowed models die:

- **Scale.** South Africa's youth cohort dwarfs Singapore's or the Nordics', turning a contained logistical problem into an enormous one.
- **Fiscal room.** The high-income economies fund their systems from a tax base South Africa does not have.
- **State capacity.** These systems run on administrative machinery South Africa cannot assume it possesses in equal measure.
- **History and security context.** South Africa's specific history of militarised control under apartheid, and its very different security situation, mean the military dimension must be handled with a care none of these comparators required.

The conclusion is not "South Africa is too different to try." It is "South Africa must adapt, not copy" — taking the principles that travel and engineering around the conditions that do not. That engineering is the work of Part 3.

## Why Service Works When It Works

Underneath the four cases lies a common explanation for why national service, well designed, does what other youth interventions cannot — and it is worth stating directly, because it is the same logic the previous article's model rests on.

The first reason is that service operates on **identity, not just incentives**. Most youth programmes try to change a young person's circumstances — give them a stipend, connect them to a job, teach them a skill. Those are valuable, but they leave the person's sense of who they are largely untouched. A shared, demanding national service does something deeper: it changes how a young person understands themselves and their place in the nation. Someone who has trained alongside compatriots from every background, served their country for a year, and been recognised for it does not merely have a new line on their CV. They have a different relationship to the country and to the strangers who are their fellow citizens. Interventions that touch identity are rarer and more powerful than interventions that touch only circumstances, and national service is one of the few that operates at that level — which is why its effects on cohesion outlast the service itself.

The second reason is the **equal-status, common-fate** structure. In ordinary life, South Africans of different backgrounds mostly meet, if at all, across a hierarchy — as employer and worker, as customer and server. Service, done right, strips the hierarchy away: recruits start equal, face the same difficulty, and succeed or fail together. Decades of research on intergroup contact, noted in the previous Part, identify exactly this configuration — equal status, common goals, mutual dependence, institutional support — as the condition under which contact actually dissolves prejudice rather than reinforcing it. National service is almost a purpose-built machine for producing that configuration at national scale. It is not that the young people are lectured into tolerance; it is that the structure of the experience makes the old categories visibly useless, and the lesson sticks because it was lived.

The third reason is **universality or fair selection as a source of legitimacy**. Because everyone serves (or is fairly chosen to), no one can dismiss the institution as something done to other people. It belongs to the whole society, which is why, in the countries where it works, it commands a respect that targeted poverty programmes never do. The poor young person is not a recipient of charity; they are a citizen discharging the same duty as everyone else. That shift — from beneficiary to citizen — is quietly one of the most important things national service does, and it is impossible to achieve in an opt-in, means-tested programme aimed only at the disadvantaged.

Hold these three together — identity over incentives, equal-status common fate, universality as legitimacy — and you have the reason national service keeps reappearing in the toolkit of societies trying to bind themselves together. They are also, precisely, the three things South Africa's past youth programmes lacked. The proposal in this series is an attempt to supply them.

## A Tool: The Comparison Table

To carry the lessons in a single glance, hold the four cases side by side against the features that matter:

| Country | Who serves | Civilian track? | Core lesson for South Africa |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Singapore** | Universal (male citizens) | Limited | Universal, prestigious, sustained service can *build a nation* from a divided start |
| **Israel** | Most young citizens | Yes — Sherut Leumi | A substantial **civilian** track can run alongside the military one, respecting conscience |
| **South Korea** | Universal (male citizens) | Limited | Fairness is everything; perceived elite evasion **destroys** legitimacy |
| **Nordics (Norway/Sweden)** | **Selective**, gender-neutral | Limited | Selection done transparently can make service an **honour**; an alternative to full universality |

Read down the "core lesson" column and you have the design brief: build something universal or fairly selective, prestigious, with a real civilian track, that is fair enough to bind the powerful as well as the poor, sustained over time, and adapted to South Africa's scale, budget, capacity, and history. No single country is the template. The template is the set of principles that survive translation — and the discipline to engineer honestly around the ones that do not.

The next article takes up the principle that appears in every successful case and that South Africa's past programmes most conspicuously lacked: prestige. It asks how a national youth service could be made not merely tolerable but genuinely desirable — something young South Africans would compete to enter because completing it visibly changes their lives. For all the differences between Singapore, Israel, South Korea, and the Nordics, this is the thread that runs through every system that works: service there is respected, even coveted, not pitied. South Africa's programmes were not respected, and that, more than any funding shortfall, is why young people with options stayed away from them. Getting prestige right is therefore not the soft part of the design to be added once the hard logistics are settled. It is one of the load-bearing walls — and, as the next article argues, it is built deliberately, out of concrete benefits, not conjured by branding. The countries that learned this lesson built institutions their young people were proud to belong to; the ones that forgot it, or never learned it, watched their schemes wither. South Africa has, so far, been firmly in the second camp. The next article is about how it could move to the first.
