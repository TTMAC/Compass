When a South African company earns a rand of profit, the state taxes it once at the company and again when what is left is paid out to the person who owns the business. The first bite is corporate income tax, currently 27% of profit. The second is Dividends Tax, 20% of whatever the company distributes. Put them together and a rand of company profit that makes its way into a founder's hands as a dividend has carried a combined effective tax burden of around 41% — before that founder has paid for anything, reinvested anything, or lived on anything. This is the classical "double taxation" of company profits, and for dividends the label is exactly right: the same economic profit is genuinely taxed twice. Capital gains face a related overlap with two problems of their own — South Africa taxes the *inflationary* portion of a gain as if it were real, and the tax discourages people from ever selling. The double taxation of business returns is real, and it bites hardest on the *active builder*: the entrepreneur who creates a company and tries to draw an income from it or cash out, rather than the passive holder of old, diversified wealth who can defer, structure and wait.

But the counter-case is real too, and the honest reading needs both halves of it. Without a dividend tax, a company becomes a shelter in which income hides from the income tax indefinitely. Without capital gains tax, a high earner converts salary into lightly taxed gains and the income tax springs a leak. The private-equity industry is the textbook case of that leak: a fund manager's reward for managing other people's money — economically a performance fee, a form of *income* — is repackaged as *carried interest*, a slice of the fund's capital gains, and so attracts the far lower capital-gains rate rather than the rate on a salary. (In South Africa the correct treatment of carried interest has been actively contested between fund managers and SARS precisely because so much rides on the income-versus-gain label.) The honest reading, the one this article works toward, is that South Africa taxes the *returns* to new capital in a way that is heavy and incoherent at once: too heavy where it double-taxes distributed equity profit, too leaky where it lets labour income be relabelled as a gain, and skewed throughout by a built-in bias that treats debt-financed investment more kindly than equity-financed investment, because interest is deductible while dividends are not. The reform that matters is therefore not a higher or a lower rate but *better tools* to tax the real return to capital once and consistently — equalising the treatment of equity and debt foremost among them. This, far more than the headline income-tax rate, is a large part of why building new wealth here is so hard. This is the fourth article in The Fiscal Levers, and it completes on the *returns* side the flow-versus-stock argument that [What Tax Is For](/articles/eg-3-3-what-tax-is-for) opened on the holding side.

## The company as a separate taxpayer

Start with the thing many people half-forget: in the eyes of the law, a company is a separate person. It can own property, sign contracts, sue and be sued — and it pays its own tax, in its own name, on its own profit, before a cent of that profit reaches the human beings who own it. That tax is corporate income tax, and in South Africa it currently sits at 27% of taxable profit, reduced from 28% for years of assessment ending on or after 31 March 2023. So a company that makes R100 of profit pays R27 to SARS and is left with R73. This is the first layer, and on its own it is uncontroversial — almost every country taxes company profit, and 27% is unremarkable by international standards, sitting a little below the rich-world average.

The controversy begins with what happens to the R73. The company does not exist for its own sake; it exists for its owners, and sooner or later they will want some of the profit. The moment the company hands it over, the tax system wakes up a second time.

## Dividends and the classical system: the cleanest case of double taxation

When a company pays out profit to its shareholders, it pays a *dividend*. And when it does, South Africa levies Dividends Tax — a 20% withholding tax on the dividend, deducted by the company (or the broker) before the money reaches the shareholder. The rate has been 20% since 22 February 2017, having started at 15% when Dividends Tax was introduced in 2012.

Now follow the rand all the way through, because the worked example is the whole argument. The company earns R100. It pays R27 in corporate income tax, leaving R73. It distributes that R73 as a dividend. The shareholder pays 20% Dividends Tax on the R73 — that is R14.60. So of the original R100 of profit, the state has taken R27 at the company and R14.60 at the shareholder: R41.60 in total. The combined effective tax rate on distributed company profit is about 41.6% — higher than the 41% bracket a salaried professional hits, and not far off the 45% top rate, even though neither the company nor the shareholder is necessarily a top earner. The same economic profit has been taxed once on the way in and again on the way out. That is what "double taxation" means, and for dividends it is not rhetoric; it is arithmetic.

This design has a name. Taxing the company and then taxing the shareholder separately on the same profit is called the *classical* system of company taxation, and South Africa, like the United States, uses it. It is not the only option. Australia abolished its classical system in 1987, under then-Treasurer Paul Keating, and replaced it with *dividend imputation*: when an Australian company pays tax on its profit, it earns "franking credits", which it passes to shareholders along with the dividend, and the shareholder uses those credits to offset their own tax on it. The effect is that the profit is taxed once — at the shareholder's rate — rather than twice. The imputation system is the standing proof that the double taxation of dividends is a *choice*, not a law of nature. A country can decide to integrate company and shareholder tax so the profit is taxed only once; South Africa has decided not to.

South Africa's own history makes the same point. Before 2012 we did not have a Dividends Tax at all; we had Secondary Tax on Companies, or STC, which taxed the *company* when it declared a dividend rather than the *shareholder* when they received it. The switch from STC to Dividends Tax in 2012 moved the legal point of collection from the company to the shareholder, but left the basic double-layer structure intact. The lesson of both the Australian comparison and our own STC history is that the second layer is a policy decision revisited more than once — not a fixed feature of reality.

Why does any of this matter beyond fairness to founders? Because the double taxation of distributed profit raises the *cost of equity capital*, and that has real consequences for how businesses are built. Money raised by selling shares (equity) is taxed twice — company tax, then dividend tax on the returns. Money raised by borrowing (debt) is not: interest is deducted before company tax, so it escapes the first layer entirely. The tax system therefore quietly pushes companies toward debt and away from equity, and discourages both the paying of dividends and the raising of new equity from outside investors. For a mature company with collateral, that bias toward debt is survivable. For a young, growing business — exactly the kind of new venture this pillar's Enablers articles argue South Africa most needs — it is a genuine handicap, because young firms cannot easily borrow and depend on equity investors who are looking at returns that will be taxed twice. The cost-of-equity point connects directly to the financing gap explored in the Enablers (the new Part 4) article on new financing vehicles, [Three Engines of Growth](/articles/eg-4-1-three-engines-of-growth).

:::expert
The Davis Tax Committee, the independent body chaired by Judge Dennis Davis that reviewed South Africa's tax system, examined the taxation of companies and dividends in detail and treated the structure of capital taxation as a deliberate design question rather than a settled inheritance. Its work set out the trade-offs of the classical system openly — the way it backstops the personal income tax by ensuring company profits do not escape tax entirely, weighed against the distortions it introduces in the cost of capital and the choice between debt and equity finance. The committee's careful, two-sided treatment is the right model for a citizen: the double taxation of dividends is neither a self-evident injustice nor a costless backstop, but a design with real benefits and real costs that a country should choose with its eyes open rather than inherit by default.
:::

## Capital gains: where "double taxation" applies, and where it does not

Now the harder case, where the slogan needs to be handled with care, because lumping capital gains in with dividends under one "double taxation" banner is where the argument usually goes wrong.

First, how capital gains tax actually works in South Africa. When you sell an asset — shares, a second property, a business — for more than you paid, the profit is a capital gain. South Africa does not tax the whole gain. For an individual, only 40% of the gain is *included* in your taxable income (the "inclusion rate"), and that included portion is then taxed at your normal marginal rate. Because the top marginal rate is 45%, the *maximum* effective CGT rate for an individual works out to 40% of 45%, which is 18%. So CGT is not a separate flat tax bolted on top; it is a partial inclusion of the gain in your ordinary income, and the effective rate — at most 18% for an individual — sits well below the rate on a salary. For companies the inclusion rate is 80%, giving an effective rate of about 21.6%. Keep that structure in mind, because it already tells you the "gains are taxed to death" story is overstated.

Now separate three distinct critiques that the single phrase "double taxation" usually smears together. They are not equally strong, and pretending they are is how the argument loses credibility.

The first is the *retained-earnings overlap*, and this is the genuine double-taxation strand. Part of the rise in a company's share price reflects profits the company earned, paid 27% tax on, and retained rather than distributed. When you later sell the share at a higher price and pay CGT on the gain, you are taxed again on value that was already taxed at the company. To that extent — and only to that extent — a capital gain on shares really is double taxation, the same overlap as dividends wearing different clothes. But notice the limit: a share's gain is *not only* retained earnings. It can also reflect genuinely new value, growth expectations, goodwill, or simple market re-rating, none of which was ever taxed at the company. So the gain is *partly* a second tax on already-taxed profit and *partly* a first tax on new value. The double-taxation label fits a portion of the gain, not all of it — which is exactly why honest analysis does not claim CGT is straightforwardly double taxation the way dividends are.

The second critique is, to my mind, the strongest, and it has nothing to do with double counting: the *inflation problem*. South Africa does not index the base cost of an asset for inflation. Suppose you buy a property for R1 million and sell it fifteen years later for R2 million, during which time general prices have doubled. In real terms you have made no gain at all — your R2 million buys what your R1 million bought. Yet SARS treats the entire R1 million "gain" as a capital gain and taxes it. You are being taxed on inflation, on a purely nominal increase that represents no real increase in wealth. This is a real, technical, and largely unanswerable objection to South Africa's CGT as currently designed, and it bites hardest on assets held for a long time — which, again, is often the first-generation builder who bought one property or one business stake and held it for decades, rather than the diversified holder churning a portfolio.

The third critique is the *lock-in effect*. CGT is only triggered when you *realise* a gain — when you actually sell. Hold the asset and you pay nothing, however much it has risen. This creates a powerful incentive never to sell, even when the capital would be better deployed elsewhere, because selling crystallises a tax bill that holding defers indefinitely. Economists call this lock-in, and it is a genuine efficiency cost: capital gets stuck in assets it has outgrown because moving it is taxed and sitting on it is not.

Notice how these three problems compound on the same person — the long-term builder holding a single, illiquid asset. Lock-in keeps her from selling; the longer she holds, the more of her eventual "gain" is pure inflation; and whatever portion does reflect the company's retained profits has already been taxed once. The diversified holder, by contrast, is far less exposed to any of them: he can realise small parcels to manage the tax, his portfolio turns over so inflation accumulates less in any single base cost, and he can borrow against assets rather than sell. So even within capital gains tax — supposedly the great equaliser that reaches accumulated wealth — the design quietly favours the holder of liquid, diversified, professionally managed wealth over the builder of a single concentrated asset. The instrument meant to tax the stock ends up landing softest on exactly the established fortunes it is imagined to reach.

So be precise: the "double taxation" label is strong and literally true for dividends, and only *partial* for capital gains. For CGT specifically, the inflation and lock-in critiques are the more robust objections, and they are objections to the *design* of the tax, not arguments for abolishing it.

## Builder versus holder: the analytical payoff

Put the two articles side by side and a paradox resolves. [What Tax Is For](/articles/eg-3-3-what-tax-is-for) showed that South Africa taxes the asset *stock* lightly in aggregate — no annual wealth tax, gains effectively capped at 18%, modest estate duty. This article shows that the *returns* to capital, when you actually extract them, are taxed heavily and twice. How can both be true? Because the system taxes *holding* lightly and *extracting* heavily. Sit on an asset and you owe little: no wealth tax on the holding, no CGT until you sell, inflation quietly inflating the value tax-free while you wait. Try to pull cash out and the bill arrives: corporate tax plus dividend tax if you distribute profit, CGT plus untaxed inflation if you sell.

Make it concrete with two people again. Sipho has built a manufacturing business from nothing; all his wealth is the company. To pay his bond and his children's school fees he draws the profit out as a dividend, and every rand he takes has already paid 27% company tax and then pays 20% on the way out — an effective 41% before it reaches his household, year after year, because he has no other source of income to live on. When, exhausted, he eventually sells the business to fund his retirement, the sale price reflects fifteen years of retained, already-taxed profit *and* fifteen years of inflation, and CGT lands on the lot. Across town, an heir holds a diversified portfolio of listed shares and rental properties. He draws what he needs by borrowing cheaply against the portfolio rather than selling — so he triggers no CGT — lets the assets compound untaxed, holds the lot inside a family trust that smooths estate duty, and passes it on. Over a lifetime Sipho, the builder, may pay tax on his capital three or four times over; the heir, the holder, may engineer his affairs to pay it almost never. Same wealth, opposite tax journeys — and the difference is not effort or merit but whether you are *extracting* capital or *sitting* on it.

That asymmetry is precisely tuned to reward one kind of person over another. The passive holder of old, diversified wealth can defer realisation, time disposals, and structure holdings through trusts and companies to soften every layer — the whole apparatus of estate planning exists to do exactly this. The active builder cannot. The first-generation entrepreneur who has put everything into one company, and who needs to draw a dividend to live on or to sell a stake to reinvest, walks straight into the double layer with no inherited structure to optimise within. The system taxes most lightly the behaviour of the established holder (wait, defer, pass on) and most heavily the behaviour of the new builder (extract, distribute, realise). In a country where the asset stock is overwhelmingly old, concentrated and racially skewed by history, and the new builders are disproportionately the rising Black professional and entrepreneurial class, that asymmetry has the same uncomfortable shape the previous article traced — only sharper, because here it falls on the very act of converting effort into capital.

## The counter-case, at full strength

If the article stopped there it would be a brief for cutting taxes on capital, and that is not where the evidence honestly lands. So here is the other side, and it is strong.

Dividend and capital-gains taxes exist for good reasons, and the first is structural integrity. Without a dividend tax, a company becomes a tax shelter. A high earner could leave income inside a company, where it is taxed at 27% and no more, and simply never draw it out as salary — converting income that would face up to 45% personal tax into profit that faces only company tax. The dividend tax is the backstop that stops the company becoming a permanent hiding place for personal income; it ensures that profit which *does* reach the owner faces something closer to the personal rate. Strip it away and the personal income tax — the progressive heart of the system — springs a large leak at the top, exactly where it is supposed to bite.

Capital gains tax does the same defensive job. Without it, high earners would have a standing incentive to convert salary into capital gains — taking pay as shares, or structuring rewards as asset appreciation — and route around the income tax entirely. CGT exists so that the income tax cannot be dodged by relabelling income as a gain. And here is the point the "tax capital to death" rhetoric ignores: the concessionary 40% inclusion rate, which caps the effective rate at 18%, is *itself* a relief already granted in recognition of the overlap and inflation problems. The system has already pulled its punch on capital gains; that is what the partial inclusion *is*.

There is a deeper counter-argument too, and it runs directly against this article's own framing. Many serious economists hold that capital in South Africa is *under*-taxed, not over-taxed — that the real distributional problem is the lightly taxed asset *stock* identified in the last article, and that the right response is *better-designed* capital taxation (indexing CGT for inflation, integrating company and shareholder tax through an imputation credit, tightening the avoidance routes the wealthy use to defer and structure) rather than *lower* taxes on capital returns. On this view the incoherence is real, but the cure is to make the taxation of capital more coherent and, in places, heavier — not lighter. The Mirrlees Review, the landmark survey of tax design led by the Nobel laureate James Mirrlees for the Institute for Fiscal Studies, argued exactly this: that the goal should be a *neutral* tax on capital that falls on genuine returns without double-counting, taxing inflation, or distorting the debt-equity choice — which can mean taxing capital more sensibly rather than simply less.

:::expert
The Mirrlees Review (*Tax by Design*, Institute for Fiscal Studies, 2011), led by Sir James Mirrlees, is the most influential modern statement of how a tax system *should* treat capital. Its conclusion is not the populist one in either direction. It rejects both the cry to slash taxes on capital and the reflex to pile them on, and argues instead for *neutrality*: tax the real return to capital once, cleanly, without the double-counting of the classical dividend system, without taxing inflationary pseudo-gains, and without favouring debt over equity. Read against South Africa's actual system — classical double taxation of dividends, CGT on unindexed nominal gains, a sharp debt-equity bias — the Mirrlees standard is less a verdict for lower taxes than an indictment of *incoherent* ones. The problem the review helps you name is not that South Africa taxes capital too much or too little, but that it taxes it in a muddle.
:::

## Where this leaves the argument

Everything so far has still assumed two things: that the state must collect a roughly fixed amount of revenue, so any relief on capital must be "paid for"; and that fairness is mainly a question of getting the tax design right. The rest of this part puts both assumptions under pressure. [Taxes and Growth](/articles/eg-3-5-taxes-and-growth) takes up the argument that the heavy, growth-costly taxation of capital this article described is itself a brake on the economy, and asks how much of that case the evidence supports. [The Household Fallacy](/articles/eg-3-6-the-household-fallacy) questions whether revenue is the binding constraint at all — and if it is not, then relieving the double taxation of capital, or reaching the lightly taxed stock instead, becomes a political choice rather than a budgetary impossibility. And [Where Money Comes From](/articles/eg-3-7-where-money-comes-from) and [The Real Lever](/articles/eg-3-8-the-real-lever) argue that the deepest determinant of who gets to build capital is not the tax on capital's *returns* at all, but access to the *credit* that creates capital in the first place. The incoherent way South Africa taxes the returns to new capital is a real problem. It is not the deepest one.

For now, hold the two-sided conclusion this article has built, because it is the honest one and it resists both of the slogans that usually capture this subject. South Africa really does tax the returns to capital twice over and in a muddle, and that muddle falls hardest on the person trying to build a first fortune from a single business rather than inherit a diversified one — a genuine grievance, not a manufactured one. And the dividend and gains taxes really are load-bearing parts of a system that would otherwise let income hide in companies and salaries masquerade as gains — so the answer is almost never simply to cut them. The reform worth wanting is coherence: tax the real return to capital once, cleanly, without taxing inflation and without rewarding the holder over the builder. That is a harder thing to chant at a rally than "stop the double tax", which is exactly why it is the thing worth understanding.

## Practical Framework: The Capital Tax Layer-Counter

:::takeaway
A rand of South African company profit paid out as a dividend is taxed about 41% — 27% company tax, then 20% on the distribution — genuine double taxation, as Australia's imputation system shows is avoidable by design. Capital gains face a partial overlap plus two real flaws: South Africa taxes inflationary "gains" that aren't real, and the tax discourages selling. The whole structure taxes *extracting* wealth heavily while taxing *holding* it lightly — penalising the active builder over the passive holder. But the taxes exist for a reason: they stop the company becoming a shelter and salary being relabelled as gains. The honest verdict is that South Africa taxes the returns to capital not too much or too little, but incoherently.
:::

The point of this article is to let you count the layers yourself, so that you can judge any dividend-tax or capital-gains proposal on whether it reduces incoherent double counting or simply opens an avoidance channel for the wealthy. Apply this to any rand of return on capital.

:::framework
**The Capital Tax Layer-Counter**

For any return on capital — a dividend, a capital gain, a business payout — work through four questions to see how many times, and how coherently, it is taxed.

**1. Has this rand already been taxed at the company level?**

If the return comes from company profit, it has almost certainly already paid corporate income tax (27%). That is the first layer. A return that has *not* been through a company — interest, say — has not paid this layer, which is part of why interest and dividends are taxed differently.

**2. Is it taxed again when it reaches the owner — on distribution or on realisation?**

A dividend is taxed a second time at 20% when distributed (genuine double taxation). A capital gain is taxed when the asset is sold, but only partially (40% inclusion for individuals, an effective rate up to 18%) — and only *part* of that gain is the already-taxed retained earnings, so only part is truly a second layer.

**3. Does any of the "gain" being taxed merely reflect inflation rather than real wealth?**

Because South Africa does not index base cost, a long-held asset can show a large nominal "gain" that is mostly or entirely inflation. Tax on that portion is tax on a wealth increase that never really happened — the strongest technical objection to CGT as designed.

**4. Could a passive holder defer or structure around this tax in a way an active builder cannot?**

Ask who can avoid the layer. The holder who can wait, time disposals, and use trusts and holding companies faces a lighter effective burden than the builder who must extract cash now from a single business. A tax that the established can structure around but the aspirant cannot is regressive in practice whatever its rate on paper.

Run a proposal through these four questions and you can tell the difference between a reform that makes capital taxation *more coherent* — indexing for inflation, integrating company and shareholder tax, closing structuring routes — and one that simply *lowers* the burden on those who already hold the most. They are not the same thing, and the rhetoric of "double taxation" is used to sell both.
:::
