## A Healthy, Schooled Person Who Cannot Earn Is the Central Puzzle of South African Development

There is a person this series has been describing for two Parts without quite naming. She survived infancy in a country with one of the world's steeper child-mortality gradients. She made it through twelve years of schooling. She can read, she can reason, she finished matric, perhaps she holds a diploma. By the measures that the [Health](/articles/hd-2-1-the-life-expectancy-deficit) and [Education](/articles/hd-3-1-the-schooling-paradox) Parts tracked, the system worked for her. She is alive, and she is educated. And she cannot find a way to earn a living. She is one of roughly twelve million working-age South Africans who want work and cannot get it, and she sits at the centre of a puzzle that the Human Development Index, for all its sophistication, tends to obscure rather than illuminate.

The puzzle is this. The HDI, the framework that [Beyond GDP](/articles/hd-1-1-beyond-gdp) introduced as the spine of this series, rests on three dimensions: a long and healthy life, knowledge, and a decent standard of living. The first two — health and education — are the subjects of the two Parts you have just read. The third — a decent standard of living, which the HDI measures through gross national income per capita — has been hovering in the background, treated as something the economy delivers rather than something a person achieves. This Part brings it to the foreground. And the moment you look at it directly, a problem appears that the index itself was not built to see: **health and education are not ends in themselves. They are means. They are supposed to convert into a life a person has reason to value — and the most basic ingredient of such a life, in a modern economy, is the capability to earn.**

In South Africa, that conversion is broken. Not slightly impaired — broken in a way that has few parallels among countries at comparable income levels. This article explains what the conversion is, why it fails here, and why two of the three legs of human development are not enough on their own. It introduces the analytical tool that organises the rest of this Part: the idea, drawn directly from the economist whose thinking underpins this entire series, that capabilities only become real when something allows them to be exercised — and that the something, in South Africa, has been allowed to corrode.

## Capabilities and Functionings: The Distinction That Changes Everything

To see the conversion problem clearly, you have to return to the conceptual foundation that [Beyond GDP](/articles/hd-1-1-beyond-gdp) laid down: Amartya Sen's capabilities approach. That article introduced the headline idea — that development is the expansion of what people can actually do and be, not merely the growth of what an economy produces. This article needs the next layer of the framework, the distinction that Sen built his whole apparatus around: the difference between a _functioning_ and a _capability_.

A **functioning** is an achievement — a "being" or a "doing" that a person has actually attained. Being well-nourished is a functioning. Being literate is a functioning. Being employed, earning an income, providing for a household, owning a home — these are functionings. They are the concrete states of a human life.

A **capability** is something different and more demanding. It is the real freedom a person has to achieve a functioning — the genuine opportunity, not merely the formal permission. A person has the capability to be employed if, given the resources and conditions actually available to her, she can convert her effort and her skills into a job. The distinction matters because two people with identical functionings can have wildly different capabilities, and two people with identical capabilities can end up with different functionings depending on what they choose. Sen's insistent point, developed across _Commodities and Capabilities_ (1985) and _Development as Freedom_ (1999), is that the proper measure of a society is not what its people have achieved but what they are genuinely free to achieve.

Now apply this to our healthy, schooled person who cannot find work. Education gave her a capability — the cognitive skills, in principle, to do productive work. Health gave her another — a body that can show up and sustain effort. But the functioning those capabilities are supposed to enable, the functioning of earning a livelihood, has not materialised. Why? Because between a capability and the functioning it points toward sits a process of _conversion_, and that process can succeed or fail. She possesses the inputs. The machinery that turns those inputs into a result has seized.

This is not a metaphor that softens the data. It is a precise description of what unemployment statistics measure. When Statistics South Africa reports an expanded unemployment rate above 40 per cent, it is reporting a conversion failure at population scale: millions of people who have, to varying degrees, the health and the schooling that development is supposed to deliver, and who cannot convert those endowments into the most elementary economic functioning. The HDI counts the health. It counts the education. It even counts national income. What it does not count — what no single index can count — is whether the typical person can actually convert the first two into a share of the third.

## Conversion Factors: Why the Same Capability Yields Different Lives

Sen gave the conversion process a more specific structure, and the philosopher Ingrid Robeyns later systematised it into a vocabulary that is useful precisely because South Africa illustrates every part of it. Between a person's resources and capabilities and the functionings she actually achieves stand **conversion factors** — the conditions that determine how efficiently a given endowment becomes a real outcome. Robeyns groups them into three kinds, and each one is, in South Africa, a site of failure.

**Personal conversion factors** are internal to the individual: physical condition, intelligence, and — crucially for our purposes — skills. A matric certificate is a resource. Whether it converts into employability depends on whether the holder possesses skills an employer can actually use, which, as [From Classroom to Paycheck](/articles/hd-3-3-from-classroom-to-paycheck) established, a credential does not guarantee. The gap between a qualification and a usable skill is a personal conversion factor, and it is the subject of the next article in this Part, [Skills Beyond Schooling](/articles/hd-4-2-skills-beyond-schooling).

**Social conversion factors** are features of the society a person lives in: public institutions, social norms, the structure of the labour market, the presence or absence of discrimination, the density of the networks through which information about work travels. A young person in a household where three generations have never held formal employment lacks something a young person in a working household has — not skill, but social connective tissue. The structure of South Africa's economy, which offers strikingly few footholds outside the formal wage sector, is itself a social conversion factor, and it is the subject of [The Missing Informal Economy](/articles/hd-4-3-the-missing-informal-economy).

**Environmental conversion factors** are features of the physical world: geography, climate, infrastructure. Here South Africa carries an inheritance that few countries share in such concentrated form. Apartheid spatial planning deliberately located the majority of the population far from centres of economic activity, and the geography has barely shifted in three decades. A capable, willing worker who lives two hours and a third of her potential wage away from where the jobs are faces an environmental conversion factor that can make employment uneconomic before she has even been considered for it.

The power of this framework is that it dissolves a false debate. South African discussions of unemployment tend to collapse into a contest between two explanations: people are unemployed because they lack skills (a supply-side story) or because the economy does not create enough jobs (a demand-side story). The conversion framework shows that this is a category error. Skills are a personal conversion factor; the supply of jobs is shaped by social conversion factors; spatial mismatch is environmental. They are not competing explanations. They are different points in a single chain, and the chain only delivers a livelihood if every link holds. South Africa has broken links of all three kinds at once.

:::expert[Amartya Sen and the Capability Approach]
Sen developed the capability approach over three decades, beginning with his 1979 Tanner Lecture "Equality of What?" and culminating in _Development as Freedom_ (1999), the work for which the framework is most widely known. His central move was to shift the evaluative space of development away from both income (what utilitarian economics measured) and primary goods (what John Rawls's theory of justice emphasised) toward capabilities — the substantive freedoms people have to lead lives they value. The methodological consequence is significant for a country like South Africa: it means that averages conceal what matters. A rising GNI per capita tells you nothing about whether the freedom to earn is distributed or hoarded. The philosopher Ingrid Robeyns, in _Wellbeing, Freedom and Social Justice_ (2017), formalised the conversion-factor taxonomy that this article uses, distinguishing personal, social, and environmental factors as the variables that determine how efficiently any given resource is transformed into a real opportunity. Applied to South African unemployment, the framework reframes the problem: the country does not primarily suffer from a shortage of human capabilities — it has invested heavily in health and education — but from a near-total breakdown in the conversion factors that would let those capabilities become livelihoods.
:::

## Two Legs Are Not a Stool: The Standard-of-Living Gap

It is worth being blunt about a structural feature of this series that the conversion problem exposes. The Human Development pillar devotes a full Part to health and a full Part to education, and those two Parts correspond to two of the HDI's three dimensions. The third dimension — a decent standard of living — has not had a Part of its own. This Part is that correction, and the reason the correction is necessary is captured in a single comparison from the opening article of the series.

[Beyond GDP](/articles/hd-1-1-beyond-gdp) reported that South Africa's headline HDI of roughly 0.71 collapses to roughly 0.46 once you adjust for inequality — a fall that drops the country from the "high human development" band into the "low" one. That collapse is the quantitative signature of the conversion problem. An index is built from three components, and when you decompose where South Africa loses the most ground under inequality adjustment, the income dimension and the distribution of education-driven earning power do the heaviest damage. The country does not, in aggregate, lack health or schooling to anything like the degree it lacks a _distributed capability to earn_. The average South African is statistically much further from a decent standard of living than from a clinic or a classroom.

This is why two legs are not a stool. You can invest in health. You can invest in education. And if the third leg — the standard of living that comes, for almost everyone, from the capability to earn — is missing, the structure does not stand. Worse, the two legs you did build begin to feel like a reproach. A person who has been kept alive and taught to read, and who is then offered no route to provide for herself, experiences her own health and education not as freedoms but as frustrated potential. The development literature has a name for the cost of this: the difference between _capability_ and _capability deprivation_ is not neutral. Unrealised capability is a distinct kind of harm, and South Africa manufactures it at scale.

There is a further reason the standard-of-living leg cannot be left to the Economic Growth pillar to carry. That pillar — covering [the structure of the economy](/articles/eg-1-1-the-seven-thousand-rand-economy), the growth strategy, and the machinery of execution — answers a macro question: does the economy grow, and does it create jobs? This Part answers a different, human-scale question: _can an individual build and exercise the capability to earn and to accumulate?_ The two questions are related but they are not the same, and conflating them is precisely the mistake that has let the standard-of-living dimension fall between the cracks of policy. An economy can grow without absorbing the unemployed. A person can be employable in principle and unemployed in fact. The macro story and the capability story diverge, and human development lives in the gap between them.

## The Conversion Failure, Quantified

Abstractions earn their keep only when they organise the numbers, so consider what the conversion failure looks like in the labour-market data — and notice, throughout, that the failure persists even among people who have the health and education that development is supposed to deliver.

Start with the broadest measure. South Africa's **absorption rate** — the share of working-age adults who are actually employed — sits at roughly 40 per cent. In most middle-income countries the figure is between 55 and 65 per cent; across the OECD the average is near 57 per cent. That gap of fifteen to twenty-five percentage points is the conversion problem expressed as a single ratio. It means that for every ten South Africans of working age, fewer than four are converting their time and capabilities into paid work, where in comparable economies six would be. This is not a number that moves with the business cycle. It has been low through booms and busts alike, which, as [From Classroom to Paycheck](/articles/hd-3-3-from-classroom-to-paycheck) argued, is the signature of a structural rather than a cyclical condition.

Now layer in education, because this is where the conversion framework does its sharpest work. If unemployment were simply a skills-supply problem, it would fall steeply with every additional rung of education, all the way to near zero for the well-schooled. It does fall — graduate unemployment, at roughly 10 to 12 per cent, is far below the national rate — but it does not fall to anything like the levels seen in other economies for their graduates, and the rungs below the degree tell an even starker story. Adults whose highest qualification is matric face unemployment around 35 per cent. The conversion is leaking at every level: a matric certificate, the thing twelve years of the system is built to produce, converts into a job little more than half the time, and a substantial share of degree-holders — the people in whom the most capability has been invested — still cannot convert.

Then there is the cohort that should trouble anyone who takes human development seriously: the roughly 3.5 million young South Africans aged 15 to 24 who are not in employment, education, or training — the NEET group, more than a third of that age band. These are young people in whom the health system has already invested two decades and the education system twelve years, who are now neither building further capability nor exercising the capability they have. They are the conversion problem caught in freeze-frame: full of latent functioning, converting nothing.

Finally, observe what fills the vacuum where earned income should be. South Africa supports roughly 28 million people through social grants — old-age pensions, child-support grants, disability grants, and the social-relief transfers introduced in recent years. The grant system is one of the developing world's more comprehensive, and it is a genuine achievement of post-1994 governance; without it, the standard-of-living collapse would be a humanitarian emergency rather than a chronic crisis. But it is worth naming precisely what it is in the language of this article: **the grant is the state substituting a transfer for a conversion that the economy failed to perform.** It delivers a sliver of the standard-of-living leg directly, bypassing the capability to earn entirely. That is a defensible emergency measure. It is not development, because it does not expand what people are free to do — it cushions the consequence of their not being able to do it. A country that converts capabilities into livelihoods does not need to transfer cash to 28 million people. A country that cannot, does.

:::takeaway
South Africa's labour market is a conversion failure at scale. The absorption rate — under 40 per cent of working-age adults in paid work, against 55–65 per cent in comparable economies — is the single clearest expression of it. Crucially, the failure persists among the healthy and the schooled: matric-holders face roughly 35 per cent unemployment, and even graduates do not convert cleanly. The 28 million people on social grants are not evidence that the system works; they are the measure of how much earning the economy could not deliver, replaced by a transfer.
:::

## Why the Conversion Broke: A First Map

The three remaining articles in this Part each take one piece of the conversion machinery apart in detail, but it helps to see the whole map before walking the streets. Three failures, corresponding to the three kinds of conversion factor, compound into the outcome the data describes.

The **personal** failure is the gap between schooling and skill. South Africa has expanded access to education dramatically since 1994 — enrolment is near universal through the compulsory phase — but, as the Education Part documented, access has not reliably produced usable cognitive and technical skills. The post-school system that is meant to close the remaining gap, the vocational and technical training architecture, has underdelivered. A person can therefore hold the credentials that signal capability without holding the capability itself, and employers, who can tell the difference, price the credential accordingly. [Skills Beyond Schooling](/articles/hd-4-2-skills-beyond-schooling) examines this layer.

The **social** failure is the structure of the economy itself, and here South Africa is a genuine outlier. In most developing countries, a person who cannot find a formal wage job has a fallback: the informal economy — street trade, small-scale services, household enterprise — absorbs a large share of the workforce, often 30 to 50 per cent or more. In South Africa the informal sector is anomalously small, employing under a fifth of the workforce. The fallback that exists almost everywhere else has been, in effect, foreclosed, leaving the unemployed with no self-directed route to a livelihood when the formal sector cannot absorb them. Why this fallback is missing, and what it would take to open it, is the subject of [The Missing Informal Economy](/articles/hd-4-3-the-missing-informal-economy).

The **environmental and structural** failure runs underneath both. Apartheid-era spatial planning, the legacy financial system, and the patterns of asset ownership inherited from a century of dispossession mean that even South Africans who do convert capability into income often cannot convert income into security — into savings, assets, and the intergenerational wealth that turns a wage into a foundation. Earning, in other words, is not the end of the chain. [From Income to Assets](/articles/hd-4-4-from-income-to-assets) follows the conversion one step further, from the paycheck to the balance sheet, and shows why the racial wealth gap that [The Problem That Has No Name](/articles/hd-1-4-the-problem-that-has-no-name) named reproduces itself even when income is earned.

Set out this way, the conversion problem stops looking like a single intractable misfortune and starts looking like what it is: a sequence of specific, identifiable breakages, each with its own history and each, in principle, repairable. That is the disposition this series tries to cultivate — not despair at the scale of the outcome, but attention to the mechanism that produces it.

## The Conversion Problem and the Reinforcing Cycle

This Part sits where it does — between Education and the Part that ties the whole pillar together — for a reason rooted in the [reinforcing cycle](/articles/hd-1-3-the-reinforcing-cycle) that animates this series. That cycle, you will recall, runs through capability: safety enables investment, investment drives growth, growth funds health and education, and health and education produce a capable population that reinforces safety and sustains growth. The cycle's final, closing link is the one this Part is about. A capable population only reinforces the cycle if its capabilities are _converted_ — if health and schooling become productive, earning, taxpaying, stabilising participation in the economy. Capability that cannot convert does not close the loop. It leaks out of the system as frustrated potential, and in the worst cases it feeds the vicious version of the cycle, because a generation of healthy, educated, unemployed young people is not a neutral outcome. It is, as the [Safety and Security](/articles/ss-2-1-the-broken-pipeline) pillar documents from the other direction, a destabilising one.

This is why the conversion problem is not a niche labour-market concern to be parked with the economists. It is the hinge on which the entire human-development cycle turns. You can fix health and you can fix education, and if you cannot fix conversion, the returns on both investments dissipate. Conversely — and this is the more hopeful reading — repairing the conversion machinery multiplies the value of every rand already spent on clinics and classrooms, because it lets accumulated capability finally do its work. The Part that follows this one, [The Reinforcing System](/articles/hd-5-1-when-the-cycle-works), can only reason about virtuous and vicious cycles once all three capabilities — health, education, and the capability to earn — are on the table. This Part puts the third one there.

## What This Means for You

The conversion problem is not an abstraction you observe from a distance. Depending on where you stand, it is the shape of your own life, your household's, or your community's — and seeing it clearly changes what you do about it.

If you are a young person who has finished school and cannot find work, the most important thing this framework offers you is a reframing that is both honest and freeing. Your unemployment is not, in the main, a verdict on your worth or your effort. It is the predictable output of a conversion machinery that was broken before you reached it — a machinery of skills gaps, a foreclosed informal economy, and a spatial geography you did not design. That is the honest part, and it is not nothing, because self-blame is a tax that the unemployed pay on top of unemployment. The freeing part is that a broken mechanism is a mechanism you can learn to work around: the articles that follow are, in part, a map of where the specific blockages are and where the gaps in them can be found.

If you are a parent investing everything in a child's schooling, this article is a caution and a redirection, not a discouragement. Education remains one of the highest-return investments you can make — but its return is realised at the point of conversion, and conversion depends on more than a certificate. The skills that sit above the qualification, the networks that carry information about work, the practical capabilities that an employer can use on day one — these are the difference between a matric that converts and a matric that does not. Ask not only "is my child passing?" but "is my child acquiring something an employer can use?"

If you are an employer, you are a conversion factor in the precise technical sense — your hiring practices, your willingness to train, your openness to candidates from outside the usual networks are part of the social machinery that determines whether a capable person becomes an employed one. That is not a guilt trip; it is a description of leverage. Firms that build their own bridges across the conversion gap — structured entry-level training, apprenticeships, recruitment that reaches past the schools and suburbs that already dominate the applicant pool — gain access to capable people their competitors are screening out.

If you are a voter and a citizen, the reframing is the most consequential of all. A political programme that promises to expand health and education while ignoring the conversion problem is promising to build two legs of a three-legged stool. Demand the third. Ask of any party, any budget, any plan: what does this do to the capability of an ordinary person to _earn_ — not to receive a grant, not to be counted in a GDP figure, but to convert her own health and schooling into a livelihood? That question is the one this Part is built to help you ask, and to judge the answers.

:::takeaway
Health and education are capabilities; a livelihood is the functioning they are meant to convert into. Between the two sits a process of conversion governed by personal, social, and environmental factors — and in South Africa all three are broken at once. The result is a country that has invested heavily in two of the three legs of human development while the third, the capability to earn, has been left to the economy to deliver and the grant system to substitute for. Repairing the conversion machinery is not a separate project from health and education. It is what makes those investments pay.
:::

:::framework
**The Conversion Audit: Tracing a Capability to a Livelihood**

Use this framework to locate where, in a specific case, the conversion from capability to livelihood is breaking down. Run it on yourself, on a family member's situation, or on a community programme you are assessing.

**Step 1: Confirm the capability exists.**
Is the underlying capability actually present — not the credential, but the thing the credential is supposed to certify? A matric or diploma that did not impart usable skills is a resource without a capability. If the capability is genuinely absent, the breakage is upstream, in education and skills (see [Skills Beyond Schooling](/articles/hd-4-2-skills-beyond-schooling)).

**Step 2: Check the personal conversion factors.**
Given the capability, are the individual-level conditions for using it in place — health to sustain work, specific applicable skills, the documentation and certification employers require? A capable person without a driver's licence, a portfolio, or a recognised qualification is blocked at the personal layer.

**Step 3: Check the social conversion factors.**
Does the person have access to the networks and institutions through which work is found? Is the formal labour market hiring at their level, and is there an informal or self-employment fallback if it is not (see [The Missing Informal Economy](/articles/hd-4-3-the-missing-informal-economy))? Is discrimination — by race, gender, age, or address — filtering them out?

**Step 4: Check the environmental conversion factors.**
Can the person physically and economically reach the work? Calculate the real return after transport cost and commuting time. A job that consumes a third of its own wage and four hours a day in travel may be a job that cannot be converted into a sustainable livelihood.

**Step 5: Follow the conversion past the paycheck.**
If income is being earned, is it converting into security — savings, assets, a foundation that outlasts the next shock (see [From Income to Assets](/articles/hd-4-4-from-income-to-assets))? Earning without accumulating is conversion that stops one step short.

The value of the audit is that it replaces a single overwhelming question — "why can't people find work?" — with a sequence of specific, answerable ones, each pointing to a different repair.
:::
