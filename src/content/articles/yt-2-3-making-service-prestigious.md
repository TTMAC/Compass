## The Bottom Line, Up Front

Compulsion without reward breeds resentment; prestige plus real benefits breeds demand. The design goal is a service that young people are proud to have completed because finishing it visibly changes their life chances.

## Outline

- **BLUF / hook** — Contrast two futures: a stipend-relief scheme people endure vs. a national rite of passage people are proud of. The difference is engineered, not accidental.
- **The prestige problem** — Why South African youth programmes read as "for those with no other option." What makes Singapore's NS, or a Chevening/Fulbright, *aspirational*: universality, visible alumni, status, selectivity signalling.
- **The benefits architecture (the heart of the article)** — Three concrete, credible pillars:
  1. **Access to jobs** — a recognised completion credential as a hiring signal; preferential entry into public-sector learnerships and posts; private-employer partnerships.
  2. **Access to finance for venture creation** — a ring-fenced concessional finance / grant facility for graduates who complete, to start enterprises; de-fragment and tie existing DFI funding (NYDA, SEFA) to the credential.
  3. **Societal recognition** — public honours, a national service day, alumni networks, civic standing as social capital.
- **Designing incentives that actually bite** — Benefits must be *automatic on completion*, legible, and large enough to change behaviour. Learn from past failure modes.
- **Guarding against capture and unfairness** — Merit vs. patronage; keeping the benefit pipeline clean (links to the Reform Agenda's anti-capture thinking).
- **Reader's struggling moment** — "What's actually in it for me?" Answer in plain, specific terms.
- **Close with the practical tool** — A "benefits ladder": complete service → certificate → job preference + finance access + recognition.
