## One Strategy, Three Angles

Growth does not come from a single sector breakthrough. It does not arrive because a government minister announces a masterplan, or because a single megaproject breaks ground, or because one industry has a good year. Sustained, broad-based economic growth — the kind that moves a country from $7,000 GDP per capita toward $15,000 and beyond — comes from systematically expanding three things at once: the number of businesses in the economy, the range of products those businesses sell to the world, and the availability of capital to finance both. If you have followed this series through the three stages of growth — [Resource Optimisation](/articles/eg-2-1-resource-optimisation), [The Efficiency Transition](/articles/eg-2-3-the-efficiency-transition), and [Innovation-Driven Growth](/articles/eg-2-4-innovation-driven-growth) — you understand what South Africa needs to produce and where the comparative advantages lie. This article is about the machinery that makes that production happen regardless of which stage you are looking at.

Think of the three engines this way. New venture creation determines how many businesses are in the game. Export diversification determines what those businesses sell and to whom. MSME (micro, small, and medium enterprise) financing determines whether those businesses can start, survive, and scale. These are not three separate policy areas that happen to coexist. They are three dimensions of the same underlying challenge: South Africa has too few firms producing too few products for too few markets with too little capital. Every comparator country that has made the transition South Africa is attempting — Chile, Malaysia, South Korea, Botswana at its best — attacked all three dimensions simultaneously. Countries that treated them as separate line items in separate departmental budgets, managed by separate institutions with separate mandates, achieved far less.

The numbers make the integration clear. South Africa's economy is dominated by a remarkably small number of large firms. The top forty JSE-listed companies account for a disproportionate share of formal employment, tax revenue, and export earnings. The export basket is narrow: minerals (platinum group metals, gold, iron ore, coal, manganese), vehicles (assembled under preferential trade agreements), and agriculture (citrus, wine, deciduous fruit) account for the bulk of merchandise exports. And the financing system is structured around the needs of those large firms. The four major commercial banks — Standard Bank, FirstRand, Absa, and Nedbank — lend overwhelmingly to established corporates and to households via mortgages and consumer credit. The small business lending gap is not a footnote in South Africa's growth story. It is one of the central explanations for why growth has stalled.

This article examines each engine in turn, but the core argument is that they only work as a system. More ventures without export market access produce businesses that compete for slices of a stagnant domestic pie. Export diversification without venture creation means that the same small number of large firms capture the new opportunities while unemployment remains structurally embedded. And financing without viable ventures to fund and viable markets to sell into produces non-performing loans and wasted development finance. The integrated system — more businesses producing more products for more markets with more access to capital — is the growth strategy. Everything else is implementation detail. The institutional coordination required to make the system work is the subject of [The Execution Machine](/articles/eg-3-2-the-execution-machine). This article gives you the substance that the execution machine must coordinate.

## Engine One: New Venture Creation and Scaling

### The Entrepreneurship Deficit

South Africa does not have a shortage of people who want to start businesses. It has a shortage of people who successfully do. The Global Entrepreneurship Monitor (GEM), which has tracked entrepreneurial activity across countries since 1999, consistently ranks South Africa's Total Early-Stage Entrepreneurial Activity (TEA) rate — the percentage of the working-age population actively starting or running a new business — well below comparator countries. South Africa's TEA rate has hovered between roughly seven and eleven per cent over the past decade. Compare that to Chile (around twenty-five per cent), Brazil (around twenty per cent), or even Botswana (around fifteen per cent in recent surveys). The gap is not marginal. South Africa's early-stage entrepreneurship rate is roughly half that of peer economies at similar income levels.

The TEA rate alone does not tell you everything. What matters as much is the composition and survival of those ventures. GEM data distinguishes between "necessity-driven" entrepreneurship — people starting businesses because they have no other income option — and "opportunity-driven" entrepreneurship — people starting businesses because they have identified a market gap they believe they can fill profitably. South Africa's entrepreneurial activity is disproportionately necessity-driven. Many of the country's micro-enterprises — spaza shops, street vendors, informal service providers — exist because the formal economy excludes their owners, not because a genuine business opportunity beckoned. There is nothing wrong with necessity entrepreneurship as a survival mechanism, but it does not drive the kind of productive, scalable, export-capable venture creation that moves GDP per capita upward.

The survival rate compounds the problem. Roughly seventy per cent of new small businesses in South Africa fail within the first two years of operation. The failure rate is not unique to South Africa — small business mortality is high everywhere — but it is notably higher than in countries with stronger entrepreneurial ecosystems. And the causes of failure point directly to structural barriers rather than individual shortcomings. When you survey failed small business owners in South Africa about why their ventures collapsed, the same factors recur: inability to access finance, regulatory compliance costs that are disproportionate to the size of the business, crime (particularly theft and robbery), unreliable electricity supply, municipal service failures (water, waste removal, road maintenance), and difficulty finding affordable commercial premises.

These are not problems that motivational speaking or entrepreneurship training programmes can solve. They are structural features of the South African business environment that systematically disadvantage small firms relative to large ones. A large corporation can absorb the cost of a diesel generator during load-shedding; a township hair salon cannot. A listed company can employ a compliance team to navigate BEE scorecards, tax obligations, labour law, and municipal licensing; a startup with three employees cannot. A multinational has the bargaining power to negotiate favourable electricity tariffs; a micro-manufacturer in a rural town pays the standard rate or goes without. The entrepreneurship deficit is not primarily a deficit of ambition or ideas. It is a deficit of enabling conditions.

### The MSME Support Ecosystem: Why It Has Underperformed

South Africa is not short of institutions mandated to support small businesses. The Small Enterprise Development Agency (SEDA) provides non-financial support — business planning, training, incubation, market access facilitation. The Small Enterprise Finance Agency (SEFA) provides financial support — loans, credit guarantees, and wholesale lending through intermediaries. The Department of Trade, Industry and Competition (the dtic) administers a suite of incentive programmes including the Black Industrialists Scheme, the Manufacturing Competitiveness Enhancement Programme, and various sector-specific grants. The National Empowerment Fund (NEF) provides funding specifically for black-owned enterprises. The Industrial Development Corporation (IDC) provides development finance across sectors.

On paper, this is a comprehensive ecosystem. In practice, the results have been deeply disappointing. SEDA's reach is limited — it operates through a network of provincial offices and technology transfer centres, but the sheer scale of the MSME population relative to SEDA's capacity means that most small business owners never interact with the agency. SEFA's loan disbursement has been plagued by high default rates, raising questions about both the quality of its credit assessment and the viability of the businesses it funds. The dtic's incentive programmes are notoriously complex to access, with application processes that can take months, documentation requirements that overwhelm small businesses without dedicated administrative staff, and disbursement timelines that are misaligned with business cash flow realities. A manufacturer who needs working capital in March to fulfil an export order in June cannot wait until November for a government incentive payment.

:::expert
Trade and Industrial Policy Strategies (TIPS), in its evaluation of South Africa's MSME support architecture, identified a fundamental design problem: the ecosystem is structured around the supply of support services rather than the demand of actual businesses. SEDA, SEFA, the dtic, the NEF, and the IDC each have their own mandates, their own application processes, their own eligibility criteria, and their own reporting requirements. A small business owner seeking a combination of training, finance, and market access must navigate multiple institutions, each with different forms, different timelines, and different definitions of what constitutes a qualifying enterprise. The transaction costs of accessing the support system are themselves a barrier to the businesses the system is supposed to serve. TIPS concluded that the fragmentation of the ecosystem across multiple departments and agencies, each defending its institutional mandate, produces duplication, gaps, and a compliance burden that falls disproportionately on the smallest firms.
:::

The fragmentation is not merely administrative. It reflects a deeper conceptual failure. South Africa's MSME policy has historically treated small business development as a social welfare programme — something you do to reduce unemployment and promote inclusion — rather than as an economic growth strategy — something you do because more competitive firms producing more goods and services is the mechanism through which economies grow. This distinction matters because it shapes everything from programme design (welfare programmes measure disbursements and beneficiaries; growth programmes measure firm survival, revenue growth, and export entry) to institutional culture (welfare-oriented institutions prioritise access and equity; growth-oriented institutions prioritise competitiveness and scale).

### International Evidence: What Works

The international evidence on effective MSME development converges on several principles that South Africa's ecosystem has not consistently applied.

The United States Small Business Administration (SBA), established in 1953, is often cited as the benchmark for public MSME support. The SBA's core innovation was not direct lending — although it does some of that — but rather a loan guarantee programme that reduces the risk for commercial banks lending to small businesses. Under the SBA's flagship 7(a) programme, the government guarantees up to seventy-five to eighty-five per cent of a qualifying small business loan made by a private bank. This means the bank bears only a fraction of the default risk, which fundamentally changes the lending calculus. Banks that would never lend to a startup barbecue restaurant or a three-person software consultancy on their own balance sheets will do so when the government absorbs most of the downside. The SBA guarantee programme has facilitated hundreds of billions of dollars in small business lending since its inception, at a cost to the taxpayer that is a fraction of what direct government lending would require.

The genius of the SBA model is that it works through the existing financial system rather than trying to replace it. Private banks make the credit decisions, manage the loan relationships, and bear partial risk. The government provides the guarantee, sets the eligibility criteria, and absorbs a defined share of losses. This hybrid structure captures the credit assessment expertise of private lenders while addressing the market failure — the systematic underpricing of small business lending because individual loan sizes are too small to justify the fixed costs of underwriting and monitoring — that causes the financing gap in the first place.

Chile's CORFO (Corporacion de Fomento de la Produccion) takes a different but complementary approach. CORFO operates a range of instruments including startup grants (through the Start-Up Chile programme, which has attracted thousands of early-stage entrepreneurs from around the world), matching grants for innovation and technology adoption, and co-investment funds that match private venture capital with public funding. The Start-Up Chile programme is particularly instructive because it was designed not merely to fund individual startups but to catalyse an entrepreneurial ecosystem. By attracting international entrepreneurs to Chile, providing them with seed funding and a visa, and embedding them in Chilean accelerators and co-working spaces, the programme aimed to shift cultural norms around entrepreneurship, build networks between Chilean and international founders, and demonstrate to Chilean investors that early-stage venture funding could generate returns.

Singapore's Enterprise Development Board (now Enterprise Singapore) pursued yet another model, focusing on helping existing MSMEs upgrade their capabilities and access international markets. Enterprise Singapore's programmes include productivity improvement grants (subsidising technology adoption and process improvement), market readiness assistance (helping firms prepare for export), and an extensive network of overseas trade offices that provide market intelligence and buyer introductions. The Singaporean approach reflects the reality that most employment growth in successful economies comes not from brand-new startups but from existing small and medium firms that grow into larger firms — if they receive the right combination of capability-building support and market access.

The common thread across these models is that they treat MSME development as an economic strategy, not a social programme. They measure success by firm performance — survival rates, revenue growth, employment creation, export volumes — not by the number of applications processed or training sessions delivered. And they work through or alongside the private sector rather than attempting to substitute for it. South Africa's MSME ecosystem has historically done the opposite: it has prioritised disbursement targets over performance outcomes, created parallel government structures rather than leveraging private sector capabilities, and fragmented support across multiple agencies with overlapping mandates and inadequate coordination.

### Schramm's Law: Why Scale Matters

The stakes of getting venture creation right extend beyond MSME survival rates. Carl Schramm, former President and CEO of the Ewing Marion Kauffman Foundation, formulated what has become known as Schramm's Law: the number of startups that reach billion-dollar revenue within twenty years is the single most important contributor to a nation's economic growth. The claim is bold but the supporting evidence is substantial. Kauffman Foundation research found that from 1980 to 2005, startups in the United States generated approximately three million new jobs annually, compared to roughly one million per year from established companies. Startups are also disproportionately responsible for disruptive innovation and productivity growth — precisely the dynamics that South Africa's low-complexity, extraction-oriented economy needs most.

Schramm's Law implies a specific policy orientation. Rather than concentrating resources on a handful of high-profile ventures — the "pick winners" approach that South African industrial policy has historically favoured — governments should focus on maximising the number and diversity of startups that enter the pipeline. The logic is probabilistic: no one can predict which ventures will scale to transformative size, so the most effective strategy is to lower barriers to entry broadly, fund a large portfolio of early-stage firms across varied sectors, and ensure that the ecosystem supports the transition from startup to scale-up. This means investing in venture capital funds, incubators and accelerators, grants and tax incentives for early-stage businesses, educational systems that foster innovation, and mentorship networks that connect founders with experienced operators. It also means accepting that most startups will fail — what matters is that the system produces enough ventures at sufficient scale to shift the growth trajectory.

For South Africa, Schramm's Law underscores a structural urgency. If the country's entrepreneurial pipeline continues to produce mostly necessity-driven micro-enterprises with seventy per cent failure rates, the cumulative effect on GDP growth, employment, and economic complexity will be devastating — not because individual failures are catastrophic, but because the system is not generating enough scaled ventures to compensate. The MSME support ecosystem reforms discussed above are not merely administrative improvements. They are preconditions for the kind of venture creation pipeline that Schramm's Law identifies as the engine of national economic growth.

:::takeaway
South Africa's entrepreneurship deficit is structural, not cultural. The country's early-stage entrepreneurial activity rate is roughly half that of comparable economies, and small business failure rates are exceptionally high. The causes are not a lack of ambition but a combination of regulatory burden, crime, energy unreliability, municipal service failures, and financing exclusion. The existing MSME support ecosystem — SEDA, SEFA, the dtic incentives, the NEF, the IDC — is fragmented, supply-driven rather than demand-driven, and oriented toward social welfare metrics rather than economic growth metrics. International models — the US SBA's loan guarantees, Chile's CORFO ecosystem approach, Singapore's Enterprise Development Board — demonstrate that effective MSME support works through the private sector, measures firm performance rather than disbursement volumes, and treats venture creation as an economic growth strategy rather than a poverty alleviation programme.
:::

## Engine Two: Export Diversification

### The Narrow Basket Problem

South Africa's export structure tells a story of unrealised potential. The Harvard Growth Lab's Atlas of Economic Complexity, which maps every country's export basket and calculates an Economic Complexity Index (ECI), ranks South Africa in the middle of the global distribution — roughly 50th to 60th out of approximately 130 countries, depending on the year. That ranking is lower than you would expect for an economy of South Africa's size, industrial history, and institutional sophistication. It is lower than Chile, lower than Malaysia, and substantially lower than South Korea or any of the East Asian manufacturing economies.

The ECI matters because it is one of the strongest predictors of future economic growth that economists have identified. Countries with high economic complexity — meaning they export a diverse range of sophisticated products that few other countries can make — tend to grow faster than countries that export a narrow range of simple products. The logic is intuitive: a country that can manufacture pharmaceuticals, precision instruments, and specialised chemicals has deeper capabilities — in education, in industrial organisation, in institutional quality — than a country that exports raw minerals and basic agricultural commodities. Those deeper capabilities generate growth opportunities across multiple sectors, making the economy more resilient and more dynamic.

South Africa's export basket is dominated by a handful of product categories. Mineral products (platinum group metals, gold, iron ore, coal, manganese, chrome) typically account for roughly thirty to forty per cent of merchandise exports. Vehicles and vehicle parts (assembled predominantly under the Automotive Production and Development Programme) account for approximately twelve to fifteen per cent. Agricultural products (citrus, wine, table grapes, macadamia nuts, maize when there is a surplus) account for roughly eight to ten per cent. Chemicals, machinery, and base metals make up much of the remainder. The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) — a standard measure of concentration where a score closer to 1.0 means extreme concentration and a score closer to 0 means perfect diversification — shows South Africa's export basket as moderately concentrated and, on some measures, becoming more concentrated over time as mineral exports have grown while manufactured goods exports have stagnated.

The problem with export concentration is not merely that it exposes the economy to commodity price volatility — although it does. The deeper problem is that it reflects and reinforces an economy stuck in the lower stages of complexity. When minerals dominate your export basket, the incentive structure of the entire economy orients around extraction. The most profitable activities, the highest-paying jobs, the most attractive investment opportunities, and the most powerful political lobbies all cluster around the extractive sector. This makes diversification harder, not easier, because the very success of the dominant sector absorbs the capital, skills, and policy attention that diversification requires.

### Nearby Products: Where Diversification Can Start

The Atlas of Economic Complexity does not merely measure where a country stands. It maps where a country can plausibly go. The concept of "nearby products" — technically called "product proximity" in the complexity literature — identifies products that a country does not currently export competitively but that are closely related, in terms of required capabilities, to products it already does export. Moving into nearby products is easier, faster, and more likely to succeed than leaping to distant products that require entirely new capabilities.

For South Africa, the nearby product analysis reveals several clusters of opportunity that align with the staged growth strategy you have already encountered in this series.

The first cluster surrounds platinum group metals. South Africa already exports refined PGMs. The nearby products include catalytic converters, fuel cell components, specialised platinum-based chemicals, and automotive emission control systems. These products require metallurgical engineering capabilities that South Africa already possesses in rudimentary form through its mining and refining operations, plus additional manufacturing and precision engineering capabilities that would need to be built. The distance from "refined platinum" to "catalytic converter" is far shorter than the distance from "refined platinum" to "smartphone," and the evidence from [Resource Optimisation](/articles/eg-2-1-resource-optimisation) shows that the binding constraints — energy reliability, manufacturing incentives, trade agreements — are identifiable and addressable.

The second cluster surrounds vehicle assembly. South Africa already assembles vehicles for several major manufacturers under the APDP. The nearby products include more sophisticated automotive components (currently many components are imported for local assembly), aerospace components (which require similar precision manufacturing capabilities), and rail rolling stock (which requires similar heavy manufacturing and assembly capabilities). The automotive cluster in the Eastern Cape and Gauteng has generated an ecosystem of component suppliers, logistics providers, and engineering services firms. Extending that ecosystem into adjacent product categories is a natural progression — and one that [The Efficiency Transition](/articles/eg-2-3-the-efficiency-transition) identified as central to Stage 2 of the growth strategy.

The third cluster surrounds agriculture. South Africa already exports citrus, wine, and fresh deciduous fruit. The nearby products include essential oils (distilled from citrus peel and other botanical sources), fruit-based beverages (juices, concentrates, flavoured drinks), processed and preserved foods (dried fruit, canned goods, frozen prepared foods), and cosmetic and pharmaceutical ingredients derived from indigenous plants (rooibos extracts, marula oil, honeybush extracts). The agricultural cluster is particularly attractive because it is labour-intensive, geographically distributed (creating employment opportunities outside the major metros), and less energy-dependent than mineral processing.

The fourth cluster, less immediately obvious but significant, surrounds South Africa's financial and professional services sector. South Africa exports financial services, actuarial services, legal services, and engineering consulting services — primarily into the rest of Africa. The nearby products in the services domain include fintech platforms, insurtech solutions, mining services consulting, and educational technology — all areas where South African firms have some existing capability and where African market demand is growing rapidly.

:::expert
The World Bank's analysis of South Africa's economic complexity, drawing on the Atlas of Economic Complexity methodology developed at Harvard's Growth Lab, identified a structural trap in the country's export diversification pattern. South Africa has become more concentrated in mineral and basic manufactured exports over the past two decades, even as its peers have diversified. The World Bank analysis noted that South Africa's product space position — the set of products it currently exports with revealed comparative advantage — is located in a relatively sparse part of the global product space, meaning that fewer "nearby products" are accessible compared to countries positioned in denser manufacturing clusters. However, the analysis also identified several promising diversification pathways, particularly in speciality chemicals derived from mining inputs, processed agricultural products, and professional and financial services for African markets. The key finding was that diversification will not happen through market forces alone: it requires deliberate industrial policy to overcome the coordination failures and capability gaps that prevent firms from moving into nearby products without public support.
:::

### AfCFTA: The Market Expansion Opportunity

Export diversification is not only about what you sell. It is about where you sell it. The African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA), which entered its operational phase in 2021, is the largest free trade area in the world by number of participating countries. When fully implemented, it will create a single market of over 1.3 billion people with a combined GDP of over $3 trillion. For South Africa, AfCFTA represents a fundamentally different kind of export opportunity than the traditional markets in Europe, the United States, and Asia.

South Africa is already the largest and most diversified economy on the African continent. It already exports a wider range of manufactured goods than any other African country. This means that within the AfCFTA framework, South Africa is uniquely positioned to be the continent's manufacturing hub — the country that supplies processed foods, chemicals, vehicles, machinery, financial services, and consumer goods to a growing African middle class. The opportunity is not to compete with China in manufacturing consumer electronics for the global market. It is to be the manufacturer of choice for African markets that are currently supplied by imports from outside the continent.

The scale of the opportunity is substantial. Intra-African trade currently accounts for roughly fifteen to eighteen per cent of total African trade — far lower than intra-regional trade in Europe (around sixty per cent), Asia (around fifty per cent), or North America (around forty per cent). If AfCFTA succeeds in raising intra-African trade to even thirty per cent — still half the European level — the additional trade volume represents hundreds of billions of dollars annually, and South Africa's diversified manufacturing base positions it to capture a disproportionate share.

But there are important caveats. AfCFTA implementation is slow. Tariff schedules are still being negotiated for sensitive products. Rules of origin — which determine whether a product qualifies for preferential tariff treatment — are complex and contested. Non-tariff barriers — border delays, customs inefficiencies, infrastructure bottlenecks, regulatory differences — remain formidable. And South Africa's own trade logistics infrastructure, as documented in successive World Bank Logistics Performance Index reports, has deteriorated. Port inefficiencies at Durban and Cape Town, rail freight constraints on the Transnet network, and border post congestion all limit the country's ability to capitalise on AfCFTA preferences even where they have been agreed.

The export diversification engine, then, requires simultaneous work on both sides: expanding the range of products South Africa can competitively produce (the supply side) and securing and operationalising market access through AfCFTA and bilateral trade agreements (the demand side). Neither alone is sufficient. Producing new products without market access leaves them stranded. Securing market access without competitive production to fill the orders leaves the opportunity for others to capture. This is why export diversification cannot be separated from venture creation (you need firms to produce the goods) or from financing (you need capital to fund the production). The three engines turn together or they do not turn at all.

:::takeaway
South Africa's export basket is narrow and, on some measures, becoming narrower. The Atlas of Economic Complexity identifies several clusters of "nearby products" — catalytic converters and fuel cell components from PGMs, aerospace and rail components from vehicle assembly, processed foods and essential oils from agriculture, fintech and professional services from the existing services sector — that represent achievable diversification pathways. AfCFTA offers a market expansion opportunity of unprecedented scale, positioning South Africa as a manufacturing and services hub for the African continent. But export diversification requires simultaneous progress on product capabilities, trade logistics, and market access — and it will not happen without the venture creation and financing engines running in parallel.
:::

## Engine Three: New Financing Vehicles

### How Businesses Are Financed

Every business, from a spaza shop to a multinational corporation, is financed through some combination of two fundamental sources: equity (ownership capital — the money that founders, partners, or investors put in, in exchange for a share of the business) and debt (borrowed capital — money that must be repaid, usually with interest, regardless of whether the business succeeds or fails). The mix between equity and debt that a business uses — its capital structure — is not arbitrary. It depends on several factors.

The age of the business matters enormously. A brand-new venture with no revenue and no track record cannot borrow from a bank because it has nothing to pledge and no cash flow to service repayments. Its only realistic source of capital is equity — the founder's savings, contributions from family and friends, or investment from angel investors or venture capitalists who accept the high risk of failure in exchange for a share of potentially large returns. As a business matures, establishes revenue, builds assets, and develops a financial track record, it becomes eligible for debt — first small amounts on unfavourable terms, then progressively larger amounts at lower interest rates as it demonstrates reliability.

Industry shapes the mix. Capital-intensive industries like manufacturing, mining, and property development tend to carry more debt because they have tangible assets (machinery, land, buildings) that can serve as collateral. Knowledge-intensive industries like technology and professional services, where the primary assets are people and intellectual property, tend to rely more heavily on equity because they have fewer physical assets for lenders to claim if things go wrong.

Geography matters because the financial infrastructure, regulatory environment, and investor culture differ from place to place. A tech startup in Cape Town has access to a growing but still limited venture capital ecosystem. The same business in Silicon Valley would have access to a much deeper pool of equity investors, different expectations about growth and returns, and a legal infrastructure purpose-built for venture finance. In rural South Africa, formal finance of any kind — equity or debt — is scarce, and businesses rely on informal sources: personal savings, stokvels, family networks, and supplier credit.

The perceived risk of the opportunity shapes what financing is available and on what terms. A business pursuing a proven model in a stable market — a franchise, a government contract, a well-understood commodity trade — is seen as lower risk and can access debt more easily and cheaply. A business pursuing something novel, unproven, or ambitious — a new technology, an untested market, a business model that has never been tried in South Africa — is perceived as higher risk, which makes lenders reluctant and pushes the business toward equity investors who are willing to bet on uncertain outcomes.

Understanding this equity-debt spectrum is essential context for what follows, because South Africa's financing gap is not a single problem. It is a series of failures across different points on the spectrum — failures of equity provision for early-stage businesses, failures of debt provision for growing businesses, and failures of blended finance for the vast middle ground where most economic activity should be happening.

### The Missing Middle

If you want to understand why South Africa has too few growing businesses despite having one of the most sophisticated financial sectors in the developing world, you need to understand the concept of the "missing middle." The missing middle is the financing gap between microfinance (small loans of a few hundred to a few thousand rands, typically provided by microfinance institutions and stokvels to survivalist enterprises and households) and commercial bank lending (loans of millions of rands, typically provided to established corporates with audited financial statements, substantial collateral, and multi-year track records). The businesses in between — a restaurant that needs R500,000 to expand to a second location, a manufacturer that needs R2 million for new equipment, a tech startup that needs R5 million for product development and market entry — fall into a financing void.

The void exists not because South Africa lacks capital. The country's financial system is deep by any global standard. The Johannesburg Stock Exchange is among the largest in the world by market capitalisation. The four major commercial banks are well-capitalised, profitable, and technologically sophisticated. The asset management industry manages trillions of rands. Pension funds, insurance companies, and sovereign entities hold enormous pools of investable capital. The problem is not the quantity of capital in the system. The problem is the allocation of capital — specifically, the systematic failure of that capital to flow toward productive small and medium enterprises.

### Why Banks Do Not Lend to Small Businesses at Scale

Commercial banks are rational institutions. They allocate capital where the risk-adjusted return is highest relative to the cost of origination, monitoring, and collection. For small business lending, the arithmetic is unfavourable on virtually every dimension.

The cost of originating a R500,000 small business loan is not dramatically less than the cost of originating a R50 million corporate loan. The bank must still assess the borrower's creditworthiness, evaluate the business plan, verify financial statements (if they exist — many small businesses have informal or incomplete accounting), assess the collateral, structure the loan terms, and process the documentation. The fixed cost of this process is spread over a much smaller loan amount, making the cost-to-revenue ratio far worse for small loans than for large ones. A corporate banker who closes a R50 million deal earns the bank far more revenue per hour of work than a small business banker who closes a R500,000 deal.

The information problem compounds the cost problem. Large corporates have audited financial statements, credit ratings, observable market positions, and track records that banks can evaluate using standardised analytical frameworks. Small businesses, particularly young ones, have none of these. Their financial records may be kept in a notebook or a spreadsheet. Their business plan may exist only in the founder's head. Their market position is unproven. Their management team is usually one person. For the bank, lending to such a business means making a credit decision with radically less information than the bank's risk models are designed to process, which means either refusing the loan (the most common outcome) or pricing the risk through a very high interest rate (which makes the loan unaffordable and increases the probability of default, creating a self-fulfilling prophecy).

The collateral problem is the third barrier. South African commercial banks rely heavily on collateral — typically fixed property — to secure business loans. This reflects both prudent risk management and the legacy of a banking system designed for an economy where property ownership was widespread among the borrowing population. But in a country where decades of dispossession and exclusion mean that millions of economically active people do not own fixed property, the collateral requirement effectively locks out the majority of potential entrepreneurs. You cannot pledge what you do not own. And the assets that small business owners do possess — vehicles, equipment, inventory, intellectual property, customer contracts — are harder for banks to value, harder to liquidate in the event of default, and therefore less acceptable as security.

The cumulative effect of these three barriers — high origination costs, information asymmetry, and collateral constraints — is that South Africa's commercial banking system rationally, predictably, and systematically excludes small businesses from credit. This is not a moral failing. It is a market failure. The cost structure of traditional bank lending is simply misaligned with the characteristics of small business borrowers. Fixing this requires not exhortation but structural intervention: changing the risk-return calculus so that lending to MSMEs becomes commercially viable.

### Development Finance Institutions: Partial Solutions

South Africa has a range of development finance institutions (DFIs) that are explicitly mandated to fill financing gaps that commercial banks leave. The Industrial Development Corporation (IDC), established in 1940, provides development finance across industrial sectors with a focus on manufacturing, mining, and agro-processing. The Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA) finances infrastructure and development projects. The National Empowerment Fund (NEF) focuses on black-owned enterprises. SEFA focuses on small and micro enterprises.

These institutions have collectively disbursed billions of rands over the years, and they have played a role in financing projects and businesses that commercial banks would not touch. But they have not solved the missing middle problem at scale. The reasons are instructive.

First, the DFIs themselves often replicate the lending practices of commercial banks — requiring extensive documentation, audited financials, and collateral — because they face the same incentive to minimise loan defaults. A DFI that experiences high default rates faces parliamentary scrutiny, media criticism, and potential fiscal liability. This creates a risk aversion that is rational at the institutional level but counterproductive at the systemic level: the DFIs that are supposed to fill the gap end up fishing in the same pool as the commercial banks, funding established businesses that could probably access commercial finance anyway, rather than taking on the genuinely risky early-stage ventures that constitute the real missing middle.

Second, the DFIs are undercapitalised relative to the scale of the problem. The missing middle in South Africa is estimated at hundreds of billions of rands — the aggregate unmet demand for financing from MSMEs that have viable business propositions but cannot access credit. No single DFI, and indeed not all of them combined, has the balance sheet capacity to fill that gap through direct lending. The solution must involve leveraging private capital, not substituting for it.

Third, coordination among the DFIs is weak. The IDC, DBSA, NEF, SEFA, and various provincial development agencies operate with overlapping mandates, different application processes, and limited information sharing. A business that is declined by one institution may not be referred to another that might be a better fit. The ecosystem is fragmented in the same way that the non-financial MSME support system is fragmented — a pattern of institutional proliferation without institutional coordination that [The Execution Machine](/articles/eg-3-2-the-execution-machine) addresses directly.

:::expert
FinMark Trust, in its series of studies on MSME access to finance in South Africa, quantified the missing middle with unusual precision. Using survey data from thousands of small business owners, FinMark estimated that the unmet demand for MSME credit — the gap between what qualifying small businesses need and what the formal financial system provides — runs into hundreds of billions of rands annually. The studies found that fewer than fifteen per cent of MSMEs had accessed formal credit from a bank or DFI, while a substantial additional proportion had been declined or had not applied because they expected to be declined. The "discouraged borrower" effect — where potential borrowers self-select out of the credit market because they perceive the application process as hopeless — was found to be as significant as actual credit rationing. FinMark's analysis concluded that the missing middle is not a temporary market imperfection that will self-correct as the economy matures. It is a structural feature of the South African financial system that requires deliberate, sustained policy intervention to address.
:::

### Structural Alternatives: What Would Actually Work

If direct DFI lending cannot solve the missing middle at scale, and commercial banks will not solve it without changed incentives, what are the structural alternatives?

The first and most impactful intervention would be an SBA-style partial credit guarantee programme, adapted to South African conditions. The principle is straightforward: the government (or a designated DFI such as SEFA or the IDC) guarantees a defined percentage — say, fifty to seventy-five per cent — of qualifying small business loans made by commercial banks. The bank originates the loan, manages the relationship, and bears partial risk. The government absorbs the remainder of the default risk, funded from a guarantee fund that is recapitalised from the national budget or from DFI revenues. This structure changes the risk-return calculus for banks without requiring the government to build a parallel lending infrastructure. It works through the existing system, leveraging the banks' branch networks, credit assessment capabilities, and collection infrastructure.

South Africa has experimented with credit guarantees in various forms — the Khula Credit Guarantee Scheme, later absorbed into SEFA, was an early attempt — but these programmes have never achieved the scale, simplicity, or bank buy-in that makes the SBA model effective. The failures were typically traced to overly complex eligibility criteria, slow claims processing when defaults occurred (causing banks to lose confidence in the guarantee), insufficient capitalisation of the guarantee fund, and a lack of political commitment to sustaining the programme through the inevitable early years of learning and adjustment. An effective guarantee programme requires not just design but persistence — the willingness to refine the programme over five to ten years as banks and borrowers learn to use it.

The second intervention is blended finance — structures that combine concessional public or development capital with commercial private capital to fund MSME lending at scale. A typical blended finance structure might involve a DFI providing a "first loss" tranche — the first ten to twenty per cent of losses in a portfolio of MSME loans — with commercial investors providing the remaining capital at market rates. The first loss tranche absorbs the most likely losses, making the remaining tranches sufficiently safe to attract institutional investors (pension funds, insurance companies) that would never invest in an unprotected MSME loan portfolio. Blended finance is not a theoretical concept. It is widely used in development finance globally, and South Africa's own institutional investors — the Government Employees Pension Fund, the Public Investment Corporation, the major retirement funds — have the capital and, in many cases, the mandate to invest in development-oriented instruments if the risk-return profile is structured appropriately.

The third intervention, and potentially the most transformative in the medium term, is fintech-enabled digital MSME lending. The cost and information barriers that prevent traditional banks from lending to small businesses are not laws of nature. They are artefacts of a lending model built around physical branches, paper documentation, and human credit officers. Digital lending platforms can fundamentally change the arithmetic. They can originate loans at a fraction of the cost of a traditional bank branch. They can use alternative data — transaction histories from mobile money and point-of-sale systems, social media activity, supply chain data, utility payment records — to assess creditworthiness without requiring audited financial statements. They can automate monitoring and collection, reducing the ongoing cost of managing a small loan portfolio. And they can process applications in hours rather than weeks, which matters enormously for small businesses whose opportunities are time-sensitive.

South Africa's fintech ecosystem is the most developed in Africa, anchored by a sophisticated payments infrastructure (including the national payment system and the growing mobile payments market), a deep pool of technology talent, and a regulatory framework (administered by the South African Reserve Bank and the Financial Sector Conduct Authority) that, while imperfect, provides more clarity than most African jurisdictions. Several South African fintech lenders — including companies focused on invoice discounting, merchant cash advances, and digital term loans — have demonstrated that MSME lending can be commercially viable when delivered through digital channels with alternative credit assessment. The challenge is scale. These platforms collectively serve a tiny fraction of the MSME population. Scaling them requires a combination of regulatory facilitation (clearer licensing frameworks for digital lenders, open banking provisions that allow fintechs to access bank transaction data with customer consent), capital market development (enabling fintechs to securitise MSME loan portfolios and raise capital from institutional investors), and the kind of credit guarantee support described above (which could apply to fintech-originated loans as well as bank-originated ones).

:::takeaway
The missing middle in MSME financing is a structural feature of South Africa's financial system, not a temporary imperfection. Commercial banks rationally exclude small businesses due to high origination costs, information asymmetry, and collateral constraints. DFIs have not filled the gap at scale because they face similar risk incentives and are undercapitalised relative to the problem. Three structural interventions could transform MSME financing access: an SBA-style credit guarantee programme that changes the risk calculus for commercial bank lending, blended finance structures that combine concessional and commercial capital for MSME loan portfolios, and fintech-enabled digital lending that reduces origination costs and uses alternative data for credit assessment. None of these is a silver bullet individually. Together, they represent a financing architecture that could channel capital toward the productive enterprises that South Africa's growth strategy requires.
:::

## The Integration Logic

By now the interconnection among the three engines should be concrete rather than abstract. Consider a practical example that runs through the entire system.

A food processing entrepreneur in Limpopo identifies an opportunity to produce dried mango and mango puree for export. South Africa already grows mangoes — the Limpopo lowveld is one of the country's primary mango-producing regions. The nearby product logic from the Atlas of Economic Complexity says that moving from raw mango exports to processed mango products is a short step in capability terms. The AfCFTA framework provides preferential access to West African and East African markets where processed fruit demand is growing. And the agricultural value chain analysis from [Resource Optimisation](/articles/eg-2-1-resource-optimisation) identifies agro-processing as one of the strongest beneficiation opportunities.

The entrepreneur needs Engine One: a viable business environment. That means a functional municipality that provides water, electricity, and waste removal to the processing facility. It means a regulatory environment where registering the business, obtaining food safety certifications, and complying with export health standards is navigable without a team of consultants. It means [Municipal Councils](/articles/3-3-municipal-councils) that treat local economic development as a core function, maintaining the roads, infrastructure, and services that make business operations possible. And it means an MSME support system — SEDA, the dtic, the provincial Department of Agriculture — that can provide practical assistance with production standards, market intelligence, and buyer introductions.

The entrepreneur needs Engine Two: market access. That means trade facilitation — efficient customs processing, phytosanitary certification that is recognised by destination countries, logistics connectivity from Limpopo to the port of Durban or to overland border crossings into Mozambique, Zimbabwe, and beyond. It means trade agreements — AfCFTA preferences that make South African processed mango competitive against imports from India, the Philippines, or Thailand. And it means market intelligence — knowing which buyers in which markets want which specifications at which price points.

The entrepreneur needs Engine Three: capital. The processing facility requires equipment — drying machines, puree extraction lines, packaging machinery, cold storage. The working capital cycle requires financing — buying raw mangoes from farmers during harvest season, processing them, storing the inventory, and waiting for payment from export buyers weeks or months later. A commercial bank is unlikely to lend the R3 million required for equipment and working capital to a first-time food processor in Limpopo without substantial collateral. An SBA-style guarantee could change that calculation. A DFI co-investment, blended with a commercial loan, could provide the capital at an affordable rate. A fintech platform that can assess the entrepreneur's transaction history, verify the purchase orders from export buyers, and monitor the business in real time could originate and manage the loan at a fraction of the cost of a traditional bank.

If any one engine fails, the venture fails. The most brilliant business plan is worthless without capital. Capital is worthless without a market to sell into. Market access is worthless without a business that can produce and deliver the goods. This is the integration logic, and it is the reason that treating the three engines as separate policy silos — with MSME development in one department, trade policy in another, and development finance in a third — consistently produces disappointing results.

## Practical Framework: The Growth Engine Dashboard

You can track the performance of the three engines using publicly available data. The Growth Engine Dashboard organises the key metrics into three categories, each with specific indicators and data sources that you can access without specialised research capabilities.

**Venture Creation Rates** measure whether the economy is generating enough new businesses and whether those businesses are surviving and growing. The Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC), which maintains the register of all companies and close corporations in South Africa, publishes monthly and annual statistics on new company registrations. These numbers tell you the flow of new ventures entering the economy. But registration alone means little if businesses fail within months. The survival rate — the percentage of registered businesses that are still active one, two, and five years after registration — is the more telling indicator, although it is harder to measure precisely because CIPC deregistration data lags actual business closure. The Global Entrepreneurship Monitor South Africa report, published annually with support from the Small Enterprise Development Agency, provides the TEA rate (Total Early-Stage Entrepreneurial Activity), the established business ownership rate, and breakdowns by motivation (opportunity versus necessity) and demographics. Statistics South Africa's Quarterly Labour Force Survey includes data on self-employment and informal business activity that supplements the formal registration data.

**Export Diversification** measures whether the economy is producing a wider range of goods and services for international markets. The South African Revenue Service (SARS), through its customs and excise division, publishes detailed trade statistics including export values by product category (using the Harmonised System classification). You can track the number of product categories in which South Africa has "revealed comparative advantage" — meaning the country's share of world exports in that product exceeds its overall share of world exports — as a direct measure of diversification. The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index of export concentration, calculable from the SARS data, tells you whether the export basket is becoming more or less concentrated over time. The Atlas of Economic Complexity, maintained by Harvard's Growth Lab (atlas.cid.harvard.edu), provides pre-calculated complexity and diversification metrics for South Africa and every other country in the database. For AfCFTA-specific volumes, the African Union Commission and the AfCFTA Secretariat publish trade data on intra-African trade flows, although data quality and timeliness vary.

**MSME Financing Access** measures whether capital is flowing to small and medium businesses at a rate and on terms that support growth. The South African Reserve Bank's BA900 returns — the regulatory reports that all banks submit — include data on lending by loan size, which can serve as a proxy for MSME lending volumes. The National Credit Regulator publishes quarterly statistics on credit extension by type of credit provider and loan category. DFI disbursement data — the IDC, DBSA, SEFA, and NEF all publish annual reports with lending and investment data — tells you how much development finance is reaching MSMEs. The FinMark Trust's FinScope MSME Survey, conducted periodically, provides the most granular picture of MSME financial inclusion, covering access to banking, credit, insurance, and savings products. And the emerging fintech lending market is increasingly tracked by the Financial Sector Conduct Authority and by industry bodies, although comprehensive data remains limited.

For each category, the dashboard should track not just the current level but the trend and the gap. What is the current venture creation rate, is it improving, and how far is it from comparator country benchmarks? What is the current export diversification index, is the basket broadening or narrowing, and how does South Africa compare to Chile, Malaysia, and other peers? What is the current volume of MSME lending, is it growing as a share of total credit, and how does it compare to the estimated demand? The gap between current performance and comparator benchmarks is your measure of unrealised potential. The trend tells you whether policy is working.

You do not need to be an economist to use this dashboard. You need three numbers for each engine — current level, trend, and comparator benchmark — and the willingness to ask why the gaps exist and who is accountable for closing them. That accountability question leads directly to the institutional architecture examined in [The Execution Machine](/articles/eg-3-2-the-execution-machine).

:::framework
**The Growth Engine Dashboard**

When assessing whether South Africa's economy is building the structural foundations for broad-based growth, track these three engines using publicly available data:

**Engine 1: Are enough new businesses being created and surviving?**

Check CIPC monthly and annual statistics (cipc.co.za) for new company registrations — this tells you the flow of new ventures entering the economy. Then check the Global Entrepreneurship Monitor South Africa report (published annually with SEDA support) for the TEA rate, established business ownership rate, and the split between opportunity-driven and necessity-driven entrepreneurship. The critical question is not just how many businesses are starting, but how many are surviving past two years and whether they are opportunity-driven ventures with growth potential. Compare South Africa's TEA rate (typically 7-11%) against peers like Chile (25%) and Brazil (20%).

**Engine 2: Is the export basket diversifying?**

Check SARS customs and excise trade statistics for export values by product category. Track the number of product categories where South Africa has revealed comparative advantage and calculate the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index of export concentration to see whether the basket is broadening or narrowing over time. Use the Harvard Growth Lab's Atlas of Economic Complexity (atlas.cid.harvard.edu) for pre-calculated complexity and diversification metrics. The key question is whether South Africa is moving into nearby products — processed minerals, agricultural value-added goods, professional services — or remaining stuck in raw commodity exports.

**Engine 3: Is capital reaching small and medium businesses?**

Check the South African Reserve Bank's BA900 returns for lending by loan size as a proxy for MSME lending volumes. Check the National Credit Regulator's quarterly statistics for credit extension by provider type. Review IDC, DBSA, SEFA, and NEF annual reports for DFI disbursement to MSMEs. For the most granular picture, consult the FinMark Trust FinScope MSME Survey for data on MSME financial inclusion, including the critical "discouraged borrower" effect where viable businesses never apply because they expect to be declined.

For each engine, track three numbers: the current level, the trend (improving or declining over recent years), and the gap relative to comparator country benchmarks. If all three engines are improving and the gaps are narrowing, the growth strategy is working. If any engine is stagnant or declining, the system is incomplete — because the three engines only generate growth when they turn together.
:::
