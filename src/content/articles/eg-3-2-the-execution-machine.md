## The Strategy Is Never the Problem

South Africa does not lack economic strategies. It lacks the institutional machinery to execute them across political cycles. If ambition on paper translated into outcomes on the ground, South Africa would be one of the most prosperous countries in the world. The Growth, Employment and Redistribution strategy of 1996. The Accelerated and Shared Growth Initiative of 2006. The Industrial Policy Action Plans that ran from 2007 to 2018. The National Development Plan of 2012. The Nine-Point Plan of 2015. The Economic Reconstruction and Recovery Plan of 2020. Each was comprehensive. Each was launched with a media event, a ministerial speech, and a bound document. Each was largely unimplemented.

The pattern is so consistent that it has become a kind of dark comedy among policy researchers. A new minister arrives. A new strategy is announced. Targets are set. Progress reports are published for a year or two. Then a cabinet reshuffle occurs, a new minister arrives, and a new strategy is announced. The old strategy is never formally abandoned — it simply stops being mentioned. No one is held accountable for the gap between what was promised and what was delivered, because the institutional memory has already moved on to the next announcement.

This article argues that the core failure is structural, not political. South Africa has no central coordinating body with the authority, technical capacity, and political insulation to drive a multi-decade economic strategy. Malaysia had the Economic Planning Unit. Singapore had the Economic Development Board. South Korea had the Economic Planning Board. South Africa has a rotating cast of ministers, departments, and task teams with overlapping mandates and none with genuine execution authority. Until this structural absence is addressed, even the best-designed strategy will share the fate of its predecessors.

If you have read [The Comparator Countries](/articles/eg-1-3-the-comparator-countries), you already know that the countries which escaped the middle-income trap did not do so through a single brilliant policy idea. They did it through institutional machinery that could sustain and adapt a strategy over decades. This article examines that machinery in detail — what it looks like, why South Africa has failed to build it, and what a credible South African version might involve. The companion article, [Watching the Watchers](/articles/eg-3-3-watching-the-watchers), will then examine the monitoring and evaluation architecture needed to keep the machinery honest.

## The Strategy Graveyard

To understand the scale of the execution problem, you need to walk through the graveyard. Each headstone tells a similar story: ambitious targets, minimal accountability, quiet abandonment.

**GEAR (1996).** The Growth, Employment and Redistribution strategy was the post-apartheid government's first comprehensive macroeconomic framework. It set a target of six per cent GDP growth by the year 2000, the creation of 400,000 new jobs per year, and a reduction of the fiscal deficit to three per cent of GDP. The fiscal consolidation targets were largely achieved — Treasury delivered on the parts it controlled directly. But the growth and employment targets were missed by a wide margin. GDP growth averaged around 2.8 per cent between 1996 and 2000, and formal employment actually declined. The strategy was not formally replaced; it was simply overtaken by events and by a new minister with a new agenda.

**ASGISA (2006).** The Accelerated and Shared Growth Initiative for South Africa aimed to halve unemployment and poverty by 2014, requiring GDP growth of at least 4.5 per cent per year from 2005 and six per cent per year from 2010. It identified six "binding constraints" on growth, including the volatility of the currency, the cost and efficiency of the logistics system, the shortage of suitably skilled labour, barriers to entry for small businesses, regulatory burdens, and deficiencies in state organisation, capacity, and leadership. The diagnosis was largely sound. The six binding constraints identified in 2006 would be recognisable to any economist analysing South Africa in 2025, two decades later — which tells you everything about the implementation record. ASGISA was quietly shelved after the 2009 change in political leadership.

**The Industrial Policy Action Plans (2007-2018).** The IPAP series was more specific than GEAR or ASGISA, setting sector-level targets for manufacturing output, exports, and employment. The first IPAP in 2007 targeted the creation of one million direct manufacturing jobs over ten years. By 2017, manufacturing employment had actually declined from approximately 1.7 million to 1.5 million. The sector's share of GDP fell from approximately 19 per cent in the early 1990s to under 12 per cent. The IPAP documents themselves grew thicker each year, running to hundreds of pages of sector-by-sector analysis and target-setting, but the gap between the documents and the outcomes widened in parallel. The Department of Trade and Industry, later the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition, did not have the cross-government authority to drive the structural reforms — in energy, transport, skills, and regulation — that its own strategy identified as prerequisites.

**The National Development Plan (2012).** The NDP remains South Africa's most ambitious and comprehensive long-term strategy. It set a target of 11 million new jobs by 2030, a reduction in the unemployment rate from approximately 25 per cent to six per cent, GDP growth averaging 5.4 per cent per year, and a reduction in the Gini coefficient from 0.69 to 0.60. The plan was endorsed by cabinet, endorsed by Parliament, and described as the lodestar for all government planning. The National Planning Commission, which produced it, was a ministerial advisory body in the Presidency — it had intellectual authority but no implementation authority, no budget authority, and no ability to compel departments to align their annual performance plans with the NDP's targets. By the mid-2020s, not a single headline target was on track. The unemployment rate was higher than when the NDP was published. GDP growth had averaged below two per cent. The 11 million jobs target required annual employment growth that South Africa had never achieved in a single year, let alone sustained over a decade.

**The Nine-Point Plan (2015).** Announced by President Zuma at the 2015 State of the Nation Address, this plan identified nine priority areas including energy, agriculture, mining, manufacturing, and tourism. It was never published as a detailed implementation document with timelines, budgets, and responsible officials. It functioned more as a rhetorical device than an operational plan. Within two years, the political attention had moved elsewhere.

**The Economic Reconstruction and Recovery Plan (2020).** The ERRP was developed in response to the COVID-19 pandemic and framed as a social compact between government, business, and labour. It contained eight priority interventions including aggressive infrastructure investment, expanding energy generation capacity, employment-oriented strategic localisation, growing the tourism sector, and accelerating structural reform. The plan committed to specific structural reforms: the auctioning of broadband spectrum (subsequently achieved, belatedly), the restructuring of Eskom, the reduction of administered prices, and the reform of the visa regime. Some reforms advanced, particularly where Operation Vulindlela provided dedicated project management capacity. But the broader targets — a GDP growth trajectory of three per cent or more, the creation of 800,000 jobs through public employment programmes — remained largely aspirational.

:::expert[National Planning Commission Assessment]
The National Planning Commission's own review of NDP implementation, published a decade after the plan's adoption, found that institutional coordination failure was the single most significant barrier to implementation. The review noted that while individual departments could point to activities aligned with NDP objectives, there was no mechanism to ensure that the sequencing, resourcing, and political prioritisation across departments added up to the systemic change the plan required. The NDP diagnosed many of South Africa's structural problems correctly. What it lacked was any theory of how to make government act as a coherent whole rather than as a collection of independent departmental silos.
:::

The common thread across three decades of strategy documents is not that the strategies were poorly designed. Many were intellectually rigorous. The diagnoses were often correct. The targets, while ambitious, were not inherently unreasonable for a country with South Africa's endowments and potential. What was missing was the institutional machinery to convert strategy into sustained action.

## Why Strategies Fail — An Institutional Diagnosis

If you want to understand why South Africa produces strategies that do not get implemented, you need to look at five structural features of the state's institutional architecture. These are not personal failings of individual ministers or officials. They are design flaws in the system itself.

The first structural problem is that no single body owns the economic strategy. Responsibility for economic policy is fragmented across the National Treasury, the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition, the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation in the Presidency, the Department of Public Enterprises, the Department of Employment and Labour, and at least a dozen other national departments. Each has its own minister, its own budget, its own strategic plan, and its own performance agreements. As we explored in [The Architecture of the State](/articles/1-1-architecture-of-the-state), South Africa's three-sphere governance system creates additional complexity, because many of the implementation levers for economic strategy — land use planning, building permits, local business licensing, bulk infrastructure connections — sit at the provincial and municipal level, beyond the direct authority of national departments.

The result is that any strategy requiring coordinated action across multiple departments (which is to say, any serious economic strategy) immediately confronts a coordination problem. Who convenes the relevant parties? Who resolves disputes between departments with competing priorities? Who ensures that the Department of Home Affairs processes critical skills visas at the speed the economic strategy requires, or that the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy issues mining rights on a timeline consistent with the investment targets? The answer, in practice, is nobody. Cabinet is supposed to play this role, but cabinet is a political body that meets for a few hours each week and deals with hundreds of competing agenda items. It is not an implementation coordination mechanism.

The second structural problem is that ministerial reshuffles reset institutional memory. South Africa reshuffles its cabinet with remarkable frequency. Between 1994 and 2024, the country had ten different ministers responsible for trade and industry (or its successor department), eight different ministers of finance, and multiple reorganisations of the departments responsible for economic planning. Each reshuffle brings new priorities, new advisors, and frequently a new strategic framework. Senior officials in the department learn to wait out each minister's agenda, knowing that the next reshuffle will bring a different set of priorities. The institutional incentive is not to implement the current strategy aggressively but to avoid making commitments that the next minister might not support.

This problem is compounded by the practice of deploying ministers without relevant expertise. A minister of trade and industry may have no background in economics, industrial policy, or trade negotiation. They arrive in office needing months to understand the policy landscape, the institutional constraints, and the stakeholder dynamics. By the time they are up to speed, the next reshuffle may already be approaching. Compare this to Singapore, where the political leadership of key economic agencies has been remarkably stable, with ministers and permanent secretaries serving for extended periods and building deep domain expertise.

The third structural problem is hollow implementation capacity. South Africa's national departments are often understaffed in critical technical areas. The state employs a large number of people — the public service payroll is the single largest item of government expenditure — but the distribution of skills does not match the requirements of economic strategy execution. There are insufficient numbers of project managers capable of overseeing complex infrastructure investments, trade negotiators with deep sector expertise, industrial economists who can evaluate the commercial viability of sector strategies, and procurement specialists who can manage large-scale contracting without legal challenge. The Government Technical Advisory Centre, known as GTAC, within National Treasury was created partly to address this gap, but it remains a relatively small unit given the scale of the challenge.

:::expert[Public Affairs Research Institute (PARI) Analysis]
Research by the Public Affairs Research Institute has documented what it terms the "institutional hollowing" of the South African state — a process whereby formal organisational structures remain in place but the technical capacity, institutional knowledge, and operational systems needed to deliver on their mandates have eroded. PARI's work shows that this hollowing is not uniform: some state institutions (the South African Revenue Service, the Reserve Bank, parts of National Treasury) have maintained high capability, while others have experienced severe degradation. The implication for economic strategy execution is that the state's implementation capacity varies enormously across the departments and agencies that any comprehensive strategy relies on.
:::

The fourth structural problem is the absence of accountability mechanisms connecting strategy targets to budgets and performance agreements. When the NDP set a target of 11 million new jobs by 2030, there was no mechanism to translate that target into binding obligations on specific departments, with budgets allocated accordingly and consequences for non-delivery. The annual budget process, managed by Treasury, is primarily a fiscal management exercise — it ensures that government spending stays within sustainable limits, but it does not systematically align budget allocations with the targets of the prevailing economic strategy. Similarly, the performance agreements of directors-general and senior officials are too vague to serve as genuine accountability instruments for strategy execution. An official can meet all the terms of their performance agreement without making any measurable contribution to the economic strategy's targets.

The fifth structural problem is that political incentives favour announcement over implementation. Announcing a new strategy generates positive media coverage, stakeholder goodwill, and a sense of momentum. Implementing a strategy requires confronting difficult trade-offs, overcoming bureaucratic resistance, managing stakeholder conflicts, and sustaining effort over years without visible results. The political reward for the announcement vastly exceeds the political reward for the unglamorous work of implementation. This is not unique to South Africa — it is a feature of democratic politics everywhere — but it is particularly damaging in a context where the institutional constraints on executive discretion are weak and where a new minister can always claim that the problem was the previous strategy rather than the failure to implement it.

Taken together, these five structural features explain why South Africa produces strategy documents of a quality that would be creditable in any developed country, yet fails to implement them. The problem is not the quality of the thinking. It is the absence of institutional machinery designed specifically for execution.

## Malaysia's Economic Planning Unit — The Gold Standard

If you want to see what an execution machine looks like in practice, Malaysia's Economic Planning Unit offers the clearest example. The EPU was established in 1961, shortly after Malaysian independence, and for more than five decades it served as the central coordinating body for Malaysia's economic development strategy. Understanding how it worked — and why it worked — is essential for thinking about what South Africa needs.

The EPU sat within the Prime Minister's Department, giving it direct access to the highest level of political authority. This positioning was not merely symbolic. It meant that the EPU's head reported directly to the prime minister, not to a line minister who might be reshuffled. It meant that the EPU could convene any ministry, any agency, and any state-owned enterprise to a coordination meeting with the authority of the PM's office behind it. And it meant that when the EPU identified a blockage — a ministry dragging its feet on a reform, a state-level government failing to release land for an industrial park — it could escalate the issue directly to the political leadership with the speed and authority that resolution required.

The EPU's core function was to prepare and coordinate the implementation of Malaysia's five-year development plans, known as the Malaysia Plans. Between 1966 and 2020, Malaysia published twelve of these plans, each running to hundreds of pages of sector-by-sector targets, investment allocations, and institutional reform commitments. The critical feature was not the plans themselves — many countries produce development plans — but the fact that the EPU had real authority over the development budget. The EPU controlled the allocation of the development expenditure budget, meaning that it could direct capital spending toward the priorities identified in the Malaysia Plan and away from projects that did not align with the strategy. This budget authority gave the EPU genuine leverage over line ministries. A ministry that refused to cooperate with the EPU's coordination requirements risked losing its development budget allocation.

The EPU maintained a permanent technical staff of several hundred professionals, including economists, engineers, statisticians, and policy analysts. These were not political appointees who rotated with each change of government. They were career civil servants who built deep expertise over decades. A senior EPU official might have worked on industrial policy through three or four different Malaysia Plans, accumulating institutional knowledge that no incoming minister could match. This technical depth allowed the EPU to engage with line ministries as an informed counterpart — it could evaluate the quality of a ministry's implementation plans, identify unrealistic assumptions, and propose alternatives based on decades of accumulated experience.

The EPU's mandate explicitly outlasted individual prime ministers. Malaysia had six prime ministers between 1961 and 2020, spanning different political factions and, eventually, different governing coalitions. The EPU continued to function through each transition, adapting the strategy to new political priorities while maintaining the institutional continuity of the planning and coordination process. When Mahathir Mohamad launched the Look East Policy in the 1980s, pivoting Malaysia's industrial strategy toward Japanese and Korean investment models, the EPU was the institution that translated that political directive into operational plans, budget allocations, and coordination mechanisms. When Abdullah Badawi shifted emphasis toward human capital development and innovation in the 2000s, the EPU adapted again. The strategy evolved, but the institutional machinery for executing it remained constant.

What made the EPU effective can be distilled to four elements. First, technical capability: the EPU employed genuinely skilled professionals who understood economics, project management, and the operational realities of the Malaysian state. Second, political protection: its location in the PM's Department gave it authority that no line ministry could override, while its professional staff culture insulated day-to-day operations from partisan interference. Third, budget authority: control over development expenditure allocation gave the EPU the single most powerful lever in government — the ability to direct money toward priorities and away from non-priorities. Fourth, long time horizons: the five-year planning cycle, combined with a permanent professional staff, allowed the EPU to think and act on timescales that transcended electoral cycles.

It is important to be honest about the EPU's limitations. Malaysia is not a governance paradise. The EPU operated within a political system that was, for most of its history, semi-authoritarian — the same coalition governed Malaysia from independence until 2018. Corruption was a serious problem, as the 1MDB scandal demonstrated spectacularly. The EPU's effectiveness was partly a function of political stability that South Africa cannot and should not try to replicate through authoritarian means. And Malaysia's development outcomes, while impressive in aggregate, were accompanied by persistent ethnic inequalities and regional disparities that the EPU was unable to fully resolve.

The lesson for South Africa is therefore not that you should copy the EPU wholesale. It is that the EPU demonstrates what becomes possible when a state creates a dedicated institution with the right combination of technical capacity, political authority, budget leverage, and institutional continuity. The specific design of a South African equivalent would need to reflect South African constitutional requirements, democratic norms, and the three-sphere governance framework. But the functional requirements — coordination authority, budget influence, technical depth, and insulation from short-term political cycles — are transferable.

## Singapore's Economic Development Board — Execution at Speed

If the EPU illustrates the value of a central coordinating institution, Singapore's Economic Development Board illustrates something complementary: what happens when you give a specialised agency a narrow mandate, staff it with exceptional talent, and let it move at the speed of business rather than the speed of bureaucracy.

The EDB was established in 1961 with a focused mission: attract foreign direct investment to Singapore and develop specific industrial sectors. It was constituted as a statutory board — a legal form in Singaporean governance that gives an institution operational independence while keeping it accountable to a parent ministry. The EDB's parent ministry was the Ministry of Trade and Industry, but the statutory board structure gave it freedom to set its own organisational culture, compensation structures, and decision-making processes. This is a crucial detail. The EDB could hire the best graduates from Singapore's universities and offer them salaries competitive with the private sector. It could make investment promotion decisions in days rather than months. It could negotiate incentive packages with multinational corporations without going through the full bureaucratic procurement process that a regular government department would face.

The EDB's approach to foreign investment attraction was remarkably proactive. Rather than waiting for investors to express interest, the EDB identified specific companies in specific sectors that it wanted to attract, researched their expansion plans, and approached them with tailored proposals. When Texas Instruments was considering locations for a semiconductor assembly plant in the 1960s, the EDB did not simply advertise Singapore's advantages in a trade magazine. It sent a team to Texas Instruments' headquarters in Dallas, presented a detailed analysis of why Singapore was the optimal location, offered a customised package of incentives including tax holidays, subsidised factory space, and workforce training programmes, and committed to having the factory site ready within a specific timeframe. This was not passive investment promotion. It was active, targeted, and executed with a speed and professionalism that most government agencies simply cannot match.

The EDB also played a central role in sector development strategy. As Singapore's economy evolved from labour-intensive manufacturing in the 1960s through capital-intensive manufacturing in the 1970s and 1980s to knowledge-intensive services from the 1990s onward, the EDB was the institution that planned and coordinated each transition. When Singapore decided to develop as a petrochemicals hub, the EDB designed the Jurong Island petrochemical complex, coordinated the land reclamation, negotiated with the major oil companies, and managed the phased development over two decades. When Singapore pivoted toward biomedical sciences in the 2000s, the EDB was again the lead agency, attracting pharmaceutical companies, negotiating research partnerships, and developing the Biopolis research campus.

What made the EDB effective was the combination of a narrow mandate, exceptional human capital, operational speed, and institutional learning. The EDB knew exactly what it was supposed to do — attract investment and develop sectors — and it was not distracted by the hundred other responsibilities that a typical government department carries. Its staff were among the best-educated and highest-paid public servants in Singapore, creating an institutional culture of competence and ambition. Its statutory board structure allowed it to make decisions quickly, which matters enormously in investment promotion where the competition is other countries that can also offer tax holidays and cheap land but cannot match Singapore's execution speed. And the EDB accumulated institutional knowledge over decades, learning from each investment promotion success and failure and refining its approach continuously.

:::takeaway
The core lesson from both the EPU and the EDB is not about the specific policies they implemented. It is about institutional design. Both institutions were purpose-built for execution. Both were staffed with skilled professionals. Both had clear mandates that were not diluted by unrelated responsibilities. Both were positioned to operate at timescales and speeds that conventional government departments cannot match. And both were insulated — though not isolated — from short-term political pressures. South Africa has nothing comparable in its current institutional architecture for economic development.
:::

The comparison between the EDB and South Africa's various investment promotion and industrial development institutions is sobering. South Africa has the Department of Trade, Industry and Competition, which combines trade policy, industrial policy, competition policy, and investment promotion in a single sprawling department. It has InvestSA, a unit within the DTIC responsible for investment facilitation, which lacks the EDB's operational independence, compensation flexibility, or decision-making speed. It has the Industrial Development Corporation, a development finance institution that provides funding but does not coordinate sector strategy. It has numerous sector-specific agencies and councils, each with its own governance structure, funding model, and reporting line. The overall picture is one of institutional fragmentation — many organisations touching pieces of the economic development mandate, but none with the authority, capability, or mandate to drive a coherent strategy.

## South Korea's Economic Planning Board — Strategy With Teeth

South Korea's experience adds a further dimension. The Economic Planning Board, established in 1961, combined the functions of budget allocation, economic planning, and foreign investment management in a single institution. The head of the EPB held the rank of Deputy Prime Minister, giving the institution cabinet-level political authority. This combination of planning authority and budget authority in the same institution was the key to the EPB's effectiveness — it could ensure that the resources followed the strategy, rather than having the planning function in one institution and the budget function in another, with inevitable disconnections between the two.

The EPB coordinated South Korea's five-year economic development plans through the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s, overseeing the country's transformation from one of the poorest nations in Asia to an industrial powerhouse. The EPB's authority was formidable. It could direct credit allocation through the state-controlled banking system, channel subsidised loans to firms in priority sectors, and impose performance requirements on companies receiving state support. If a firm received subsidised credit to develop export capacity in a targeted sector and failed to meet its export targets, the subsidy would be withdrawn and redirected to a competitor. This created a system of disciplined support — the state helped firms, but it expected results.

South Korea eventually dissolved the EPB in 1994, merging it into the Ministry of Finance and Economy. By that point, the Korean economy had reached a level of development and complexity where the centralised coordination model was less necessary. The market economy had matured, the private sector was globally competitive, and the developmental state's role needed to evolve. But the EPB had served its purpose during the critical decades of catch-up growth — precisely the phase that South Africa is currently navigating.

The Korean model is more authoritarian than either the Malaysian or Singaporean versions, and it is important to be clear-eyed about this. The EPB operated under military-backed governments for much of its existence. The government-business relationships it managed were not always free of corruption. And the heavy industrial strategy it coordinated led to the dominance of the chaebol — the large conglomerate groups like Samsung, Hyundai, and LG — which created their own problems of market concentration and political capture. South Africa cannot and should not replicate the authoritarian elements of the Korean model. But the functional lesson remains: when a state creates an institution that combines planning authority, budget authority, and political backing in service of a long-term strategy, the results can be transformative.

## What This Looks Like for South Africa

What would a South African execution machine look like? Not a copy of the EPU or the EDB — South Africa's constitutional framework, democratic requirements, and governance challenges demand a distinct design. But an institution that performs the same core functions, adapted to South African conditions.

The first design requirement is a legislated mandate that outlasts individual ministers. The institution should be established by an Act of Parliament, not by a ministerial directive or a presidential proclamation. This matters because an institution created by legislation cannot be abolished, reorganised, or defunded by a single minister or a single cabinet reshuffle. Changing its mandate requires a parliamentary process with public hearings, committee deliberation, and a recorded vote. This is the most basic form of institutional insulation — making the institution's existence and core mandate a matter of law rather than a matter of political convenience.

The second design requirement is authority over economic development budget allocation. This is the most contentious element, because it means taking some budget authority away from line ministers and concentrating it in a coordinating body. The logic is straightforward: if the coordinating body cannot influence how money is spent, it has no leverage. It becomes another advisory body producing reports that departments are free to ignore. The EPU model, where the coordinating body controls the development expenditure budget, is the most direct version. A softer version would give the coordinating body formal sign-off authority over the economic development components of departmental budgets, without full allocation control. Either way, the institution needs budget leverage, or it will be toothless.

The third design requirement is genuine technical capacity. The institution needs to employ economists, sector specialists, project managers, trade analysts, and data scientists at a level of competence that allows it to engage credibly with line departments, the private sector, and international institutions. This almost certainly means departing from the standard public service salary scales, which are not competitive for the kind of talent required. The statutory board model used in Singapore — where the institution is publicly owned but operationally independent, with the ability to set its own compensation structures — offers a possible template. South Africa already has precedent for this: the South African Reserve Bank employs staff at market-competitive rates precisely because monetary policy requires exceptional technical skill.

The fourth design requirement is publicly reported and independently audited key performance indicators. Every serious economic strategy sets targets. The difference between a strategy that drives action and a strategy that gathers dust is whether those targets are tracked, reported, and audited by an independent body. The Auditor-General of South Africa already audits the financial performance of government departments and entities. An analogous function is needed for the performance indicators of the economic strategy — not just whether the money was spent in accordance with procurement rules, but whether the spending achieved the outcomes the strategy committed to. This performance audit function should be public, with results reported to Parliament and made available to citizens.

The fifth design requirement is a parliamentary accountability mechanism. The institution's leadership should be required to appear before a dedicated parliamentary committee at regular intervals — quarterly, not annually — to account for progress against strategy targets. The committee should have the power to summon officials, request documents, and commission independent assessments. This is the democratic counterweight to the institution's concentrated authority. The more power you give an execution body, the more robust the accountability mechanisms need to be.

:::takeaway
The design principles for a South African execution machine are not exotic or untested. Legislated mandates, budget authority, technical capacity, public KPIs, and parliamentary accountability — each of these elements exists somewhere in the South African state already. The Reserve Bank has a legislated mandate and operational independence. National Treasury has budget authority. GTAC has specialist technical capacity. The Auditor-General provides independent auditing. What is missing is an institution that combines all five elements in service of economic strategy execution.
:::

The institutional form matters less than the functional design. It could be a new statutory body, a reconstituted and empowered version of the National Planning Commission, or a legislated coordinating unit in the Presidency. The label is less important than whether it has the five attributes described above. Without them, any new institution will simply become another node in the existing network of advisory bodies, producing excellent analysis that nobody is obliged to act on.

## The Democratic Objection

The proposal to concentrate economic strategy authority in a technocratic body raises a legitimate democratic concern. In a constitutional democracy, economic policy is supposed to be decided by elected representatives accountable to voters. Should unelected technocrats have authority over budget allocation and strategy execution?

The concern is valid, but the framing is misleading. The proposal is not to remove economic policy from democratic control. It is to create an institution that executes the democratically determined strategy more effectively. The strategy itself — the priorities, the targets, the trade-offs — would still be determined through the democratic process. Cabinet would still set the direction. Parliament would still approve the budget. Voters would still hold the governing party accountable for economic outcomes. What changes is that between elections, there would be a professional institution ensuring that the strategy is actually implemented, rather than leaving implementation to the ad hoc coordination of dozens of departments with competing priorities and no shared accountability.

Consider the analogy with monetary policy. South Africa, like most democracies, has chosen to delegate the operational management of monetary policy to an independent central bank. The Reserve Bank sets interest rates based on its professional assessment of inflation dynamics, without seeking cabinet approval for each rate decision. This delegation is not anti-democratic — it is democratic in the deepest sense, because it reflects a considered judgement by elected representatives that monetary policy outcomes are better when operational decisions are insulated from short-term political pressures. The same logic applies to economic strategy execution. Democratic legitimacy requires that citizens choose the direction. It does not require that every operational decision in pursuit of that direction be made by politicians.

The safeguards are critical. First, the institution's mandate should be set by Parliament through legislation, and any change to the mandate should require a parliamentary process. Second, the Auditor-General should audit both the institution's financial management and its performance against strategy targets. Third, the Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation should conduct independent evaluations of the institution's effectiveness. Fourth, the institution's leadership should appear before a parliamentary committee at regular intervals, and the committee should have genuine oversight powers. Fifth, all strategy targets, progress reports, and evaluation findings should be publicly available, enabling civil society, the media, and researchers to hold the institution accountable.

:::expert[Government Technical Advisory Centre (GTAC) Research]
GTAC's research on institutional models for economic development coordination, drawing on comparative experience from East and Southeast Asia, has identified a consistent finding: the effectiveness of coordinating institutions depends less on their formal powers and more on the quality of their relationships with political leaders, line departments, and the private sector. An institution with extensive formal authority but poor relationships will be circumvented. An institution with modest formal authority but strong relationships can achieve significant coordination outcomes. The implication is that institutional design is necessary but not sufficient — the calibre of the institution's leadership and staff, and the political commitment to using the institution as a genuine coordinating mechanism, are equally important.
:::

The democratic objection is not wrong, but it is incomplete. The current system — where economic strategy is nominally under full democratic control but where no institution has the authority or capability to implement it — does not serve democratic accountability either. When a governing party promises 11 million jobs and delivers a fraction of that number, the democratic deficit is not caused by too much technocratic authority. It is caused by too little institutional capacity. Voters cannot hold a government accountable for outcomes if the government itself cannot translate its policies into outcomes. A well-designed execution institution, with proper democratic safeguards, would strengthen rather than weaken democratic accountability by making it possible for the government to deliver on its commitments and for voters to evaluate whether it has done so.

## Operation Vulindlela — What Is Possible and What Is Missing

Operation Vulindlela offers the most relevant recent precedent for what a South African execution machine might look like, and its experience reveals both the potential and the limitations of the current approach.

Launched in October 2020 as a joint initiative of the Presidency and National Treasury, Operation Vulindlela was established to accelerate the implementation of structural reforms identified in the ERRP. Its mandate covered a specific set of priority reforms: the restructuring of Eskom and the electricity market, the auctioning of broadband spectrum, the reform of water use licensing, the modernisation of the visa regime, the improvement of freight rail access, and the streamlining of municipal licensing for businesses. The unit was staffed with a small team of skilled professionals — many drawn from the private sector and from GTAC — who combined technical expertise with project management capability.

Operation Vulindlela demonstrated that a small, focused unit with political backing and technical skill can achieve results that the normal machinery of government cannot. The auctioning of broadband spectrum, which had been delayed for over a decade through a combination of bureaucratic inertia, legal challenges, and departmental turf battles, was completed within two years of Operation Vulindlela's involvement. The Electricity Regulation Amendment Act, enabling a more competitive electricity market, was advanced through the legislative process with sustained coordination support. The National Water Resource Strategy was updated after years of stagnation. In each case, the pattern was similar: Operation Vulindlela did not replace the responsible department. It provided the coordination support, political escalation capability, and project management discipline that the department lacked.

However, Operation Vulindlela also illustrates the limitations of a model that depends on political will rather than institutional design. The unit was established by presidential directive, not by legislation. Its continued existence depends on the continued commitment of the president and the minister of finance. A new president or a new minister could dissolve it overnight. Its mandate is limited to a specific list of structural reforms rather than encompassing the full scope of economic strategy execution. Its team is small relative to the scale of the challenge. And its effectiveness depends heavily on the personal relationships between its leadership and the political principals in the Presidency and Treasury — a dependency that makes the model fragile and difficult to replicate.

The lesson of Operation Vulindlela is not that South Africa needs more ad hoc units. It is that the principles which make Operation Vulindlela effective — political backing, technical capability, focused mandate, coordination authority, and a culture of delivery — need to be institutionalised in a permanent, legislated body that does not depend on the commitment of any individual president or minister. Operation Vulindlela is proof of concept. The next step is to move from proof of concept to permanent institution.

:::takeaway
Operation Vulindlela proves that the South African state can execute when it creates the right institutional conditions. The question is whether the country will learn the lesson and build a permanent execution institution, or whether it will continue to rely on ad hoc interventions that rise and fall with individual political leaders. The pattern of the strategy graveyard suggests that without institutional reform, the ad hoc approach will not survive the next political transition.
:::

## The Execution Quality Audit — A Practical Framework

The analysis above gives you a framework for evaluating any economic strategy announcement. When a government minister announces a new economic strategy, a new sector plan, or a new set of economic targets, you can assess its likelihood of implementation by asking five questions. If the answers to most of these questions are unfavourable, the strategy is likely to join its predecessors in the graveyard, regardless of how impressive the document looks.

**Question one: Is the strategy backed by a legislated mandate or a ministerial directive?** A legislated mandate means the strategy has been debated in Parliament, encoded in law, and given a degree of permanence that survives cabinet reshuffles. A ministerial directive can be withdrawn by the same minister or their successor without any formal process. Strategies backed by legislation have a significantly higher probability of sustained implementation, because changing or abandoning them requires a visible, accountable political act. If the strategy exists only as a government gazette notice, a cabinet decision, or a ministerial speech, its institutional foundations are weak.

**Question two: Is there a dedicated agency responsible for execution, or does the strategy rely on a department with a hundred other priorities?** A dedicated agency with a clear mandate, adequate staffing, and organisational focus on the strategy in question is far more likely to drive implementation than a line department for which the strategy is one of dozens of competing priorities. When a strategy relies on the "coordination" of multiple departments without a lead agency that has authority and accountability, implementation depends on voluntary cooperation between bureaucratic actors with different incentives. This rarely succeeds.

**Question three: Are the key performance indicators publicly reported and independently verified?** Targets that are tracked internally but not publicly reported create no external accountability pressure. Targets that are publicly reported but not independently verified create incentives for creative accounting. The gold standard is a set of clear, measurable KPIs that are published at regular intervals and audited by an independent body such as the Auditor-General or an equivalent institution. If you cannot find the strategy's KPIs in a public document, or if the KPIs are vague qualitative statements rather than specific measurable outcomes, the accountability framework is inadequate.

**Question four: Is the budget ring-fenced or subject to annual reallocation?** Economic strategies require sustained investment over multiple years. If the funding for a strategy is subject to annual reallocation through the normal budget process — meaning that Treasury can redirect the money to other priorities in any given year — the strategy's resourcing is inherently uncertain. Ring-fenced budgets, multi-year appropriations, or dedicated funding mechanisms (such as levies or earmarked revenues) provide greater funding certainty. This does not mean the budget should be immune from adjustment — circumstances change, and flexibility is important — but there should be a presumption of continuity rather than a presumption of annual renegotiation.

**Question five: What happens when the minister changes?** This is the most revealing question. If the strategy depends on a specific minister's personal commitment, relationships, or political authority, it is vulnerable to the next reshuffle. If the strategy is embedded in institutional structures — a legislated mandate, a permanent agency, a published set of KPIs, a ring-fenced budget — it can survive a ministerial change. Ask yourself: if the current minister were replaced tomorrow, would the strategy continue on its current trajectory? If the honest answer is no, the strategy is built on sand.

Apply this framework to any of the strategies in the graveyard section above, and the results are consistent. GEAR had no dedicated agency and no legislated mandate. ASGISA had no independent KPI verification and no institutional continuity mechanism. The NDP had no budget authority and no dedicated implementation agency. The ERRP relies on Operation Vulindlela, which has no legislated mandate and depends on the commitment of individual political leaders. In each case, the Execution Quality Audit would have predicted the implementation gap.

This is the framework you can carry forward into [From Strategy to Reality](/articles/eg-5-2-from-strategy-to-reality), where we examine what a properly sequenced implementation plan looks like in practice — and why the sequencing of reforms matters as much as their content.
