## The Largest Budget Item, the Weakest Results

Basic education touches more South Africans more directly than any other government function. Approximately 13 million learners attend roughly 23,000 public ordinary schools across nine provinces. The Department of Basic Education's budget — combining national spending and provincial allocations — exceeds R300 billion annually, making education the largest single expenditure item in the consolidated government budget. Section 29 of the Constitution guarantees the right to basic education — a right that the Constitutional Court has held is immediately realisable, not subject to progressive realisation. And yet, despite this constitutional priority, this massive budget, and the existential importance of education to South Africa's future, the system produces results that are — by any international benchmark — deeply inadequate.

## The Education Value Chain

In the 2023 National Senior Certificate examinations, the national pass rate was 82.9 per cent. That figure sounds respectable until you examine what it conceals. **The pass threshold for a subject is 30 per cent** — a level that would constitute failure in most education systems globally. **Only 37.5 per cent of candidates achieved a bachelor's pass** — the minimum requirement for university entrance. The Progress in International Reading Literacy Study (PIRLS) has consistently shown that *roughly 81 per cent of South African Grade 4 learners cannot read for meaning*. The Trends in International Mathematics and Science Study (TIMSS) ranks South African learners near the bottom of participating countries. These outcomes are not the result of insufficient money. **They are the result of a broken delivery chain.**

To understand why education fails, you need to trace the value chain from national policy to the classroom — every institution, every handoff, every point where the chain can break. What you will find is not a single chain but a web of overlapping mandates, divided authorities, and fragmented responsibilities that no institutional designer would create from scratch.

Start at the national level. The Department of Basic Education sets the curriculum (the Curriculum and Assessment Policy Statements — CAPS — which prescribe what is taught in every grade, in every subject, in every school). DBE also determines the norms and standards for school funding through the National Norms and Standards for School Funding, which classify schools into wealth quintiles and prescribe per-learner funding allocations. DBE sets the assessment framework, administers the National Senior Certificate examination, and publishes matric results. Through the Employment of Educators Act, DBE determines the conditions of employment for all public school teachers — a critical piece of authority that sits at national level even though the employers are provincial departments.

Now add the salary bargaining layer. The Education Labour Relations Council is a national bargaining council where teacher unions and the government negotiate salary scales, working conditions, leave provisions, and disciplinary procedures. Decisions taken at the ELRC bind all nine provinces. A provincial MEC for Education cannot offer higher salaries to attract teachers to rural schools, cannot alter the leave conditions that contribute to teacher absenteeism, and cannot restructure disciplinary processes that make it notoriously difficult to remove poorly performing teachers. The ELRC is not under DBE's control either — it is a statutory bargaining council with its own governance structure.

The South African Council for Educators handles teacher registration and professional ethics nationally. A teacher must be SACE-registered to teach in a public school. SACE investigates complaints of misconduct and can strike a teacher from the register. But SACE processes are slow, its capacity is limited, and its sanctions have historically been too weak to function as an effective professional accountability mechanism.

Move to the provincial level. Each of the nine provincial departments of education is the actual employer of teachers and the manager of schools within its province. The provincial department allocates teachers to schools through a post provisioning model (which follows national norms but allows some provincial discretion), appoints principals and deputy principals, manages district offices, and is responsible for school infrastructure. The provincial MEC for Education is politically accountable for the province's educational outcomes — the MEC signs a performance agreement with the Premier and faces public scrutiny when matric results are released.

Within each province, education is delivered through a district structure. South Africa has 75 education districts, each headed by a district director. Districts are the critical link between provincial policy and school-level implementation. District offices house circuit managers (each responsible for a cluster of schools) and subject advisors (specialists who support teachers in specific subjects). The quality of district support — the frequency and quality of school visits, the availability of subject-specialist guidance, the responsiveness to school-level problems — varies enormously across and within provinces.

At the school level, the School Governing Body provides a layer of democratic governance. SGBs, established under the South African Schools Act, include parents, teachers, non-teaching staff, and (in secondary schools) learners. SGBs set school policy on matters like the language of instruction, codes of conduct, and school fees. Section 21 schools receive their funding directly and manage their own procurement of learning materials and maintenance. Non-Section 21 schools have their procurement managed by the provincial department — an arrangement that often results in delayed delivery of textbooks and supplies.

Finally, add the infrastructure chain. School buildings are either constructed through the Education Infrastructure Grant — a conditional grant from national government to provinces, worth approximately R13.7 billion in 2024/25 — or through provincial budgets. In some provinces, the provincial department of public works manages construction. In others, the education department manages it directly. The infrastructure grant comes with national conditions on how it must be spent, requires project registration, and demands compliance with procurement regulations that can add months to project timelines.

Count the entities: DBE, nine provincial education departments, 75 district offices, ELRC, SACE, provincial departments of public works (in some provinces), National Treasury (which controls the conditional grant framework), and approximately 23,000 School Governing Bodies. The value chain has at least four institutional layers, crosses two spheres of government, and involves multiple handoff points where decisions made by one entity constrain the options available to another.

:::takeaway
The education value chain involves at least seven distinct institutional actors across two spheres of government. No single entity owns the chain from curriculum design to learning outcomes. This fragmentation is not accidental — it is the predictable consequence of listing education as a concurrent function in Schedule 4 of the Constitution without designing binding coordination mechanisms to make the concurrency work.
:::

## RAARICLE™ Diagnosis

### Responsibility — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

Who does the work of delivering basic education? The honest answer is: it depends which part of "the work" you mean. If you mean setting what is taught, DBE does the work. If you mean employing the people who teach it, the provincial department does the work — within a framework set by DBE and bargaining conditions set by the ELRC. If you mean building the schools, it depends on whether the project is funded by a conditional grant (national conditions) or provincial budget, and whether the province uses its works department or its education department for construction. If you mean supporting teachers to teach better, the district office does the work — if it has subject advisors. If you mean governing the school, the SGB does the work.

The responsibility is fragmented across at minimum five distinct institutional layers. No single entity can say "I own the delivery of basic education from policy to learning outcome." Each entity owns a piece. When the overall outcome is poor — when learners cannot read, when matric results decline, when school infrastructure is crumbling — each entity can point to its piece and say "I did my part." DBE published the curriculum. The province employed the teachers. The district sent the subject advisor. The SGB adopted the policy. And yet the child cannot read.

This earns a severity rating of 2 — the element exists (there are entities doing work) but the fragmentation means that end-to-end responsibility is functionally absent. The gap between "entities doing pieces of work" and "one entity owning the outcome" is the gap where educational failure lives.

### Accountability — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

The provincial MEC for Education is the most visible accountability point. The MEC signs a performance agreement, faces annual matric results scrutiny, and answers to the provincial legislature's education committee. But as established above, the MEC does not control the critical inputs — teacher employment conditions, salary bargaining, curriculum, infrastructure grant conditions. The MEC is therefore accountable for an outcome that depends on decisions made by national entities over which the MEC has no authority. This misalignment is a structural feature of concurrent functions as defined in Schedule 4 of the Constitution, not a failure of any particular MEC or provincial government.

At the school level, the principal is accountable to the district director, who is accountable to the provincial head of department, who is accountable to the MEC. But the principal's authority is limited. A principal cannot hire or fire a teacher — appointments go through the provincial department, often with SGB participation. A principal cannot adjust the curriculum. A principal cannot increase the school's funding allocation. A principal cannot decide to offer higher pay to attract a scarce-skills mathematics teacher.

The Auditor-General's education audits focus on financial management rather than educational outcomes. A provincial education department can receive a clean audit — meaning its finances are in order — while the learners in its schools cannot read. Financial accountability and educational accountability are measured through entirely different systems with no binding link between them. You can be financially compliant and educationally catastrophic at the same time.

:::expert
The Department of Planning, Monitoring and Evaluation's assessments of provincial education performance have noted a persistent disconnect between process compliance and outcome achievement. Provincial departments that submit reports on time, follow procurement rules, and meet administrative deadlines may nonetheless preside over declining educational outcomes — because the performance framework measures administrative compliance more rigorously than it measures whether children are actually learning. The DPME has recommended that education accountability frameworks shift from input and process measures toward outcome-based performance indicators, but implementation has been slow because outcome-based accountability would require giving the accountable entities the authority to control the inputs that determine outcomes.
:::

### Authority — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

The 30-day authority test reveals the depth of the problem. Take a concrete scenario: a provincial MEC identifies that matric results in a particular district have declined for three consecutive years because of a shortage of qualified mathematics and science teachers. Can the MEC fix this within 30 days?

The MEC cannot offer higher salaries to attract qualified teachers — salary scales are determined nationally through the ELRC. The MEC cannot recruit teachers outside of the post provisioning model — teacher allocation follows national norms. The MEC cannot change the incentive structures for rural or difficult schools — those structures are determined by national policy. The MEC cannot procure additional textbooks or learning materials outside the provincial procurement process — which typically takes months. The MEC cannot restructure the district office to improve support — organisational design changes require consultation processes that extend over months or years. The MEC cannot dismiss a district director who has failed to support schools effectively — the disciplinary process, governed by the Public Service Act and collective agreements, is lengthy and procedurally complex.

What can the MEC do within 30 days? The MEC can issue a directive. The MEC can convene a meeting. The MEC can announce a plan. **But the MEC cannot execute a material operational change within 30 days using only provincial authority.** This is the *authority deficit* that the RAARICLE™ framework identifies as the most critical structural flaw in South African governance — and in education, it is severe.

### Resources — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

Education absorbs the largest share of the provincial equitable share — approximately 40 per cent of total provincial spending in most provinces goes to education. The equitable share itself is unconditional, but the dominance of personnel costs (teacher salaries consume approximately 78-82 per cent of provincial education budgets) means that provincial discretion over how to spend education funding is limited. After salaries, what remains for non-personnel expenditure — textbooks, maintenance, learner transport, nutrition — is modest.

The Education Infrastructure Grant provides ring-fenced funding for school construction and rehabilitation. In the 2024/25 financial year, the grant was approximately R13.7 billion. But the grant comes with conditions: projects must be registered, procurement must follow prescribed processes, and spending must be reported against agreed milestones. Several provinces have persistently underspent their infrastructure grants — not because they do not need schools, but because they lack the project management capacity to plan, procure, and manage construction projects at the required pace.

The National Norms and Standards for School Funding prescribe per-learner funding allocations that vary by school quintile, with the poorest schools (Quintile 1) receiving the highest per-learner allocation. In 2024, the Quintile 1 allocation was approximately R1,536 per learner — a figure that school principals and SGBs consistently describe as insufficient for basic operational needs. But the norms are set nationally. Provinces cannot increase the per-learner allocation without funding it from other budget lines.

The resources element earns a 3 because funding exists — education is not unfunded. But the combination of salary dominance (leaving little for non-personnel spending), conditional grant complexity (limiting provincial flexibility), and nationally set funding norms (constraining school-level discretion) creates significant friction between the money available and the ability to deploy it effectively.

### Information — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

South Africa's education data infrastructure is more developed than in many other government functions. The South African School Administration and Management System (SA-SAMS) is deployed across most schools and captures enrolment, attendance, and some performance data. The Annual Survey of Schools provides a national snapshot. Matric results provide a high-stakes, high-visibility annual data point. The Annual National Assessments (though discontinued in their original form) and subsequent systemic evaluation programmes provide periodic measures of learning outcomes below Grade 12.

But the quality and timeliness of this data varies enormously. SA-SAMS data quality depends on school-level administrators entering data accurately and timeously — and in many under-resourced schools, the administrative capacity to do this is limited. District offices often lack real-time visibility into school-level performance. A circuit manager responsible for 25 schools may visit each school once or twice per term — not enough to identify emerging problems before they become crises. Provincial head offices receive aggregated data that may be weeks or months old, making real-time operational adjustments impossible.

The information deficit is most severe where it matters most: in the poorest, most rural, most under-resourced schools. The schools with the greatest need for oversight and support are often the schools with the weakest data systems, the least reliable connectivity, and the fewest administrative staff to capture and transmit information. The result is an information asymmetry that mirrors the service delivery asymmetry — the system knows least about the schools that need help most.

### Capability — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

The capability crisis in education operates at three levels: teacher capability, district support capability, and institutional management capability.

At the teacher level, South Africa has approximately 440,000 teachers in public schools. The quantity is broadly sufficient — but the quality and distribution are not. **There is a persistent shortage of qualified teachers in mathematics, physical sciences, technology, and languages** (particularly in African languages at senior phase). Rural and township schools struggle to attract and retain qualified teachers because the employment framework does not allow schools or provinces to offer meaningful incentives for difficult postings. *The national salary scale is uniform* — a mathematics teacher in a Johannesburg suburban school earns the same as a mathematics teacher in a remote Limpopo village, despite vastly different living costs and working conditions.

At the district level, subject advisor vacancies are a chronic problem. A district that lacks a mathematics subject advisor cannot provide specialist support to mathematics teachers across dozens of schools. Circuit managers are often responsible for more schools than they can meaningfully support — some circuits encompass 30 or more schools spread across vast rural geographies. The district office is supposed to be the critical support mechanism that translates policy into practice, but in many districts, it lacks the human resources to fulfil this function.

:::expert
The South African Institution of Civil Engineering's assessment of school infrastructure, combined with the DBE's own infrastructure reports, has documented that as of 2023, approximately 3,898 schools still lacked adequate sanitation (with many relying on pit latrines), 239 schools had no water supply, and approximately 19,000 schools lacked libraries. The infrastructure backlog reflects not just a funding shortfall but a capability gap — many provinces cannot spend their infrastructure grants because they lack the project management, engineering, and procurement capacity to manage construction programmes at scale. The Eastern Cape, Limpopo, and KwaZulu-Natal have been the most persistent underspenders of education infrastructure grants, returning unspent funds while their schools deteriorate.
:::

At the institutional level, many provincial education departments have experienced leadership instability — HODs replaced frequently, MECs rotated through cabinet reshuffles, organisational restructuring that disrupts institutional continuity. Capability is not just about having qualified individuals in posts. It is about building institutional depth — the systems, processes, and institutional knowledge that allow the organisation to function even when individuals change.

### Legitimacy — Rating: 4 (Functional)

Education is one area where the constitutional mandate is clear and the democratic legitimacy of the responsible entities is broadly recognised. Section 29 of the Constitution establishes the right to basic education. The South African Schools Act provides the legislative framework. Parents, through SGBs, participate directly in school governance. Provincial MECs are democratically accountable through provincial elections. The DBE's authority to set curriculum and standards is not contested.

The legitimacy element earns a 4 — it is largely intact. Where legitimacy frays is not at the level of mandate but at the level of trust. In communities where schools have been chronically underperforming — where learners attend for 12 years and emerge unable to read, write, or calculate at grade-appropriate levels — the institutional legitimacy of the education system is eroded by experience. Parents who have lost confidence that the school can educate their children exercise exit where they can (sending children to better-resourced schools in other areas or in the private sector) and voice where they cannot (through SGB activism, community protest, or litigation). The right to education is constitutionally legitimate. Whether the institutions charged with delivering it retain the social legitimacy to govern effectively is a different question — and in many communities, the answer is increasingly uncertain.

### Enforcement — Rating: 1 (Critical Failure)

Enforcement is the weakest element in education governance and arguably the most consequential. When educational outcomes deteriorate — when a province's matric results decline, when district after district produces literacy rates below national targets, when infrastructure backlogs persist for decades — what automatic consequences follow?

**The answer, overwhelmingly, is: none.**

Section 100 of the Constitution empowers national government to intervene when a province "cannot or does not fulfil" a constitutional obligation. Section 100 has been invoked in education — most notably in Limpopo in 2012, when the national government placed the provincial education department under administration after a textbook delivery crisis left hundreds of thousands of learners without learning materials. But the Limpopo intervention demonstrated the limitations of Section 100 as an enforcement mechanism. The intervention was politically contentious, legally challenged, operationally disruptive, and ultimately temporary. When the intervention ended, many of the structural problems that caused the crisis remained.

**There are no automatic triggers in education governance.** There is no provision that says "if a province's matric pass rate falls below X per cent for three consecutive years, the following corrective measures are automatically activated." There is no automatic consequence for persistent underspending of infrastructure grants. There is no mandatory intervention when a district's literacy assessments show that 80 per cent of learners cannot read for meaning. *Enforcement depends entirely on political will — and political will is, by definition, discretionary.*

:::takeaway
Education governance scores a 1 on enforcement — critical failure. The system can identify failing provinces, failing districts, and failing schools. It publishes the data annually. But it has no mechanism for automatically converting identified failure into mandatory corrective action. Reports are written. Concern is expressed. And the children who cannot read remain unable to read.
:::

## The Root Cause

The RAARICLE™ diagnosis reveals education's primary failure pattern: **cross-sphere misalignment**. The pattern identified in [Why South Africa's Institutions Keep Failing](/articles/ra-1-2-why-institutions-fail) as Pattern 2 — the wrong entity holds the wrong element — is the dominant structural flaw in basic education governance.

Authority sits primarily at the national level. DBE controls the curriculum. The ELRC controls salary bargaining. The Employment of Educators Act sets employment conditions. National Treasury controls the conditional grant framework. These are not peripheral inputs — they are the critical levers that determine whether education succeeds or fails.

Delivery sits at the provincial level. Provincial departments employ the teachers, manage the schools, maintain the infrastructure, and support the districts. The province is where the work happens.

Accountability is split. The provincial MEC is politically accountable for matric results and educational outcomes. But the national Minister of Basic Education is accountable for curriculum, policy, and the employment framework. **Neither entity bears full accountability because neither entity controls the full system.**

This misalignment produces the *accountability theatre* described in [The Eight Tests](/articles/ra-1-1-eight-tests) — a system where accountability is assigned but not genuine, because the entity that is accountable lacks the authority to deliver. The provincial MEC can be blamed for poor results, but the MEC cannot fix the underlying causes because the levers of control sit at the national level.

The misalignment is compounded by fragmentation (too many entities in the value chain), absence (no automatic enforcement), and, in some provinces, capture (political deployment of unqualified district directors and school principals). But the primary pattern — the one that, if fixed, would create the conditions for addressing the others — is misalignment. Co-locating authority with accountability is the structural reform that education requires.

## The Reform Blueprint

The diagnosis points to a clear structural prescription: authority must be co-located with accountability. The entity that is accountable for educational outcomes must control the inputs that determine those outcomes. There are three possible approaches, and each must be evaluated against all eight RAARICLE™ tests.

### Option A: Full Devolution

Give provinces complete authority over basic education — curriculum adaptation, teacher employment conditions, salary bargaining, infrastructure management, and assessment. Provinces would set their own teacher salary scales, negotiate with unions at provincial level, adapt the national curriculum to local conditions, and manage their own infrastructure programmes. The national department would shrink to a policy coordination and standard-setting body.

This option scores well on co-location — the province would hold responsibility, accountability, authority, and resources in a single institutional chain. But it fails on two critical tests. First, capability — the provinces with the weakest educational outcomes (Eastern Cape, Limpopo, KwaZulu-Natal) are also the provinces with the weakest institutional capacity. Devolving authority to provinces that lack the capability to exercise it would deepen inequality rather than reduce it. Second, it creates a fragmentation risk at national level — nine different salary scales, nine different curriculum variants, and nine different assessment standards would undermine labour mobility, qualification portability, and national coherence. Full devolution solves the misalignment problem but creates new problems of unequal capability and national fragmentation.

### Option B: Full Centralisation

Make the national Department of Basic Education directly responsible for school management, teacher employment, and educational outcomes. Convert provincial education departments into regional offices of DBE. Remove the provincial MEC's accountability for education and place it squarely on the national minister.

This option also scores well on co-location — DBE would hold all eight elements in a single institutional chain. It would eliminate the misalignment that is the primary structural flaw. But it fails the legitimacy and information tests. Centralising control over 23,000 schools in a single national department would create a span of management that exceeds any ministry's capacity. The information requirements alone — real-time visibility into school performance across every province, every district, every school — would be overwhelming. And it would remove the democratic accountability link between provincial voters and the entity managing their schools, weakening the legitimacy element.

### Option C: Structured Autonomy (Recommended)

The third option addresses the misalignment without incurring the costs of full devolution or full centralisation. It works as follows.

National government retains control over three functions: setting minimum curriculum standards (what must be taught), setting minimum qualification requirements for teachers (who may teach), and administering a national assessment framework (how outcomes are measured). These functions preserve national coherence without constraining provincial operational authority.

Provincial government gains full operational authority within the national framework. This means the province controls teacher employment — including the authority to set salary supplements for scarce skills and difficult postings, to negotiate operational conditions at provincial level, and to manage the disciplinary process through a streamlined procedure that can be completed within a defined timeframe. The province controls infrastructure — not through a conditional grant with national conditions, but through a ring-fenced, unconditional education infrastructure allocation that the province manages using its own procurement and project management systems. The province controls district management — including the authority to restructure district offices, appoint and remove district directors based on performance, and allocate subject advisors based on provincial priorities.

The critical innovation is in enforcement. The structured autonomy model introduces automatic triggers tied to outcome indicators. If a province's performance on a defined set of education outcome indicators — not just matric pass rates but standardised literacy and numeracy assessments at multiple grade levels — falls below a prescribed threshold for two consecutive assessment cycles, an automatic support mechanism is activated. This is not a punitive Section 100 intervention. It is a mandatory technical support programme, delivered by a national Education Support Unit, that provides the province with specialist capability (curriculum support, infrastructure project management, financial management) until its performance indicators return to acceptable levels. The support is automatic — not discretionary. The province does not choose whether to accept it. The national department does not choose whether to offer it. The trigger is objective, the response is prescribed, and the exit criteria are defined in advance.

### Element-by-Element Design Rules

**Responsibility:** One provincial education department owns the delivery chain from district management to school-level outcomes. DBE retains responsibility only for curriculum standards, teacher qualification standards, and national assessment.

**Accountability:** The provincial MEC signs a binding performance agreement with specific, measurable education outcome indicators — literacy rates, numeracy rates, matric bachelor pass rates, infrastructure completion targets — with consequences that are defined in advance. The national minister is accountable for curriculum quality and assessment integrity, not for delivery.

**Authority:** The provincial MEC passes the 30-day authority test. The province can adjust teacher salary supplements within 30 days using its own budget. The province can initiate a disciplinary process against a school principal and conclude it within 90 days using a streamlined procedure. The province can procure textbooks and learning materials through its own procurement process without national approval.

**Resources:** Education funding is ring-fenced. The provincial equitable share includes a separately identified education allocation that cannot be reprioritised to other functions without National Treasury approval. Infrastructure funding is unconditional — the province receives a block allocation based on its infrastructure backlog and manages it according to its own priorities.

**Information:** A national Education Information System provides standardised, real-time data on school performance — enrolment, attendance, assessment results, teacher presence, infrastructure condition — accessible to school principals, district directors, provincial heads of department, DBE, and the public. Data quality is assured through annual independent audits of the information system.

**Capability:** Every district must have a full complement of subject advisors in core subjects (mathematics, science, languages). Every district director must meet prescribed professional qualifications. A national Education Leadership Academy provides ongoing professional development for district and school leaders. These are binding requirements, not aspirational targets.

**Legitimacy:** The provincial MEC remains democratically accountable through provincial elections. SGBs retain their school-level governance functions. Parents and communities retain voice through the SGB structure. The national Education Support Unit operates under a transparent mandate with clear entry and exit criteria, preventing legitimacy challenges.

**Enforcement:** Automatic triggers replace discretionary interventions. A province whose literacy assessment results fall below a defined threshold for two consecutive cycles receives mandatory support. A province whose education infrastructure spend falls below 80 per cent of allocation for two consecutive years has its infrastructure programme placed under joint management with the national Education Support Unit. A provincial MEC whose province fails to meet minimum outcome targets for a full term has the failure formally reported to the provincial legislature with a mandatory accountability hearing.

:::expert
Research conducted by the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development on education governance in decentralised systems has consistently found that the most effective models combine national standard-setting with subnational operational autonomy — precisely the structured autonomy approach. The OECD's 2023 review of education decentralisation found that countries where subnational authorities control teacher deployment, infrastructure management, and operational budgets — within a framework of nationally defined standards and nationally monitored outcomes — outperform both fully centralised systems (where responsiveness to local conditions is weak) and fully devolved systems (where national coherence is lost). The key design feature, the OECD found, is not the degree of centralisation but the alignment of authority with accountability — the principle that the RAARICLE™ framework identifies as the fundamental requirement for institutional effectiveness.
:::

### Anti-Capture Protections

The structured autonomy model must address all five capture vectors identified in [Why South Africa's Institutions Keep Failing](/articles/ra-1-2-why-institutions-fail).

Political capture is addressed through merit-based appointment requirements for all education leadership positions — provincial heads of department, district directors, and school principals. Appointments must follow a prescribed process with independent selection panel participation, and minimum professional qualifications must be specified in legislation rather than policy (making them harder to waive).

Commercial capture is addressed through procurement transparency requirements — all education procurement above a defined threshold must be published on a public procurement portal, with contract details, supplier information, and pricing available for public scrutiny.

Labour capture is addressed through the restructured bargaining framework — provincial-level operational bargaining gives provinces the authority to negotiate conditions that affect service delivery (teaching loads, working hours, performance management processes) while national bargaining covers wage levels and core employment protections.

Regulatory capture is addressed through the structural separation of the support function from the delivery function — the national Education Support Unit that provides mandatory technical support to underperforming provinces is institutionally separate from DBE and reports to an independent board.

Information capture is addressed through the national Education Information System, which provides direct data access to oversight bodies — the Auditor-General, the DPME, provincial legislatures, and the public — without requiring that information pass through provincial management filters.

:::framework
**The Education Accountability Tracker**

Use this framework to assess whether your province's education system has the structural ingredients for success — and to identify exactly where the design breaks down.

**Step 1: Map the delivery chain for your child's school.**

Identify who is responsible for each input your child's school needs: curriculum (national DBE), teacher employment and salaries (provincial PED via ELRC national conditions), textbooks (provincial procurement under national catalogue), school infrastructure (provincial via conditional grant from national), learner transport (provincial), school nutrition (provincial via conditional grant). Write down every entity and note where decisions are made versus where delivery happens.

**Step 2: Identify your province's education MEC and their performance agreement.**

Find out what specific, measurable outcomes the MEC has committed to — matric pass rate targets, literacy benchmarks, infrastructure completion timelines. If these targets are vague or not publicly available, the accountability is weak. A meaningful performance agreement should include outcome indicators (not just input or process measures) with specific numbers and deadlines.

**Step 3: Apply the 30-day authority test.**

Pick a concrete problem at your child's school — a vacant teaching post, a broken window, a missing textbook. Ask: can the school principal, the circuit manager, or the district director fix this within 30 days using their own authority and budget? If the answer requires escalation to the provincial head office, a procurement process that takes months, or a decision by a national entity, the authority is not co-located with the delivery point.

**Step 4: Follow the education money.**

Check whether your province ring-fences education spending or whether education budgets are vulnerable to in-year reprioritisation. Examine whether the school's Section 21 allocation (direct funding from the province) is sufficient for basic operations, and whether conditional grant funds (like the Education Infrastructure Grant) are being spent on schedule or underspent.

**Step 5: Check your district's capability.**

Find out whether your education district has a full complement of subject advisors for every subject offered, whether the circuit manager responsible for your child's school has a manageable number of schools to oversee, and whether key positions are filled by permanent appointees with the prescribed qualifications — not acting officials or political deployees.

**Step 6: Assess enforcement.**

Ask: when your province's matric results decline, what happens automatically? When the Auditor-General qualifies the education department's financial statements, what mandatory corrective action follows? If the answer is "a report is issued" or "concern is expressed" but no automatic trigger compels change, the enforcement mechanism is absent.

This six-step process gives you a structured basis for engaging your school governing body, your ward councillor, your provincial legislature's education committee, or civil society organisations — with specific, evidence-based observations rather than generalised complaints.
:::

## Implementation Pathway

The structured autonomy reform does not require a constitutional amendment. Education is listed as a concurrent function in Schedule 4, and the Constitution does not prescribe the specific division of authority between national and provincial government — it leaves that to legislation. The reform can be implemented through legislative amendment and policy change.

### Phase 1: Legal Foundation (Year 1-2)

The Employment of Educators Act must be amended to transfer operational employment authority — including the power to set salary supplements, manage disciplinary processes through streamlined procedures, and determine post provisioning at provincial level — from the national framework to provincial education departments. The ELRC's mandate must be restructured to separate national-level bargaining (salary scales, core employment protections) from provincial-level bargaining (operational conditions, performance management, working arrangements).

National Treasury must amend the Division of Revenue framework to convert the Education Infrastructure Grant from a conditional grant with national conditions to a ring-fenced, unconditional block allocation. The PFMA regulations must be amended to allow provinces to manage education infrastructure procurement through their own processes, subject to Auditor-General oversight.

The Education Support Unit must be established through legislation — not ministerial decree — with a statutory mandate, an independent governing board, clear entry and exit criteria for mandatory support, and ring-fenced funding from the national education budget. Establishing the unit through legislation rather than policy protects it from being weakened or abolished by future ministers.

Automatic enforcement triggers must be legislated in the amended Education Laws. These triggers — defining the outcome indicators, the threshold levels, the mandatory responses, and the exit criteria — must be specified in legislation to remove ministerial discretion from the enforcement process.

### Phase 2: Pilot (Year 2-3)

The restructured framework should be piloted in two to three provinces that represent different governance contexts — ideally one well-performing province (to test whether structured autonomy improves an already functional system), one poorly performing province (to test whether it can address deep structural failure), and one medium-performing province (to test the transition process). The pilot provinces would operate under the new framework while the remaining provinces continue under the existing system. The pilot period should be at least two full academic years — enough time to observe the effect on educational outcomes rather than just administrative processes.

The pilot must include rigorous, independent evaluation — not self-assessment by the participating provinces or by DBE, but evaluation by an independent body (the DPME's evaluation unit or an externally commissioned assessment) using pre-defined outcome indicators agreed before the pilot begins.

### Phase 3: National Rollout (Year 3-5)

Based on pilot evaluation results, the framework is rolled out to all nine provinces. The rollout should be phased — provinces with adequate institutional capacity transition first, while provinces requiring more preparation receive transitional support from the Education Support Unit. The full rollout should be completed within five years of the legal foundation being laid.

:::takeaway
The structured autonomy reform is constitutionally feasible — it requires legislative amendment, not constitutional change. The key legislative targets are the Employment of Educators Act, the ELRC mandate, the Division of Revenue framework, and new legislation establishing the Education Support Unit and automatic enforcement triggers. A three-phase approach — legal foundation, pilot, national rollout — allows for rigorous testing before system-wide implementation.
:::

The reform outlined in this article is ambitious but structurally necessary. **South Africa cannot continue to operate an education system where the entity accountable for results does not control the inputs that determine results.** The RAARICLE™ diagnosis makes the structural flaw precise, and the reform blueprint makes the structural fix specific. What remains is the political will to implement it — and that is a question not of institutional design but of democratic pressure from citizens who understand, as you now do, exactly what is broken and exactly what would fix it.
