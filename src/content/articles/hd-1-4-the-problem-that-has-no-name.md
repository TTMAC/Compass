## What You Name, You Can Solve

In 1929, the Carnegie Corporation of New York funded what would become one of the most consequential social science investigations in South African history. The Carnegie Commission of Investigation on the Poor White Question assembled a team of researchers — economists, psychologists, educationalists, and health specialists — to study a phenomenon that had alarmed white South African society for three decades: the emergence of a substantial class of white people who were visibly, measurably, and persistently poor. The Commission produced five volumes totalling over 3,000 pages. It quantified the problem. It diagnosed its causes. It proposed solutions. And — this is the part that matters most — the state acted on its findings with a coherence, scale, and sustained political will that transformed the material conditions of an entire population within a single generation.

The "Poor White Problem" was not merely studied. It was *named*. Naming it created a political object — something that could be debated in Parliament, funded in budgets, tracked with metrics, and evaluated against targets. The name gave the problem boundaries, assigned it urgency, and implied a corresponding obligation: if there is a "Poor White Problem," then there must be a solution to the Poor White Problem, and the state must find and implement that solution. The Carnegie Commission did not invent white poverty in South Africa. But by naming it, framing it as a systemic crisis rather than a collection of individual misfortunes, and producing an evidence base that demanded institutional response, the Commission created the conditions for one of the most effective — and most racially exclusive — anti-poverty programmes in twentieth-century history.

Now consider the inverse. Since 1994, South Africa has confronted a phenomenon that dwarfs the Poor White Problem in every measurable dimension: the persistent, structural, intergenerational poverty of the black majority. According to Stats SA's *National Poverty Lines* series for 2023, approximately 18.2 million South Africans live below the food poverty line of R760 per person per month, and approximately 30.4 million — more than half the population — live below the upper-bound poverty line of R1,558. The overwhelming majority of these people are black. The unemployment rate among black South Africans exceeds 40 per cent on the expanded definition. Black households earn, on average, one-fifth of what white households earn. Black children attend schools that produce the worst learning outcomes in the developing world. Black communities experience homicide rates that would constitute a public health emergency in any other country. The Inequality-adjusted Human Development Index reveals that the average black South African experiences conditions closer to "low human development" than the national HDI figure of 0.713 suggests.

This is not a collection of individual misfortunes. It is a systemic crisis. It has identifiable structural causes. It is measurable. It is reproducible across generations. By every analytical criterion that the Carnegie Commission applied to the Poor White Problem in 1929, it qualifies as a "problem" in exactly the same sense — a bounded, diagnosable, solvable phenomenon that demands an integrated institutional response.

And yet it has no name.

There is no "Poor Black Problem" in South African political discourse. There is no Carnegie-scale commission that has framed the structural poverty of the black majority as a single, coherent phenomenon requiring a single, coherent response. There are anti-poverty programmes. There are social grants. There are housing projects and school feeding schemes and free basic services. There is the National Development Plan, which identifies poverty and inequality as binding constraints. But there is no moment at which the South African state looked at the condition of its black majority and said, with the same clinical precision the Carnegie Commission applied in 1932: *This is a problem. Here is its anatomy. Here is what we will do about it. Here is how we will know when we have succeeded.*

This article argues that the asymmetry between the named "Poor White Problem" and the unnamed "Poor Black Problem" is not a historical curiosity. It is a diagnostic marker for the most fundamental failure of the post-1994 development project: the failure to convert political liberation into a development programme with the specificity, coherence, and institutional commitment that the problem demands. The comparison is not offered to suggest that the apartheid state's methods were admirable — they were deployed in the service of racial exclusion and were funded in part by the exploitation of black labour. The comparison is offered because it reveals something essential about the relationship between naming, framing, political will, and development outcomes. What you name, you can solve. What you refuse to name — or what you scatter across a hundred programmes without ever consolidating into a single actionable diagnosis — you manage at best and ignore at worst.

## The Carnegie Commission: Anatomy of a Named Problem

To understand what it means to *name* a development problem, you must understand what the Carnegie Commission actually did — not as a historical curiosity, but as a case study in the mechanics of effective state intervention.

### The Context: White Poverty as Political Crisis

By the early twentieth century, a substantial number of white South Africans — predominantly Afrikaners — had been reduced to poverty by a convergence of structural forces. The Anglo-Boer War of 1899-1902 had devastated the rural Afrikaner economy. British scorched-earth policies destroyed farms, killed livestock, and displaced families into concentration camps where approximately 26,000 Afrikaner women and children died. The survivors returned to farms that had been burned and to an agricultural economy increasingly dominated by large-scale commercial operations they could not compete with. Successive droughts — particularly the devastating drought of 1919 — pushed marginal farmers off the land entirely. Rinderpest had already wiped out cattle herds in the 1890s. The transition from subsistence agriculture to a mining and industrial economy created a labour market that demanded skills many rural Afrikaners did not possess.

The result was a visible, growing class of white people who were poor by any measure — landless, unskilled, often illiterate, living in conditions that white society associated exclusively with black South Africans. By the 1920s, estimates placed the number of "poor whites" at approximately 300,000 out of a total white population of roughly 1.8 million — approximately one in six white South Africans. They congregated on the edges of towns and cities, in conditions that shocked middle-class white observers precisely because the poverty was *white*. A black person living in identical conditions would not have constituted a "problem" in the political consciousness of the era. The "problem" was that whiteness and poverty were supposed to be incompatible, and reality was contradicting the ideology.

This is worth pausing on, because it reveals the first asymmetry. The Poor White Problem was not defined by the severity of the poverty. It was defined by the *incongruity* of the poverty — white people were not supposed to be poor. The naming of the problem was itself an act of racial ideology: it presupposed that poverty was the natural condition of black South Africans and the unnatural condition of white South Africans, and it mobilised state resources to correct the unnatural condition while leaving the natural one untouched.

### The Commission: Five Volumes, One Diagnosis

The Carnegie Commission, formally constituted in 1929 and reporting in 1932, was remarkable for its methodological rigour and its integrated approach. It was not a single study. It was five coordinated studies, each examining a different dimension of the problem:

**Volume I: Economic Report** (J.F.W. Grosskopf) examined the economic causes of white poverty — land dispossession, drought, lack of capital, inability to compete with cheap black labour, and the structural transition from agrarian to industrial economy. Grosskopf documented the economic mechanisms that were producing white poverty and identified the policy levers that could reverse them.

**Volume II: Psychological Report** (R.W. Wilcocks) examined the psychological and behavioural dimensions — what the Commission called the "mentality" of poor whites, including attitudes toward manual labour, education, and self-improvement. This volume reflected the racial pseudoscience of its era but also contained genuine observations about the psychological effects of persistent poverty, including learned helplessness, shortened time horizons, and the erosion of aspiration — phenomena that modern development economics would recognise under different terminology.

**Volume III: Educational Report** (E.G. Malherbe) examined the educational deficits of poor white communities — low school attendance, high dropout rates, inadequate rural schools, and the relationship between educational attainment and economic mobility. Malherbe's analysis of the education-poverty cycle was, within its racial boundaries, analytically sound and directly actionable.

**Volume IV: Health Report** (W.A. Murray) examined the health conditions of poor white communities — malnutrition, disease, inadequate sanitation, and the relationship between health and economic productivity. Murray documented conditions — pellagra, hookworm, tuberculosis, malnutrition-related developmental delays — that were directly attributable to poverty and directly addressable through public health intervention.

**Volume V: Sociological Report** (J.R. Albertyn) examined the social and community dimensions — family breakdown, urbanisation pressures, the erosion of traditional community structures, and the role of the church and civil society in either ameliorating or perpetuating poverty.

The five volumes together constituted an integrated diagnosis. The Commission did not treat economic poverty, educational failure, health deficits, psychological adaptation, and social breakdown as separate problems with separate solutions. It treated them as facets of a single phenomenon — a mutually reinforcing system of deprivation in which each dimension exacerbated the others. A farmer dispossessed by drought (economic) whose children did not attend school (educational) because they were malnourished (health) and whose family had internalised a sense of helplessness (psychological) in a community where traditional support structures had collapsed (sociological) was not facing five problems. He was facing one problem with five dimensions.

If this sounds familiar, it should. It is precisely the systems framework described in [The Reinforcing Cycle](/articles/hd-1-3-the-reinforcing-cycle) — the recognition that safety, growth, and development form a single interconnected system, not three separate policy domains. The Carnegie Commission understood this in 1932, within the boundaries of its racial ideology, and acted on it. The post-1994 state understands it in theory — the National Development Plan says as much — but has not acted on it with comparable coherence.

### The Response: What Institutional Will Looks Like

The Carnegie Commission's findings did not gather dust. They catalysed a state response that was comprehensive, sustained, and effective — at least for the population it was designed to serve. The mechanisms included:

**Employment reservation and industrial policy.** The "civilised labour policy," which predated the Commission but was reinforced by its findings, reserved skilled and semi-skilled jobs in mining, railways, and public works for white workers at wages above market rates. The state-owned enterprises — South African Railways and Harbours, the Iron and Steel Corporation (ISCOR), the Electricity Supply Commission (ESKOM) — became instruments of white employment creation, absorbing tens of thousands of poor whites into stable, unionised, pensionable employment. The Industrial Conciliation Act of 1924 and its successive amendments formalised job reservation along racial lines, ensuring that white workers were insulated from competition with cheaper black labour.

**Agricultural support.** The state provided poor white farmers with subsidised credit through the Land Bank, drought relief, veterinary services, agricultural extension officers, cooperative marketing arrangements, and — crucially — access to land through racially restricted land policies that prevented black competition for agricultural land. The 1936 Native Trust and Land Act, which consolidated and extended the 1913 Natives Land Act, was not merely an instrument of dispossession against black South Africans; it was simultaneously an instrument of protection for white farmers by eliminating black competition for land and labour.

**Education.** The state invested massively in white education — building schools, training teachers, introducing compulsory education for white children, providing free textbooks and school meals, and establishing a system of vocational and technical training specifically designed to equip poor white youth for industrial employment. By the 1960s, white illiteracy had been virtually eliminated, and the educational attainment gap between poor whites and middle-class whites had narrowed dramatically.

**Social welfare.** The state established old-age pensions, disability grants, and child welfare services that — while nominally available to all races — were administered at dramatically different levels. A white old-age pensioner received several times what a black pensioner received. The welfare system was explicitly designed to prevent white people from falling below a defined minimum standard of living — a racial floor beneath which the state would not allow white citizens to fall.

**Housing.** The state built housing for poor white families — not temporary shelters but permanent homes with sanitation, electricity, and access to schools and clinics. The housing programme was part of the broader urbanisation strategy, moving poor whites from rural destitution into urban settings where they could access industrial employment, education, and health services simultaneously.

The cumulative effect was transformative. Within a single generation — roughly 1933 to 1970 — the Poor White Problem was effectively eliminated. The approximately 300,000 poor whites of the early 1930s were absorbed into the industrial economy, educated, housed, and provided with a social safety net that ensured intergenerational upward mobility. By the 1970s, white poverty in South Africa was statistically negligible. The white population enjoyed living standards comparable to those of Western Europe, with universal literacy, near-universal employment, comprehensive social services, and life expectancy approaching developed-world levels.

This was not an accident. It was not the result of market forces. It was the result of a state that named a problem, diagnosed it with analytical rigour, designed an integrated response, funded that response over decades, and sustained political will long enough for the compounding effects to take hold. It was also, of course, funded in significant part by the exploitation and dispossession of black South Africans — a point to which we will return.

## The Unnamed Problem: Post-1994 Black Poverty

### The Scale

The "Poor Black Problem" — the term this article uses deliberately, to make the asymmetry visible — is not merely larger than the Poor White Problem. It is larger by an order of magnitude that should, by rights, have generated an even more determined institutional response.

The Carnegie Commission studied approximately 300,000 poor whites out of a white population of 1.8 million — roughly 17 per cent of the white population. Post-1994 South Africa confronts approximately 30.4 million people below the upper-bound poverty line out of a total population of approximately 62 million — roughly 49 per cent of the total population. The vast majority — over 93 per cent — of these poor South Africans are black. In absolute terms, the number of poor black South Africans is approximately 100 times the number of poor whites the Carnegie Commission studied. In proportional terms, the share of the black population living in poverty far exceeds the share of the white population that was poor in the 1930s.

The dimensions of the problem mirror the Carnegie Commission's five-volume framework with eerie precision:

**Economic dimension.** Black South Africans are disproportionately unemployed (over 40 per cent on the expanded definition), disproportionately employed in precarious and low-wage work, disproportionately excluded from asset ownership, and disproportionately dependent on social grants. The median black household income is approximately one-fifth that of the median white household. Black entrepreneurs face barriers to capital, markets, and networks that white entrepreneurs do not. The economic structure — the specific industries, ownership patterns, and labour market institutions that determine who gets what — continues to produce racially skewed outcomes three decades after political liberation.

**Educational dimension.** Black children overwhelmingly attend former Department of Education and Training (DET) schools — the schools built for black education under apartheid — that continue to produce catastrophically poor learning outcomes. The 2021 PIRLS study found that 81 per cent of South African Grade 4 learners could not read for meaning, and the racial distribution of this failure is not uniform. Schools serving predominantly black communities in townships, informal settlements, and rural areas produce the worst outcomes. The educational inheritance of apartheid — systematically inferior infrastructure, undertrained teachers, a culture of low expectations — has not been dismantled. It has been perpetuated under new management.

**Health dimension.** Black South Africans have lower life expectancy, higher maternal and infant mortality, higher HIV prevalence, higher TB incidence, and worse access to quality healthcare than white South Africans. The public healthcare system — on which the vast majority of black South Africans depend — is chronically underfunded, understaffed, and underperforming. The private healthcare system — which serves primarily white and middle-class South Africans — absorbs approximately half of total health expenditure while serving approximately 16 per cent of the population. The health dimension of the Poor Black Problem is not a residual of apartheid that is slowly correcting itself. In several indicators — particularly primary healthcare access in rural areas and the quality of district hospitals — it has worsened since 1994.

**Psychological dimension.** Three decades of freedom have not eliminated what Steve Biko diagnosed as the "most potent weapon in the hands of the oppressor" — the colonisation of the mind. Persistent unemployment, educational failure, and social marginalisation produce psychological effects that are functionally identical to those the Carnegie Commission documented among poor whites: shortened time horizons, learned helplessness, erosion of aspiration, and the internalisation of failure as personal rather than structural. The difference is that these psychological effects are now compounded by the specific cruelty of a nominally democratic dispensation: unlike the poor whites of the 1930s, poor black South Africans live under a government that was supposed to liberate them from precisely these conditions. The gap between promise and reality generates a particular form of disillusionment that the Carnegie Commission never had to study.

**Sociological dimension.** The social fabric of black communities has been under sustained assault for over a century — from migrant labour policies that separated families, to forced removals that destroyed communities, to the current crisis of absent fathers (approximately 62 per cent of South African children grow up without a father in the home), to the violence that pervades daily life in townships and informal settlements. Community structures that once provided informal social protection — extended family networks, community savings groups (stokvels), church-based support — are under strain from urbanisation, unemployment, and the corrosive effects of substance abuse and violence. The sociological dimension of the Poor Black Problem is, if anything, more severe than the corresponding dimension of the Poor White Problem, because the structural forces that destroyed black community cohesion operated for longer, at greater scale, and with more deliberate brutality.

### The Response: What Fragmentation Looks Like

The post-1994 state has not ignored black poverty. It has spent enormous sums on anti-poverty programmes. The social grant system — which distributes approximately R250 billion annually to over 28 million beneficiaries — is the largest cash transfer programme in Africa and one of the largest in the developing world. The state provides free basic services (water, electricity, sanitation) to indigent households. It has built over 4.5 million housing units since 1994. It funds a national school nutrition programme that feeds approximately 9 million learners daily. It provides free primary healthcare and antiretroviral treatment that has turned a terminal diagnosis into a manageable chronic condition for millions.

These programmes have prevented catastrophe. Without social grants, South Africa's poverty rate would be significantly higher. Without antiretroviral treatment, millions more South Africans would have died from HIV/AIDS. Without housing delivery, millions more would be living in informal structures without basic services. The post-1994 social safety net, for all its deficiencies, has prevented the poorest South Africans from falling into the kind of absolute destitution that characterises the poorest countries in sub-Saharan Africa.

But preventing catastrophe is not solving the problem. And the distance between the two is precisely where the comparison with the Carnegie Commission response becomes most revealing.

The Carnegie Commission response was *integrated*. Economic policy, educational policy, health policy, social welfare policy, and housing policy were designed to work together, each reinforcing the others. A poor white family did not receive a job reservation *or* a school for their children *or* a health service *or* a house. They received all four simultaneously, because the Commission had demonstrated that the problem was systemic and required a systemic response.

The post-1994 response is *fragmented*. Anti-poverty programmes are scattered across at least a dozen government departments and three spheres of government. The Department of Social Development administers grants. The Department of Human Settlements builds houses. The Department of Basic Education runs schools. The Department of Health runs clinics. The Department of Employment and Labour runs skills programmes. Each department has its own strategic plan, its own targets, its own accountability framework, and its own budget. There is no institution responsible for the *system* — for ensuring that a poor black family in Limpopo receives not just a grant *or* a school *or* a clinic *or* a house, but an integrated package of interventions designed to break the cycle of deprivation within a defined timeframe.

The National Development Plan of 2012 was supposed to be this integrated framework. It identified poverty, inequality, and unemployment as the three binding constraints on South Africa's development. It set targets for 2030: eliminate income poverty below the lower-bound poverty line, reduce the Gini coefficient from 0.69 to 0.6, reduce unemployment to 6 per cent. These were ambitious, specific, and measurable. But the NDP was never operationalised as a Carnegie Commission-style programme. It was adopted as a strategic framework — which means it was filed, referenced in departmental strategic plans, and progressively ignored as the implementation challenges became apparent. No single institution was given authority, budget, and accountability for achieving the NDP's poverty targets. No monitoring framework tracked progress with the specificity needed to identify failures and trigger corrections. By the mid-2020s, every major NDP target was off track, and the plan had quietly transitioned from policy framework to historical document.

The contrast is not between a state that cared about poor whites and a state that does not care about poor blacks. It is between a state that *named a problem, diagnosed it as a system, and built an integrated institutional response* and a state that has *identified a problem in general terms, diagnosed its components in isolation, and scattered its response across fragmented institutions*. The Carnegie Commission produced a diagnosis that could be translated into a programme. The post-1994 state has produced a diagnosis that has been translated into a hundred programmes, none of which is responsible for the outcome.

## Why the Problem Has No Name

The absence of a "Poor Black Problem" frame in post-1994 South African discourse is not an oversight. It reflects several interacting political and intellectual dynamics that are worth examining because they illuminate the constraints on development policy-making in a post-liberation democracy.

### The universalism trap

The post-1994 constitutional settlement was explicitly non-racial. The Constitution of 1996 — rightly celebrated as one of the most progressive in the world — establishes rights for all South Africans regardless of race. The policy framework that emerged from this settlement aspires to universalism: development policies are framed in terms of poverty, inequality, and unemployment, not in terms of race. The Reconstruction and Development Programme, the Growth, Employment and Redistribution strategy, the Accelerated and Shared Growth Initiative for South Africa, and the National Development Plan are all formally race-neutral — they target the poor, not poor black people.

This universalism is constitutionally appropriate. It is also analytically misleading. When 93 per cent of the poor are black, a "race-neutral" poverty programme is, in effect, a programme for poor black people — but one that cannot say so, cannot design interventions specific to the structural causes of black poverty, and cannot be held accountable for outcomes disaggregated by race. The universalism trap means that the specificity the Carnegie Commission brought to the Poor White Problem — this is a *white* problem with *white*-specific causes requiring *white*-specific solutions — is structurally unavailable to the post-1994 state. The problem cannot be named because naming it would violate the non-racial premise of the constitutional settlement.

The irony is considerable. The Carnegie Commission's racial specificity was deployed in the service of racial exclusion — the solutions were for whites only. But the *analytical method* — identifying a racially specific pattern of deprivation, diagnosing its racially specific causes, and designing racially specific interventions — produced results precisely because it was specific. The post-1994 state's racial universalism is deployed in the service of racial inclusion — the solutions are for everyone. But the analytical method — identifying poverty in general terms, diagnosing its causes in general terms, and designing interventions in general terms — produces diffuse results precisely because it is general.

### The liberation movement's ideological inheritance

The African National Congress, as a liberation movement, framed its struggle in terms of freedom, democracy, and human rights — not in terms of development economics. The Freedom Charter of 1955 envisioned a South Africa in which "the people shall share in the country's wealth" and "the doors of learning and culture shall be opened." These are aspirational statements, not programme designs. The transition from liberation movement to governing party required a corresponding transition from aspiration to implementation — from "the doors of learning shall be opened" to "here is the budget, here is the institutional design, here is the accountability framework, and here is the timeline for achieving specific, measurable improvements in learning outcomes for black children in quintile 1 and 2 schools."

That transition has been incomplete. The ANC's policy discourse retains the aspirational register of a liberation movement — speaking of "radical economic transformation," "a better life for all," and "the second phase of the transition" — without the programmatic specificity that converts aspiration into outcome. The "Poor White Problem" was not a slogan. It was a diagnosis. "A better life for all" is a slogan, not a diagnosis.

### The political economy of naming

Naming the "Poor Black Problem" would create political obligations that powerful interests would prefer to avoid. If black poverty is a named, bounded, diagnosable problem — in the way the Poor White Problem was named, bounded, and diagnosable — then the state's failure to solve it becomes measurable, attributable, and politically consequential. Politicians prefer problems that are vaguely defined, because vaguely defined problems cannot produce clearly defined failures. If the target is "a better life for all," any improvement in any indicator for any population can be claimed as progress. If the target is "eliminate structural black poverty by 2040 through the following specific interventions measured by the following specific indicators," then failure is visible, specific, and attributable.

There is also the uncomfortable question of beneficiaries. The Carnegie Commission's solutions — job reservation, agricultural subsidies, preferential access to state employment, racially exclusive social welfare — were funded, in significant part, by the exploitation of black labour and the dispossession of black land. The moral clarity of addressing the Poor Black Problem is more complex: the post-1994 state cannot solve black poverty by dispossessing another racial group. The solutions must be found within a framework of constitutional rights, fiscal sustainability, and democratic accountability. This is harder. But harder is not impossible — it merely requires more sophisticated institutional design, not less determination.

### The complexity defence

A final reason the problem has no name is that its complexity is used as a defence against naming it. Black poverty is caused by apartheid's legacy, by global economic forces, by technological change, by corruption, by educational failure, by health crises, by demographic pressures, by cultural factors, by geographic isolation, by institutional weakness — the list of causes is long enough to justify inaction by implying that the problem is too complex for any single intervention or institutional framework to address. The Carnegie Commission faced a problem of comparable complexity — the Poor White Problem was also caused by war, drought, technological change, global economic forces, educational failure, health crises, and cultural factors — but it did not use complexity as a defence against action. It used the five-volume structure to *decompose* the complexity into manageable analytical dimensions, each of which could be addressed by specific institutional mechanisms, while maintaining the integration needed for the components to reinforce each other.

Complexity is not a reason not to name a problem. It is a reason to name it carefully.

## What the Asymmetry Reveals

The comparison between the named Poor White Problem and the unnamed Poor Black Problem is not offered as a historical parallel. It is offered as a diagnostic tool — a way of identifying the specific institutional failures that have prevented the post-1994 state from converting its unprecedented political legitimacy and constitutional framework into development outcomes commensurate with its stated ambitions.

### Lesson one: Naming creates accountability

The Carnegie Commission did not merely describe white poverty. It created a *political object* — the "Poor White Problem" — that could be tracked, debated, and evaluated. Parliament could ask: is the Poor White Problem getting better or worse? Officials could be held accountable for specific indicators tied to a specific diagnosis. The absence of a comparable political object for black poverty means that no institution is accountable for the *system* of black deprivation. Individual departments are accountable for individual programmes — grant delivery, housing construction, school enrolment. But no one is accountable for the question: is structural black poverty being systematically dismantled, and if not, why not?

### Lesson two: Integration beats fragmentation

The Carnegie Commission's five-volume structure was not bureaucratic excess. It was the analytical architecture for an integrated response. Each volume corresponded to a dimension of the problem, and the solutions were designed to interact: employment reservation (economic) worked because workers' children attended free schools (educational), received healthcare (health), and lived in state-provided housing (social) in communities with functioning institutions (sociological). Remove any one dimension and the others would have been less effective.

The post-1994 response fails precisely where the Carnegie response succeeded: integration. A child who receives a school meal (Department of Basic Education) but attends a dysfunctional school (same department, different programme), lives in an RDP house without reliable water (Department of Human Settlements and municipal government), has a parent who receives a social grant but cannot find work (Department of Social Development and Department of Employment and Labour), and lives in a community with a murder rate of 60 per 100,000 (South African Police Service) is not receiving an integrated development intervention. She is receiving five fragments of five interventions designed by five institutions that do not coordinate, do not share data, and do not jointly account for her development outcomes.

### Lesson three: Political will is not the same as political spending

The post-1994 state spends more on anti-poverty programmes, in real terms and as a share of GDP, than the pre-1948 state spent on the Poor White Problem. Spending is not the constraint. The constraint is the conversion of spending into outcomes — and that conversion depends on institutional design, coordination, accountability, and sustained strategic focus that spending alone cannot buy.

The Carnegie Commission response worked not because the state spent a lot of money — though it did — but because the money was spent within an institutional framework designed to produce a specific outcome. The post-1994 state spends a lot of money on programmes designed to produce individual outputs — grants delivered, houses built, meals served — without an institutional framework designed to produce the systemic outcome those outputs are supposed to contribute to.

### Lesson four: The moral asymmetry does not excuse the analytical failure

The Carnegie Commission's programme was morally indefensible. It was designed to uplift white people at the expense of black people, within a system of racial domination that denied the majority of South Africans basic human rights. The post-1994 state's programme is morally admirable. It aspires to uplift all South Africans within a framework of constitutional rights, democratic accountability, and non-racialism.

But moral admiration does not compensate for analytical failure. The Carnegie Commission was morally wrong and analytically right — it identified a systemic problem, diagnosed it with rigour, and designed an integrated institutional response. The post-1994 state is morally right and analytically incomplete — it has identified a systemic problem, diagnosed its components in isolation, and designed a fragmented institutional response. The moral superiority of the post-1994 project does not excuse its analytical deficiency. If anything, the moral imperative — the obligation to dismantle the structural poverty that apartheid created and democracy promised to address — makes the analytical failure more, not less, consequential.

## What a Named Problem Would Look Like

If South Africa were to apply the Carnegie Commission's analytical method — stripped of its racial exclusivity but retaining its diagnostic rigour — to the structural poverty of the black majority, what would it look like?

**A bounded definition.** The "Poor Black Problem" would need to be defined with the same precision the Carnegie Commission brought to the Poor White Problem: not all poverty, not all inequality, but the specific, structural, intergenerational poverty that affects approximately 25-30 million black South Africans and that is reproduced across generations through identifiable mechanisms. The definition would distinguish between transient poverty (a middle-class family temporarily impoverished by job loss) and structural poverty (a family that has been poor for three or more generations and whose children face statistically negligible prospects of escaping poverty through existing institutional pathways).

**A multi-dimensional diagnosis.** Following the Carnegie model, the diagnosis would cover at minimum five dimensions: economic (the specific labour market, asset ownership, and income patterns that produce and reproduce black poverty), educational (the specific school-level failures that prevent poor black children from acquiring capabilities), health (the specific disease burdens and healthcare access failures that reduce capabilities and productivity), psychological (the specific effects of persistent deprivation on aspiration, agency, and time horizons), and sociological (the specific community-level dynamics — family structure, social capital, violence, substance abuse — that either ameliorate or exacerbate individual deprivation).

**An integrated institutional response.** The response would not be 12 departments each running their own anti-poverty programmes. It would be a single institutional framework — call it a Commission, an Authority, or a dedicated delivery unit within the Presidency — with the mandate, budget, and accountability to coordinate the full spectrum of anti-poverty interventions for defined geographic areas and population groups, measured against defined outcomes within a defined timeframe.

**Measurable targets and accountability.** The programme would set specific, time-bound targets: reduce the number of black South Africans below the food poverty line from 18.2 million to X by year Y. Increase the proportion of black Grade 4 learners reading for meaning from 19 per cent to Z by year W. Reduce the life expectancy gap between black and white South Africans from Q years to R years by date S. Each target would be assigned to a specific institution with the authority and resources to achieve it, and failure to achieve it would have defined consequences.

**Sustained political commitment.** The Carnegie Commission's response was sustained over approximately four decades — from the 1930s to the 1970s — with consistent political support across changes of government. The post-1994 state has not sustained any anti-poverty framework for more than a decade. The RDP was replaced by GEAR, which was replaced by ASGISA, which was replaced by the NDP, which has been replaced by — it is not entirely clear what. Each framework is announced with fanfare, implemented with diminishing enthusiasm, and quietly abandoned when the next administration arrives with its own vision document. The Poor Black Problem cannot be solved in a single electoral cycle. It requires a multi-generational commitment of the kind that the apartheid state, for all its moral bankruptcy, demonstrated was possible.

## The Development Lesson

This article has drawn a comparison that some readers will find uncomfortable. The apartheid state's upliftment of poor whites was achieved through racial exclusion, labour exploitation, and the systematic dispossession of the black majority. It is not a model to emulate. It is a case study to learn from — specifically, to learn what institutional characteristics distinguish effective development interventions from ineffective ones, regardless of the moral framework within which they operate.

The institutional characteristics are clear: naming the problem creates political accountability. Diagnosing it as a system prevents fragmented responses. Designing integrated interventions ensures that each component reinforces the others. Setting measurable targets enables course correction. Sustaining political commitment across electoral cycles allows compounding effects to take hold.

None of these characteristics is morally loaded. They are institutional design principles that can be deployed in the service of racial exclusion (as the apartheid state demonstrated) or racial inclusion (as the post-1994 state has yet to demonstrate at comparable scale and coherence). The failure is not moral — the post-1994 state's intentions are immeasurably superior to those of its predecessor. The failure is institutional: the inability to convert superior intentions into institutional arrangements with the diagnostic precision, integration, and sustained commitment that the problem demands.

South Africa does not lack the analytical capacity to diagnose the Poor Black Problem. It has some of the finest development economists, sociologists, and public health researchers in the world. It does not lack the fiscal resources — it already spends over R250 billion annually on social grants alone, plus hundreds of billions more on education, health, and housing. It does not lack the constitutional framework — the Bill of Rights enshrines socioeconomic rights that provide both the mandate and the legal basis for an integrated anti-poverty programme. What it lacks is the political and institutional will to *name* the problem with the same unflinching specificity the Carnegie Commission brought to the Poor White Problem, to *diagnose* it with the same multi-dimensional rigour, to *design* an integrated response with the same institutional coherence, and to *sustain* that response with the same multi-generational commitment.

The Human Development Index, as explored in [Beyond GDP](/articles/hd-1-1-beyond-gdp), measures three dimensions of development: health, education, and standard of living. South Africa's HDI of 0.713 — "high human development" — is a national average that masks a racial distribution so skewed that it constitutes two countries sharing one flag. If black South Africa were measured as a separate country, its HDI would place it firmly in the "medium" to "low" human development category. If white South Africa were measured separately, its HDI would place it among developed nations. The aggregate figure — 0.713 — is not a description of South African reality. It is an arithmetic artefact that conceals the most important fact about South African development: that the country has not one development challenge but two entirely different development realities, and the one affecting the majority has not been named, diagnosed, or addressed with anything approaching the institutional determination that the other — the smaller one, the one affecting a racial minority — received nearly a century ago.

The problem that has no name is the central problem of South African development. Until it is named — not as "poverty" in general, not as "inequality" in the abstract, but as the specific, structural, intergenerational deprivation of the black majority with specific causes, specific dimensions, and specific institutional remedies — it will continue to be managed rather than solved. And managed problems do not go away. They compound.

:::key-takeaway
The Carnegie Commission named the "Poor White Problem," diagnosed it across five dimensions, and built an integrated state response that eliminated white poverty within a generation. Post-1994 South Africa confronts a structurally identical phenomenon 100 times larger — the persistent poverty of the black majority — but has never named it, never produced a comparable integrated diagnosis, and never built a comparable institutional response. The asymmetry is not about moral intent. It is about institutional design: naming creates accountability, integration beats fragmentation, and political spending without institutional coherence produces outputs, not outcomes.
:::

:::expert
**Betty Friedan** titled her 1963 book *The Feminine Mystique* by identifying "the problem that has no name" — the systematic oppression of women that was so pervasive it had become invisible. Her argument was that naming the problem was the precondition for solving it. South Africa's structural black poverty is a problem that has no name — not because it is invisible, but because naming it with the specificity required for institutional action has proven politically and intellectually more difficult than leaving it as a diffuse, general, and ultimately unaccountable aspiration called "a better life for all."
:::
