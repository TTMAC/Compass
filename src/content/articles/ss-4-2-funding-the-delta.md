## The Cost of Failure Exceeds the Cost of Reform

Every conversation about criminal justice reform in South Africa eventually arrives at the same objection: "We can't afford it." Treasury officials invoke fiscal constraints. Politicians point to competing priorities — health, education, social grants, infrastructure. The national debt-to-GDP ratio is cited. The constrained fiscal envelope is lamented. And the conversation ends, as it has ended for two decades, with the implicit conclusion that reform is a luxury South Africa cannot afford.

## The Wrong Question

This is the wrong question. The right question is not "can we afford reform?" The right question is "can we afford to keep paying for failure?"

Because the status quo is not free. South Africa's dysfunctional criminal justice system imposes enormous economic costs — costs that are diffuse, often invisible in the national budget, but collectively dwarf the investment required for structural reform. Lost foreign direct investment because crime makes South Africa a riskier capital destination. Tourism depression because visitors read crime statistics before they book flights. The health system burden of gender-based violence — emergency room visits, long-term care, lost productivity. The economic deadweight of a 40 per cent recidivism cycle in which the state pays to arrest, prosecute, and incarcerate the same people over and over. A private security industry that has grown into a R70 billion annual shadow tax on citizens — money they pay because the state cannot keep them safe. And the human capital destruction of a million young South Africans who cycle through a corrections system that makes them less employable than they entered.

This article puts numbers to these costs. It examines what reform would actually cost, where the money would come from, and why — when you do the maths — the status quo is more expensive than transformation. As [Following the Money](/articles/2-1-following-the-money) explains in the Government Structure pillar, understanding where public money flows is the first step to holding government accountable. This article applies that principle to the criminal justice cluster specifically.

## The Current Cost Baseline

South Africa's criminal justice system is funded through four main budget votes in the national budget: the South African Police Service (SAPS), the Department of Justice and Constitutional Development (DoJ&CD), the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA, funded through the DoJ&CD vote), and the Department of Correctional Services (DCS). Together, these constitute the Justice, Crime Prevention, and Security (JCPS) cluster.

In the 2024/25 financial year, the combined allocation to the JCPS cluster was approximately R230 billion. SAPS alone accounts for roughly R110 billion — nearly half the cluster's budget — making it one of the largest line items in the national budget after social grants, education, and health. The NPA receives approximately R5 billion (through the DoJ&CD vote), a figure that has barely kept pace with inflation despite the NPA's expanding mandate. DCS receives approximately R28 billion, the vast majority of which goes to incarceration (facility operations, staffing, prisoner maintenance) rather than rehabilitation programming.

The trend line tells a clear story. In real terms — adjusted for inflation — per capita spending on criminal justice has declined over the past decade. SAPS's budget has grown in nominal terms but shrunk relative to population growth and inflation. The NPA's budget has been squeezed consistently, with real-terms cuts masked by nominal increases. DCS's budget has been consumed by the rising cost of incarceration — more prisoners, higher maintenance costs, staff wage increases — leaving progressively less for the rehabilitation mandate that the Correctional Services Act requires.

The allocation within each department is equally revealing. Within SAPS, the Detective Service — the component responsible for investigation quality, which [Sequencing Reform](/articles/ss-4-1-sequencing-reform) identifies as the critical first reform — receives a fraction of the total budget. Visible Policing (patrol, sector policing, crowd management) and Administration (headquarters, human resources, supply chain) absorb the majority. The system spends most of its money on presence and administration, not on the investigative function that determines whether crime is actually converted into consequences.

Within DCS, incarceration operations consume over 70 per cent of the budget. Rehabilitation, development, and care programmes — the activities that the White Paper on Corrections identifies as the system's primary purpose — receive a residual allocation. The system's spending reveals its actual priorities, regardless of what policy documents say.

The allocation patterns are self-reinforcing. Because investigation is underfunded, cases are poorly investigated, leading to low prosecution rates, which reduces deterrence, which increases crime, which increases the demand for visible policing — and the budget cycle responds by allocating more to visible policing rather than to the investigation function whose underfunding created the problem. Because rehabilitation is underfunded, recidivism is high, which increases the prison population, which increases incarceration costs, which consumes more of the DCS budget — leaving even less for the rehabilitation function whose underfunding drove the recidivism. These are fiscal vicious cycles. Breaking them requires the political will to shift allocations toward the functions that address root causes rather than the functions that manage symptoms.

:::takeaway
South Africa spends approximately R230 billion annually on criminal justice. The allocation is overwhelmingly skewed toward containment (incarceration), presence (visible policing), and administration — with investigation, prosecution, rehabilitation, and reintegration chronically underfunded. The budget reveals a system designed to react to crime and warehouse offenders, not to prevent crime, convert investigation into prosecution, or transform offenders into productive citizens.
:::

## The Cost of the Status Quo

Now consider what the dysfunctional system costs — not in budget allocations, but in economic damage.

**The Recidivism Tax.** As [Rehabilitation as Constitutional Mandate](/articles/ss-3-5-rehabilitation-as-constitutional-mandate) documented, South Africa's recidivism rate exceeds 40 per cent. Of approximately 100,000 inmates released annually, at least 40,000 will be re-arrested, re-prosecuted, and re-incarcerated. The cost of incarcerating one person for one year is approximately R130,000 to R150,000 (DCS budget divided by inmate population). If the average re-incarceration period is three years, the direct fiscal cost of one recidivism event is approximately R400,000 to R450,000 — before adding the policing cost of re-arrest, the prosecutorial cost of re-trial, the court cost of re-adjudication, and the social cost to the offender's family and community. At 40,000 recidivism events per year, the annual fiscal cost of the recidivism cycle is conservatively R16 billion to R18 billion. This is money the state spends because the corrections system does not rehabilitate — it is the price of warehousing.

Reducing the recidivism rate from 40 per cent to 25 per cent — still higher than international benchmarks of 15 to 20 per cent achieved in Scandinavian countries — would eliminate approximately 15,000 re-incarcerations per year, saving R6 billion to R7 billion annually in direct fiscal costs alone. That saving exceeds the total investment required for comprehensive rehabilitation programming.

**The Crime Tax on GDP.** Crime imposes costs on the economy that are difficult to measure precisely but directionally enormous. The World Bank has estimated that violence costs South Africa between 10 and 14 per cent of GDP annually — a figure that encompasses direct costs (property damage, medical treatment, criminal justice system operations), indirect costs (lost productivity, reduced investment, increased insurance premiums), and opportunity costs (economic activity that does not occur because of crime risk). Even the lower bound of this estimate — 10 per cent of GDP, approximately R700 billion — represents an economic burden that dwarfs the criminal justice budget.

Foreign direct investment is particularly sensitive to crime. International investors assess crime risk as a component of country risk, and South Africa's crime levels — especially violent crime — are a documented deterrent. The exact quantum of FDI foregone because of crime is debatable, but the direction is not. Countries with comparable economic fundamentals but lower crime rates attract more FDI per capita than South Africa. Every rand of investment lost to crime risk is a rand that does not create jobs, does not generate tax revenue, and does not contribute to the economic growth that South Africa desperately needs.

**The Tourism Depression.** South Africa's tourism industry earned approximately R100 billion in international tourist spending in 2023 — but this figure is substantially below its potential. Crime is the single most frequently cited concern among international tourists considering South Africa as a destination. Travel advisories from major source markets (the United Kingdom, Germany, the United States, Australia) prominently feature crime warnings. The tourism industry estimates that crime perceptions depress international arrivals by 15 to 25 per cent relative to what South Africa's natural and cultural attractions would otherwise generate. If the lower bound is correct, crime costs the tourism industry approximately R15 billion to R25 billion annually in foregone revenue — revenue that would support hundreds of thousands of jobs in a sector that is one of South Africa's largest employers.

**The GBV Health Burden.** Gender-based violence, documented in [The System's Most Catastrophic Failure](/articles/ss-2-4-the-systems-most-catastrophic-failure), imposes a health system cost that is rarely quantified but staggering. Emergency room visits, hospitalisation, surgical intervention, long-term physical and psychological care, HIV post-exposure prophylaxis, and chronic condition management for survivors — these costs are borne by the public health system but are not attributed to criminal justice failure in the national budget. The South African Medical Research Council has estimated that intimate partner violence alone costs the health system billions of rands annually. Add sexual violence, child abuse, and other forms of GBV, and the health burden is a significant fraction of the Department of Health's budget — a cost that would decline if the criminal justice system actually deterred and prosecuted gender-based violence.

Lost productivity compounds the health cost. GBV survivors who cannot work — because of physical injury, psychological trauma, or the ongoing threat of violence — represent human capital that the economy loses. The International Monetary Fund has estimated that violence against women reduces labour force participation in affected countries by measurable percentages. In an economy with 30 per cent unemployment, any reduction in labour force participation is an economic loss that South Africa cannot afford.

**The Private Security Shadow Tax.** South Africa's private security industry employs over 2.5 million registered security officers — more than the combined strength of SAPS and the South African National Defence Force. The industry generates revenue exceeding R70 billion annually. This is money that South African households and businesses spend because the state cannot keep them safe. It is, in effect, a private tax levied by state failure.

As [The Private Security Paradox](/articles/ss-2-5-the-private-security-paradox) documented, this expenditure is deeply regressive. Wealthy households and formal businesses can afford armed response, CCTV, electric fencing, and access-controlled estates. Poor households and informal businesses cannot. The private security industry does not replace public policing for the population that needs it most — it supplements it for the population that can afford the premium. Every rand spent on private security is a rand that could have been spent on education, savings, business investment, or consumption — economic activity with multiplier effects that private security spending largely lacks.

:::expert
The Government Technical Advisory Centre (GTAC), which provides analytical support to National Treasury and government departments, has conducted cost-benefit analyses of criminal justice interventions in the South African context. Their analysis of the fiscal dynamics of incarceration versus rehabilitation found that the direct fiscal saving from reducing recidivism — fewer re-arrests, fewer re-prosecutions, fewer re-incarcerations — is only a fraction of the total economic benefit. The larger returns come from the economic contribution of formerly incarcerated persons who are successfully reintegrated: tax revenue from employment, consumer spending that stimulates local economies, reduced social grant dependence, and the avoided social costs of crime victimisation. GTAC's modelling suggests that a rand invested in evidence-based rehabilitation programming generates a return of between R3 and R5 in avoided costs and economic contribution over a ten-year horizon — a return that exceeds virtually any other public investment currently in the government's portfolio. Their analysis has been cited by Treasury officials who support rehabilitation investment but face the political challenge of arguing for increased corrections spending in a constrained fiscal environment.
:::

## The Investment Case

What would reform actually cost? The honest answer is that precise estimates are impossible — the cost depends on implementation choices, procurement efficiency, and institutional factors that cannot be predicted with certainty. But the directional magnitudes are estimable.

**Phase 1 Investment (Years 1–3): R8–12 billion total.** The investigating magistracy pilot requires funding for JIO appointments, training, and operational support — estimated at R500 million to R800 million over three years for a pilot in two to three metropolitan areas. SAPS detective capacity upgrades — ring-fenced recruitment, specialised training, reduced caseloads — require R3 billion to R5 billion over three years (primarily personnel costs). NPA restoration — additional prosecutors, improved conditions, institutional restructuring — requires R2 billion to R3 billion over three years. The digital case management system — procurement, development, deployment, change management — requires R2 billion to R4 billion over three years (with significant risk of cost overruns given the government's IT procurement history).

**Phase 2 Investment (Years 3–5): R6–10 billion total.** Community courts establishment — infrastructure, CJO training and appointment, operational costs — requires R3 billion to R5 billion over two years, offset partially by municipal co-funding. The sentencing commission requires R200 million to R400 million over two years (a relatively inexpensive institutional investment with outsized impact). Three-tier policing pilots require R3 billion to R5 billion over two years, but much of this is reallocation of existing SAPS expenditure rather than net new spending.

**Phase 3 Investment (Years 5–10): R15–25 billion total.** Nationwide three-tier policing rollout is the largest single investment — R8 billion to R15 billion over five years, though again much is reallocation. Rehabilitation-centred corrections — staffing, programmes, infrastructure upgrades, community corrections expansion — requires R5 billion to R8 billion over five years. Full ICJS buildout requires R2 billion to R4 billion.

The total reform investment over a decade is therefore in the range of R30 billion to R47 billion — or R3 billion to R5 billion per year on average.

Set this against the cost of the status quo. The recidivism tax alone costs R16 billion to R18 billion per year. Crime's GDP impact is estimated at R700 billion or more annually. Tourism depression costs R15 billion to R25 billion per year. The private security shadow tax is R70 billion per year. Even if reform investment delivers only a modest reduction in these costs — say, a 10 per cent improvement across the board — the annual economic return exceeds the annual reform investment by an order of magnitude.

The fiscal question is not whether South Africa can afford reform. The fiscal question is whether South Africa can continue to afford the catastrophic economic cost of a broken criminal justice system.

## The Human Capital Destruction

There is one more cost of the status quo that resists easy quantification but may be the most devastating of all: the destruction of human capital through a corrections system that makes people worse.

South Africa's correctional facilities hold over 150,000 people at any given time. Approximately 100,000 are released each year. The majority are young men — the demographic that should be entering the labour force, acquiring skills, building careers, supporting families, and contributing to the economy. Instead, they enter facilities that are overcrowded, violent, gang-dominated, and devoid of meaningful rehabilitation programming. They spend months or years in environments where the primary skills acquired are those needed for survival in a violent institution — and where gang affiliation, substance abuse, and criminal networks are reinforced rather than disrupted.

When they are released, they carry a criminal record into a labour market with 30 per cent unemployment. Employers who have dozens of applicants for every position will not hire someone with a criminal record when they have applicants without one. The released person cannot find work. Without work, they cannot find stable housing. Without housing and employment, they return to the environment and the networks that contributed to their offending. Within months, they are re-arrested. The cycle repeats.

The economic cost of this cycle extends far beyond the direct fiscal cost of re-incarceration. Each person who cycles through the corrections system without being rehabilitated represents decades of lost economic productivity — the wages they will not earn, the taxes they will not pay, the goods and services they will not produce or consume. Their families bear the cost of lost breadwinners — children who grow up in poverty, partners who carry sole economic responsibility, extended families who shoulder additional dependants. Their communities bear the cost of reduced social cohesion, increased crime risk, and the stigma of being associated with a "crime area" that deters investment and employment opportunities.

If even a modest proportion of the 40,000 people who reoffend each year could instead be successfully reintegrated — employed, housed, contributing to their communities — the economic impact would compound over decades. A person who earns R8,000 per month (the lower end of South Africa's urban working middle segment) generates approximately R96,000 in annual income, pays income tax and VAT, supports dependants who then have better educational and health outcomes, and contributes to local economic activity with multiplier effects. Multiply this by thousands of successfully reintegrated individuals, and the economic returns of rehabilitation investment become transformative rather than merely fiscal.

## The Political Economy: Who Benefits from Failure

If the fiscal case for reform is so clear, why has reform not happened? The answer lies in the political economy — the distribution of benefits and costs from the status quo.

The status quo has beneficiaries. Not beneficiaries in the sense that anyone consciously desires a broken criminal justice system, but beneficiaries in the sense that specific interests profit from — or are protected by — the current dysfunction.

**Politically connected appointments.** The current system concentrates appointment power in the executive. The President appoints the National Commissioner of Police, the National Director of Public Prosecutions, and the Commissioner of Correctional Services. These appointments have been used — across multiple administrations — as instruments of political patronage and, in some cases, political protection. A National Commissioner who owes their appointment to the President is less likely to pursue investigations that threaten the President's allies. An NDPP who serves at the President's pleasure is more vulnerable to political pressure than one who has fixed-term, parliamentary-confirmed independence. Reform that strengthens institutional independence — as the NPA Act amendments in [Sequencing Reform](/articles/ss-4-1-sequencing-reform) propose — threatens this political control. Those who benefit from the current appointment system will resist changes that reduce their influence.

**Procurement networks.** The criminal justice cluster spends hundreds of billions of rands on procurement — vehicles, equipment, technology, facilities, uniforms, food services, maintenance. Where procurement systems are weak — and in SAPS and DCS they are demonstrably weak, as the Auditor-General's reports consistently document — procurement becomes a source of patronage and corruption. Contracts are awarded to connected suppliers at inflated prices. Reform that improves procurement transparency, strengthens internal controls, and reduces the opportunities for corrupt extraction threatens the networks that profit from the current system.

**Criminal syndicates.** Organised crime groups have a direct interest in an ineffective criminal justice system. When investigation is poor, prosecution is weak, and the probability of conviction for serious crime is low, the operating environment for criminal enterprises is favourable. There is substantial evidence — documented by the Zondo Commission and investigative journalism — that criminal networks have cultivated relationships with individuals within SAPS, particularly within Crime Intelligence, to protect their operations. These relationships are enabled by the current system's opacity and lack of accountability. Reform that improves investigation quality, strengthens prosecution, and increases the probability of conviction for serious crime directly threatens the profitability and safety of criminal enterprise.

**The private security industry.** This is the most counterintuitive beneficiary. The private security industry's growth is driven by public policing failure. If SAPS were effective — if citizens felt safe without private armed response, if businesses did not need private security guards — the R70 billion industry would contract substantially. The industry's business model depends on the state continuing to fail at its core safety function. This does not mean the private security industry actively undermines police reform — many industry leaders genuinely advocate for improved public policing. But the structural incentive is clear: the industry's growth rate is inversely correlated with public policing effectiveness.

**Fear as political currency.** Crime fear is politically useful. Politicians who campaign on "tough on crime" platforms benefit from a public that feels unsafe — because frightened voters respond to authoritarian promises (more police, harsher sentences, military-style operations) rather than the patient, structural reform that would actually improve outcomes. The political incentive structure rewards performative toughness — visible police deployments, dramatic arrests, crackdown operations — over the less visible but more effective investments in detective capacity, prosecution quality, and rehabilitation. Reform that shifts resources from performative policing to effective policing threatens politicians who have built their brands on security theatre.

:::expert
Gareth Newham, head of the Justice and Violence Prevention programme at the Institute for Security Studies, has written extensively on the political economy of policing in South Africa. His research has documented the pattern across multiple administrations: incoming police ministers and national commissioners announce "turnaround strategies" and "back to basics" programmes that promise visible results — increased deployments, higher arrest numbers, crackdown operations — but avoid the structural reforms that would address the underlying dysfunction. Newham has argued that this pattern is not a failure of knowledge (the reforms needed are well understood) but a failure of political incentives: structural reform is slow, invisible to voters, and threatens vested interests, while performative policing is fast, visible, and politically rewarding. Breaking this cycle, Newham argues, requires changing the accountability framework — specifically, measuring police performance by investigation and prosecution outcomes rather than by arrest numbers and deployment statistics, and holding political leaders accountable for systemic improvement rather than for security theatre. The scorecard proposed in [Measuring Progress](/articles/ss-4-3-measuring-progress) is designed to enable precisely this shift in accountability.
:::

## The Fiscal Pathway: Where Would the Money Come From?

Acknowledging the political economy obstacles, and accepting that reform requires R3 billion to R5 billion per year in additional investment over a decade, the practical question remains: where does the money come from in a constrained fiscal environment?

Four sources.

**Reallocation within existing budgets.** The criminal justice cluster already spends R230 billion per year. The problem is not the total amount — it is the allocation. The system overspends on containment and administration and underspends on investigation, prosecution, rehabilitation, and reintegration. Reallocation does not require new money — it requires different choices. Moving R2 billion from SAPS administrative overhead to detective capacity, or R1 billion from DCS incarceration operations to rehabilitation programming, changes outcomes without changing the budget envelope. Reallocation is politically difficult (existing budget lines have constituencies that defend them) but fiscally neutral.

**Efficiency gains from technology.** The digital case management system and eventual ICJS will generate efficiency savings. Virtual remand hearings reduce prisoner transport costs (currently a significant DCS expense) and court scheduling inefficiency. Electronic docket management reduces the administrative burden on detectives and prosecutors, allowing them to handle more cases with the same capacity. AI-assisted court scheduling reduces postponements and the costs associated with them. These savings are difficult to quantify precisely in advance, but international experience (the UK's court digitalisation, Estonia's e-governance, Rwanda's case management modernisation) suggests that well-implemented justice technology generates efficiency savings of 10 to 20 per cent within five years — which, applied to South Africa's R230 billion cluster budget, would be R23 billion to R46 billion per year.

**Recidivism reduction savings.** Every percentage point reduction in recidivism saves the system money — fewer arrests, fewer prosecutions, fewer incarcerations. At current costs, reducing recidivism from 40 per cent to 25 per cent would save R6 billion to R7 billion per year in direct fiscal costs. These savings materialise in years three to seven as rehabilitation investments begin to produce results — creating a fiscal space for further investment in later phases.

**The economic growth dividend.** This is the most important but least direct source. A more effective criminal justice system — one that deters crime, protects property rights, and enforces contracts — is a precondition for higher economic growth. Higher growth generates higher tax revenue. The precise relationship between criminal justice improvement and GDP growth is debatable, but the directional relationship is not. Countries that successfully reformed their criminal justice systems — Colombia, Georgia, Rwanda — experienced measurable improvements in economic growth, FDI, and government revenue. If criminal justice reform contributes even one additional percentage point of GDP growth (conservative given the 10 to 14 per cent of GDP currently lost to violence), the additional tax revenue generated would exceed the reform investment by a substantial margin.

**International comparators reinforce the pathway.** Colombia's criminal justice reform programme in the 2000s was funded primarily through budget reallocation and efficiency gains — the total reform investment represented less than 2 per cent of the existing justice sector budget, yet produced measurable improvements in homicide rates, conviction rates, and investor confidence. Georgia's police reform in 2004–2008 cost approximately $200 million over four years — a fraction of the economic cost of the corruption and crime it addressed — and contributed to a dramatic improvement in the country's business environment ranking and FDI inflows. Rwanda's post-genocide justice system reconstruction, while operating in a vastly different context, demonstrated that a phased approach to technology investment (starting with simple electronic case management and scaling to integrated systems) could be achieved at a fraction of the cost that traditional IT procurement estimates suggested. None of these comparators is directly analogous to South Africa — the context, scale, and institutional starting points differ. But they demonstrate that the fiscal pathway from reform investment to economic return is real, not theoretical.

The fiscal pathway is not a fantasy. It requires political courage to reallocate within existing budgets, technical competence to implement technology systems, patience to wait for recidivism savings to materialise, and economic governance that connects criminal justice improvement to growth. None of these is easy. All of them are achievable.

## The Budget Watchdog Guide

If you want to hold government accountable for criminal justice spending — and for reform investment specifically — you need to be able to read the budget. This guide shows you how.

:::framework
**The Budget Watchdog Guide**

**Where to Find Criminal Justice Budgets:**
The National Treasury publishes the Estimates of National Expenditure (ENE) annually, usually in February alongside the Budget Speech. The ENE is available on Treasury's website (www.treasury.gov.za) and provides detailed programme-level allocations for every government department, including SAPS, DoJ&CD, NPA, and DCS. The Medium Term Budget Policy Statement (MTBPS), published in October, provides updated allocations and forward estimates.

**What to Look For in SAPS:**
Programme 1 (Administration) — the overhead cost of running the organisation. Programme 2 (Visible Policing) — patrol, sector policing, crowd management. Programme 3 (Detective Services) — the investigation function. Programme 4 (Crime Intelligence). Programme 5 (Protection and Security Services). The key ratio to watch: what proportion of the total SAPS budget goes to Programme 3 (Detective Services)? If reform is being taken seriously, this proportion should be increasing. If it is static or declining, the system is continuing to prioritise presence over performance.

**What to Look For in the NPA:**
The NPA's budget is within the DoJ&CD vote. Look for the NPA sub-programme allocation and compare it year-on-year in real terms (adjusting for inflation using the CPI). The NPA has been subjected to real-terms budget cuts for years while its mandate has expanded. If reform is being funded, the NPA allocation should be growing faster than inflation.

**What to Look For in DCS:**
Programme 1 (Administration). Programme 2 (Incarceration) — facility operations, prisoner maintenance. Programme 3 (Rehabilitation) — educational, vocational, social, and psychological programmes. Programme 4 (Care) — health, nutritional, and hygiene services. Programme 5 (Social Reintegration) — parole, community corrections. The key ratio: what proportion goes to Programme 3 (Rehabilitation) and Programme 5 (Social Reintegration) versus Programme 2 (Incarceration)? If the corrections system is transitioning from warehousing to rehabilitation, Programmes 3 and 5 should be growing as a share of the total.

**How to Read Budget Trends:**
Always adjust for inflation. A budget that grows by 4 per cent in a year when inflation is 5 per cent has been cut in real terms. Treasury often presents nominal increases that mask real decreases — this is how budget cuts are made invisible. Use the CPI (available from Stats SA at www.statssa.gov.za) to calculate real-terms changes.

**How to Track Reform Investment:**
Once specific reform Bills are passed (as tracked in the Legislative Tracker in [Sequencing Reform](/articles/ss-4-1-sequencing-reform)), look for the corresponding budget allocations in the ENE. If a Bill has been passed but no money has been allocated for implementation, the reform is dead on paper — passed but unfunded. This is one of the most common failure modes in South African governance: legislation that creates obligations without budget provision.

**Where to Find Audit Results:**
The Auditor-General of South Africa (AGSA) audits every government department annually. AGSA's reports (available at www.agsa.co.za) reveal whether allocated budgets have been spent, whether spending was lawful, and whether expenditure achieved its intended purpose. Look specifically at the criminal justice cluster departments' audit outcomes and the AGSA's findings on irregular, fruitless, and wasteful expenditure. These findings tell you whether reform money — if allocated — is being used as intended.
:::

The fiscal case for criminal justice reform is clear. The investment required is a fraction of the cost the status quo imposes on the economy. The political obstacles are real but not insurmountable. The fiscal pathway — reallocation, technology efficiency, recidivism savings, growth dividends — provides a credible funding model. The remaining question is measurement: how would you know if reform investment is producing results? That is the subject of [Measuring Progress](/articles/ss-4-3-measuring-progress), the final article in this series.
