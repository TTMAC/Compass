## R49.9 Trillion in Assets, R150 Billion Short

South Africa's total financial assets reached R49.9 trillion in 2021 — approximately eight times GDP. The country has the 16th largest pool of pension assets in the world, a sophisticated banking sector, and a deep capital market. Yet investment in Gross Fixed Capital Formation (GFCF) — the physical assets on which any economy depends: infrastructure, factories, equipment, buildings — has averaged around 15 per cent of GDP since 1994, half the 30 per cent target set by the National Development Plan (NDP). It has declined dramatically since 2014. The infrastructure funding gap alone — across water, energy, and digital infrastructure — is approximately **R150 billion per year, or R1.5 trillion over ten years.** The World Bank recently estimated a total infrastructure funding gap of R4.8 to R6.2 trillion for *all* infrastructure.

The money exists. The investment does not happen. Understanding why requires looking beyond government budgets and into the structure of the entire financial system — the web of interlocking balance sheets through which capital flows between banks, development finance institutions (DFIs), pension funds, shadow banks, state-owned enterprises, the South African Reserve Bank (SARB), and National Treasury.

A landmark 2025 report prepared for the National Planning Commission's Finance Task Team — *The Transformation of South Africa's Monetary Architecture, 1983–2024* by Mark Swilling and Steffen Murau, in collaboration with the Centre for Sustainability Transitions at Stellenbosch University — maps this architecture across four historical snapshots (1983, 1996, 2014, and 2024). What emerges is a picture of a financial system that has never been reconfigured to serve the developmental goals of the post-apartheid state. Instead, it continues to reproduce patterns of financial exclusion, underinvestment in fixed capital, and extractive capital allocation that prioritise short-term returns and offshore flows over long-term productive investment.

The report introduces the concept of *monetary architecture* — the financial system understood not as a single market but as a complex adaptive system of interlocking balance sheets, where every asset is another institution's liability. This framing reveals why traditional policy levers — changing interest rates, adjusting fiscal ratios, tweaking regulations in isolation — have failed to redirect capital toward infrastructure. No single actor can unilaterally reconfigure the system. What is needed is a coordinated approach to *balance sheet reconfiguration* — identifying the "elasticity spaces" where new flows of capital can be unlocked through negotiated changes in how institutions relate to each other.

## The Investment Gap in Numbers

The NPC's Infrastructure Task Team, working with the DBSA, National Treasury, and the Presidential Climate Commission, calculated the investment requirements for three critical infrastructure sectors through 2035:

- **Water security**: R214 billion per annum, or R2.1 trillion through to 2035, representing the lowest-cost and most ecologically sustainable option
- **Energy security and net zero by 2050**: at least R120 billion to R150 billion per annum
- **Digital transformation**: R40 billion to R50 billion per annum, or R500 billion in total by 2035

The combined requirement for these three sectors alone is **R3.5 trillion to R4 trillion by 2035 — approximately R400 billion per year.** If current investment levels remain constant, approximately R250 billion per annum will be spent on water, energy, and digital infrastructure over the next decade (R2.5 trillion total). The gap is therefore approximately R150 billion per annum, or R1.5 trillion over ten years.

The Cabinet approved the National Infrastructure Plan 2050 in 2022 — South Africa's first long-term strategic investment framework for the country's energy, water, digital, and transport infrastructures. The NPC was mandated to investigate the investment requirements to achieve its goals. The results confirmed the scale of the challenge: the current fiscal and monetary policy arrangements cannot close this gap on their own. If fiscal and monetary policies remain unchanged — as National Treasury's own Macro-Economic Trends Report expects — major increases in infrastructure funding from the fiscus will not be forthcoming. The question becomes: where will the money come from?

## Where the Money Actually Sits

The NPC report's most striking contribution is its mapping of the total assets held across South Africa's financial system. By 2024, these included:

- **Commercial banks**: R6.7 trillion in assets
- **Pension and insurance funds (ASISA members)**: R6.7 trillion
- **Non-financial corporations (listed)**: R12.7 trillion
- **Formal small businesses (unlisted)**: R2.5 trillion
- **Shadow banks**: R3.2 trillion (half the size of the banking sector)
- **Government Employees Pension Fund (GEPF)**: R1.6 trillion (managed by the Public Investment Corporation)
- **State-owned enterprises**: R1.3 trillion
- **SARB**: R1.2 trillion
- **National Revenue Fund (managed by NT)**: R2.1 trillion
- **Development Finance Institutions**: R345 billion (across 45 DFIs, of which 14 hold 97 per cent of assets)
- **Stokvels**: R50 billion (held by 11 million members)
- **Households**: R11 trillion (including offshore wealth)

The mismatch is stark. DFIs — the institutions explicitly mandated to finance development — hold only R345 billion in assets, a fraction of the commercial banking sector. Pension fund assets comprise 53 to 63 per cent of GDP, yet according to a SARB report, less than 10 per cent of pension assets are re-invested in the so-called 'real economy,' and even less flows into infrastructure. The GEPF, with R2.3 trillion in accumulated funds, held virtually no net new investments in government bonds in 2023/24 and has seen its share of outstanding government debt drop from over 35 per cent in 1994 to just 15 per cent by 2024.

The NPC report identifies three systemic failures that explain this mismatch:

1. **The failure to integrate poor households into the financial architecture.** Despite the expansion of basic bank accounts and credit access, low-income households remain structurally excluded from asset accumulation. This perpetuates inequality and undermines economic resilience.

2. **The failure to coordinate investment across public, private, and hybrid balance sheets.** DFIs, SOEs, municipalities, and government departments often operate with misaligned mandates, fragmented project pipelines, and conflicting accountability frameworks. Blended finance remains underutilised, and catalytic investment opportunities available to the private sector go unrealised.

3. **The failure to discipline or direct private capital toward domestic reinvestment.** The corporate sector's shift to global asset markets and the weak regulatory framework around pension fund investment mandates have created an extractive model of capital allocation that prioritises short-term returns and offshore flows over long-term developmental investments in GFCF within South Africa.

## How Banks Turned Away from the Real Economy

By 2024, bank balance sheets had changed in three important respects. First, while lending to the private sector had levelled out due to weak economic growth, lending to government — not SOEs — had been rising steadily in recent years. Second, as deposits by households declined, deposits by non-financial corporations (NFCs) increased, mainly because these NFCs preferred to retain high levels of liquidity rather than invest in GFCF. Third, banks redirected approximately R80 billion to fund nearly 6 gigawatts of rooftop solar between 2021 and 2024 — a productive balance sheet reconfiguration driven by the loadshedding crisis.

Credit extension — loans, overdrafts, and advances — grew from less than R500 billion in 1994 to over R5 trillion by 2023. But the composition changed fundamentally. The significant expansion of credit for mortgages since 1993 reveals the driver of debt-funded consumption-led economic growth since democracy. By 2024, banks were increasing their lending to government while not increasing lending to the NFC sector — the businesses that actually invest in fixed assets and create jobs. Private-sector lending as a share of GDP declined from over 70 per cent in 2008 to approximately 60 per cent in 2024.

The reason is structural. Government debt yields have risen sharply as South Africa's sovereign risk ratings dropped from BBB/BBB- to sub-investment grade (BB/BB-) by 2013. Government bonds do not require capital holdings and carry no default risk for the banks, making them extraordinarily profitable in a high-yield environment. South African banks, with an average return on equity of approximately 20 to 25 per cent — among the most profitable in the world — have consistently preferred consumption-related lending and government securities over extending credit to SMEs, light industry, or infrastructure projects. The 2008 Competition Commission Banking Enquiry confirmed this pattern, finding that banks artificially retained high costs for payment services because of 'oligopolistic' behaviour, and that they benefited from prudential regulations premised on the assumption that banking faces higher risks than it really does in practice.

The bank-led financing of rooftop solar installations stands out as a genuine success story of balance sheet reconfiguration. In response to loadshedding, banks set up new lending facilities to support rooftop solar, even formally accrediting approved installers that their customers were required to use to qualify for loans. Installed rooftop photovoltaic capacity surged from 983 megawatts in March 2022 to 5,790 megawatts by June 2024 — a total investment of approximately R80 billion. This demonstrated that when the right incentive structures exist (tax incentives, customer demand, a clear commercial case), private balance sheets can be rapidly redirected toward productive fixed assets.

:::takeaway
The post-2008 pattern is clear: banks lend to government (profitable, risk-free) and to consumers (mortgages, vehicle finance). They have moved away from lending to businesses that invest in fixed assets or create jobs. The banking sector did well from declining levels of confidence in the economy — as bond yields climbed and investor confidence declined in the wake of state capture, banks shifted from conventional lending to government to investments in government bonds to take advantage of rising bond rates. The rooftop solar exception proves the rule: when incentives align, banks can scale infrastructure lending rapidly.
:::

## The Pension Fund Paradox

South Africa has the 16th largest pool of pension assets in the world — larger than India, Ireland, France, Spain, or Chile. Pension assets comprise 53 to 63 per cent of GDP and are growing faster than the average annual growth rate of disposable income. By 2020, total pension assets had risen from R3.6 trillion in 2014 to R4.3 trillion, averaging 3.5 per cent year-on-year growth. By 2024, 880 'active' registered pension funds were regulated by the Financial Sector Conduct Authority (FSCA), managed by seven major pension fund administrators.

But the regulatory framework governing these assets has evolved in ways that actively work against domestic infrastructure investment.

The critical inflection point was the 2022 amendment to Regulation 28 of the Pensions Act, which raised the aggregate limit for foreign asset exposure from 30 per cent to 45 per cent. The SARB issued an Exchange Control Circular in February 2022 that increased the foreign investment limit for South African institutional investors, and this was formalised with the implementation of Regulation 28 in January 2023. Within months of this reform coming into effect, over R600 billion flowed offshore, with the potential for outflows to reach R2.5 trillion before hitting the new ceiling.

The stated rationale — diversifying portfolios and maximising returns for members — was legitimate on its own terms. Pension funds successfully argued that weak economic growth resulted in limited investment opportunities, thus prejudicing the interests of pension fund members. But the macroeconomic effect has been to deepen the vicious circle that the NPC report identifies: weak economic growth reduces domestic investment opportunities, which pushes pension funds to externalise assets, which further reduces the capital available for growth-catalysing investment, which reinforces weak growth. The solution to this problem — approved by the Minister of Finance in 2022 — exacerbated the problem of low levels of investment in GFCF and thus reinforced weak economic growth rates.

Regulation 28 does allow up to 45 per cent in infrastructure investments across all asset classes. The definition of eligible infrastructure is deliberately broad — utilities, transport, digital, health, education, data centres, and more. But in practice, pension fund managers are reluctant to invest in infrastructure assets because of concerns about governance, project bankability, returns, and liquidity. ASISA members have argued that prescribing infrastructure investment targets would yield below-market returns due to inefficient governance — particularly if SOEs are the primary beneficiaries. The savings and investment industry, with R6.2 trillion in assets as of 2019, had roughly R958 billion placed with banks, R775 billion in government bonds, and R3.3 trillion in listed and unlisted equities — but only R93 billion in property and negligible amounts in direct infrastructure.

The GEPF — by far the largest pension fund, with R2.3 trillion in accumulated funds and reserves, 1.2 million active members, and 450,000 pensioners — requires particular attention. It manages its fund by appointing the Public Investment Corporation (PIC) as its asset manager, which executes its mandate via thirty-nine appointed external asset managers. The PIC's assets under management grew from R0.3 trillion in 2003 to R2.5 trillion in 2022. But the GEPF's investment in government or parastatal securities declined from nearly 100 per cent in 1994 to just 24 per cent by 2023/24. Even more surprising, the GEPF made virtually no further net investments in government bonds in 2023/24 — at a time when key government agencies such as the SOEs were finding it increasingly difficult to source debt from private capital markets. As former GEPF Board member Andrew Donaldson concluded: 'it is hard to avoid the conclusion that the GEPF's investment strategy should be reconsidered.'

The distribution of pensions is also highly unequal. Only 23 per cent of the working-age population were members of pension funds in 2017 (16.9 million people). The bulk of the population over 65 relies on state-provided old age pensions — means-tested income allocations made through the annual budget. Of the total 18.8 million recipients of all state welfare grants in 2023, 3.8 million were old-age grant beneficiaries. The expenditure for old age grants rose from R22 billion to R90 billion over the 2007–2023 period. Significantly, the majority of beneficiaries are women.

## Development Finance Institutions: Undercapitalised and Fragmented

The role of DFIs around the world has changed dramatically since the 2007 financial crisis. During the 15 years after 2007, their collective asset base doubled globally, the total number of DFIs rose to 522, and their total annual investments rose to 10 per cent of total global investment — amounting to USD 2.5 trillion in 2022.

South Africa's DFIs did not follow this global trend. Instead of receiving massive injections of capital from the fiscus to leverage co-funding to accelerate development, they were regarded as self-financing during the 1994 period and therefore dependent on retained earnings, capital markets, and international DFIs to finance the gradual expansion of their investment portfolios. By 2024, there were 45 DFIs and Development Finance Agencies: 12 at national, 16 at provincial, and 17 at local government levels. Of these, only fourteen had assets of at least R1 billion each.

The four largest — the IDC (R159 billion), DBSA (R109 billion), LBK (R35 billion), and Ithala Development Finance Corporation (R8 billion) — account for 97 per cent of total DFI assets. By 2023, the combined asset base of the fourteen largest had grown to R346 billion, equal to nearly 5 per cent of GDP — higher than the average DFI/GDP ratio in Sub-Saharan Africa, but tiny compared to banks at R6.7 trillion and shadow banks at R3.2 trillion.

The NPC report identifies a fundamental structural failure in how DFIs have been positioned within the financial system. Unlike in most other developing countries, South Africa's DFIs are not supervised by the SARB's Prudential Authority. They are governed by policy departments rather than the central bank, which constrains their access to capital markets. They do not hold large contingent liabilities on their balance sheets, nor are they a contingent liability for the SARB as they are in many other developing countries.

The DBSA stands out as the key exception: having avoided state capture, it is self-financing, has a strong balance sheet, enjoys sound credit ratings, consistently declares a profit, always has clean audits, and remains operationally efficient. The IDC survived the Oakbay fiasco and has since then done as well as the DBSA. When the DBSA investigated the implications of migrating from National Treasury to SARB supervision, feedback from capital markets suggested the DBSA's loan book could quadruple from R120 billion in 2023 to approximately R400 billion — **an extra R300 billion unlocked not by policy change but by a balance sheet reconfiguration** that aligns the DBSA's backstop to the SARB rather than the fiscus. Theoretically, using the DBSA experience as a benchmark, a fourfold expansion of all DFIs' balance sheets would increase their collective size from R350 billion to R1.4 trillion without any further capital injections from the fiscus.

Government guarantees to the three largest DFIs (LBK, DBSA, IDC) totalled only R18.5 billion in 2022/23, with actual exposure of just R6 billion — a negligible 2 per cent of total exposure to all national state-owned entities, down from R7.4 billion in March 2021. This level of guarantees does not enable DFI balance sheets to grow. This is an obvious elasticity space where expanded guarantees — or alignment with the SARB — could massively expand DFI lending capacity for infrastructure.

Provincial-level development finance corporations have been plagued by a constant flow of corruption allegations before, during, and after the state capture years. Their balance sheets are negligible and their developmental impact minimal.

:::expert[NPC Finance Task Team — Monetary Architecture Framework]
The NPC's monetary architecture framework draws on the Money View tradition in economics and critical macro-finance. It conceptualises the financial system not as a single market but as a web of interlocking balance sheets that are historically specific and path-dependent. Every asset on one balance sheet is a liability on another; no single actor can unilaterally reconfigure the system. This means traditional approaches — changing interest rates, adjusting fiscal ratios, tweaking regulations in isolation — are unlikely to redirect the deep structural flows of capital that determine where investment goes. What is needed, the report argues, is a macro-financial coordination platform within the state, possibly housed in the Presidency or National Treasury, tasked with tracking, modelling, and governing system-wide balance sheet reconfigurations and flows. The concept of 'elasticity spaces' — points where balance sheet configurations can be expanded through contingent assets and liabilities provided by hierarchically higher institutions — provides a practical entry point for identifying where new flows of capital can be unlocked.
:::

## The Fiscal Trap: National Treasury Under Pressure

National Treasury has been the primary driver of South Africa's macroeconomic policy framework since the 1990s. 'Fiscal consolidation' and 'macro-economic stabilisation' have been its central focus. Its powerful Assets and Liabilities Unit manages the vast array of state-owned institutions, and it has become the organisational linchpin of the entire fiscal ecosystem.

But the long-term goal of fiscal stabilisation has been compromised by the need to increase spending and raise debt levels to deal with the legacy of state capture and the shock effects of the Covid-19 pandemic. Gross public debt ballooned from R627 billion (26 per cent of GDP) in 2008/9 to R4.73 trillion in 2022/23 (71.1 per cent of GDP), resulting in sovereign risk ratings dropping from BBB/BBB- to sub-investment grade (BB/BB-) by 2013. Bailouts of SOEs have cost the fiscus R308.7 billion, of which R220.4 billion went to Eskom alone. The others included SAA (R47.3 billion), Denel (R5.8 billion), SA Express (R2.1 billion), SABC (R3.2 billion), LBK (R8 billion), and SASRIA (R22 billion).

SOE investments in GFCF plummeted from 2014 onwards as state capture set in and capital markets effectively 'redlined' the SOEs. The same downward trend applies to government departments at national and provincial levels. The public sector share of investments in GFCF has dropped to 30–35 per cent of total GFCF, down from a high of 55 per cent in the 1970s. At the same time, the incremental capital-output ratio (ICOR) — the measure of how much economic value each Rand of infrastructure investment generates — more than tripled between the 2000s and the 2010s. Less economic value is being generated for every Rand the state invests in infrastructure.

Since 2014, the effective interest rate on public debt has consistently exceeded the GDP growth rate — and so, too, the bond yield. This means South African debt is now unsustainable under current conditions. The 2024 Budget Review acknowledged that GDP growth has averaged only 0.8 per cent since 2012, 'a rate of economic growth that is insufficient to address high levels of unemployment and poverty.'

The NPC report argues that the over-optimistic growth predictions published in each year's budget speech are thwarted by the need for equity injections into unproductive SOEs and to service unsustainable levels of debt. This makes for the perfect storm at the centre of South Africa's monetary architecture. The NT's *ideal monetary architecture* consists of a well-capitalised SARB, banks with capital to lend, robust NFC balance sheets, households that are not over-indebted, and tight fiscal controls. But this ideal has been compromised beyond repair by state capture, SOE collapse, the pandemic, and rising debt — signalling the unviability of the monetary architecture put in place in the 1990s.

## The NPC's Fourteen Recommendations

The report proposes fourteen specific balance sheet reconfigurations, each targeting a different "elasticity space" where capital flows can be unlocked without requiring changes to monetary or fiscal policy. Together, these aim to unlock at least **R5 trillion** in new investment for GFCF and the Just Transition:

1. **DFI-SARB alignment** — Migrate DFI supervision to the SARB's Prudential Authority to harmonise governance, regularise capital market participation, and potentially expand collective DFI balance sheets to R1.4 trillion
2. **Pension fund reallocation** — Gradually reduce the 45 per cent offshore limit in tandem with expanded infrastructure investment options backed by sovereign guarantees, potentially unlocking a R1 trillion project pipeline
3. **New Guarantee Company** — A Rand-denominated guarantee company co-funded by Treasury and DFIs to unlock R50 billion in infrastructure investment without increasing sovereign debt (the Credit Guarantee Vehicle is already underway)
4. **Infrastructure Fund expansion** — Accelerate the DBSA-led Infrastructure Fund to meet its R1 trillion target, leveraging R100 billion in public finance to secure R900 billion in private investment (as of 2025, R340 billion in projects had been approved)
5. **SOE balance sheet reform** — Diversified shareholder models to leverage R1.3 trillion in SOE balance sheets to attract R650 billion in capital without diluting overall public ownership beyond 60 per cent
6. **Bank sector risk-reward shifts** — Regulatory reforms to redirect even 1 per cent of annual bank lending (~R55 billion) toward productive sectors and SMEs, including reforms that allow banks to invest in intermediaries with relevant SME expertise
7. **Corporate reinvestment mandates** — ESG-aligned reforms with tax incentives for domestic fixed asset expansion by non-financial corporations
8. **SME financing** — Targeted support through green finance, cooperative credit, and public-private schemes such as the Transformation Fund, leveraging household-business linkages
9. **Shadow bank mobilisation** — Channelling R3.2 trillion in shadow bank assets toward GFCF through blended structures (REITs, Credit Guarantee Vehicle-backed vehicles, listed notes), requiring policy certainty and competitive returns
10. **Project-level blended finance** — Build-own-operate-transfer and build-operate-transfer models, potentially raising R80 billion while ensuring fiscal neutrality (an example under consideration is the NTCSA's Independent Transmission Projects programme backed by the R400 billion transmission investment plan)
11. **Building a stable middle class** — Matched savings, cooperative finance, and small formal business support, particularly for women-led households, aligned with spatial and gender equity
12. **Gender equality measures** — Credit access and targeted social services for poor women-led households, expansion of grants, and support for women entrepreneurs to close the economic gender gap
13. **SARB climate role** — Integrating climate risks (estimated R1.8 trillion in stranded assets between 2013 and 2035) into monetary policy and banking supervision, reforming prudential controls to allow banks into the riskier but credit-hungry SME space
14. **GEPF alignment with GFCF targets** — Realigning the GEPF's mandate with the NDP's target to increase investment to 30 per cent of GFCF by funding SOEs, BEE contractors, and domestic productive companies, rebalancing away from dual-listed, offshore-oriented firms

These recommendations are not a prescriptive blueprint. The NPC report emphasises that coercive regulatory interventions that compromise returns for investors will likely reinforce capital flight. Instead, a balance is needed between regulatory interventions that influence the *directionality* of capital investments, non-regulatory interventions to build the project pipeline, and interventions that address institutional collapse at local government and SOE level. Rising levels of investment in GFCF could well create upward inflationary pressures if there is a lack of capacity to effectively deploy this capital. The governance constraint and the financing constraint must be solved simultaneously.

## What This Means for the Growth Strategy

The financing architecture analysis transforms the infrastructure debate. As [Infrastructure Foundations](/articles/eg-2-2-infrastructure-foundations) documents in detail, South Africa's infrastructure crisis is severe across energy, logistics, ports, water, and digital connectivity. The conventional view is that these are purely institutional delivery failures. That diagnosis is correct — but incomplete.

Delivery failures exist within a financial ecosystem that systematically underinvests in productive fixed assets. Banks prefer government bonds. Pension funds send capital offshore. DFIs lack the backstop to scale. Shadow banks accelerate financial flows within the financial sector rather than channelling them to the real economy. SOE balance sheets have been hollowed out by state capture and fiscal bailouts. The corporate sector shifts surpluses offshore rather than reinvesting domestically.

The NPC report does not argue that fixing the financing architecture alone will solve the crisis. It explicitly acknowledges that increasing capital supply without addressing governance constraints — skills shortages, procurement failures, institutional collapse at local government level — will produce inflation rather than infrastructure. But it argues, convincingly, that the financing architecture and the delivery problem must be addressed simultaneously. As the report concludes: effective execution capability does not *precede* implementation; it gets built *in order to* implement. Waiting for a fully capacitated developmental state before reconfiguring the financial system will ensure that neither happens.

The concept of *balance sheet reconfiguration* offers a practical way forward that does not depend on politically unlikely changes to fiscal or monetary policy. It asks: given the current balance sheets, where are the elasticity spaces? Where can new flows of capital be unlocked by changing how institutions relate to each other — through guarantees, supervision changes, regulatory adjustments, and negotiated agreements — rather than through new government spending? The rooftop solar revolution showed this is possible. The question is whether the political economy will allow it to happen at the scale the infrastructure crisis demands.

:::framework
**The "Financing Architecture" Assessment Framework**

When evaluating any infrastructure financing proposal — whether from government, the private sector, or international institutions — use this five-step process to assess whether it addresses the structural financing problem or merely adds another layer of complexity to a broken system.

**Step 1: Identify the balance sheets involved.**

Every infrastructure financing proposal involves at least two balance sheets — the entity providing the capital and the entity receiving it. Ask: whose assets and liabilities change? If the proposal depends entirely on the fiscus (National Treasury's balance sheet), it will face the same constraints that have limited infrastructure investment for two decades. If it involves reconfiguring private or hybrid balance sheets — banks, DFIs, pension funds — it has the potential to unlock additional capital without increasing sovereign debt.

**Step 2: Check for the elasticity space.**

An elasticity space is a point where a balance sheet configuration can be expanded through guarantees, backstops, or regulatory changes provided by a hierarchically higher institution. Ask: is there a contingent asset or liability that could be activated to expand the available capital? The DBSA-to-SARB supervision migration is a textbook example — no new money, but a reconfigured institutional relationship that quadruples lending capacity. If a proposal does not identify a specific elasticity space, it is likely a conventional spending request dressed up in innovative language.

**Step 3: Assess the blended finance quality.**

Not all blended finance is equal. As Mazzucato argues, traditional blended finance rests on the mistaken assumption that if the state steps back, private capital will fill the gap. Mission-oriented blended finance is different — it is directional, aimed at structural transformation, and catalyses productive capacity rather than simply derisking private investments. Ask: does the proposal direct capital toward building tangible productive assets, or does it primarily create financial instruments that generate returns for intermediaries?

**Step 4: Check for the governance precondition.**

The NPC report explicitly warns that increasing capital supply without addressing governance constraints will produce inflation, not infrastructure. Ask: does the proposal include credible mechanisms for project preparation, procurement integrity, and delivery management? If it proposes large new capital flows without addressing who will prepare, procure, and manage the projects, it will fail. The Infrastructure Fund's slow start is a cautionary example — the blended finance concept was sound, but the project preparation pipeline was thin.

**Step 5: Identify the political economy constraint.**

Every balance sheet reconfiguration involves winners and losers. Pension fund managers who earn fees on offshore investments will resist reductions in the foreign asset limit. Banks that profit from government bonds will resist regulatory pressure to lend to SMEs. SOE managers will resist governance reforms that reduce their autonomy. Ask: who benefits from the current configuration, and what is the mechanism for overcoming their resistance? If the proposal ignores political economy, it will join the long list of technically sound ideas that never get implemented.
:::
