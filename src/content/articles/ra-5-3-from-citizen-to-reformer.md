**The Citizen Reform Action Framework**

You have the diagnostic tools. Here is how to turn analysis into action, step by step:

**Step 1: Pick one function and one institution.**

Do not try to reform everything at once. Choose the government function that affects your daily life most — water, electricity, waste, roads, education, healthcare — and identify the specific institution responsible. Use the sphere assignment rules from earlier in this series to confirm you are targeting the right entity.

**Step 2: Run the RAARICLE diagnostic.**

For your chosen function and institution, assess all eight elements: Responsibility (is it clearly assigned?), Accountability (can someone be held answerable?), Authority (do they have the legal power?), Resources (do they have the money?), Information (is performance data available?), Capability (do they have qualified people?), Legitimacy (is there a constitutional or legal mandate?), Enforcement (are there consequences for failure?). Write down your findings for each element.

**Step 3: Gather evidence from public sources.**

Support your diagnosis with data. The key sources are: Auditor-General reports (www.agsa.co.za), National Treasury municipal finance data (municipaldata.treasury.gov.za), departmental annual reports (available on department websites), your municipality's Integrated Development Plan and annual report, and Blue/Green Drop reports for water. You have a constitutional right to this information under Section 32 of the Constitution, enforceable through PAIA requests.

**Step 4: Name the structural problem — not the person.**

Frame your finding as a structural diagnosis, not a personal complaint. Instead of "the mayor is corrupt," say "procurement data is not published, creating structural vulnerability to commercial capture." Instead of "the water department is useless," say "the WSA and WSP functions are conflated, and there are no qualified process controllers." Structural framing is harder to dismiss and survives changes in political leadership.

**Step 5: Propose a specific structural fix.**

Match your diagnosis to a reform proposal. If the problem is conflation, propose separation of functions. If it is a capability gap, propose mandatory professional qualifications. If it is an enforcement failure, propose automatic triggers. Reference the legal pathway — does your fix need a constitutional amendment, legislation, or just regulation? The more specific your proposal, the harder it is to ignore.

**Step 6: Use the channels that exist.**

Submit your analysis through formal channels: ward committee meetings, municipal public participation processes, PAIA requests for information you cannot find, written submissions to parliamentary portfolio committees, or engagement with civil society organisations that do governance work (such as the Public Affairs Research Institute, the Institute for Security Studies, or your local community advice office). Keep records of every submission and every response.

With these six steps, you move from being a frustrated citizen to being an informed reformer — someone who can diagnose structural problems, gather evidence, and propose specific fixes through legitimate channels. That is how governance actually changes.
