## The Bottom Line, Up Front

Gross domestic product tells you what an economy produces. The Human Development Index tells you what that production means for actual human lives — whether people live long enough to see their grandchildren grow up, whether they acquire the knowledge to navigate a complex world, and whether they command the resources to make meaningful choices about how they live. South Africa's gross national income per capita of approximately $13,000 in purchasing power parity terms sounds impressive — it places the country in the World Bank's upper-middle-income bracket and earns an HDI score of 0.741, technically classifying the nation as one of "high human development." But that number is a statistical mirage. When you adjust for inequality — when you ask not what the average South African theoretically commands but what the typical South African actually experiences — the HDI collapses to 0.462, a figure that places the country in the "low human development" category. The gap between those two numbers, 0.741 and 0.462, is not an academic curiosity. It is the quantitative expression of sixty million people's lived reality in a country where the economy produces enough for everyone but distributes it as though designed to ensure that most people receive almost nothing.

This article introduces the conceptual framework that underpins this entire series on human development. It begins with a question that may seem obvious but whose answer has shaped — and distorted — global development policy for seventy years: what do we mean when we say a country is "developing"? If development means growing GDP, then South Africa is a moderate success story. If development means expanding what human beings can actually do and be — live healthy lives, acquire knowledge, participate meaningfully in their communities and economies — then the picture is far grimmer, and far more urgent.

The intellectual architect of this shift in thinking is Amartya Sen, the Indian economist and philosopher who received the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1998 for his contributions to welfare economics and social choice theory. Sen's capabilities approach, developed over three decades of work, does not merely add human considerations to an economic framework. It inverts the entire logic of development. Economic growth is not the goal with human welfare as a pleasant side effect. Human capability expansion is the goal, and economic growth is one of several means to achieve it. That distinction — between means and ends — is the conceptual foundation of everything that follows in this series.

If you have read [The R7,000 Economy](/articles/eg-1-1-the-seven-thousand-rand-economy), you already have the economic baseline: South Africa's GDP per capita, its position on the Economic Complexity Index, and its structural stagnation relative to peer countries. This article adds the human dimension. It asks: even if the economy were growing robustly, would that growth translate into lives worth living for the majority of South Africans? The answer, as the evidence will show, is: not necessarily, and certainly not automatically.

## The GDP Illusion

Gross domestic product was never designed to measure human welfare. This is not a matter of interpretation or political spin — it is a matter of historical record. Simon Kuznets, the economist who developed the national income accounting framework that became GDP in the 1930s and 1940s, explicitly warned the United States Congress in 1934 that "the welfare of a nation can scarcely be inferred from a measurement of national income." Kuznets understood that his creation measured the monetary value of goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given period. It did not measure, and was not intended to measure, whether that production made people's lives better.

The warning went unheeded. In the decades following World War II, GDP growth became the dominant metric by which countries measured their progress and by which international institutions — the World Bank, the International Monetary Fund, development agencies — assessed and ranked nations. The logic was seductively simple: more production means more stuff, more stuff means more consumption, more consumption means better lives. For countries emerging from the devastation of war or the stagnation of colonialism, the appeal was obvious. GDP growth was measurable, comparable across countries, and politically useful — a rising GDP line on a graph was proof that the government was doing something right.

But GDP has blind spots so large that you could drive the entire South African social crisis through them without the indicator noticing.

**What GDP misses: unpaid care work.** South Africa's economy depends fundamentally on millions of hours of unpaid labour — overwhelmingly performed by women — that GDP does not count. According to Statistics South Africa's Time Use Survey, South African women spend an average of 4.5 hours per day on unpaid household and care work, compared to 1.5 hours for men. This labour — cooking, cleaning, caring for children and elderly relatives, fetching water in communities without reliable supply — is the invisible infrastructure upon which the formal economy rests. When a grandmother in Limpopo raises her grandchildren because both parents have died of HIV/AIDS-related illness, her labour enables those children to eventually enter the workforce. GDP counts their future wages. It does not count her years of care.

**What GDP misses: environmental degradation.** When a platinum mine in the Bushveld Complex extracts ore, processes it, and exports it, GDP records the value of the platinum sold. It does not subtract the cost of the acid mine drainage that poisons the groundwater that communities downstream depend on for drinking water. It does not subtract the health costs borne by mineworkers who develop silicosis or tuberculosis. It does not subtract the long-term rehabilitation costs of the land left scarred by extraction. South Africa's mining sector contributed approximately 8% of GDP in recent years, but no one has comprehensively calculated the environmental and health liabilities that mining has created over a century and a half. GDP counts the benefit. It externalises the cost.

**What GDP misses: inequality.** This is the blind spot most relevant to South Africa. GDP per capita is an average — total production divided by total population. In a country with the world's highest Gini coefficient (0.63 by consumption, higher by income), the average is a fiction that describes almost no one's actual experience. Consider: South Africa's GDP per capita of approximately $7,000 in nominal terms (or $13,000 in purchasing power parity terms) suggests a middle-income standard of living. But the top 10% of earners capture approximately 65% of all income, while the bottom 50% capture less than 6%. The "average" is a mathematical artefact of extreme wealth at the top dragging the mean far above the median. For the majority of South Africans, the lived economic reality is closer to that of citizens in low-income countries, regardless of what the national average suggests.

**What GDP misses: the quality of what is produced.** GDP counts spending, not outcomes. When South Africa spends R400 billion annually on education, that expenditure adds to GDP regardless of whether learners can actually read. When the state spends billions on healthcare, GDP records the spending even if patients wait twelve hours in emergency rooms and leave without treatment. GDP is agnostic about quality — it measures the resources going in, not the capabilities coming out.

**The Easterlin Paradox and South Africa.** In 1974, the economist Richard Easterlin published research showing that beyond a certain income threshold, increases in GDP per capita do not reliably increase reported life satisfaction. This finding, confirmed repeatedly across countries and time periods, suggests that once basic material needs are met, further economic growth matters less for human wellbeing than the distribution of that growth, the quality of social relationships, the security of one's environment, and the sense of agency over one's own life. South Africa offers a particularly stark illustration. The country's GDP grew in real terms between 1994 and 2008, yet survey data from the South African Social Attitudes Survey and Afrobarometer consistently showed that life satisfaction among the majority of the population did not improve commensurately. Growth was occurring, but it was not translating into felt improvements for most people. The Easterlin Paradox was not just a theoretical proposition in South Africa. It was lived experience.

:::takeaway
GDP measures monetary production, not human welfare. It misses unpaid care work, environmental degradation, inequality, and the quality of what is produced. In South Africa, where the Gini coefficient is 0.63 and the top 10% capture 65% of income, GDP per capita is a statistical fiction that describes almost nobody's actual life. A different measure is needed — one that starts with human beings rather than monetary flows.
:::

## Amartya Sen and the Capabilities Revolution

The intellectual revolution that would eventually produce the Human Development Index began not in an economics department but at the intersection of economics, philosophy, and a deeply personal confrontation with human suffering.

Amartya Sen was born in 1933 in Santiniketan, Bengal, in what was then British India. As a nine-year-old, he witnessed the Bengal Famine of 1943 — a catastrophe that killed an estimated three million people not because there was insufficient food in Bengal, but because the food that existed was allocated by market mechanisms that priced the poorest out of survival. Wartime inflation, speculative hoarding, and the colonial government's refusal to declare a famine (which would have triggered mandatory relief operations) combined to create a situation in which people starved to death surrounded by adequate food stocks. Sen would later write that the famine taught him two things: first, that deprivation could be caused by the structure of entitlements and institutions rather than by physical scarcity; and second, that the way you measure a problem determines which solutions you consider.

These insights shaped a career that would fundamentally reorient development economics. In his 1981 book *Poverty and Famines: An Essay on Entitlement and Deprivation*, Sen demonstrated that every major famine of the twentieth century occurred without an absolute decline in food availability. Famines were caused not by a lack of food but by a lack of entitlements — people's ability to command food through legal, economic, and social mechanisms. A fisherman in Bengal had no entitlement to the rice in the merchant's warehouse, even if there was plenty of it. His entitlement depended on his ability to sell fish and use the proceeds to buy rice. When wartime disruption collapsed the price of fish while inflating the price of rice, his entitlement set shrank to zero. He starved. The rice remained.

This analysis had revolutionary implications for how economists thought about poverty and deprivation. The conventional view was that poverty was a matter of insufficient resources — the solution was to produce more. Sen showed that poverty was a matter of insufficient capabilities — the solution was to expand what people could actually do and be, which required attention not just to production but to distribution, institutions, rights, and social arrangements.

Through the 1980s and 1990s, Sen developed these insights into a comprehensive philosophical framework: the capabilities approach. The framework distinguishes between two concepts that everyday language often conflates. **Functionings** are the things a person actually does and is — being well-nourished, being educated, participating in community life, having self-respect. **Capabilities** are the real freedoms a person has to achieve those functionings — not just whether they are well-nourished, but whether they have the genuine opportunity to be well-nourished. The distinction matters because it centres freedom and agency. A person who chooses to fast has the capability of being well-nourished but has chosen a different functioning. A person who starves because they cannot afford food lacks the capability entirely. Their outcomes may look similar on a nutrition survey, but their situations are fundamentally different.

The capabilities approach redefines development. Development is not an increase in GDP. Development is not an increase in consumption. Development is the expansion of the substantive freedoms — the capabilities — that people have reason to value. Economic growth may contribute to capability expansion, but it is neither necessary nor sufficient. A country can grow its GDP while leaving millions of its citizens without the capability to live past sixty, to read a newspaper, to travel safely from their home to their workplace, or to participate meaningfully in the political decisions that affect their lives. Sound familiar?

Sen's framework resonated powerfully with the Pakistani economist Mahbub ul Haq, who had served as Finance Minister of Pakistan and had grown disillusioned with the dominant GDP-centric approach to development. Ul Haq famously asked: "Why should we be so obsessed with production when the real issue is people?" In the late 1980s, ul Haq recruited Sen to help design a new measure of development — one that would start from human beings rather than from economic aggregates. The result was the Human Development Index, first published in the United Nations Development Programme's Human Development Report of 1990.

:::expert-perspective
**Amartya Sen**, Nobel Laureate in Economics (1998): "Human development, as an approach, is concerned with what I take to be the basic development idea: namely, advancing the richness of human life, rather than the richness of the economy in which human beings live, which is only a part of it." This distinction — between the richness of human life and the richness of the economy — is the conceptual key to understanding why South Africa's GDP performance and its citizens' lived experience diverge so sharply.
:::

The capabilities approach has a particular resonance in South Africa. The South African Constitution of 1996 is, in its Bill of Rights, essentially a capabilities document. Section 26 (housing), Section 27 (healthcare, food, water, social security), Section 28 (children's rights), Section 29 (education) — these are not merely lists of aspirational goals. They are constitutional guarantees of the capabilities that the state is obligated to progressively realise. When the Constitutional Court ruled in *Government of the Republic of South Africa v Grootboom* (2000) that the state had a constitutional obligation to provide for people in desperate need of housing, it was applying — whether or not the justices used the terminology — a capabilities logic. The question was not whether the economy was growing. The question was whether specific human beings had the capability to access adequate shelter. The capabilities approach gives South Africans a framework for evaluating their government that goes far beyond "is GDP going up?"

## The Human Development Index: Anatomy of a Measure

The Human Development Index is built on three dimensions, each intended to capture a fundamental aspect of what it means to live a decent human life. Understanding how these dimensions are measured, why they were chosen, and what they capture and miss is essential to using the HDI as an analytical tool rather than treating it as a magical number that tells you everything you need to know.

**Dimension One: A long and healthy life.** The indicator is life expectancy at birth. This is not a measure of healthcare quality or access directly — it is an outcome measure that captures the cumulative effect of nutrition, disease environment, healthcare access, environmental quality, violence, and social determinants of health. Life expectancy at birth represents the average number of years a newborn would be expected to live if current age-specific mortality rates were to remain constant throughout their life. In the HDI calculation, life expectancy is normalised using a minimum value of 20 years and a maximum of 85 years. South Africa's life expectancy of approximately 63 years yields a health index of approximately 0.662.

**Dimension Two: Knowledge.** The indicators are mean years of schooling for adults aged 25 and older, and expected years of schooling for children of school-entering age. The use of two indicators — one backward-looking (what adults actually achieved) and one forward-looking (what current children are expected to achieve) — captures both the stock of education in the population and the trajectory. Mean years of schooling is normalised using a minimum of 0 and a maximum of 15. Expected years of schooling is normalised using a minimum of 0 and a maximum of 18. The two sub-indices are combined using an arithmetic mean. South Africa's mean years of schooling of 11.3 and expected years of 14.0 yield an education index of approximately 0.720.

**Dimension Three: A decent standard of living.** The indicator is gross national income per capita in purchasing power parity (PPP) dollars, using a natural logarithm transformation. The logarithm is crucial and deliberate — it reflects the principle of diminishing marginal utility of income. The difference between $1,000 and $2,000 per year matters enormously for human wellbeing; the difference between $50,000 and $51,000 matters relatively little. The log transformation ensures that income gains at lower levels contribute more to the index than equivalent gains at higher levels. GNI per capita is normalised using a minimum of $100 and a maximum of $75,000. South Africa's GNI per capita of approximately $12,948 PPP yields an income index of approximately 0.741.

**How the three dimensions combine.** The HDI is calculated as the geometric mean of the three dimension indices: HDI = (Health index x Education index x Income index)^(1/3). The use of the geometric mean rather than the arithmetic mean was introduced in 2010. The geometric mean has an important property: it penalises imbalance across dimensions. A country that scores very high on income but very low on health will have a lower HDI than a country with moderate scores across all three dimensions. This reflects the philosophical conviction that the dimensions are not substitutable — more money does not compensate for dying young.

South Africa's HDI of approximately 0.741 places it in the "high human development" category (0.700–0.799), ranking approximately 110th out of 193 countries. This sounds respectable. It is also, as we shall see, deeply misleading.

## Why These Three Dimensions

Why health, education, and income? Why not security, political freedom, environmental quality, or cultural expression? The choice was deliberate and pragmatic, and understanding the reasoning helps you both appreciate what the HDI captures and remain alert to what it does not.

Mahbub ul Haq, in designing the index, sought dimensions that met four criteria simultaneously. First, they had to be universally valued — no serious moral framework argues that shorter lives, less knowledge, or deeper poverty are desirable. Second, they had to be measurable with data available for virtually every country — an index that can only be computed for forty countries is useless for global comparison. Third, they had to be relatively independent of each other — if all three dimensions measured essentially the same thing, a three-dimensional index would add no information over a one-dimensional measure. Fourth, they had to be sufficiently broad to capture the essence of human development without being so numerous as to make the index unintelligible.

**Health as the foundation.** There is no capability more fundamental than being alive. Life expectancy at birth serves as a summary statistic for a society's overall health environment. It captures not just healthcare access but nutrition, sanitation, violence levels, environmental quality, and the social determinants of health. A country where people die in their fifties is a country where something has gone fundamentally wrong — regardless of its GDP. In South Africa, life expectancy dropped from approximately 62 years in 1990 to approximately 52 years in 2005 at the nadir of the HIV/AIDS epidemic — a decline that would have been considered a national catastrophe in any other context and that the HDI captured with devastating clarity. The subsequent recovery to approximately 63 years, driven by the belated rollout of antiretroviral treatment, is one of the most significant public health achievements in South African history, but it merely restored life expectancy to roughly where it was thirty years ago.

**Education as agency.** Sen's capabilities framework places enormous emphasis on agency — the ability of individuals to make informed choices about their lives, to participate in collective decision-making, and to hold their governments accountable. Education is the primary mechanism through which agency is developed. A citizen who can read a government gazette, interpret a budget speech, understand the implications of a proposed law, or evaluate competing political promises has capabilities that an illiterate citizen lacks. The education dimension of the HDI captures this, albeit imperfectly — it measures years of schooling, not the quality of what is learned, a distinction that is particularly important in South Africa, where 11.3 mean years of schooling coexists with functional illiteracy rates that would embarrass countries with far fewer years of formal education.

**Income as means, not ends.** The inclusion of income in the HDI may seem to contradict the whole point of moving "beyond GDP." But Sen and ul Haq were clear: income is included not as a proxy for wellbeing but as a proxy for the command over resources that enables people to achieve functionings that the other two dimensions do not capture. Income enables access to food, shelter, transportation, communication, cultural participation, and countless other elements of a decent life. The log transformation ensures that income is treated as a means with diminishing returns, not as an end that can be accumulated without limit.

**What was deliberately excluded.** The original HDI does not measure political freedom, personal security, environmental sustainability, gender equality, or cultural expression. This is not because these things do not matter — Sen himself has written extensively about the importance of political freedom as both a constitutive and instrumental component of development. It is because adding more dimensions to the headline index would make it less communicable and because, at the time of its creation in 1990, reliable cross-country data on many of these dimensions simply did not exist. The response has been to develop a family of indices — the Inequality-adjusted HDI, the Gender Development Index, the Gender Inequality Index, the Multidimensional Poverty Index — each addressing a dimension that the headline HDI omits. We will examine each of these in detail in the next article.

:::takeaway
The HDI combines health (life expectancy), education (mean and expected years of schooling), and income (GNI per capita PPP, log-transformed) into a single number using a geometric mean. These three dimensions were chosen because they are universally valued, measurable across all countries, and capture the most fundamental capabilities: being alive, being knowledgeable, and having resources to make choices. The geometric mean penalises imbalance — you cannot compensate for dying young by being rich.
:::

## South Africa in the HDI Landscape

South Africa's HDI of 0.741 places it in a peer group that, on paper, looks respectable but that, on closer examination, reveals just how much the headline number conceals.

Consider the comparison with countries at similar income levels. Brazil, another highly unequal upper-middle-income country with a colonial legacy and deep racial stratification, has an HDI of 0.760 — modestly higher than South Africa's, driven primarily by higher life expectancy (76 years versus 63). Mexico, at 0.781, outperforms South Africa across all three dimensions. Turkey, at 0.838, is in a different league — "very high human development" — despite having a GNI per capita only modestly higher than South Africa's. Thailand, at 0.800, achieves more human development than South Africa with substantially less income per capita. These comparisons are instructive because they suggest that South Africa is not converting its income into human development as effectively as its peers.

The comparison with Thailand is particularly illuminating. Thailand's GNI per capita in PPP terms is approximately $19,000 — higher than South Africa's but not dramatically so. Yet Thailand's life expectancy is 79 years (sixteen years longer than South Africa's), and its education index is comparable. Thailand spends a smaller share of GDP on health than South Africa does, yet achieves better health outcomes. The explanation is not primarily about money — it is about the effectiveness of institutions, the distribution of resources, and the coherence of policy. Thailand invested systematically in primary healthcare from the 1970s onward, achieving universal health coverage through a system that prioritises community-level preventive care. South Africa has been debating National Health Insurance for over a decade while its public health system deteriorates.

The comparison with Botswana offers a different lesson. Botswana, South Africa's northern neighbour, has a lower GNI per capita but an HDI that has risen faster over the past two decades. Botswana was devastated by HIV/AIDS — its life expectancy dropped even more precipitously than South Africa's in the early 2000s — but its government responded earlier and more decisively with antiretroviral treatment programmes. Botswana's health dimension recovery has been faster, and its overall HDI trajectory is more positive. This comparison suggests that government responsiveness and institutional quality matter as much as income levels in determining human development outcomes.

At the global scale, South Africa's rank of approximately 110th out of 193 countries places it in the bottom half of the global distribution. This is remarkable for a country with the most industrialised economy in Africa, the continent's most sophisticated financial sector, world-class universities, and constitutional protections for socioeconomic rights that are among the most progressive in the world. South Africa has the institutional architecture and the economic resources to be a "very high human development" country. It is not, and the reason is not poverty — it is inequality.

:::expert-perspective
**Mahbub ul Haq**, architect of the Human Development Index (1990): "The basic purpose of development is to enlarge people's choices. In principle, these choices can be infinite and can change over time. People often value achievements that do not show up at all, or not immediately, in income or growth figures: greater access to knowledge, better nutrition and health services, more secure livelihoods, security against crime and physical violence, satisfying leisure hours, political and cultural freedoms and a sense of participation in community activities." South Africa's HDI of 0.741 captures some of these achievements. Its IHDI of 0.462 reveals how unequally they are distributed.
:::

## The Deception of Averages

The HDI is a national average. In a country like Norway (HDI 0.966) or Switzerland (HDI 0.967), where inequality is relatively low and the population is relatively homogeneous in its access to health, education, and income, the national average is a reasonable approximation of most people's experience. In South Africa, the national average is a reasonable approximation of almost nobody's experience.

Consider what the HDI would look like if you could compute it separately for different segments of the South African population. No official disaggregated HDI exists at the subnational level in South Africa, but researchers have constructed estimates using census and survey data, and the results are staggering.

**The geographic divide.** Gauteng — South Africa's smallest province by area but its economic powerhouse, home to Johannesburg, Pretoria, and approximately 16 million people — would likely have an HDI above 0.800 if calculated independently, placing it in the "very high human development" category alongside countries like Malaysia or Serbia. The Western Cape, with Cape Town as its economic engine, would be comparable. By contrast, the Eastern Cape — one of the former apartheid "homeland" dumping grounds, with high poverty, poor infrastructure, and weak institutions — would likely have an HDI below 0.600, placing it in the "medium human development" category alongside countries like Bangladesh or Kenya. Limpopo and KwaZulu-Natal would fall somewhere between these extremes but closer to the lower end. These are not differences between neighbouring countries. These are differences within a single country, between provinces governed by the same Constitution, funded (in theory) by the same equitable share formula, and served (in theory) by the same public service.

**The racial divide.** South Africa's apartheid legacy means that race remains a powerful predictor of human development outcomes nearly thirty years after the transition to democracy. Life expectancy, educational quality (not just quantity — years of schooling tell one story, but learning outcomes tell another), and income differ dramatically across racial groups. The statistical average HDI of 0.741 blends a population in which some households resemble those in Portugal and others resemble those in Mozambique.

**The gender divide.** South African women have higher life expectancy than men (67 versus 60 years, approximately) and more years of schooling, but lower income and dramatically higher exposure to gender-based violence. The Gender Development Index, which compares female and male HDI, reveals a significant gap — not because women lack educational access, but because the returns to their education in the labour market are suppressed by structural discrimination, occupational segregation, and the burden of unpaid care work.

**The urban-rural divide.** Access to healthcare, quality education, and income-earning opportunities differs enormously between urban and rural South Africa. A child born in central Johannesburg and a child born in rural Mqanduli in the Eastern Cape are born into the same country but into entirely different development realities. The urban child will have access to better-resourced schools, closer proximity to healthcare facilities, more diverse employment opportunities, and a physical environment with reliable water, sanitation, and electricity. The rural child may face a sixty-kilometre journey to the nearest hospital, a school without textbooks, and a household without piped water.

The Gini coefficient of 0.63 — the highest in the world by most measurements — is the summary statistic for all of these divides. It tells you that South Africa is not a single country in developmental terms. It is several countries stacked on top of each other, sharing a flag and a constitution but experiencing profoundly different realities. The HDI of 0.741 is the weighted average of these realities. It is not wrong — the mathematics are correct. But it is misleading in the same way that saying the average temperature of a hospital is comfortable is misleading if some wards are freezing and others are on fire.

This is why the Inequality-adjusted HDI exists, and why it matters so much for South Africa. When you adjust the HDI for inequality — when you ask not what the average is but how the distribution affects actual human lives — South Africa's score drops from 0.741 to 0.462. That 37.6% penalty is the largest among all upper-middle-income countries. It is the mathematical expression of a social structure that produces enough wealth for a comfortable middle-income country but distributes it with the brutality of an oligarchy.

## Beyond the HDI: The Family of Indices

The HDI was always intended as a starting point, not a destination. Mahbub ul Haq described it as a "vulgar measure" — deliberately simple, designed to compete with the even more vulgar simplicity of GDP. But the UNDP has progressively developed a family of indices, each addressing a dimension that the headline HDI omits. Each of these will receive detailed treatment in subsequent articles in this series, but a brief overview here establishes the analytical landscape.

**The Inequality-adjusted Human Development Index (IHDI).** Introduced in the 2010 Human Development Report, the IHDI adjusts each of the three HDI dimensions for inequality using the Atkinson inequality measure. In a perfectly equal society, the IHDI equals the HDI. In an unequal society, the IHDI is lower — the greater the inequality, the greater the gap. For South Africa, the IHDI penalty is devastating: the score drops from 0.741 to 0.462, a loss of 37.6%. The losses are not equal across dimensions — income inequality accounts for the largest share (approximately 57% loss), followed by health inequality (approximately 25% loss) and education inequality (approximately 22% loss). The IHDI will be the centrepiece of the next article in this series.

**The Gender Development Index (GDI).** The GDI compares female and male HDI scores to assess gender-based disparities in human development. South Africa's GDI reveals a complex picture: women have higher life expectancy and educational attainment than men, but significantly lower income. The overall GDI places South Africa in a middle group — not the worst gender disparities, but far from gender parity.

**The Gender Inequality Index (GII).** The GII goes beyond the GDI to measure gender-based disadvantage across three dimensions: reproductive health, empowerment, and labour market participation. South Africa performs poorly on this measure, driven by high adolescent birth rates, high maternal mortality relative to income level, and the pervasive impact of gender-based violence — which, while not directly measured by the GII, undermines women's capabilities across all three dimensions.

**The Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI).** Developed by the Oxford Poverty and Human Development Initiative in partnership with the UNDP, the MPI identifies people who are simultaneously deprived across multiple dimensions — health, education, and living standards — using ten weighted indicators. Unlike income poverty measures, which draw a monetary poverty line and count people below it, the MPI asks whether people are deprived in specific capabilities regardless of their income. A person can be above the income poverty line but MPI-poor if they lack access to clean water, adequate sanitation, and quality education simultaneously. South Africa's MPI headcount has declined over the post-apartheid period — driven largely by the expansion of social grants and infrastructure — but significant pockets of multidimensional poverty persist, concentrated in the former homelands and in informal settlements.

**The Planetary Pressures-adjusted HDI (PHDI).** The most recent addition to the family, the PHDI adjusts the HDI for a country's per capita carbon dioxide emissions and material footprint. This index addresses the environmental blind spot that critics have long identified in the HDI — a country can achieve a high HDI through resource-intensive development that degrades the natural environment on which future development depends. South Africa, with its energy-intensive economy, faces a PHDI adjustment. However, the PHDI's narrow focus on CO2 and material throughput misses arguably more important environmental dimensions for a developing country — biodiversity loss, water pollution, soil degradation, and habitat destruction. The index is a useful corrective but an incomplete measure of environmental responsibility.

:::takeaway
The HDI is one member of a family of indices. The IHDI adjusts for inequality (devastating for South Africa: 37.6% penalty). The GDI and GII capture gender disparities. The MPI identifies multidimensional deprivation beyond income. The PHDI adjusts for environmental pressures. No single index captures the full picture — understanding the relationships between them is essential for evaluating South Africa's development trajectory.
:::

## What This Series Will Argue

This series on human development advances a single core thesis: that safety, economic growth, and human development are not separate policy domains to be addressed by separate government departments with separate budgets and separate accountability frameworks. They are a single reinforcing system — a cycle that, when functioning, produces compounding progress and, when broken, produces accelerating decline.

The thesis is not original. Development economists, political scientists, and governance scholars have documented the interdependencies between security, economic performance, and human capability for decades. What this series does is apply the thesis specifically to South Africa, using the country's own data, institutions, and policy history to trace the causal mechanisms through which the cycle operates — or fails to operate.

The argument proceeds as follows.

**Part 1** (this part, comprising three articles) establishes the conceptual framework. This article has introduced the HDI and the capabilities approach. The next article will conduct a detailed examination of South Africa's scores across the full family of human development indices, with particular attention to the IHDI collapse and what it reveals about lived inequality. The third article will present the reinforcing cycle thesis in full — mapping the causal links between safety, growth, and development, and showing how South Africa's data fits the model.

**Part 2** will examine the health dimension of human development in detail — life expectancy, the HIV/AIDS crisis and recovery, tuberculosis, maternal mortality, the primary healthcare system, and the long-debated National Health Insurance proposal. It will ask why South Africa spends more on health as a share of GDP than most peer countries but achieves worse outcomes.

**Part 3** will examine the education dimension — not just the quantity of schooling (which South Africa provides in reasonable measure) but the quality of learning (which South Africa fails to deliver for the majority of its learners). It will engage with the extensive evidence from international assessments like PIRLS and TIMSS, and with the domestic research programme on what works in South African classrooms.

**Part 4** will examine the income dimension and the inequality adjustment — why South Africa's income distribution is the most unequal in the world, how the social grant system partially mitigates this, and what a more equitable distribution would mean for human development outcomes.

**Part 5** will return to the reinforcing cycle and ask what it would take to reverse the vicious spiral — to move from a system in which broken safety, stagnant growth, and deteriorating development reinforce each other to one in which improving safety, accelerating growth, and expanding development compound upward.

Throughout, the series will maintain this publication's commitment to non-partisan analysis. The reinforcing cycle does not respect party lines. It does not care whether the government is led by the ANC, the DA, the EFF, the MK Party, or a coalition of any or all of them. The cycle responds to institutional quality, policy coherence, and implementation capacity — not to ideology, rhetoric, or party affiliation. Every South African, regardless of political allegiance, has an interest in understanding this system, because every South African is trapped within it.

The goal is not to tell you what to think about any particular policy proposal. The goal is to give you the analytical framework to evaluate policy proposals for yourself — to ask, when a politician promises to "improve education" or "grow the economy" or "reduce crime," whether the proposed intervention addresses the systemic interdependencies that determine whether any individual policy can succeed. As Amartya Sen wrote, the purpose of development analysis is not to replace people's judgment but to inform it. That is the purpose of this series.

## From Numbers to Lives

Let us close by returning from the abstractions of indices and frameworks to the concrete reality that they attempt to capture.

An HDI of 0.741 is not a number about a country. It is a summary — a deeply imperfect summary — of sixty million individual stories. It is the story of a nurse in a rural clinic in KwaZulu-Natal who works twelve-hour shifts treating patients with tuberculosis, HIV, diabetes, and hypertension, with insufficient medication and no specialist backup, whose own life expectancy is shortened by the stress and occupational hazards of her work. It is the story of a fourteen-year-old in Khayelitsha who walks forty minutes to school each morning through areas where gang violence is a daily reality, who sits in a classroom with forty-five other learners and a teacher who was never adequately trained in the subject they are teaching, and who will complete twelve years of schooling without acquiring the literacy and numeracy skills that the curriculum intended. It is the story of a retrenched mineworker in Rustenburg whose silicosis diagnosis means he will never work again and whose family survives on a social grant that covers food but not the transport costs to reach the clinic where he needs treatment.

These stories are not exceptional. They are, in statistical terms, the norm. They are the lived experience behind the national average, the human reality that GDP does not capture and that even the HDI captures only partially. The capabilities approach asks us to evaluate a society not by its aggregate production or its average scores but by the actual freedoms that its least advantaged members enjoy. By that measure, South Africa has a long way to go.

But the capabilities approach also insists on something else: that capability expansion is possible. It is not inevitable — there is no developmental escalator that automatically carries countries upward. But it is achievable. Countries that were poorer, less institutionally developed, and less favourably endowed than South Africa have achieved dramatic improvements in human development within a generation. South Korea's HDI was below 0.600 in 1980; it is above 0.920 today. Botswana's life expectancy dropped below 50 during the AIDS epidemic; it has recovered to above 65. Bangladesh, one of the poorest countries in the world, has increased life expectancy from 48 years in 1980 to 73 years today through systematic investment in primary healthcare and women's education.

The question for South Africa is not whether improvement is possible. The question is whether the political will, institutional capacity, and civic engagement exist to make it happen. That question is the subject of this entire series. The next article examines, in unflinching detail, exactly what South Africa's human development numbers reveal about the country's successes, failures, and the vast distance between what its economy produces and what its citizens experience.

:::expert-perspective
**Mahbub ul Haq**, writing in the first Human Development Report (1990): "People are the real wealth of a nation. The basic objective of development is to create an enabling environment for people to enjoy long, healthy, and creative lives. This may appear to be a simple truth. But it is often forgotten in the immediate concern with the accumulation of commodities and financial wealth." Thirty-four years later, South Africa has accumulated considerable commodities and financial wealth. The question is whether its sixty million people are living long, healthy, and creative lives. The Human Development Index — and especially the inequality-adjusted version — suggests that for the majority, the answer is no.

:::framework
**The HDI Literacy Guide**

When you encounter human development claims — in the news, in political speeches, or in policy documents — use these four steps to evaluate them.

**Step 1: Identify which index is being cited.** The HDI, IHDI, GDI, GII, MPI, and PHDI each measure different things. An HDI score tells you about national averages. An IHDI score tells you about distribution. An MPI score tells you about the poorest. A politician citing the HDI without mentioning the IHDI is showing you the shop window, not the stockroom. Always ask: which index, and what does it measure?

**Step 2: Check the dimensions.** The HDI combines health (life expectancy at birth), education (mean years of schooling and expected years of schooling), and income (GNI per capita in PPP dollars). If a claim focuses on one dimension — "we've improved education" — check whether the other dimensions have moved. Improvement in one dimension that comes at the expense of another is not development; it is reallocation.

**Step 3: Compare like with like.** HDI scores are most meaningful when compared across time (is this country improving?), across peers (how does this country perform relative to others at similar income levels?), and across the HDI-IHDI gap (how much development is lost to inequality?). A score of 0.741 means little in isolation. It means a great deal when you know that the inequality-adjusted score is 0.462, that peer countries at similar income levels score higher, and that the gap has not closed over two decades.

**Step 4: Look beneath national averages.** South Africa's national HDI masks enormous variation by province, by municipality, by race, and by gender. Ask: whose HDI? A national average that combines Sandton and Lusikisiki is an arithmetic exercise, not a development assessment. Wherever possible, seek disaggregated data — provincial HDI estimates, district-level health indicators, school-level education outcomes — to understand what the national average conceals.
:::
