Everyone understands government finance through a single, powerful image: the state is like a household. A household must earn before it can spend; it cannot write cheques against money it does not have; if it overspends it falls into debt and eventually ruin. So too, the image says, the government must collect its income — taxes — before it can spend, and a government that spends more than it taxes is living beyond its means just as a reckless family would. This metaphor sits underneath almost every budget debate in the country, and underneath every calculation in the first article of this part, where we kept saying: the state must raise R563 billion, so if you cut it here it must come back there.

The metaphor is not worthless. But for an entity that _issues_ the currency it collects, it is incomplete in a way that matters — and this article is about the gap. A country that issues its own rands is not revenue-constrained in the way a household is. It cannot run out of the rands it itself creates. What it _can_ run out of is the real things rands buy — goods, skills, productive capacity, and crucially foreign currency — and it can debase the value of its currency through inflation if it pushes too hard against those real limits. In this framing, associated with Modern Monetary Theory, taxes are not the government's "income" at all. They are _tools_: for cooling an overheating economy, for making people want the national currency, for steering behaviour, and for compressing inequality. That is a genuine insight, and it is worth taking seriously. It also sharpens the thread running through this whole part: if taxes are instruments rather than the state's housekeeping money, then how much to tax, and at what rate, is even more plainly a matter of design and judgement than of necessity — one more reason the rate schedule carries no special claim to sit at the centre of the argument, and certainly no sacred one. The household image is not just incomplete as accounting; it quietly smuggles in the idea that the tax take is fixed by need rather than chosen by policy.

It is also dangerous if taken too far. South Africa is a small, open, emerging-market economy with a floating, heavily traded currency and significant exposure to foreign capital and foreign-currency debt. Those facts sharply limit how far the sovereign-money logic can be pushed here — far more than for a large, relatively closed, reserve-currency economy like the United States. The point of this article is emphatically _not_ to license unlimited spending. It is to show that "how will you pay for it?" is a more complicated and more interesting question than the household metaphor admits — and that the answer, for South Africa, is constrained less by a shortage of rands than by inflation, the exchange rate, and the confidence of the people who lend the country money. This is the sixth article in The Fiscal Levers, and it is the pivot of the whole part: the place where we stop assuming revenue is the binding constraint and start asking what actually is.

## The household metaphor, and what it gets right

Start by giving the metaphor its due, because the temptation in this territory is to knock it down too quickly, and a caricatured takedown is how people end up believing genuinely reckless things.

What the household image gets right is profound: you cannot conjure _real_ things out of nothing. A government cannot summon doctors who were never trained, electricity from power stations that were never built, or imported machinery without the foreign currency to pay for it. If it tries to command more real resources than the economy can supply — more goods, more labour, more imports — the result is not free prosperity; it is inflation, shortages, and a falling currency. Deficits have consequences. Debt has to be serviced, and if it grows faster than the economy, the interest bill crowds out everything else a government wants to do — a dynamic South Africa knows intimately, with debt-service costs now among the largest single items in the budget, larger than what the state spends on health or on the police. That figure is the household metaphor's strongest card, and it is a real card: every rand spent on interest is a rand not spent on a clinic or a classroom, and the bill grows automatically as long as deficits persist and rates stay high. A sovereign-money enthusiast who shrugs at South Africa's debt-service burden is not being sophisticated; they are ignoring a constraint that is already biting, visibly, in every budget. A household that ignores its constraints comes to grief, and so does a government that ignores _its_ constraints. The metaphor's error is not in saying there are limits — there are, and debt service is one of them. Its error is in identifying the _wrong_ limit at the moment of spending — pointing at a shortage of money, when at that moment the binding limit is a shortage of the real things money buys, and in the longer run the sustainability of the debt and the patience of those who hold it.

## Monetary sovereignty, stated precisely

So what does it mean to say a government "issues its own currency", and why does it loosen the revenue constraint without removing all constraints? Precision matters here, because the loose version of this idea is where the trouble starts.

A monetarily sovereign state has three powers a household lacks: it issues its own currency, it controls its own central bank and monetary policy, and it regulates the banks that operate in its currency. Because it issues the rand, the South African state can always create more rands to meet any obligation denominated in rands; it can never be _forced_ to default on rand debt the way a household can be forced to default, because it is not using a currency someone else controls. This is the kernel of truth in the sovereign-money view, and at the level of pure logic it is hard to dispute: an issuer of a currency is in a fundamentally different position from a user of it.

But South Africa's institutions are deliberately built so that the government does _not_ simply print rands to fund itself, and the reasons are instructive. The South African Reserve Bank is constitutionally independent, mandated to protect the value of the currency — to keep inflation low and stable. As of late 2025 it operates to a new inflation target of 3%, with a tolerance band of one percentage point either side, tightened from the 3–6% range that had held for a quarter-century. The government borrows in financial markets by selling bonds; it does not, in normal operation, instruct the Reserve Bank to create money to pay its bills. That separation is not an accident or an oversight that MMT has cleverly spotted — it is a hard-won institutional safeguard, designed precisely because the power to create money to fund spending is so dangerous that countries deliberately tie their own hands against using it casually.

It is worth being concrete about how the plumbing actually works, because "the government prints money" is a caricature that obscures more than it reveals. When the South African government wants to spend more than it collects, it does not instruct anyone to run a printing press. The National Treasury issues bonds, which are bought by banks, pension funds, insurers and foreign investors with money that already exists; the Reserve Bank, separately, sets the short-term interest rate to hit its inflation target and manages the banking system's liquidity. The two institutions coordinate, but the central bank does not simply credit the government's account into existence to cover the deficit — that direct "monetary financing" is exactly the line that disciplined systems refuse to cross, because crossing it is the historical road to runaway inflation. So when the sovereign-money argument says the state "can always create the rands", it is describing a _latent capacity_ that South Africa's institutions are deliberately built to keep latent. The capacity is real; the restraint is the policy. Confusing the two — treating a power the country has chosen not to use as a resource it is foolishly ignoring — is how the analytical insight curdles into reckless advice.

It also helps to see that creating money is not a single act but three, only one of which is the state running a press. New rands enter the economy in three main ways: the issuing of notes and coins — physical cash, the form everyone pictures and, in a modern economy, much the smallest; the creation of bank reserves by the Reserve Bank, the electronic central-bank money on which the banking system settles; and private bank money creation, the deposits that commercial banks bring into being when they lend. "The government prints money" points only at the first, and a little at the second. It misses the third entirely — which, it turns out, is much the largest source of all, and the one this part returns to in force.

This is why the careful version of the argument distinguishes two very different claims. The first is _analytical_: a currency issuer is not revenue-constrained in the way a currency user is — it is constrained by real resources and inflation rather than by a shortage of its own money. This claim is, in some form, widely accepted, including by economists who would never call themselves MMT supporters. The second is a _policy_ claim: that _because_ of the first, the government can and should spend much more freely, running large deficits to pursue full employment, with inflation managed after the fact through taxation. This second claim is where the serious disagreement lives, and where, for an economy like South Africa's, the mainstream has powerful objections. Keep the two apart. Accepting the analytical point does not commit you to the policy programme, and conflating them is the single most common error in this whole debate.

## The four purposes of taxation, through the sovereign-money lens

If taxes are not the government's income, what are they for? The sovereign-money view gives four answers, and each is illuminating even if you reject the larger policy programme.

The first is _inflation control_. If the government spends rands into the economy and taxes some back out, then tax is a way of draining excess purchasing power — cooling demand so it does not outrun the economy's capacity to supply real goods, which is what causes inflation. On this view, tax fights inflation directly, by removing money from circulation, and it connects straight to the Reserve Bank's price-stability mandate and its 3% target: managing inflation is the real constraint, and tax is one of the instruments that bears on it.

The second is _currency demand_. Why does anyone accept rands at all? One answer is that the government requires you to pay your taxes in rands, and will accept nothing else. That tax liability, hanging over every household and business, creates a permanent, society-wide demand for the currency — you must obtain rands, whatever else you do, to settle what you owe the state. Tax, in this sense, is part of what gives the currency its value in the first place.

The third is _behaviour modification_ — the disincentive logic of [What Tax Is For](/articles/eg-3-3-what-tax-is-for). Taxes steer behaviour: the carbon tax discourages emissions, the sugar levy discourages sugary drinks, the sin taxes discourage smoking and drinking. This purpose has nothing to do with funding and everything to do with shaping what people do.

The fourth is _redistribution_. A progressive tax compresses the distribution of income and wealth, taking proportionately more from those at the top — a social goal in its own right, independent of how much revenue it raises.

Notice what has happened to "funding". In this framing it has slipped from being the _sole_ purpose of taxation, as the household metaphor assumes, to being one consideration among several — and arguably not even the most important. That reframing is genuinely useful. It explains why a tax can be worth keeping even if it raises little (a carbon tax), and why the question "what is this tax _for_?" rarely has the simple answer "to fund the government". It also explains a puzzle in the everyday politics of tax: why governments that issue their own currency still agonise over deficits and still insist taxes must "pay for" spending. Part of the answer is that the funding framing, even if analytically incomplete, is a useful discipline — it forces a public reckoning with the real costs of spending that the looser sovereign-money framing can let slip. The household metaphor survives partly because it is wrong in a convenient direction: it over-states the constraint, and over-stating a constraint is a safer error than under-stating it.

:::expert
The clearest statements of this position come from the economists most associated with Modern Monetary Theory — L. Randall Wray, whose _Modern Money Theory_ (2015) is the standard text; Stephanie Kelton, whose _The Deficit Myth_ (2020) brought the argument to a wide public; and Warren Mosler, the financial practitioner whose writing seeded the school. Their shared claim is that a currency-issuing government faces an inflation constraint, not a revenue constraint, and that taxes and bonds do jobs other than financing. It is essential to represent them accurately and not as straw men: they do _not_ claim deficits never matter or that governments can spend without limit — Kelton in particular is explicit that inflation is the binding constraint and that real resources are finite. The disagreement with the mainstream is not over whether limits exist; it is over _which_ limit binds, how much room a given country actually has, and how reliably inflation can be managed through fiscal rather than monetary tools — a disagreement that gets sharper, not softer, for a small open economy.
:::

## The limits — and why they bite harder in South Africa

This section is the heart of the matter, because the gap between the _analytical_ truth of monetary sovereignty and its _safe application_ in South Africa is very wide.

The sovereign-money argument is far stronger for a large, relatively closed, reserve-currency economy than for a small, open, emerging-market one. The United States issues the world's reserve currency; the world _wants_ dollars, holds them, and lends to America in its own currency at low rates. America can run very large deficits for a long time before markets blink. South Africa is in a completely different position, and the differences are not details — they are the whole game.

Consider the rand. It floats freely and is one of the most heavily traded emerging-market currencies, which means its value is set, minute by minute, by global markets that can turn on the country quickly. If investors come to believe South Africa is creating rands recklessly, they sell the rand, and it falls — sharply. A falling rand is not an abstraction; it raises the price of everything the country imports, from fuel to machinery to medicine, and that imported inflation passes through to the shelves of every shop. So the inflation constraint that MMT acknowledges in theory arrives in South Africa through a specific and brutal channel — the exchange rate — that a reserve-currency issuer barely feels. Push the money-creation lever here and the rand tells you within days.

Trace the mechanism concretely, because it is the crux. Suppose the government decided to fund a large new programme by leaning on money creation rather than taxes or honest borrowing. Foreign and domestic investors, watching, conclude that South Africa is no longer defending the value of the rand. They sell rands and rand assets. The rand drops — say it weakens sharply against the dollar over a few weeks. Immediately, the petrol price rises, because oil is priced in dollars; imported food, machinery, medicines and electronics all cost more; and because so much of what South Africa consumes has an imported component, general prices follow. Inflation climbs, the Reserve Bank is forced to raise interest rates to defend the currency and its 3% target, and those higher rates choke the very growth the spending was meant to create — while the higher import prices eat the wages of the poor. The programme that was supposed to be "free", because the state can create rands, turns out to have a price after all, paid in inflation and a weaker currency and higher interest rates. The rands were free; the consequences were not. That sequence is not hypothetical for an emerging economy; it is the standard way these experiments end, and it is why the exchange rate, not the printing press, is the true governor on the engine.

Then there is foreign capital and foreign-currency debt. South Africa borrows mostly in rands — around four-fifths of government borrowing is rand-denominated — which is genuinely protective, and a fair point in the sovereign-money argument's favour: the country cannot easily be forced into the kind of foreign-currency default that has wrecked other emerging economies. But "mostly" is not "entirely". Roughly a fifth of borrowing is in foreign currency, which the state cannot create and must earn or buy, and that portion is fully exposed to a falling rand. More importantly, foreign investors hold a substantial share of the rand-denominated government bonds too — historically around a quarter — and they can sell and leave. When they do, bond prices fall, the government's borrowing costs rise, and the discipline of the market reasserts itself regardless of any theory about monetary sovereignty. The credit-rating agencies enforce the same discipline from another direction. None of this is fatal to the analytical point, but all of it means the _room_ South Africa has to act on it is a fraction of what a reserve issuer enjoys.

And there is a distributional sting that should weigh especially heavily in a country like this one. Inflation, once it gets loose, is the most regressive force in economics. The wealthy hold assets — property, shares, foreign currency — that tend to rise with or ahead of inflation; the poor hold cash and earn wages that lag it, and they spend everything they have on the very food and transport whose prices inflation drives up first. A government that gambles on the sovereign-money logic and loses — that creates too many rands, triggers a currency slide and an inflationary spiral — does not hurt the rich most. It devastates the poor, the very constituency in whose name the spending was usually proposed. This is why even economists sympathetic to the analytical insight are cautious about the policy programme in an emerging-market setting: the downside of getting it wrong falls hardest on those least able to bear it.

:::expert
South Africa's own monetary authorities have been pointed about this. The Reserve Bank, under Governor Lesetja Kganyago, has consistently defended both its independence and the inflation target precisely against the argument that a sovereign currency-issuer should loosen the monetary reins to fund development — Kganyago's standing reply is that disciplined, credible monetary policy _is_ the modern and responsible course for an economy as exposed as South Africa's, not a colonial hangover to be thrown off. Business and mainstream economic commentators have made the same case bluntly: that MMT-style prescriptions, whatever their theoretical interest, would in South African conditions risk the currency, the bond market and the inflation rate all at once, with the poor absorbing the damage. These are not knee-jerk dismissals; they are arguments from the specific vulnerabilities of a small open economy, and they are the reason this article treats the sovereign-money insight as a lens for asking better questions rather than as a budget the country can safely run.
:::

## What this does to the rest of the part

Hold the balance — insight on one side, hard limits on the other — and watch what it does to the arguments of the earlier articles. It does not blow them up. It reframes them.

Go back to the flat-tax arithmetic of [The Disincentive Question](/articles/eg-3-1-the-flat-tax-mirage). We said a flat income tax forces a compensating VAT increase, as a matter of revenue necessity: the money has to come from somewhere. The sovereign-money lens shows that the word "necessity" was doing too much work. If the state is not strictly revenue-constrained, then "compensating" for an income-tax cut with a VAT rise is not an iron law of arithmetic; it is a _choice_ about how to manage inflation and distribution. The country _could_, in principle, accept a larger deficit instead of raising VAT — the constraint on doing so is not "we have no rands" but "would the resulting demand and the market's reaction be worth the inflation and currency risk?" That is a real and serious constraint, often a binding one. But it is a different question from the household metaphor's "we cannot afford it", and noticing the difference changes the debate. The same applies to reaching the lightly taxed asset stock of [What Tax Is For](/articles/eg-3-3-what-tax-is-for), or relieving the double taxation of capital in [The Double Taxation of Capital](/articles/eg-3-4-the-double-taxation-of-capital): these are political and economic choices about inflation, distribution and incentives — not budgetary impossibilities dictated by a fixed pot of money.

The honest reader will notice this cuts both ways, and that is exactly right. If revenue is not the hard constraint, then neither "we cannot afford to help the poor" nor "we can afford anything we like" survives. What replaces both is a harder, more grown-up question: what _real_ constraint actually binds — inflation, the exchange rate, the capacity of the economy, the confidence of lenders — and is this particular spending worth pushing against it? That question cannot be answered by pointing at the size of the deficit alone. It has to be answered case by case, on the real economics. That is more demanding than the household metaphor, not less — and it is why the sovereign-money insight, handled responsibly, makes the budget debate harder rather than easier.

## The blind spot MMT shares with everyone else

There is one more thing to say, and it sets up the rest of the part. For all that Modern Monetary Theory overturns the household metaphor, it shares a curious blind spot with the very mainstream it criticises. Both sides of this argument focus on _government_ as the source of money — whether the government is revenue-constrained, how much it can spend, what its taxes are for. Both underplay a fact that turns out to be larger than the entire debate: most of the money in the economy is not created by the government at all. It is created by _private banks_, when they make loans. Even the sharpest South African critics of MMT have noticed this gap, pointing out that the rands "created in the shadows" by commercial bank lending dwarf anything the Treasury and the Reserve Bank do directly.

If that is true — and the next article shows that it is, with the Bank of England as a witness — then this entire chapter, government-versus-household, sovereign-money-versus-sound-finance, has been conducted on a stage that holds only a fraction of the action. We have spent this article arguing about the smaller of the two money taps and ignoring the larger. The bigger question is not whether the government is revenue-constrained. It is who creates most of the money in the first place, and what they create it _for_ — production that builds the country, or the bidding-up of assets that already exist. [Where Money Comes From](/articles/eg-3-7-where-money-comes-from) takes us there, and it is where this part's argument finally turns from tax to the thing that was underneath it all along.

## Practical Framework: The Constraint Locator

:::takeaway
The image of the government as a household that must earn before it spends is incomplete for a currency issuer: South Africa cannot run out of the rands it creates, only out of the real things rands buy — goods, skills, capacity, and foreign currency — and it can debase the rand through inflation. In that frame, taxes are tools (managing inflation, creating currency demand, steering behaviour, redistributing), not just income. But South Africa's open economy, floating rand, foreign-currency debt and foreign-held bonds mean the real constraints — inflation and the exchange rate — bite hard and fast, and inflation hurts the poor most. The lesson is not "spend freely". It is that "can we afford it?" should be replaced by "which real constraint binds, and is this worth pushing against it?"
:::

The practical payoff of this article is a better question to ask whenever a politician or commentator declares a policy "unaffordable" or, conversely, "fully funded". Both phrases usually smuggle in the household metaphor. Here is how to locate the constraint that actually matters.

:::framework
**The Constraint Locator**

When someone says a policy is "unaffordable" or "fully funded", work through four questions to find the real constraint.

**1. Is the claimed constraint _revenue_ or _real_?**

Are they saying the government lacks the _rands_ (a revenue constraint), or that the economy lacks the _real resources_ — the doctors, the power, the construction capacity, the imports (a real-resource constraint)? For a currency issuer, the second is the binding one. "We have no money" is rarely the true obstacle; "we have no spare capacity" often is.

**2. Would the spending draw on idle resources or compete for scarce ones?**

Spending that puts unemployed people and unused capacity to work is far less inflationary than spending that bids for resources already fully used. Ask whether the policy mobilises slack or fights over what is already spoken for — that, not the deficit number, determines the inflation risk.

**3. Does it create demand for foreign currency the country must earn or borrow?**

This is the emerging-market trap. Rands the state can create; dollars and euros for imports it cannot. A policy that drives up imports or foreign-currency obligations runs into a constraint monetary sovereignty does _not_ relax. Watch the exchange-rate and import exposure specifically.

**4. Who bears the risk if inflation results?**

If the spending does push against a real constraint and inflation follows, who pays? In South Africa the answer is almost always the poor, who hold cash and earn lagging wages. A responsible case for spending against the constraint has to reckon honestly with that distributional risk, not wave it away.

These questions will not tell you to spend freely or to refuse all deficits. They will move the argument off the false ground of "the government has run out of money" and onto the real ground of inflation, capacity, the currency, and who bears the risk — which is where it always belonged.
:::
