## The Two Questions That Decide Everything

The previous article established what a national youth service would cost: a contained sum in its early phases, and a large one — illustratively R55–70 billion a year — at mature, universal scale. This final article answers the two questions that determine whether that cost is a reason to proceed or a reason to stop. Where does the money come from? And how would the country know it was getting its money's worth?

These are the questions on which the whole series rests, because a proposal that cannot answer them is not a proposal at all — it is a wish. The temptation, for an advocate, is to wave them away with one of two slogans: "it would pay for itself," or "it's an investment, not a cost." Both are evasions. This article does not use them. It sets out, honestly, where the money could realistically be found and what each source would cost in trade-offs; it is candid about what is *not* affordable; and it insists that every rand be tied to a published scorecard, so that the country can see, year after year, whether the service is delivering what it promised. Funding without accountability is how good intentions become captured boondoggles. The two questions — how to pay, and how to know — have to be answered together.

## Where the Money Comes From

There is no single source that funds a programme of this scale, and any honest answer is a combination, assembled deliberately and phased in over time. Four sources, in roughly descending order of importance, make up the realistic picture.

### One: Consolidate What Is Already Spent

The most important point about funding the youth service is also the most overlooked: a substantial part of the money already exists, scattered across the fragmented landscape Part 1 described. South Africa already spends significant sums on youth and public-employment programmes — the National Youth Development Agency, the Expanded Public Works Programme and the Community Work Programme, the Presidential Youth Employment Intervention, NARYSEC, and a range of learnership and skills initiatives. This spending is real, recurring, and — as Part 1 argued — fragmented, duplicative, and weakly coordinated.

The youth service offers a way to *consolidate* it. Rather than funding a new institution entirely from new money, a large share of its cost could be assembled by folding existing, overlapping youth programmes into the single coherent envelope the service provides. This is not a sleight of hand; it is the financial expression of the whole argument of Part 1, which held that the existing landscape fails precisely because it is fragmented. Consolidation does two things at once: it reduces the genuinely *new* money the service requires, and it improves the coherence and impact of money the country is already spending less effectively. The exact amount that can be consolidated is a question for detailed work, but the principle is central: the headline cost of the service substantially overstates its *additional* cost, because a meaningful fraction is reallocation of existing youth spending into a better-designed vehicle.

It is worth dwelling on the second of those effects, because it is easy to undersell. Consolidation is not merely an accounting convenience that lowers the new-money figure; it is, on its own terms, a reform worth doing even setting the youth service aside. The current landscape's fragmentation, Part 1 argued, is not a neutral feature but an active cause of failure: it scatters accountability, duplicates administration, confuses the young people it is meant to serve, and makes outcomes nearly impossible to track across the maze. Pulling that spending into one coherent, measured institution would improve the return on money the country is *already spending*, regardless of how much new money is added on top. In that sense, part of the youth service's cost is not a new cost at all but the better use of an existing one — and a government persuaded of nothing else in this series might still find that consolidating the fragmented youth landscape into something coherent is worth doing for its own sake. The youth service is, among other things, the vehicle through which that overdue consolidation could finally happen.

A caution belongs here too, in the spirit of honesty this Part insists on. Consolidation is easy to assert and hard to do. Each existing programme has a department that runs it, staff whose jobs depend on it, and beneficiaries who rely on it, and folding them into a single new institution means navigating exactly the turf and transition politics that have defeated coordination efforts before. The financial logic of consolidation is sound; the political and administrative execution of it is genuinely difficult, and the phased rollout is part of how that difficulty is managed — programmes absorbed gradually as the service proves itself, not abolished overnight on a promise. The point stands that much of the money exists; the work of actually redirecting it should not be underestimated.

### Two: The Skills Levy

South Africa already levies employers to fund skills development, through the system that feeds the Sector Education and Training Authorities. That system exists precisely to build the skills of the workforce — which is, in large part, what the civilian streams of the youth service do. A portion of existing skills-development funding could therefore legitimately be directed toward the skilling that the service delivers, aligning a funding stream that already exists with an institution that pursues the same goal more coherently. This is not without controversy or trade-off — the skills-levy system has its own claimants and its own problems — but it is a logical and existing source for the specifically skills-building dimension of the service, and it belongs in the funding mix.

### Three: Phased Fiscal Allocation

Beyond consolidation and the levy, some genuinely new fiscal allocation would be required — but, crucially, it ramps with the phases rather than arriving all at once. In Phase One, the new money needed is small, on the order of a couple of billion rand a year, a modest and defensible allocation for buying hard evidence. In Phase Two it grows as the service scales. Only at mature, universal scale does the allocation become large — and by then, the service will have years of demonstrated results to justify its claim on the budget against competing priorities. This phasing is itself a funding strategy: it means the large fiscal commitment is never made on faith, only ever on evidence, and it spreads the build-up of cost over a decade rather than demanding it in a single budget. A government is asked to commit billions, not tens of billions, until the service has proven it deserves more.

### Four: Partnership and Development-Finance Co-Funding

Finally, specific components of the service can draw co-funding from beyond the national budget. The venture-finance facility of Part 2 — the capital door for graduates who start enterprises — is a natural candidate for co-funding from development-finance institutions and private corporate-social-investment, since it produces exactly the enterprise development those funders exist to support. Private employers who benefit from a pipeline of work-ready, credentialed young people have reason to contribute to the system that produces them. These sources will not fund the core of the service — the stipends and training are a public responsibility — but they can support specific components, particularly the enterprise-finance facility, easing the burden on the fiscus at the margins.

## What Is Not Affordable, and the Trade-Offs That Are Real

Honesty about funding requires naming what the money cannot do, because a case that pretends every constraint away deserves to be disbelieved.

The service cannot be funded by magic, and "it pays for itself" is not a funding plan. Even granting every offset and every consolidation, a mature universal service requires real resources that have real alternative uses. Every rand spent on it is a rand not spent on something else the country also needs — more teachers, more clinics, more grants, debt service. That is a genuine opportunity cost, and the case for the service has to be made *against* those alternatives, not as if the money were free. This series' honest position is that the youth service is a strong claim on resources because it addresses a structural crisis that undermines everything else — but it is a claim that must compete, on the merits, with other strong claims, and in a constrained fiscus not everything that is worth doing can be done at once.

There are also real trade-offs *within* the funding mix. Consolidating existing programmes means ending or absorbing those programmes, which have their own constituencies, staff, and defenders — a political cost, not only an administrative one. Directing skills-levy funding toward the service means less of it for its current uses. Phased fiscal allocation still requires the fiscus to find the new money each year, in budgets that are already tight. None of these is a reason not to proceed; all of them are reasons the funding must be assembled deliberately and honestly, with the trade-offs acknowledged rather than hidden. A funding plan that admits its costs is more trustworthy, and more durable, than one that promises a free lunch.

The fiscal context makes this harder and demands even more honesty. South Africa's public finances are under genuine strain — debt-service costs consume a growing share of the budget, economic growth has been weak, and the room for new spending is tighter than it has been in years. It would be dishonest to pretend the youth service could be switched on at full scale into that fiscal reality without displacing other priorities or worsening the debt position. This is, in fact, one of the strongest arguments *for* the phased approach rather than against the service itself: the phasing means the large fiscal commitment is deferred until the economy and the evidence can better support it, while the early phases cost little. It also means the service competes for new money modestly at first and substantially only later — by which point a successful programme can make its own case, and an unsuccessful one will have been stopped at a gate. A constrained fiscus is a reason to build carefully and gradually, not a reason to do nothing while the cost of the cliff compounds. But it is also a reason no honest advocate should promise the mature service tomorrow: the money for it, at scale, depends partly on a stronger economy and a healthier budget than South Africa has today, and saying so plainly is part of deserving to be believed.

## The Return Side: What the Money Buys Back

A cost is only half of a financial picture; the other half is the return, and the youth service has an unusual one because its participants produce value even as they are being invested in. This is the cost-benefit frame, and it must be handled with discipline — claimed in ranges, not inflated into the "pays for itself" slogan the previous section rejected.

The returns come in several forms. There is the **direct service delivered**: every classroom supported, every clinic assisted, every facility built and maintained is a public good produced by the civilian streams, value the state would otherwise have to buy or forgo. There is the **reduced cost of youth idleness**: every young person moved from the NEET population into work, study, or enterprise reduces the fiscal and social costs the previous article described — less spent on social support, less of the crime-related cost the Safety pillar traces, more tax paid. There is the **enterprise and employment created** through the capital door and the skills built. And there is the **stipend multiplier**: money paid to young people in poor households is spent immediately and locally, supporting demand and other livelihoods, so that the stipend works partly as economic stimulus in exactly the places that need it most.

The discipline these returns demand is the discipline of the credible range, and it is worth being explicit about why over-claiming is not just dishonest but self-defeating. It is easy, and tempting, to model the offsets aggressively — to assume that a high share of graduates find sustained work, that every supported classroom translates into measurable learning gains, that crime falls by a confident percentage — and to arrive at the seductive conclusion that the service nearly pays for itself. Resist it. Aggressive offset assumptions are exactly the kind of unfalsifiable optimism that discredits a proposal the moment reality underperforms the model, and they hand critics an easy target. The honest approach models the returns conservatively, in ranges, with the low end assuming modest success, and treats any better outcome as upside rather than as the baseline the case depends on. A proposal that survives on conservative assumptions is robust; one that needs optimistic ones is fragile. The measurement scorecard, discussed next, is what eventually replaces assumed offsets with measured ones — but until those measurements exist, the offsets should be claimed with deliberate modesty.

The disciplined way to use these returns is not to claim they cancel the cost — they do not, and a serious advocate should never pretend otherwise — but to recognise that the *net* cost of the service is meaningfully lower than its gross cost, by an amount that a proper evaluation could estimate within a range. The service is expensive; it is also productive; and the honest figure is the gross cost minus a credible, evidence-based estimate of the value produced. That net figure, not the frightening gross one, is the real price of the service — and establishing it is one of the most important jobs of the measurement system this article turns to next.

## How the Country Would Know It Works: The Scorecard

No amount of public money should be spent on a programme that cannot show what it achieves. Part 1 identified weak measurement as a failure mode of the entire existing youth landscape — programmes that ran on good intentions and unverified claims, unable to demonstrate their results or improve on them. The youth service must be the opposite: an institution built around a public scorecard from its first pilot cohort, so that the country can see, in numbers it can trust, whether the money is working.

A credible scorecard tracks a focused set of outcomes, published regularly and independently verified:

- **Completion.** What share of participants complete the service to standard? A falling completion rate is an early warning that something is wrong.
- **Graduate outcomes.** What share of graduates are in employment, further study, or running an enterprise, six months and a year after completing? This is the single most important measure — it is what the whole exercise is for.
- **Enterprise creation.** How many graduates start businesses through the capital door, and how many of those survive their first years and employ others?
- **Cohesion indicators.** Harder to measure but not impossible — through surveys of participants' attitudes, networks, and sense of national belonging before and after, tracking whether the crucible delivers the cohesion it promises.
- **Cost per outcome.** Not just cost per participant, but cost per *successful* outcome — per graduate in sustained work, per enterprise created — which is the figure that tells the country whether it is getting value, and which improves or worsens over time.
- **Audit outcomes.** Clean, independent audits of the money — whether it was spent as intended and reached whom it was meant to, the first defence against the capture risk.

A word on the hardest of these to measure, because it is the one most likely to be quietly dropped: cohesion. It is tempting to track only the things that are easy to count — completion rates, employment rates — and to let the cohesion claim float free, unmeasured and therefore unfalsifiable. That would be a mistake, because cohesion is one of the two central justifications for the whole model, and a justification that is never tested is a justification that cannot be trusted. Cohesion is hard to measure but not impossible: validated survey instruments can track participants' interracial trust, the breadth of their social networks, their sense of national belonging, and their attitudes toward fellow citizens from other backgrounds, measured before the crucible and again after, and followed up over time. If the service builds cohesion as claimed, those measures should move; if they do not move, that is a finding the country needs, not one it should be allowed to avoid. Holding the cohesion claim to measurement is part of the intellectual honesty the whole series has tried to model — making the proposal falsifiable on its own central promises rather than asserting them as articles of faith.

Two features make the scorecard real rather than decorative. It must be **public** — published where citizens, journalists, and Parliament can see and interrogate it — because measurement that the powerful can hide is no accountability at all. And it must be tied to the **phase gates** of the implementation article: the decision to proceed from one phase to the next is made against the scorecard, so that the measurement has teeth, capable of stopping or redirecting the programme rather than merely describing it. A scorecard that cannot trigger an honest "no" is public relations; one that can is accountability. The Reform Agenda and Human Development pillars of this series develop the same measurement discipline; the youth service must be held to it from day one.

The scorecard also has a political function beyond accountability: it is how a multi-term, beyond-one-government project earns the cross-party trust the implementation article said it would need. A programme whose results are published, independently verified, and tied to clear gates is far harder for a successor government to either quietly gut or cynically inflate, because the numbers are in the open for everyone to check. Transparency is what lets an institution outlive the administration that started it — supporters cannot exaggerate it, opponents cannot strangle it in the dark, and the public can judge it on its record. For a nation-building institution meant to span generations, the public scorecard is not just an accountability tool; it is part of the survival strategy.

## Guarding the Money

A programme dispensing tens of billions of rand, much of it as stipends and enterprise finance, is a target — and in a country with South Africa's experience of state capture, a funding plan that ignored that danger would be naive to the point of negligence. The protections are the ones this series develops throughout, applied to the money.

Benefits and stipends paid automatically against verifiable facts — completion, enrolment — are far harder to skim than discretionary disbursements. The enterprise-finance facility needs rule-based eligibility, transparent criteria, and published records of who received what. Procurement for training, kit, and facilities — historically a prime site of capture — needs the full apparatus of open, competitive, audited process. And the whole system needs independent oversight with real power to investigate and to halt funding where it is being abused. None of this guarantees a clean programme; nothing can. But the difference between a programme designed for transparency from the start and one that bolts on controls after the money is flowing is the difference between a system that mostly resists capture and one that invites it.

For this programme, getting that right is not merely good governance; it is existential, in a way that deserves to be stated bluntly. South Africans have watched, repeatedly, as large public initiatives launched with fine intentions became feeding troughs for the connected. That experience has produced a justified public wariness toward any new mega-programme, and a youth service would face that wariness from the start — fairly. If such a service were captured, the damage would go beyond the stolen money. It would betray the very young people the service exists to serve, teaching a new generation, at the most formative moment of their citizenship, the same bitter lesson about a state that enriches insiders while failing everyone else. A youth service meant to rebuild the bond between young people and their country, that instead modelled corruption to them, would be worse than no service at all. The stakes of clean administration are therefore not only fiscal but moral and civic: the programme's integrity *is* part of its product. This is why the anti-capture design cannot be an afterthought bolted on once the money flows, and why it runs, in this series, through every Part rather than appearing only at the end. Guarding the money is not separate from funding it. A programme that cannot protect its budget does not deserve one — and, in this case, a programme that cannot protect its budget would corrupt the very thing it was built to repair.

## A Tool: The Funding-and-Scorecard One-Pager

The whole of this Part — what it costs, how to pay, how to know — reduces to a single page a citizen or a legislator could hold a government to. For any proposed version of the service, demand these answers on one page:

- **Gross cost**, built bottom-up, per participant and per phase, with assumptions shown.
- **Funding sources**, itemised: how much from consolidating which existing programmes, how much from the skills levy, how much new fiscal allocation, how much co-funded — and what each costs in trade-offs.
- **Net cost**, after a credible, ranged estimate of the value produced.
- **The scorecard**: the specific outcomes that will be published, how often, and verified by whom.
- **The gates**: the thresholds on that scorecard that must be met to proceed from each phase to the next.

If a government — or an opposition, or a manifesto — proposes a national youth service and cannot fill in that one page, you are looking at a slogan, not a plan. If it can, you are looking at something that deserves serious debate. That one-pager is the final tool of this series, and it is the standard to which this very proposal should be held.

## The End of the Series, and the Beginning of the Argument

This series has travelled a long way: from the post-matric cliff and the cohesion deficit, through the model of a universal crucible and two tracks, the lessons of countries that have done this for generations, and the benefits that make service prestigious, to the phasing, the cost, and the funding that decide whether any of it is real. The proposal that emerges is demanding and expensive, and it is offered without the pretence that it is easy or free.

But it is also, this series has argued, a serious answer to a problem South Africa has not solved and cannot afford to keep not solving — a structural crisis of young people stranded after school, a nation that has never shared a room, and a fiscus already paying, year after year, the uncounted cost of doing nothing. The youth service is one possible answer. It may not be the right one; readers will judge, and the six tests of Part 1, the comparative lessons of Part 2, and the funding-and-scorecard one-pager of this Part are offered precisely so that judgement can be informed rather than reflexive.

There is a larger idea running underneath the whole proposal that is worth naming as the series closes, because it sits a little against the grain of how South African politics usually talks. Most political promises are about what the state will *deliver to* citizens — more grants, more services, more jobs created somewhere by someone. Those things matter, and this series' other pillars examine them in detail. But a national youth service is built on a different premise: that nation-building also requires asking something *of* citizens, particularly the young, and that being asked to contribute is not only a burden but a form of inclusion. A young person who is only ever a recipient of the state's services, or its failures, remains a spectator of their own country. A young person who is asked to serve it — and given the means, the training, and the recognition to do so — is made a participant in it. That shift, from spectator to participant, repeated across a generation, is the deepest thing the service is for, beyond the jobs and the skills and even the cohesion. It treats young people not as a problem to be managed or a cost to be minimised, but as citizens with something to give, whom the country has so far failed to ask. There is a quiet respect in that framing which the language of "youth unemployment" entirely misses, and it is, in the end, the heart of why a proposal this demanding might nonetheless be worth the demand.

What should not survive this series is the easy dismissal — "we can't afford it," "government can't run it," "young people won't do it" — offered without engaging the structured case against each. Those objections have answers, set out here: phasing for the capacity, a bottom-up cost and a real funding mix for the affordability, and benefits and prestige for the uptake. Whether the answers persuade you is for you to decide. That you now have a concrete, costed, honestly-qualified proposal to decide *about* — rather than a slogan to cheer or jeer — is the most this series set out to achieve.

It is worth saying, finally, what this series has *not* claimed, because the discipline of a good argument is as much about its limits as its reach. It has not claimed the youth service is certain to work — only that it is designed to pass the tests its predecessors failed, and built to be stopped if the evidence turns against it. It has not claimed it is cheap — only that its cost can be honestly built, substantially funded from existing spending, and weighed against the larger cost of inaction. It has not claimed it is easy — the implementation article was candid that it is a project of a decade or more, demanding capacity, money, and a cross-party patience South Africa finds difficult. And it has not claimed it is the only answer to the crisis of the young; it is one serious answer, offered for scrutiny alongside whatever others the country can devise. What it has tried to do is the thing this whole publication exists to do: turn a vague national anxiety into a concrete, examinable proposition that citizens can hold, test, and argue over on the evidence. The argument starts now.
