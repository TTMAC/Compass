## Three Scorecards in Part 4, One Cycle to Turn

Part 4 of this series has built three different measurement frameworks. [The Municipal Performance Index](/articles/eg-4-1-the-municipal-performance-index) gave you a single 0-to-100 score for each of South Africa's 257 municipalities, anchored in four pillars and assembled from publicly available data. [The Metro Rankings](/articles/eg-4-2-the-metro-rankings) put that index to work on the eight metros, showing how Cape Town's 84.0 and Johannesburg's 52.2 emerged from the same methodology applied to two cities that operate under identical national rules. [Infrastructure Planning vs Reality](/articles/eg-4-3-infrastructure-planning-vs-reality) added the national layer — a sector-by-sector tracker for energy, water, transport, and digital infrastructure that compares what was promised against what has been built.

Each framework is useful on its own. The MPI tells you whether the place you live in is governed competently. The metro rankings tell you how your city compares to its peers. The infrastructure tracker tells you whether the strategic projects that underpin every other reform are moving from cabinet memo to construction site. **But none of them, individually, tells you whether the system that connects them is turning.** A metro can hold a Clean Audit and still preside over a stagnating local economy. A municipality can execute its capital budget at 95 per cent and still lose half of its water to leaks. A Strategic Integrated Project can hit its construction milestones and still fail to translate into household-level service improvements. The scorecards in Part 4 measure outputs at specific points in the system. They do not, by themselves, measure whether the system as a whole is producing the cumulative outcome that growth is supposed to produce: rising real incomes that fund education and health, education and health that build the capable workforce, and a capable workforce that sustains the next round of growth.

That is what the [Reinforcing Cycle Scorecard](/reform/scorecard) at the gateway level does — and what its second link, **Growth → Development**, measures specifically. This article connects the two layers. It explains how the municipal and infrastructure scorecards in Part 4 feed the national Growth → Development indicators, why a scorecard without a reform roadmap is a diagnosis without an intervention, and how the [Reform Roadmap](/reform/roadmap) closes the loop by specifying the 80-plus concrete reforms that, if executed in sequence, would move the indicators on every layer. By the end you should be able to read all three layers — local MPI, national cycle, reform programme — as a single integrated measurement architecture rather than three disconnected dashboards.

## The Three Measurement Layers, Side by Side

Before the synthesis, a short recap of what each Part 4 framework measures, expressed deliberately in terms that make the layering visible.

The first layer is the **Municipal Performance Index**. It operates at the level of the 257 municipalities. It scores each municipality on four pillars — Economic Development, Service Delivery, Governance and Finance, and Infrastructure and Sustainability — using a weighted geometric mean that penalises unbalanced performance. Its data comes from the Auditor-General, National Treasury's Section 71 reports, Stats SA, the Department of Water and Sanitation, and a small number of additional public datasets. The MPI's question is narrow but consequential: is this place being governed in a way that makes growth and service delivery possible?

The second layer is the **Metro Rankings**, which is not really a separate framework so much as the worked application of the MPI to the eight metropolitan municipalities, with peer comparisons that surface the variation between cities operating under identical national rules. Its question is comparative: which metros are converging upward, which are diverging downward, and what does the gap between them tell you about the difference governance makes?

The third layer is the **Infrastructure Tracker** built in [Infrastructure Planning vs Reality](/articles/eg-4-3-infrastructure-planning-vs-reality). It operates at the national level rather than the municipal one, because infrastructure planning is co-ordinated through Infrastructure South Africa, the National Infrastructure Plan, and the Strategic Integrated Projects framework — institutions that sit above any single municipality. It scores each major sector — energy, water, transport, digital — against five McKinsey delivery dimensions: selection, finance, delivery, regulation, and capability. Its question is structural: is the country building the bulk infrastructure that the municipalities then plug into, or are the plans accumulating faster than the pipelines, substations, and rail corridors that those plans describe?

These three layers measure different things, at different geographic scales, with different time horizons. The MPI is updated annually, lags audited data by about twelve months, and reflects governance quality over the financial year. The metro rankings inherit that cadence. The infrastructure tracker moves on a longer cycle — most strategic projects measure their progress in years, not quarters, and the gap between plan and delivery is therefore measured in years too. **The cumulative effect of all three layers, operating together, determines whether the local economy in any given place is growing or shrinking.** A municipality with a high MPI inside a country that is delivering its infrastructure plans will see its economic indicators improve over time. A municipality with a low MPI inside a country that is failing to deliver its infrastructure plans will see its economic indicators stagnate or decline. The layers compound.

## Why Municipal Scorecards Alone Don't Tell You Whether the Cycle Is Turning

The MPI is designed to make municipal performance comparable. It is not designed to tell you whether South Africa as a whole is moving from the $7,000 per-capita economy described in [The R7,000 Economy](/articles/eg-1-1-the-seven-thousand-rand-economy) toward the $15,000 economy that Stage 1 of the growth arc targets. That question requires national indicators, not municipal ones. And it requires indicators that measure not just inputs (budgets allocated, plans approved) or outputs (audits passed, kilometres of pipeline laid) but **outcomes** — the changes in household-level reality that the reform programme exists to produce.

Consider an illustrative scenario. Imagine, hypothetically, that the average MPI score across South Africa's metros rose from 60 to 70 over five years. Every metro improved its audit outcomes. Capital expenditure execution climbed. Service delivery indicators moved upward. By the metric the MPI is designed to capture, the country would be governing itself better. **But the MPI improving does not, by itself, tell you whether real GDP per capita is growing, whether real per-learner education spending is rising, or whether expanded unemployment is falling.** Those are the questions the Growth → Development scorecard is built to answer, and they cannot be answered from MPI data alone, because the MPI does not measure them.

This is why the gateway-level Reinforcing Cycle Scorecard exists. It sits above the function-specific and place-specific scorecards in the rest of the site, and it tracks **outcome indicators** that capture whether the system as a whole is producing the cumulative effect that all the local-level reforms are supposed to add up to. If the MPI is improving but the cycle is not turning, that tells you that the governance reforms are happening but failing to translate into the economic and developmental outcomes they were supposed to enable — which is itself a critical piece of diagnostic information.

## The Growth → Development Link: Five National Indicators

The second link in the [Reinforcing Cycle Scorecard](/reform/scorecard#growth-development) — labelled Growth → Development — asks a precise question: is economic growth generating the fiscal space needed to fund education, health, and social investment in real terms, and is that funding reaching the front line rather than being captured by debt service? It answers that question with five indicators, each drawn from a public data source, each set against a threshold target, and each tracked against a baseline that reflects current performance.

The first indicator is **real GDP per capita growth**, sourced from Stats SA and the South African Reserve Bank. The threshold is above 1.5 per cent annually, against a baseline of approximately zero. The most recent reading at the time the scorecard was last reviewed was approximately negative 0.3 per cent — meaning the average South African was, on a real per-person basis, slightly poorer than the year before. The trend is recorded as worsening. This is the single most important macro indicator in the link, because everything else in the chain depends on it. Without per-capita growth, fiscal space contracts. Without fiscal space, the spending indicators in the link become impossible to move regardless of how well the budget is managed.

The second indicator is **real per-learner education spending**, sourced from National Treasury's Estimates of National Expenditure. The threshold is a year-on-year real increase; the baseline is real decline. The most recent reading is real decline, and the trend is worsening. This indicator captures whether the additional fiscal space — when growth does generate it — is being directed toward the human-capital investment that closes the third link of the cycle. When real per-learner spending falls, the schooling system loses absorptive capacity even before learner numbers are accounted for. Combined with the demographic pressure of growing cohorts, the per-learner squeeze translates into larger classes, fewer textbooks, deferred maintenance, and the slow erosion of learning outcomes that the third link of the cycle then tries to measure.

The third indicator is **real per-capita health spending**, also sourced from the ENE. Threshold and baseline mirror the education indicator: year-on-year real increase from real decline. The current reading is real decline; the trend is worsening. The fourth is **debt service as a percentage of revenue**, drawn from National Treasury's headline fiscal data. The threshold is below 15 per cent, against a baseline of approximately 22 per cent. The most recent reading sits at approximately 22 per cent, and the trend is worsening. This indicator is the fiscal equivalent of a vital sign: when more than one rand in every five of revenue goes to interest payments, the budget becomes structurally unable to fund the human-capital investment the cycle depends on, regardless of political will.

The fifth indicator is **expanded unemployment**, sourced from the Quarterly Labour Force Survey. The threshold is below 35 per cent, against a baseline of approximately 42 per cent. The most recent reading is approximately 41.9 per cent, and the trend is recorded as stalled. Expanded unemployment is the labour-market translation of the per-capita growth indicator. Even when the economy expands in absolute terms, if growth is not labour-absorbing, the unemployment indicator does not move — and the political and social consequences of mass unemployment continue to compound, no matter what the headline GDP figure shows.

These five indicators are designed to be read together. They form a chain: per-capita growth generates the fiscal space, debt service determines how much of that space is captured by the past, the education and health spending indicators show whether the remaining space is being directed at the future, and the unemployment indicator shows whether growth is reaching households in the form that matters most — paid work. **When all five indicators are improving, the link from growth to development is intact. When even one is broken, the link is interrupted, and the cycle stops compounding.** The current readings — four worsening, one stalled — are the empirical confirmation that the link is, at the time of the latest scorecard review, not turning.

## How Municipal Performance Feeds the National Indicators

The connection between the municipal-level MPI and the national-level Growth → Development indicators is neither automatic nor mechanical, but it is real, and it runs through specific causal channels.

The first channel is **revenue collection**. Municipalities that collect their rates and service charges efficiently generate the own-revenue base that funds local infrastructure maintenance and reduces dependence on national transfers. When the MPI's Governance and Finance pillar shows debt collection rates above 95 per cent in places like Cape Town and below 60 per cent in deeply distressed municipalities, the difference is not just an accounting one. Strong municipal revenue collection reduces the fiscal burden on the national equitable share and conditional grants system, which in turn relieves pressure on the national budget that the Growth → Development indicators are tracking. Weak municipal revenue collection forces national government to make up the shortfall through transfers, which competes directly with the education and health spending captured in indicators two and three of the link.

The second channel is **service delivery quality**. The MPI's Service Delivery pillar — measured through access to water, sanitation, electricity, and refuse removal, plus quality indicators like the Blue Drop and Green Drop scores — feeds directly into the foundations on which any local economy depends. A small business cannot operate in a township where load shedding is unscheduled and the municipal grid is unmaintained. A clinic cannot meet its catchment population's needs if the municipal water supply is unreliable. The expanded unemployment indicator in the national scorecard cannot improve if municipal service delivery collapses to the point where firms relocate, contract, or close. The MPI's Service Delivery scores are, in this sense, leading indicators of the national unemployment indicator.

The third channel is **infrastructure execution**. The MPI's Infrastructure and Sustainability pillar — capital expenditure execution, non-revenue water, public transport coverage — connects directly to the national infrastructure tracker built in [Infrastructure Planning vs Reality](/articles/eg-4-3-infrastructure-planning-vs-reality). Municipalities are the last-mile delivery agents for most national infrastructure investment. A bulk water pipeline that terminates at the municipal boundary achieves nothing if the municipal distribution network is losing 40 per cent of what it carries. A new power station achieves nothing if the municipal electricity department cannot maintain the substations that deliver power to homes. When the infrastructure tracker shows national delivery falling short of plan, and when municipal MPI scores show that the last-mile networks cannot absorb what national infrastructure does deliver, the combined effect is to suppress the per-capita growth indicator that anchors the entire Growth → Development link.

The fourth channel is **economic development**. The MPI's Economic Development pillar measures local unemployment, business registrations, LED budget execution, and other indicators of local economic vitality. Aggregated across the 257 municipalities, these local-level indicators sum to the national labour-market and growth figures that the Growth → Development link tracks at the macro level. A national expanded unemployment rate of 41.9 per cent is, when you decompose it, a weighted average of vastly different local labour markets — from metros with broad unemployment in the high 20s to rural municipalities with broad unemployment above 50 per cent. The national indicator moves when the underlying local distribution moves. The MPI provides the disaggregation that lets you see which parts of the country are pulling the national indicator up or down.

The connection between the layers is therefore not just rhetorical. The municipal indicators feed the national indicators through these four channels, and you can — at least in principle — trace any movement in the national scorecard back to underlying movements in the MPI distribution. **What you cannot do is rely on the national scorecard alone to tell you where to act, or on the MPI alone to tell you whether your actions are working.** You need both, plus the infrastructure tracker, to see the full picture of where the system is improving, where it is stalling, and where it is going backwards.

## Diagnosis Without Intervention: Why Scorecards Need a Reform Roadmap

A scorecard, no matter how well constructed, is a diagnostic instrument. It tells you what is happening. It does not, by itself, tell you what to do. If real GDP per capita growth is at minus 0.3 per cent and worsening, the scorecard has done its job — it has named the problem. But the question of what specific institutional changes would move that indicator from minus 0.3 to plus 1.5 over five years is not a question the scorecard can answer. That question requires a reform programme: a sequenced set of concrete interventions, assigned to specific institutions, with timelines, legislative requirements, and accountability mechanisms.

This is the role of the [Reform Roadmap](/reform/roadmap). It is structured as four workstreams — safety and security, reform agenda, economic growth, and human development — phased over ten years across three implementation stages and one foundational architecture stage. It contains 80-plus specific reforms, each linked to the article in this series that explains the underlying analysis, each tagged to a phase and a sub-group within its workstream. The legislative programme that accompanies it specifies new statutes, amendments to existing legislation, and possible constitutional amendments, each tied to the specific reforms they enable.

The relationship between the scorecard and the roadmap is symmetric. The scorecard, on its own, is a thermometer. The roadmap, on its own, is a treatment plan. Each is necessary; neither is sufficient. A roadmap without a scorecard is a plan with no feedback loop — you cannot tell whether the treatment is working. A scorecard without a roadmap is a diagnosis with no intervention — you can watch the indicators deteriorate without being able to do anything about them. **The synthesis Part 4 has been building toward is the recognition that the MPI, the metro rankings, and the infrastructure tracker need to be read alongside the Growth → Development scorecard and the Reform Roadmap, not as separate instruments but as components of a single measurement-and-intervention architecture.**

The Reform Roadmap also makes explicit what the scorecards leave implicit: the **sequencing** of reform. Indicators on the scorecard cannot all move at once, because the interventions that would move them depend on other interventions that have not yet been completed. You cannot expect real per-learner education spending to rise if the fiscal space has not been generated by per-capita growth. You cannot expect per-capita growth to rise if the infrastructure foundations are not in place. You cannot expect the infrastructure foundations to be delivered if the institutional architecture that would deliver them is not yet built. The roadmap's phase structure — Phase 1 foundations, Phase 2 activation, Phase 3 scaling — encodes this sequencing. Reading the scorecard without the roadmap leaves you with the impression that all indicators should be moving simultaneously. Reading the roadmap alongside the scorecard tells you which indicators should be moving first, and which ones you should expect to lag.

## The Economic Growth Workstream: From Phase 1 to Phase 3

The economic-growth workstream within the Reform Roadmap contains nineteen specific reforms organised across three phases and seven sub-groups. Reading them in sequence shows how the roadmap is designed to move the Growth → Development indicators in a specific order.

**Phase 1** focuses on removing barriers and building the institutional infrastructure for growth. Its first sub-group, MSME Ecosystem Restructuring, contains three reforms: integrating the fragmented MSME support agencies (SEDA, SEFA, the dtic, NEF, IDC) into a single demand-responsive system; regulatory simplification with a 48-hour target for business registration; and extending banking and payment systems to informal businesses. Its second sub-group, Credit and Financing Architecture, contains the Small Business Guarantee Fund (modelled on the US SBA 7(a) programme), alternative financing mechanisms (supply chain financing, invoice discounting, microfinance, peer-to-peer lending), and venture capital mobilisation through tax incentives and matching funds. These six reforms target the small-firm employment channel that drives the expanded unemployment indicator. They do not move the indicator immediately — there is a lag between regulatory simplification and the businesses that eventually register, employ, and grow — but they create the institutional conditions without which the indicator cannot move sustainably.

**Phase 2** activates the three engines of growth. Its New Venture Creation sub-group includes startup ecosystems modelled on Chile's Start-Up Chile and dedicated township and rural enterprise programmes. Its Export Diversification sub-group contains four cluster reforms — platinum (catalytic converters, fuel cell components, emission control systems), automotive (high-value components, aerospace components, rail rolling stock), agricultural (essential oils, fruit-based beverages, processed foods), and services (fintech, insurtech, mining services consulting, edtech). Its Trade and Market Access sub-group covers trade agreement optimisation and AfCFTA positioning. These seven reforms target the per-capita growth indicator more directly, because they generate the higher-complexity export earnings and the new ventures that compound into broader employment.

**Phase 3** scales successful programmes and builds self-sustaining growth ecosystems. Its Metro-Level Economic Development sub-group contains two reforms drawn directly from the Part 4 measurement architecture: a Metro Economic Rankings system (the operationalisation of the MPI for the eight metros) and Metro Growth Compacts that bind national government and metros to defined targets with consequences. Its Institutional Quality for Growth sub-group contains regulatory quality reforms, competition and market access reforms (strengthening enforcement against the concentration that suppresses MSME entry), and political economy management — addressing the rent-seeking and patronage networks that distort resource allocation and which [The Political Economy of Growth](/articles/eg-5-1-the-political-economy-of-growth) examines in detail. The Phase 3 reforms are the ones that lock in the gains from the earlier phases by changing the institutional environment rather than the specific policies. They are also the ones that, if not implemented, leave the earlier reforms vulnerable to reversal.

The legislative programme that accompanies the economic-growth workstream includes the Small Business Guarantee Fund Act as a new statute. Other reforms operate through existing legislation or through amendments and regulatory changes that do not require new primary law. The phasing is deliberate: the institutional foundations in Phase 1 must be in place before the activation reforms in Phase 2 can produce their intended effects, and the scaling reforms in Phase 3 cannot succeed without the Phase 2 activation. The same logic applies to the indicators: you should expect the small-firm employment indicators to move first as Phase 1 reforms take effect, the per-capita growth and export indicators to move next as Phase 2 reforms compound, and the metro-level and institutional indicators to move last as Phase 3 reforms institutionalise the gains.

## The Triangular Reading: How to Use All Three Together

The synthesis this article has been building toward is what might be called the triangular reading. At any given moment, the question of whether South Africa is moving toward the growth and development outcomes that Part 4 of this series targets requires three readings, taken together.

The first reading is the **MPI distribution**, taken from the framework in [The Municipal Performance Index](/articles/eg-4-1-the-municipal-performance-index) and applied at the metro level in [The Metro Rankings](/articles/eg-4-2-the-metro-rankings). This tells you, place by place, where governance is improving and where it is collapsing. It is the local-level diagnostic.

The second reading is the **national infrastructure tracker** from [Infrastructure Planning vs Reality](/articles/eg-4-3-infrastructure-planning-vs-reality). This tells you, sector by sector, where the bulk infrastructure that local economies depend on is being delivered against plan and where the gap between plan and delivery is widening. It is the national input diagnostic.

The third reading is the **Growth → Development scorecard** at [reform-scorecard#growth-development](/reform/scorecard#growth-development). This tells you, indicator by indicator, whether the cumulative effect of the local-level governance and the national-level infrastructure delivery is translating into the macro outcomes that define whether the cycle is turning. It is the national outcome diagnostic.

Each reading is anchored to specific reforms on the [Reform Roadmap](/reform/roadmap). When the MPI distribution shows a cluster of municipalities with deteriorating Governance and Finance scores, the reforms in the reform-agenda and economic-growth workstreams targeted at municipal capability are the ones to track. When the infrastructure tracker shows a sector falling further behind plan, the relevant infrastructure-execution reforms in Phase 1 and the institutional reforms in Phase 3 are the ones to pressure for implementation. When the Growth → Development indicators show debt service consuming more than 20 per cent of revenue, the macroeconomic-management reforms that sit at the intersection of the reform-agenda and economic-growth workstreams become the binding constraint. The roadmap is the bridge between any reading on any scorecard and the specific intervention that, if implemented, would move that reading.

This is the same logic that the Scorecard itself anticipates when it notes that it is designed to be tabled annually in Parliament from Year 5 of the reform programme onwards, as part of the Reform Coordination Office's expanded cycle-management mandate described in Phase 4 of [The Integrated Reform Programme](/reform/articles/ra-5-3-the-integrated-reform-programme). The annual tabling is what makes the scorecard a live instrument rather than a static dashboard. It forces an annual public account of which indicators have moved, in which direction, by how much, and what the response will be. The Reform Coordination Office's role is to take the scorecard readings, cross-reference them against the roadmap, and report to Parliament on whether the sequenced reform programme is producing the expected indicator movements or whether the sequencing needs to be revisited.

## What the Citizen Should Do With This

If you are a citizen, a journalist, a councillor, a researcher, or a member of an advocacy organisation, the triangular reading gives you a practical toolkit. Start with the MPI for your own municipality — find your municipality's score across the four pillars, identify the pillar with the weakest score, and use it as the diagnostic that anchors your local advocacy. If your municipality scores poorly on Governance and Finance, the reforms targeting municipal capability are the ones to demand from your council. If it scores poorly on Service Delivery, the reforms targeting bulk infrastructure and last-mile distribution are the ones to track at the national level through the infrastructure tracker.

Then move outward. Read the Growth → Development indicators to see what is happening at the national level. If real per-learner education spending is in decline while your municipality's MPI is improving, the constraint is not local — it is the national fiscal squeeze, and the reforms that would relieve it sit in the macroeconomic and political-economy parts of the roadmap. If your municipality's MPI is deteriorating while the national indicators are moving in the right direction, the constraint is local, and the reforms that would relieve it sit in the municipal-capability parts of the roadmap.

Then move to the Reform Roadmap and identify the specific reforms that target the constraints you have diagnosed. The reforms are tagged by workstream and phase, linked to the underlying article in this series, and accompanied by the legislative changes required to make them real. The roadmap converts the diagnosis from the scorecards into a concrete agenda for advocacy: not a vague demand that government should "do better" but a specific call for the implementation of a named reform within a named phase, supported by a named piece of legislation, tracked against a named indicator.

This is how a measurement architecture becomes a tool for accountability rather than a static record of decline. The three readings — local, national input, national outcome — give you a complete picture of what is happening. The roadmap gives you the bridge from picture to action. Parliament's annual scorecard tabling, once the reform programme reaches Year 5, gives you a regular public moment at which the bridge can be inspected. **The citizen's role is to use all three together, and to refuse to accept any single reading as the whole story.**

## The Bridge to Part 5

Part 4 has built the measurement architecture. Part 5 turns to the question of why, even when the architecture is built and the indicators are clear, the reforms that would move the indicators are so difficult to implement. [The Political Economy of Growth](/articles/eg-5-1-the-political-economy-of-growth) examines the winners and losers of economic reform, the social compact that any successful reform programme must construct, and the trade diplomacy and parliamentary accountability mechanisms that distinguish countries that have moved from middle-income to high-income status from those that have not.

The scorecard synthesis you have just read does not, on its own, produce reform. It produces clarity about what reform would have to achieve. The political economy that Part 5 begins with is the explanation of why clarity alone has never been enough — and what additional conditions, beyond clarity, have to be assembled for reform to actually happen. The measurement architecture is necessary. It is not sufficient. The remaining articles in this series, beginning with the next one, address what sufficiency would require.

Until then, the Part 4 synthesis stands as the closing argument of the measurement case: the MPI tells you about places, the infrastructure tracker tells you about sectors, the Growth → Development scorecard tells you about outcomes, and the Reform Roadmap tells you what to do. Read them together, and the question of whether South Africa is moving toward the economy and society its Constitution promises becomes a question with an answer — an answer you can test against the public data every year, and an answer that, when it comes back wrong, points you directly to the reforms that would change it.
