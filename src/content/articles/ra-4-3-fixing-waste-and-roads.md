## The Failures You Can See From Your Front Door

You do not need the RAARICLE™ framework to know that waste collection and road maintenance are failing across South Africa. You can see it. The uncollected refuse piling up on street corners in townships. The illegal dump sites growing on vacant land. The potholes that swallow tyres on roads that were last resurfaced a decade ago. The stormwater drains blocked with rubbish that flood every time it rains. These are the municipal failures that South Africans encounter every day — the ones that shape their experience of government more directly than any policy document or budget speech.

## Two Functions, One Pattern

The Automobile Association estimated in April 2025 that South Africa has more than 25 million potholes — a 67 per cent increase over five years. The SAICE Infrastructure Report Card 2022 graded municipal paved roads at D and unpaved roads at E — unfit for purpose. Only 60 per cent of households nationally have access to regular refuse removal, a figure that drops to 12.5 per cent in rural areas. More than 80 per cent of municipal landfill sites do not meet minimum regulatory requirements. Nearly half of the country's approximately 1,327 documented waste disposal sites are unlicensed.

These two functions — waste management and municipal roads — are combined in a single article not because they are the same service but because the RAARICLE™ diagnosis reveals that they fail for the same structural reasons. The failure pattern is identical: resource starvation (revenue not ring-fenced, maintenance chronically underfunded), capability collapse (qualified professionals absent), and enforcement absence (non-compliance carries no automatic consequences). If you understand why one fails, you understand why the other fails. And if you can fix the structural conditions for one, the same fix applies to the other.

This shared diagnosis matters because it reveals something important about municipal failure in South Africa. The problem is not function-specific. It is not that waste management is uniquely difficult or that roads are uniquely expensive. The problem is that the municipal institutional architecture — the way resources flow, the way capability is (or is not) deployed, the way enforcement does (or does not) work — produces the same outcome regardless of which function you examine. As [The Provincial Performance Crisis](/articles/ra-3-1-provincial-performance-crisis) showed for the provincial sphere, the consistency of failure across functions tells you that the cause is structural, not managerial.

:::takeaway
Waste management and municipal roads share an identical RAARICLE™ failure pattern: resource starvation, capability collapse, and enforcement absence. The consistency of this pattern across two very different services confirms that the cause is structural — rooted in the municipal institutional architecture itself — not a function-specific management problem that better officials could solve.
:::

## Waste Management: The Value Chain and Diagnosis

### The Value Chain

Waste management flows through three institutional layers. The Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment sets national policy, establishes norms and standards for waste management, and administers the National Environmental Management: Waste Act of 2008. DFFE is responsible for the national waste management strategy, the licensing framework for waste disposal facilities, and the South African Waste Information System that is supposed to aggregate municipal waste data.

Provincial environmental departments handle the licensing of waste management facilities — landfills, transfer stations, and recycling facilities must be licensed by the provincial authority. Provincial departments are also supposed to monitor compliance with licence conditions and enforce environmental regulations at waste disposal sites.

Municipalities do the work. They collect waste from households and businesses, transport it to transfer stations or directly to landfill sites, and manage the disposal facilities. In metropolitan municipalities, the responsibility is clear — the metro handles everything from kerbside collection to landfill management. In district and local municipality areas, the responsibility may sit with either the district or the local municipality, depending on how the district has allocated functions under the Municipal Structures Act.

The chain is shorter than water's and simpler than electricity's. But it breaks at the municipal level for the same reason the others do: the municipality that is supposed to collect waste, manage landfills, and comply with environmental regulations lacks the resources, the capability, and the enforcement pressure to do so.

### Responsibility — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

The responsibility assignment is reasonably clear. Waste management is a Schedule 5 Part B function — exclusively assigned to local government. Unlike water (where the WSA/WSP conflation muddies responsibility) or electricity (where the Eskom/municipality boundary creates confusion), waste management has a single responsible entity: the municipality. There is no conflation problem.

The tension comes from the district/local split. In areas served by district municipalities, the allocation of the waste function between the district and its constituent local municipalities is not always clear. Some districts have assumed the waste function; others have left it with local municipalities that lack the scale to manage it efficiently. A small rural local municipality with 30,000 residents cannot justify the cost of a licensed landfill, a fleet of collection vehicles, and qualified waste management staff — but it is responsible for providing the service regardless.

### Accountability — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

The municipal council is accountable for waste management. Councillors approve the budget, set refuse removal charges, and oversee the department that manages collection and disposal. But the accountability chain is weak because waste management is politically invisible in a specific way — it only becomes visible when it fails. A functioning waste collection service generates no political benefit. Uncollected waste generates political crisis. This asymmetry means that councils allocate the minimum budget needed to prevent visible crisis rather than the budget needed to sustain the service properly.

The Auditor-General audits municipal waste expenditure as part of the MFMA cycle, but the audit focuses on financial compliance, not service quality. A municipality can spend its waste budget in compliance with procurement regulations while its landfill contaminates the local groundwater. The environmental accountability — whether the municipality is complying with its waste management licence conditions, whether landfill operations meet environmental standards, whether waste is being disposed of safely — falls to the provincial environmental department, which typically lacks the capacity to monitor compliance across dozens of municipalities.

### Authority — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

The municipality has the authority to set refuse removal charges, manage collection logistics, and operate disposal facilities. The 30-day authority test is modestly positive: a municipal manager who identifies a collection route failure can reassign vehicles and adjust schedules within days. This is more operational authority than a provincial MEC has over education or a WSA has over water tariffs.

The constraint is on the disposal side. A municipality that needs a new or expanded landfill must obtain an environmental licence from the provincial department — a process that can take years, involves environmental impact assessments, public participation, and technical studies, and requires expertise that most municipalities do not have in-house. A municipality whose existing landfill is reaching capacity cannot simply open a new site. The licensing process creates an authority bottleneck that can leave municipalities operating unlicensed facilities because the legal alternative takes too long.

### Resources — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

Waste management revenue — refuse removal charges collected from households and businesses — is not effectively ring-fenced. Like electricity and water revenue, it flows into the general municipal account and competes with every other spending priority. Landfill management, the most capital-intensive part of the waste function, is chronically underfunded because it generates no visible political return. A councillor who redirects landfill maintenance funds to a more visible project faces no immediate consequence — the landfill's environmental compliance deteriorates slowly, over years, until it reaches crisis.

The resource gap extends to collection coverage. Only 60 per cent of households have access to regular refuse removal. In rural areas, the figure drops to 12.5 per cent. Over 5 million households lack regular waste collection entirely. Extending collection to these households requires vehicles, staff, fuel, and transfer infrastructure that municipalities cannot fund from current revenue. The Municipal Infrastructure Grant can fund waste infrastructure (transfer stations, landfill cells), but municipalities often struggle to spend it — not because the money is unavailable but because they lack the project management capacity to plan, procure, and build. Makana Municipality left 83 per cent of its R27.4 million MIG allocation unspent in 2022/23.

The economics of waste management are further complicated by the extended producer responsibility regulations that came into effect in May 2021. EPR requires producers and importers of packaging to fund the collection and recycling of their products, and currently diverts approximately 3.2 million tonnes of waste from landfills annually. But the EPR system operates alongside — not integrated with — municipal waste management. The interaction between municipal collection, EPR-funded recycling, and informal waste pickers who form a critical part of the recycling value chain is uncoordinated, creating overlaps and gaps.

### Information — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

The South African Waste Information System is supposed to aggregate municipal waste data nationally. Municipalities must submit monthly summaries of waste quantities received at disposal facilities. In practice, reporting compliance is poor — DFFE has acknowledged the need for "regulatory interventions to make reporting to SAWIS mandatory" and a "training and outreach strategy to improve capacity to report, particularly on the part of municipalities." The national waste generation figure — more than 125 million tonnes annually — is acknowledged as an estimate based on unreliable data. Many municipalities do not know how much waste they collect, how much reaches the landfill, what the remaining landfill capacity is, or when the facility will reach its end of life.

At the municipal level, waste management information systems are rudimentary or absent. Few municipalities track collection routes with GPS. Few weigh waste at the landfill gate. Few have landfill airspace surveys that tell them how many years of capacity remain. Without this data, the municipality cannot plan — it cannot estimate when it will need a new landfill, cannot optimise collection routes, and cannot calculate whether refuse removal charges cover the true cost of service.

### Capability — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

Section 10 of the National Environmental Management: Waste Act requires every municipality to designate a Waste Management Officer from its administration. The WMO must coordinate all matters pertaining to waste management in the municipality. Compliance with this requirement is uneven — no centralised data exists on how many municipalities have actually designated a WMO, and the Act prescribes no specific qualification standard for the role.

The capability gap goes beyond the WMO. Landfill management requires specific technical knowledge — liner systems, leachate management, gas extraction, daily cover, compaction, cell construction, groundwater monitoring. A properly managed landfill is an engineered facility. An improperly managed one is a contamination source. The Green Drop equivalent for waste — a systematic assessment of whether disposal facilities meet technical and environmental standards — does not exist at a comparable level of rigour. The finding that 80 per cent of municipal landfills do not meet minimum requirements tells you what the capability deficit produces.

The Institute of Municipal Engineering of Southern Africa reported in February 2026 that no municipality in the country meets the Municipal Staff Regulations' requirement of a 3&#58;1 ratio of technical to support staff. The best-performing municipalities achieve roughly 1&#58;1. This deficit affects every technical function — waste, roads, water, electricity — but in waste management, where the consequences of capability failure include groundwater contamination and illegal dumping, the deficit is particularly dangerous.

### Legitimacy — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

Waste management has a clear constitutional mandate — Schedule 5 Part B assigns refuse removal, refuse dumps, and solid waste to local government. The democratic chain exists. But waste management lacks the powerful constitutional right that water has (Section 27) and the revenue significance that electricity has. It sits in a political middle ground — important enough that failure generates complaints, but not important enough to attract the investment, attention, or reform energy that water and electricity receive.

### Enforcement — Rating: 1 (Critical Failure)

The enforcement failure in waste management is the starkest in any municipal function. Nearly half of South Africa's waste disposal sites are unlicensed. Operating an unlicensed waste disposal facility is a criminal offence under the Waste Act. And yet these sites operate year after year, decade after decade, with minimal consequences. In Mpumalanga alone, 20 landfill sites operate without licences. An audit of 127 landfill sites found 78 per cent non-compliant with minimum requirements.

Provincial environmental departments are supposed to enforce compliance. But they are understaffed, under-resourced, and politically reluctant to prosecute municipalities that fall within their province. DFFE can issue compliance notices and prosecute — but national enforcement of municipal waste compliance is the exception, not the norm. The enforcement architecture for waste management is entirely discretionary, entirely under-resourced, and entirely ineffective.

:::expert
The Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment's own assessment of waste management compliance has acknowledged the scale of the enforcement failure. Of approximately 1,327 documented waste disposal sites nationally, only 19 per cent are compliant with regulations. The department has identified the need for regulatory interventions to strengthen mandatory reporting to the South African Waste Information System and for increased provincial enforcement capacity. However, the enforcement tools available — compliance notices, directives, and criminal prosecution — remain discretionary instruments that require the provincial or national authority to decide to act. No automatic triggers exist: a landfill site can operate unlicensed for years without activating a mandatory consequence. The gap between the legal framework (which is comprehensive) and its enforcement (which is negligible) is the defining feature of waste governance in South Africa.
:::

## Municipal Roads and Stormwater: The Value Chain and Diagnosis

### The Value Chain

South Africa's road network spans approximately 317,000 kilometres across three institutional layers. The South African National Roads Agency manages 21,403 kilometres of national roads — the arterial network that connects cities and major economic centres. Provincial roads departments manage roughly 205,000 kilometres of provincial roads (47,348 km paved, 157,482 km unpaved). Municipalities manage approximately 90,700 kilometres of municipal roads, plus the stormwater drainage infrastructure that protects those roads from water damage.

The institutional separation is clear: SANRAL handles national roads, provinces handle provincial roads, municipalities handle municipal roads. The problem is not who is responsible — it is that the entity responsible for municipal roads (the municipality) cannot fund, maintain, or manage the 90,700 kilometres of road it owns.

SANRAL demonstrates what is possible when the institutional architecture is aligned. SANRAL is a professionally managed entity with ring-fenced revenue (from the fuel levy and tolls), qualified engineers, modern asset management systems, and the authority to plan and execute maintenance programmes. SANRAL reports that all reported potholes on national roads are fixed within 14 days. SANRAL spent R32 billion on its non-toll network (R18 billion capital, R14 billion maintenance) and plans R100 billion over the medium term. SANRAL's national roads receive a SAICE grade of B+.

Municipal roads receive a SAICE grade of D for paved roads in major urban areas, D- for other municipalities' paved roads, and E — unfit for purpose — for unpaved roads. The difference between B+ and E is not geography or traffic volume. It is institutional architecture: ring-fenced resources, professional capability, and operational authority.

### Responsibility — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

Municipal roads responsibility is clearly assigned — the municipality owns and manages the roads within its area. Stormwater management is typically housed within the roads department. The tension arises at the boundary between provincial and municipal roads, where responsibility for specific roads is sometimes disputed, and in areas where the distinction between a provincial road and a municipal road is unclear.

### Accountability — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

Councillors are accountable for municipal roads, but the accountability mechanism is weak for the same reason as waste: roads maintenance is politically invisible until it fails catastrophically. A council that defers road resurfacing for five years faces no consequence until the potholes become a political crisis. And because road maintenance competes with every other municipal priority in the general budget, it is always deferred. New roads attract ribbon-cutting ceremonies. Maintenance attracts no political credit.

The accountability gap is compounded by the absence of systematic road condition data. Many municipalities do not maintain a pavement management system — they do not know the condition of their road network, the rate of deterioration, or the maintenance backlog. Without this data, councillors cannot make informed budget decisions, citizens cannot hold the council accountable against objective benchmarks, and the Auditor-General cannot assess whether the roads budget is adequate.

### Authority — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

Municipalities have the authority to maintain their roads — they can budget for maintenance, procure contractors, and manage repair programmes. The 30-day authority test is modestly positive for routine maintenance: a municipal manager can authorise pothole repairs within days if budget exists. The constraint is financial, not legal. The municipality has the authority to fix the road. It does not have the resources.

For capital projects — new roads, road reconstruction, stormwater system upgrades — the authority constraint tightens. These require budget approval, MFMA-compliant procurement, environmental authorisation (for stormwater infrastructure), and engineering design. A municipality without a qualified civil engineer cannot design a road reconstruction project — it must appoint a consulting engineer, which requires procurement, which takes months.

### Resources — Rating: 1 (Critical Failure)

Roads have no dedicated revenue stream at the municipal level. Unlike water (which generates tariff revenue) or electricity (which generates surplus revenue), roads are funded entirely from the general municipal budget — property rates, the equitable share, and conditional grants. Roads maintenance must compete with salaries, electricity purchases, water operations, and every other municipal priority. It loses that competition every year.

The numbers tell the story. Eighty per cent of South Africa's roads have exceeded their 20-year design life. Thirty per cent of the paved network is in poor to very poor condition. Seventy-seven per cent of gravel roads are in poor or very poor condition. UCT research estimated the total road maintenance backlog at R417 billion — R242 billion for municipal roads, R150 billion for provincial roads. All 257 municipalities collectively spent only R18 billion on infrastructure maintenance, representing just 4 per cent of the total R450 billion value of municipal assets, against an international benchmark of 6 to 8 per cent.

The Municipal Infrastructure Grant — R17.5 billion in 2023/24, the largest conditional infrastructure grant — can fund road infrastructure. But at the second quarter of 2024/25, only 54.9 per cent of the allocation had been spent. The problem is not that money is unavailable. The problem is that municipalities lack the capability to plan, design, procure, and manage road infrastructure projects. The money sits unspent while the roads deteriorate.

:::takeaway
Municipal roads are the only major infrastructure function with no dedicated revenue stream. Unlike water and electricity (which generate tariff and surplus revenue), roads compete for general budget funding and lose every year. The result: a R242 billion maintenance backlog, 25 million potholes, and SAICE grades of D to E across the network. SANRAL's national roads score B+ because SANRAL has ring-fenced revenue and professional capability. Municipal roads score D- because municipalities have neither.
:::

### Information — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

Most municipalities do not maintain a pavement management system. They do not systematically survey road conditions, track deterioration rates, or model future maintenance requirements. Without this data, maintenance is reactive — fix the pothole that generates the complaint — rather than planned. Reactive maintenance is always more expensive than planned maintenance. A road that receives timely preventive treatment (resealing, crack sealing) costs a fraction of what it costs to reconstruct after years of neglect.

The depreciated replacement cost ratio for roads and stormwater combined stands at 58 per cent — indicating that municipal road assets have lost nearly half their value through deterioration. This figure tells you the cumulative cost of the information failure: municipalities that do not know the condition of their roads cannot plan maintenance, cannot budget accurately, and cannot prevent the deterioration that is now reflected in the asset values.

### Capability — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

Municipal roads require civil engineers for design, project management, and asset management. Stormwater systems require hydraulic engineering expertise. The IMESA February 2026 report confirmed that no municipality meets the 3&#58;1 technical-to-support staff ratio required by Municipal Staff Regulations. With only 141 accredited civil engineers employed across all municipalities, the capability base is fundamentally insufficient.

The capability deficit produces a specific failure pattern for roads: municipalities cannot design maintenance programmes (no engineers to assess conditions and specify treatments), cannot manage construction projects (no engineers to supervise contractors and ensure quality), and cannot plan infrastructure investment (no engineers to model future requirements). When municipalities do spend on roads, the work is often of poor quality — the Business Report noted in May 2025 that South Africa's pothole crisis is substantially driven by poor workmanship in road construction and repair, which creates a cycle of repeated failure on the same road sections.

### Legitimacy — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

Municipal roads are a Schedule 5 Part B function with clear constitutional assignment. The legitimacy is not in question. Like waste management, roads lack the powerful constitutional right framing that water enjoys — there is no "right to pothole-free roads" in the Bill of Rights — but the democratic mandate is clear.

### Enforcement — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

There is no regulatory body for municipal roads equivalent to NERSA for electricity or DWS for water. No entity systematically assesses the condition of municipal roads, publishes comparative performance data, or enforces minimum maintenance standards. SAICE publishes its Infrastructure Report Card periodically (the most recent in 2022), but this is a voluntary professional assessment, not a regulatory instrument. The Auditor-General audits road expenditure for financial compliance but cannot compel a municipality to maintain its roads.

SANRAL's 14-day pothole repair standard demonstrates that enforcement works when it is built into institutional design — SANRAL has the authority, resources, and capability to meet the standard, and it reports publicly on its performance. Municipal roads have no equivalent standard, no reporting requirement, and no consequence for failure.

## The Common Root Cause

The parallel diagnoses reveal an unmistakable pattern. Compare the RAARICLE™ scorecards side by side.

For waste management: Responsibility 3, Accountability 2, Authority 3, Resources 2, Information 2, Capability 2, Legitimacy 3, Enforcement 1. Average: 2.1.

For municipal roads: Responsibility 3, Accountability 2, Authority 3, Resources 1, Information 2, Capability 2, Legitimacy 3, Enforcement 2. Average: 2.3.

The scores are nearly identical. Resources, information, capability, and enforcement are weak in both functions. Authority and responsibility are moderately adequate in both. Legitimacy is present in both. The two functions have different value chains, different technical requirements, different regulatory frameworks — and the same structural diagnosis. This is the pattern that [The Provincial Performance Crisis](/articles/ra-3-1-provincial-performance-crisis) identified at the provincial level: when the same elements fail across multiple functions, the cause is not function-specific but sphere-level.

The common root cause has three components.

First, neither function has ring-fenced revenue. Waste management charges flow into the general municipal account. Roads have no dedicated revenue stream at all. Both functions compete for general budget allocations and both lose to more politically visible priorities.

Second, both functions require technical capability that municipalities systematically lack. Waste management requires qualified waste management officers and landfill engineers. Roads require civil engineers and pavement management specialists. Neither function can attract or retain these professionals in the municipal environment — salaries are too low, political instability is too high, and the professional environment is too compromised.

Third, both functions operate in an enforcement vacuum. Unlicensed landfills operate for decades without consequences. Roads deteriorate without triggering any mandatory response. Neither function has the equivalent of a Blue Drop or Green Drop assessment that systematically evaluates performance and publishes the results.

The reinforcing cycle is the same for both: without enforcement pressure, councils have no incentive to ring-fence revenue. Without ring-fenced revenue, there is no money to hire qualified professionals. Without qualified professionals, there is nobody to build the information systems that would make the case for ring-fenced revenue. Without information, there is nothing to trigger enforcement.

:::expert
The Institute of Municipal Engineering of Southern Africa's February 2026 analysis identified the technical staffing crisis as the single greatest threat to municipal infrastructure. IMESA emphasised that "patronage rather than technical capacity and merit is weakening local government administrations" and that the consistent failure to meet the 3&#58;1 technical-to-support staffing ratio mandated by Municipal Staff Regulations means that municipalities lack the professional capacity to plan, design, manage, and maintain infrastructure across all functions. IMESA's position is that the staffing crisis is not a resourcing problem alone — it is a governance design problem. When technical appointments are made on political rather than professional criteria, the capability element of RAARICLE™ collapses regardless of budget. The finding that the same capability failure affects waste, roads, water, and electricity confirms that this is a systemic institutional condition, not a function-specific shortfall.
:::

## The Reform Blueprint

### Ring-Fenced Revenue for Both Functions

Waste management revenue — refuse removal charges — must be ring-fenced for waste operations: collection, transport, disposal, and landfill management. This is the same reform proposed for [water](/articles/ra-4-1-fixing-water-and-sanitation) and [electricity](/articles/ra-4-2-fixing-electricity), and it can be implemented through the same National Treasury regulation under the MFMA. Operation Vulindlela Phase II, launched in May 2025, has already identified revenue ring-fencing as a core element of municipal trading services reform. The R100 billion performance-based grant for metros explicitly requires ring-fencing of solid waste revenue. This principle must extend to all municipalities, not just metros.

Roads present a harder problem because there is no direct revenue stream to ring-fence. The reform must create one. Three mechanisms are available. First, a dedicated roads maintenance levy on property rates — a defined percentage of property rates ring-fenced exclusively for road maintenance, set at a level that funds the 6 to 8 per cent of asset value that the international benchmark requires. Second, a share of the fuel levy — currently allocated entirely to SANRAL and the general fiscus — hypothecated to municipal road maintenance based on the length and condition of each municipality's road network. Third, a performance-based conditional grant (similar to the Municipal Infrastructure Grant but specifically for maintenance rather than new construction) that rewards municipalities that demonstrate systematic road condition assessment and maintenance planning.

### Mandatory Professional Appointments

Both functions require a binding requirement for qualified professionals — not as an aspiration but as a condition of operating. For waste management, the Waste Act's requirement for a Waste Management Officer must be strengthened with prescribed qualifications (environmental science or engineering at NQF Level 6 minimum), verification by DFFE, and automatic consequences for non-compliance (municipalities without a qualified WMO within 12 months face transfer of the waste function to the district municipality or a shared services arrangement).

For roads, the Engineering Profession Act's requirement for registered professionals must be enforced at the municipal level. Every municipality that manages more than 100 kilometres of road must employ or contract a registered professional engineer or professional engineering technologist for roads. Municipalities that cannot meet this requirement — because they are too small, too poor, or too remote to attract a qualified engineer — must participate in a shared services arrangement.

### Shared Services for Small Municipalities

The Municipal Structures Act mandates that district and local municipalities cooperate and share financial, technical, and administrative support. In practice, shared services remain largely theoretical. An LGSETA feasibility study in 2022-2023 found growing interest but identified barriers: insufficient skills, political interference, lack of cooperation, and limited knowledge sharing.

The reform must make shared services mandatory, not aspirational. For municipalities below a defined capacity threshold (based on population, budget, and road network length), the district municipality must provide technical services — waste management planning, landfill management, road condition assessment, and maintenance programme design — through a district technical services unit staffed with qualified professionals. The cost is shared across local municipalities within the district, achieving economies of scale that no individual small municipality can achieve alone.

This model already has a partial precedent in the water sector, where Water Boards provide bulk services to multiple municipalities. The same logic applies: a single qualified civil engineer serving five small municipalities through a district technical services unit is vastly more effective than five municipalities each failing to fill an engineering vacancy.

### A Waste and Roads Performance Assessment

Neither function has an equivalent of the Blue Drop or Green Drop assessment. The reform must create one.

For waste management: DFFE should establish a "Clean Drop" assessment that systematically evaluates municipal waste management performance across defined criteria — collection coverage, landfill licence compliance, landfill operational standards, waste diversion rates, environmental monitoring compliance, and financial sustainability. The assessment should be conducted annually, results published per municipality, and automatic enforcement triggers linked to the scores.

For roads: a national municipal road condition index should be established, using standardised survey methods (visual condition index, roughness measurement) applied consistently across all municipalities. The index should be published annually, benchmarked against maintenance spending, and linked to the allocation of the roads maintenance conditional grant.

These assessments serve the dual purpose of creating information (municipalities must assess their own performance to participate) and enabling enforcement (automatic triggers can be tied to published scores).

### Automatic Enforcement Triggers

For waste management: when a municipality operates an unlicensed landfill for more than 12 months after notification by the provincial environmental department, the provincial department must assume management of the facility and the municipality must fund the remediation from its ring-fenced waste revenue. When waste collection coverage falls below 50 per cent of households for two consecutive years, the district municipality must assume the collection function.

For roads: when a municipality's road condition index shows more than 50 per cent of its paved network in poor or very poor condition, the municipality must submit a remediation plan to the provincial roads authority within 90 days. When a municipality fails to spend more than 70 per cent of its roads maintenance conditional grant for two consecutive years, the unspent funds must be redirected to the district technical services unit for direct maintenance intervention.

These triggers are calibrated to be achievable — they do not demand perfection. They demand minimum competence. A municipality that cannot keep half its roads out of "poor" condition or cannot spend the maintenance money it receives is a municipality that needs structural intervention, not encouragement.

### Anti-Capture Architecture

Political capture in waste and roads takes predictable forms: procurement fraud in waste collection contracts and road construction tenders (commercial capture), bloated non-technical staffing at the expense of engineering posts (labour capture), and deferred maintenance to fund politically visible projects (political capture). The structural reforms address each vector.

Ring-fenced revenue prevents political capture of waste and roads funding for other priorities. Mandatory professional appointments prevent labour capture of technical positions. Standardised procurement frameworks for waste collection and road maintenance contracts — with automated price benchmarking and open contracting data — reduce commercial capture opportunities. The performance assessment and automatic enforcement triggers prevent information capture by making performance data public and automatic rather than filtered through management.

:::framework
**The Visible Services Diagnostic Framework**

Waste collection and road maintenance are the municipal services you see every day. Use these six checks to diagnose why they are failing in your area:

**Check 1: Is waste collection actually a municipal function where you live?**

In metropolitan municipalities, waste collection is always a municipal function. In district and local municipalities, the responsibility may sit with the district or the local municipality depending on the municipal structures. Check your municipality's IDP to confirm who is responsible — you cannot hold the right institution accountable if you are directing complaints to the wrong sphere.

**Check 2: Is there a licensed landfill?**

Every municipality must dispose of waste at a licensed landfill. Many municipalities operate unlicensed dumps that contaminate groundwater and violate environmental law. Check the Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment (DFFE) waste management licence database. If your municipality's landfill is unlicensed, this is both an environmental crime and a RAARICLE™ enforcement failure.

**Check 3: Are waste management fees ring-fenced?**

Request your municipality's financial statements and check whether refuse removal charges fund waste management operations or disappear into the general budget. If the revenue is not ring-fenced, the waste function is being starved to fund other priorities.

**Check 4: What is the road condition data?**

Municipalities should maintain a roads asset register and pavement management system that tracks the condition of every road. Ask your municipality whether this data exists. If it does, request the percentage of roads in "poor" or "very poor" condition. If the data does not exist, the information element of RAARICLE™ has collapsed — the municipality cannot plan maintenance if it does not know what condition the roads are in.

**Check 5: Is there a qualified professional in charge?**

Both waste management and roads maintenance require specific technical qualifications. Check whether your municipality has a qualified civil engineer for roads and a qualified waste management officer. If these posts are vacant or filled by acting officials without the right qualifications, no amount of budget will produce competent service delivery.

**Check 6: What is the maintenance-to-capital ratio?**

Municipalities often spend on new infrastructure (ribbon-cutting projects) while neglecting maintenance of existing roads and waste facilities. Check the capital budget versus the maintenance budget for these functions. If new construction spending dwarfs maintenance spending, existing infrastructure is deteriorating — and the new infrastructure will follow the same path.

With these six checks, you can identify exactly which RAARICLE™ elements are broken in your municipality's waste and roads functions and build a specific, evidence-based case for structural reform.
:::

## Implementation Pathway

### Phase 1: Regulatory Foundation (Year 1)

Issue a binding National Treasury regulation mandating ring-fencing of waste management revenue. Establish the roads maintenance levy on property rates through municipal fiscal powers regulation. Publish prescribed qualifications for Waste Management Officers and enforce the designation requirement under Section 10 of the Waste Act. Gazette the terms of reference for the "Clean Drop" waste performance assessment and the municipal road condition index. Require every municipality to submit a road condition assessment within 12 months, using standardised survey methods published by the provincial roads authority. These actions require no new primary legislation.

### Phase 2: Pilot District Shared Services (Years 2-3)

Select two or three district municipalities — one with a mix of functional and failing local municipalities, one that is predominantly rural — and pilot the mandatory shared services model. Staff the district technical services unit with qualified professionals (civil engineer, waste management officer, environmental scientist). Transfer the waste disposal function (landfill management) from local municipalities to the district. Establish a district roads maintenance programme using ring-fenced revenue and the conditional grant. Publish the first "Clean Drop" assessment and municipal road condition index for the pilot districts. Independently evaluate the pilot against defined performance indicators: waste collection coverage, landfill compliance rate, road condition index improvement, and infrastructure maintenance spending as a percentage of asset value.

### Phase 3: National Rollout (Years 3-5)

Based on the pilot evaluation, mandate the shared services model for all municipalities below the defined capacity threshold. Roll out the "Clean Drop" assessment and road condition index nationally. Activate automatic enforcement triggers linked to the published assessments. Amend the Division of Revenue Act to include a dedicated roads maintenance conditional grant tied to road condition data and maintenance spending. As discussed in [Infrastructure Planning vs Reality](/articles/eg-4-3-infrastructure-planning-vs-reality), the gap between infrastructure plans and implementation is the defining failure of South African municipal governance. The reform pathway proposed here does not add new plans. It adds the structural conditions — ring-fenced revenue, qualified professionals, published performance data, and automatic enforcement — that make existing plans executable.
