## The Reform Blueprint

<!-- Section: Element-by-element proposed design. NHI as opportunity or risk for RAARICLE alignment. -->

:::framework
**The Clinic Performance Diagnostic**

Use this framework to assess whether your local clinic or community health centre has the structural conditions to deliver quality primary healthcare — and to identify precisely where the governance design fails.

**Step 1: Identify your clinic's position in the delivery chain.**

Determine which provincial health department operates your clinic, which health district it falls under, and who the facility manager is. Then map the key dependencies: the provincial pharmaceutical depot (medicine supply), the district health management team (clinical oversight), the national Department of Health (policy, essential medicines list, staffing norms), and any community health worker teams linked to the facility. Each dependency is a potential point of failure.

**Step 2: Test medicine availability.**

Ask whether your clinic has experienced stockouts of essential medicines in the past six months. If it has, trace the cause: did the facility manager have the authority and budget to procure medicines directly, or did they depend on the provincial pharmaceutical depot? If the depot failed to deliver, could the facility manager source medicines from an alternative supplier? If the answer is no — if the facility manager had no procurement authority — you have identified an authority failure. The person responsible for patient care cannot control a critical input.

**Step 3: Check the Ideal Clinic scorecard.**

The national Department of Health's Ideal Clinic Realisation and Maintenance programme assesses clinics against a set of standards covering infrastructure, staffing, medicine availability, and clinical quality. Find out whether your clinic has been assessed, what its score is, and whether the score is publicly available. If the clinic has not been assessed, or if the results are not disclosed, you have identified an information failure.

**Step 4: Assess staffing against the norms.**

Compare your clinic's actual staffing against the Staffing Norms and Standards for Fixed Primary Health Care Facilities. Does the clinic have the prescribed number of professional nurses, enrolled nurses, and community health workers for its patient headcount? Are key clinical posts filled permanently or by agency staff and acting appointments? If the clinic consistently operates below the staffing norms, check whether the provincial department has the budget and authority to fill the posts — or whether a nationally imposed personnel ceiling prevents hiring.

**Step 5: Follow the healthcare money.**

Identify what proportion of your province's health budget comes from the equitable share versus conditional grants (such as the National Health Insurance Indirect Grant, the HIV/AIDS conditional grant, or the Health Facility Revitalisation Grant). For each conditional grant, check whether the funds are being spent on schedule or underspent. Underspending on conditional grants often signals a capability or authority problem — the province has the money but cannot execute the projects.

**Step 6: Look for enforcement triggers.**

Ask: if your clinic consistently fails its Ideal Clinic assessment, what happens automatically? If patients die from a preventable cause linked to stockouts or understaffing, what mandatory investigation and corrective action follows? If there is no automatic trigger — if consequences depend on ministerial discretion or media attention — the enforcement element is absent.

This diagnostic equips you to engage constructively with your clinic committee, district health council, or provincial health department — armed with specific structural observations rather than generalised dissatisfaction.
:::
