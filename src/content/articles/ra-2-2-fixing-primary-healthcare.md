## The Delivery Chain Breaks Before It Reaches the Nurse

When you walk into a public clinic with a cough that will not clear, a child with diarrhoea, or a chronic condition that needs its monthly medication, you are entering the final link of a delivery chain that stretches from the national Department of Health in Pretoria through provincial bureaucracies, district management structures, pharmaceutical supply networks, and multiple conditional grant funding streams before it reaches the nurse who examines you. Understanding that chain — who controls each link, where the handoff points are, and where the chain routinely breaks — is the first step toward understanding why your clinic experience is what it is.

## The Healthcare Value Chain

Start at the national level. The National Department of Health sets health policy for the country, determines the Essential Medicines List (which prescribes exactly which medicines should be available at each level of care), publishes clinical guidelines and treatment protocols, and administers several of the largest conditional grants in the Division of Revenue. The Comprehensive HIV/AIDS Grant — approximately R29 billion in 2024/25 — funds antiretroviral treatment, prevention of mother-to-child transmission, and HIV testing across all provinces. The Health Facility Revitalisation Grant funds clinic and hospital infrastructure construction and rehabilitation. The National Health Insurance Indirect Grant funds NHI pilot activities. Each grant comes with its own conditions, reporting requirements, and spending timelines.

The South African Health Products Regulatory Authority (SAHPRA) regulates the registration and safety of medicines and medical devices. The Office of Health Standards Compliance (OHSC) is mandated to inspect health facilities and enforce compliance with national quality standards — though, as we will see, its enforcement capacity falls far short of its mandate. These national bodies set the regulatory environment within which provincial health systems operate.

Move to the provincial level. Each of the nine provincial departments of health is the operational engine of healthcare delivery. The provincial department employs the doctors, nurses, pharmacists, and allied health professionals who staff hospitals and clinics. It manages the provincial pharmaceutical depot — the warehousing and distribution system that supplies medicines from manufacturers to facilities. It manages district health services, which are the primary vehicle for delivering primary healthcare to communities. The provincial head of department is the accounting officer, responsible for the department's financial management and compliance with the PFMA. The provincial MEC for Health is the political principal, accountable to the Premier and the provincial legislature for health outcomes.

Within each province, the district health system organises PHC delivery. South Africa has 52 health districts, each with a district health management team responsible for coordinating PHC services across clinics, community health centres, and ward-based outreach teams. The district is supposed to be the critical management unit — the level at which population health needs are assessed, resources are allocated across facilities, referral pathways are managed, and performance is monitored. In practice, district management capacity varies enormously. Some districts function as genuine management units with qualified managers, functional information systems, and meaningful operational authority. Others are administrative shells — understaffed, under-resourced, and unable to exercise meaningful oversight over the facilities in their jurisdiction.

At the facility level, each clinic or community health centre has a facility manager — typically a professional nurse with additional management responsibilities. The facility manager is responsible for the day-to-day operations of the clinic: managing staff rosters, ensuring medicine availability, maintaining the physical facility, and delivering clinical services. The facility manager is the person closest to the patient. And yet, as the RAARICLE™ diagnosis will reveal, **the facility manager controls almost none of the inputs that determine whether the patient receives adequate care.**

Finally, add the community health worker layer. Ward-based primary healthcare outreach teams — comprising community health workers supervised by a professional nurse — provide household-level health promotion, screening, referral, and adherence support. Community health workers are the frontline of the health system, particularly for chronic disease management, tuberculosis treatment adherence, and maternal and child health. But their employment arrangements are fragmented. In some provinces, community health workers are employed directly by the provincial department. In others, they are contracted through non-profit organisations, with different terms of employment, different training standards, and different levels of integration with the formal health system. The formalisation of community health worker employment has been a policy priority for over a decade, with uneven progress across provinces.

:::takeaway
The PHC delivery chain involves at least six institutional layers: the national department (policy, essential medicines list, conditional grants), SAHPRA (medicine regulation), the provincial department (facility management, staffing, pharmaceutical depot), the district management team (coordination, oversight), the facility manager (clinical delivery), and community health workers (community outreach). Each layer introduces a handoff point where the chain can break. No single entity owns the journey from policy to patient.
:::

## RAARICLE™ Diagnosis

### Responsibility — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

Who is responsible for delivering primary healthcare? The provincial department of health, through the district health system. That is the formal answer. The operational reality is far more fragmented.

The provincial department employs the clinical staff and manages the facilities. But the medicines those staff administer are regulated by SAHPRA (national), listed on the Essential Medicines List by NDoH (national), procured through provincial pharmaceutical depots (provincial, but subject to national contract arrangements), and funded partly through the equitable share (unconditional) and partly through conditional grants (each with national conditions). The infrastructure in which care is delivered is funded through a separate conditional grant with its own conditions and timelines. The community health workers who extend the facility's reach into the community may be employed by the province, contracted through an NPO, or operating under a hybrid arrangement that differs from one district to the next.

When a patient arrives at a clinic and finds no medication, no doctor, a leaking roof, and a two-hour queue, which entity is responsible? The facility manager will point to the pharmaceutical depot that failed to deliver. The depot will point to the province's procurement delays. The province will point to the conditional grant conditions that constrain how infrastructure money can be spent. The district will point to staff vacancies that the province has not filled. Everyone has a piece of the responsibility. Nobody owns the outcome.

The responsibility fragmentation is compounded by the NHI Bill, signed into law in 2024. The NHI proposes to create a National Health Insurance Fund that will serve as the single purchaser of healthcare services, contracting with accredited providers (including public facilities) and paying them for services rendered. If fully implemented, NHI would add another entity to an already crowded value chain — a national fund that purchases services from provincial facilities, introducing a new principal-agent relationship and a new set of contractual conditions that facilities must meet.

### Accountability — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

The provincial HOD is the accounting officer for the provincial health department under the PFMA. The provincial MEC for Health is politically accountable for health outcomes. At the facility level, the facility manager is accountable for clinical operations and patient outcomes.

But accountability without authority is theatre, as established in [The Eight Tests](/articles/ra-1-1-eight-tests). The provincial MEC is accountable for health outcomes but cannot control the medicine supply chain (which depends on national procurement frameworks), cannot determine which medicines are available (the Essential Medicines List is national), and cannot restructure community health worker employment without navigating national policy frameworks and labour relations processes. The facility manager is accountable for patient care but cannot procure medicines independently, cannot hire additional staff without provincial approval, cannot undertake facility maintenance above a minimal threshold without a procurement process, and cannot adjust clinic hours or service models without district and provincial authorisation.

The Auditor-General's audits of provincial health departments assess financial compliance — whether money was spent according to the rules. They do not systematically assess whether patients received adequate care. A provincial health department can receive a clean financial audit while patients die from medicine stockouts, because financial accountability and clinical accountability operate through entirely separate systems. The OHSC is supposed to bridge this gap by inspecting facilities against quality standards, but its inspection coverage is limited and its enforcement powers are weak.

### Authority — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

The 30-day authority test exposes the most acute governance failure in primary healthcare: the facility manager's inability to control the medicine supply chain.

Consider a concrete scenario. A clinic in a rural district runs out of antiretroviral medication — the ARVs that keep HIV-positive patients alive. The facility manager identifies the stockout. Can the facility manager resolve it within 30 days using their own authority and budget?

**The answer is no.** The facility manager cannot procure ARVs independently. The medicine supply chain runs through the provincial pharmaceutical depot, which receives medicines from contracted suppliers under national or provincial tender arrangements. The facility manager can place an order with the depot. If the depot has stock, it will deliver — eventually. Delivery schedules depend on the depot's logistics capacity, which in several provinces has been documented as chronically inadequate. If the depot does not have stock — because the provincial procurement process has stalled, because the national contract has expired and a new one has not been finalised, because the manufacturer has a production shortage — *the facility manager has no alternative procurement pathway*. The patients wait. The patients default on treatment. Some patients die.

:::expert
The Stop Stock Outs Project, a civil society monitoring initiative, has tracked medicine availability at public health facilities across South Africa since 2013. Their data has consistently shown that medicine stockouts affect facilities in every province, with antiretrovirals, tuberculosis medicines, contraceptives, and chronic disease medications among the most frequently affected. The project's reports have documented that stockouts are not primarily caused by national-level supply failures but by provincial depot inefficiencies — medicines that are nationally available but not reaching facilities because the provincial distribution system cannot execute timely deliveries. This is an authority failure at its most consequential: the entity closest to the patient (the facility manager) has no power to resolve the problem, and the entity that controls the supply chain (the provincial depot) has no direct accountability to the patient.
:::

The authority deficit extends beyond medicines. A facility manager who identifies that the clinic roof is leaking cannot commission repairs above a trivial threshold without a provincial procurement process. A facility manager who needs an additional professional nurse to manage patient volumes cannot hire one — staffing decisions are made at provincial level. A facility manager who wants to extend clinic operating hours to reduce waiting times may need district and provincial approval. The person closest to the problem has the least power to solve it.

### Resources — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

Healthcare funding is substantial but structurally complex. Provincial health departments receive funding through three main streams: the provincial equitable share (unconditional — the province decides how much to allocate to health), conditional grants (each with national conditions), and a small amount of own revenue (primarily from patient fees, which are minimal for public facilities).

The conditional grant landscape in health is particularly fragmented. The Comprehensive HIV/AIDS Grant, the largest health conditional grant at approximately R29 billion, funds the entire HIV treatment programme including ARV procurement. The Health Facility Revitalisation Grant funds infrastructure. The National Health Insurance Indirect Grant funds NHI pilot activities. Each grant has its own conditions, its own reporting requirements, and its own spending timelines. A provincial health department must manage multiple funding streams simultaneously, each with different rules, different accountability channels, and different performance indicators.

The personnel budget dominates provincial health spending. **Approximately 60 to 65 per cent of provincial health budgets is consumed by the compensation of employees** — salaries for doctors, nurses, pharmacists, allied health workers, and administrative staff. This leaves limited fiscal space for non-personnel expenditure: medicines, medical supplies, equipment, maintenance, and infrastructure. When provincial budgets are under pressure — as they have been during the fiscal consolidation period — the non-personnel budget is typically the first to be squeezed, because personnel costs are sticky (you cannot easily reduce the wage bill without retrenchments, which are politically and legally difficult). *The result is that facilities may be fully staffed but lack the medicines, equipment, and infrastructure maintenance to deliver care effectively.*

Cross-subsidisation between hospital and PHC budgets within provinces adds another layer of complexity. Provincial hospitals — particularly academic and tertiary hospitals — consume a disproportionate share of the provincial health budget because they are expensive to operate. In provinces with large tertiary hospitals (Gauteng, KwaZulu-Natal, the Western Cape), the hospital-PHC budget split can mean that primary healthcare — the level of care that serves the most people — is chronically underfunded relative to hospital-based care.

### Information — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

South Africa's District Health Information System (DHIS) is one of the more developed health information systems in sub-Saharan Africa. It captures routine data from facilities on patient visits, disease burden, medicine availability, and service delivery indicators. The system feeds into the annual District Health Barometer, published by the Health Systems Trust, which provides a comprehensive assessment of district-level health performance.

The Ideal Clinic Realisation and Maintenance programme adds a facility-level assessment framework. Clinics are evaluated against a set of standards covering infrastructure, staffing, medicine availability, clinical governance, and patient experience. The programme has improved visibility into facility-level performance and created a common benchmark against which clinics can be assessed.

But significant information gaps remain. DHIS data quality depends on facility-level data capture — and in busy, understaffed clinics, data capture is often incomplete, delayed, or inaccurate. The system captures service delivery data (how many patients were seen, how many immunisations were given) more reliably than outcome data (whether treatment was effective, whether patients adhered to medication, whether health status improved). Real-time visibility is limited — most data flows upward through monthly or quarterly reporting cycles, not in real time. A district manager reviewing DHIS data is looking at what happened weeks or months ago, not what is happening today.

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed the information system's limitations dramatically. The system was not designed for real-time surveillance of a fast-moving epidemic. Provinces struggled to report case numbers accurately, hospital bed occupancy data was unreliable in several provinces, and the excess mortality data published by the South African Medical Research Council ultimately provided a more accurate picture of COVID-19's impact than the official case reporting system.

### Capability — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

The healthcare capability crisis is severe and unevenly distributed. South Africa has approximately 4,200 public PHC facilities — clinics and community health centres — that serve the **approximately 84 per cent of the population without medical aid coverage**. These facilities are supposed to be staffed according to the Staffing Norms and Standards for Fixed Primary Health Care Facilities, which prescribe minimum staffing levels based on patient headcount.

In practice, many facilities operate below the prescribed norms. Professional nurse vacancies are chronic, particularly in rural areas. **The vacancy rate for medical officers (doctors) in rural districts can exceed 50 per cent.** Specialist doctors — obstetricians, paediatricians, psychiatrists — are almost entirely absent from district-level services in several provinces. The Eastern Cape, Limpopo, and Mpumalanga have the most severe health workforce shortages, driven by an inability to attract and retain qualified professionals who can earn higher salaries and work in better conditions in metropolitan areas or the private sector.

:::expert
The Health Systems Trust's District Health Barometer has consistently documented a correlation between health workforce density and health outcomes at district level. Districts with higher ratios of professional nurses and doctors per population consistently achieve better immunisation coverage, lower maternal mortality, and higher rates of treatment adherence for HIV and tuberculosis. The Barometer's analysis shows that the health workforce deficit is not evenly distributed — it is concentrated in precisely the districts that have the highest disease burden and the most vulnerable populations. Rural districts in the Eastern Cape, Limpopo, and KwaZulu-Natal carry a disproportionate share of South Africa's disease burden (HIV, TB, maternal and child mortality) while operating with the thinnest health workforce. This is the capability paradox of South African healthcare: the places that need the most capable health workers are the places least able to attract them.
:::

Facility management capability is a separate but equally critical gap. The Ideal Clinic programme has highlighted that many facility managers lack the management skills — financial management, human resource management, supply chain management, quality improvement — needed to run a healthcare facility effectively. These are nurses who have been promoted to management positions without management training, expected to manage budgets, staff, procurement, and clinical quality simultaneously while also maintaining their own clinical caseloads.

### Legitimacy — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

Section 27 of the Constitution establishes the right of access to healthcare services. This constitutional mandate provides a strong legitimacy foundation for public healthcare governance. The right to healthcare is not contested — it is one of the most broadly supported constitutional rights.

But the NHI Bill, signed into law in May 2024, has introduced legitimacy tensions. The NHI proposes a fundamental restructuring of healthcare financing — creating a single-payer National Health Insurance Fund that would pool all public healthcare funding and purchase services from accredited providers. The NHI has been subject to legal challenge and policy critique from across the political spectrum — including medical aid schemes, healthcare practitioners' organisations, and political parties — raising questions about its constitutional validity, its practical feasibility, and its implications for the existing provincial healthcare system.

The legitimacy tension is not about whether South Africans deserve universal healthcare — that principle is broadly supported. It is about whether the institutional mechanism proposed by the NHI — a centralised national fund purchasing services from providers — is the right design. For the RAARICLE™ framework, the question is whether the NHI design co-locates the eight governance elements or further fragments them. If the NHI Fund becomes another entity in an already crowded value chain — setting purchasing conditions that provincial facilities must meet while those facilities continue to depend on provincial budgets, staffing, and supply chains — it may worsen the fragmentation rather than resolve it.

### Enforcement — Rating: 1 (Critical Failure)

The enforcement deficit in healthcare governance is as severe as in education — and the consequences are measured in lives rather than test scores.

The Office of Health Standards Compliance is mandated to inspect health facilities and enforce compliance with national quality standards. The OHSC can issue compliance notices, impose conditions on facility operations, and ultimately recommend that a facility be closed if it poses a danger to patients. But the OHSC's inspection capacity is severely constrained. With over 4,200 public PHC facilities nationally, the OHSC cannot inspect all facilities regularly. Its inspection reports identify problems — infrastructure deficiencies, staffing shortages, medicine stockouts, poor infection control — but the consequences for persistent non-compliance are weak and slow.

There are no automatic triggers in healthcare governance. A clinic that fails its Ideal Clinic assessment three times in succession faces no mandatory consequence. A provincial pharmaceutical depot that fails to deliver essential medicines on time faces no automatic penalty. A provincial health department whose maternal mortality ratio exceeds a defined threshold for multiple years triggers no mandatory intervention. Enforcement is entirely discretionary — it depends on ministerial will, media attention, or litigation by affected patients.

The Section 100 intervention mechanism exists in theory but has rarely been used for health. Even where it has been considered, the same problems documented in education apply: interventions are politically contentious, operationally disruptive, and often temporary without addressing the structural causes of failure.

:::takeaway
Healthcare enforcement scores a 1 — critical failure. The system can identify underperforming facilities through the Ideal Clinic programme and OHSC inspections. But there is no mechanism that automatically converts identified failure into mandatory corrective action. Patients who receive substandard care at a failing facility have no assurance that the system will self-correct. The enforcement gap is filled, when it is filled at all, by civil society litigation, media exposure, or patient deaths that are too visible to ignore.
:::

## The Root Cause

The RAARICLE™ diagnosis reveals that primary healthcare governance suffers from two compounding failure patterns: **fragmentation** and **misalignment**.

Fragmentation is the primary pattern. The PHC delivery chain involves too many entities — NDoH, SAHPRA, provincial health departments, provincial pharmaceutical depots, district management teams, facility managers, community health workers employed through multiple contractual arrangements, NPOs, and now potentially the NHI Fund. No single entity owns the journey from policy to patient outcome. The medicine supply chain alone involves at least four entities (NDoH for the Essential Medicines List, SAHPRA for regulation, national or provincial procurement for contracting, and the provincial depot for distribution) before a medicine reaches the facility manager's shelf.

Misalignment compounds the fragmentation. The facility manager — the person closest to the patient — has the least authority in the system. The facility manager cannot procure medicines, cannot hire staff, cannot commission repairs, and cannot adjust service models without approvals from entities further up the chain. Authority is concentrated at provincial and national levels, while delivery happens at facility level. The entity accountable for patient care does not control the inputs that determine whether patients receive care.

**The NHI, as currently designed, risks adding a new layer of fragmentation rather than resolving the existing layers.** If the NHI Fund operates as a separate national entity that contracts with provincial facilities — setting its own accreditation standards, its own payment mechanisms, and its own reporting requirements — it adds another principal to an already complex multi-principal system. The facility manager would then answer to the district, the province, and the NHI Fund simultaneously, each with different performance expectations and different accountability channels. This is not co-location. *It is further dispersion.*

Any healthcare financing reform — whether NHI in its current form or an alternative mechanism such as strengthened provincial autonomy without centralised purchasing — must address the fragmentation and misalignment that the RAARICLE™ diagnosis has identified. **A new funding mechanism layered on top of a broken delivery chain will not fix the delivery chain; it will add complexity to dysfunction.** The structural question is whether the chosen design *co-locates authority with accountability*, not which party champions which financing model.

## The Reform Blueprint

The structural prescription for primary healthcare follows the same principle as education: authority must be co-located with accountability at the point of delivery. But healthcare has a complexity that education does not — the medicine supply chain, which introduces a logistics dimension that requires specialised institutional capacity. The reform blueprint must address both the governance architecture and the supply chain architecture simultaneously.

### The Core Design: District-Based PHC Authorities

The reform proposes creating District Primary Healthcare Authorities as the primary institutional unit for PHC delivery. Each of South Africa's 52 health districts would have a PHC Authority — a semi-autonomous entity within the provincial health department — with the following design features.

The District PHC Authority would hold operational authority over all PHC facilities within its jurisdiction. This means the authority to hire and deploy clinical staff (within a national qualification framework but with local salary supplement flexibility), manage facility maintenance and minor capital works, and determine service delivery models appropriate to the district's population health profile. The authority would be headed by a professionally qualified district health manager appointed through a merit-based process with prescribed minimum qualifications.

Critically, the District PHC Authority would have delegated procurement authority for essential medicines. This does not mean that each clinic procures its own ARVs from the open market. It means that the district operates under a national framework contract — negotiated centrally by NDoH to achieve volume pricing — but has the authority to place orders directly with contracted suppliers and manage distribution to its facilities. The provincial pharmaceutical depot would be restructured as a logistics service provider, not a gatekeeper. The district decides what medicines its facilities need and when. The depot (or a contracted logistics provider) delivers them. If the depot fails to deliver within a prescribed timeframe, the district has the authority to source from an alternative contracted supplier. The facility manager's dependence on a provincial bureaucratic chain for medicine access is eliminated.

### Element-by-Element Design Rules

**Responsibility:** One District PHC Authority owns PHC delivery end-to-end within its jurisdiction — from community health worker outreach through facility-based care to referral coordination with hospitals. Community health workers are formally employed by the authority, not contracted through NPOs, with standardised employment conditions across all districts.

**Accountability:** The district health manager signs a binding performance agreement with specific, measurable health outcome indicators — immunisation coverage rates, antenatal care attendance, TB treatment completion rates, chronic disease management indicators, and facility-level Ideal Clinic scores. The agreement includes consequences that are defined in advance: a district manager whose district fails to meet minimum outcome thresholds for two consecutive assessment periods is placed on a performance improvement plan with mandatory technical support.

**Authority:** The district health manager passes the 30-day authority test. The district can place a medicine order and receive delivery within 14 days through the framework contract system. The district can hire a professional nurse to fill a vacancy within 30 days using its own delegated recruitment authority. The district can commission facility repairs up to a defined threshold using its own maintenance budget. The facility manager, in turn, receives delegated authority from the district for operational decisions — adjusting clinic hours, managing staff rosters, ordering routine supplies — without requiring upward approval for every decision.

**Resources:** PHC funding is ring-fenced. The provincial equitable share includes a separately identified PHC allocation that is transferred directly to the District PHC Authority. The allocation is calculated on a weighted capitation basis — adjusted for the district's population size, disease burden, rurality, and poverty profile. Conditional grants for PHC-related activities (HIV treatment, community health workers, facility infrastructure) are consolidated into a single PHC conditional grant with unified reporting requirements, replacing the current fragmented grant landscape. The district manages its budget as an integrated whole rather than managing multiple funding streams with different rules.

**Information:** A national Health Information Platform provides real-time data on facility performance — patient volumes, medicine stock levels, staffing ratios, clinical quality indicators — accessible to facility managers, district health managers, provincial heads of department, NDoH, and the public. The platform integrates data from the DHIS, the Ideal Clinic monitoring system, the medicine stock management system, and the community health worker reporting system into a single dashboard. Data quality is assured through automated validation rules and periodic independent audits.

**Capability:** Every district health manager must hold prescribed professional qualifications in health management. Every facility manager must complete a mandatory management development programme within two years of appointment. Every district must maintain minimum staffing ratios for professional nurses, medical officers, and community health workers, with staffing levels monitored through the national Health Information Platform and shortfalls flagged automatically. A national Health Workforce Development Strategy — funded through a dedicated allocation — provides bursaries, rural allowances, and career pathway incentives to address the rural-urban distribution imbalance.

**Legitimacy:** District PHC Authorities operate within the provincial health system and derive their mandate from the National Health Act and provincial health legislation. District Health Councils — existing structures that include community representatives — provide democratic oversight and community input into district health priorities. The NHI Fund, if implemented, contracts with District PHC Authorities as accredited providers rather than with individual facilities, simplifying the contracting relationship and aligning purchasing with the governance unit that holds operational authority.

**Enforcement:** Automatic triggers replace discretionary enforcement. If a district's immunisation coverage falls below 80 per cent for two consecutive quarters, mandatory support from the provincial health department is activated. If a facility's Ideal Clinic score falls below a defined threshold for three consecutive assessments, the facility is placed under a mandatory improvement programme with external technical support. If a provincial pharmaceutical depot fails to deliver medicines within the prescribed timeframe for more than 10 per cent of orders in a quarter, the district automatically activates its alternative procurement pathway. If a district health manager's performance agreement outcomes are not met for two consecutive years, the position is advertised and filled through a competitive process.

:::expert
The World Health Organization's framework for primary healthcare system strengthening, published in its 2018 Astana Declaration implementation guidance, emphasises that effective PHC governance requires "decision-making authority at the level closest to the population served." The WHO's comparative analysis of PHC governance models across middle-income countries has found that systems that delegate meaningful operational authority to district-level management units — while maintaining national standard-setting and quality assurance — consistently outperform both fully centralised systems (where responsiveness to local health needs is slow) and fully fragmented systems (where quality standards are inconsistent). The district-based PHC authority model proposed here aligns with this evidence base, adapting the WHO's recommendations to South Africa's specific governance architecture.
:::

### NHI Through the RAARICLE™ Lens

The NHI represents both a risk and an opportunity for PHC governance reform. The risk is that the NHI Fund becomes another entity in an already fragmented value chain — adding a layer of national purchasing bureaucracy on top of provincial delivery bureaucracy without co-locating authority with the point of care. The opportunity is that the NHI's funding consolidation could be the mechanism through which PHC funding is ring-fenced and allocated on a weighted capitation basis to District PHC Authorities.

The RAARICLE™-aligned approach to NHI integration is straightforward. The NHI Fund should contract with District PHC Authorities, not with individual facilities. The contract should specify outcome indicators (health outcomes, not process compliance), and payment should be linked to performance on those indicators. The District PHC Authority uses the NHI contract funding — combined with the ring-fenced PHC allocation from the provincial equitable share — to manage all PHC services within its jurisdiction. This design gives the NHI Fund what it needs (a contracting counterpart with defined accountability for population health outcomes) while giving the district what it needs (consolidated funding with operational authority to deploy it).

What the NHI should not do, from a RAARICLE™ perspective, is create a parallel accountability system that adds reporting requirements without adding operational authority. If the NHI Fund imposes accreditation conditions, performance reporting requirements, and payment conditions on individual facilities — while those facilities continue to depend on provincial systems for staffing, medicines, and infrastructure — it will deepen the misalignment rather than resolve it. The facility manager would then face accountability to the district, the province, and the NHI Fund simultaneously, with each principal imposing different requirements and none providing the authority needed to meet them.

### Anti-Capture Protections

Political capture is addressed through merit-based appointment requirements for district health managers and facility managers. Minimum professional qualifications must be specified in legislation, and appointment processes must include independent selection panel participation. The district health manager's appointment must be approved by the provincial HOD (not the MEC alone), and removal must require documented performance grounds.

Commercial capture is addressed through the framework contract procurement system for essential medicines. National framework contracts are negotiated centrally with transparent pricing. District-level procurement operates within the framework — the district cannot award contracts outside the framework, eliminating the opportunity for local procurement manipulation. All procurement above a defined threshold is published on a public health procurement portal.

Labour capture is addressed through standardised employment conditions for community health workers — ending the fragmented NPO contracting system that has created inconsistent terms and conditions. Professional staff employment conditions continue to be governed by national frameworks, but the district has delegated authority over deployment, rostering, and performance management.

Regulatory capture is addressed through the structural independence of the OHSC. The OHSC's inspection and enforcement function must be strengthened with additional resources and binding enforcement powers — including the authority to place a facility under mandatory improvement with prescribed timelines and consequences for non-compliance.

Information capture is addressed through the national Health Information Platform, which provides direct data access to the OHSC, the Auditor-General, the DPME, and the public without requiring that data pass through provincial or district management filters. Facility-level performance data is publicly available, ensuring that communities can see their clinic's performance relative to other facilities.

:::framework
**The Clinic Performance Diagnostic**

Use this framework to assess whether your local clinic or community health centre has the structural conditions to deliver quality primary healthcare — and to identify precisely where the governance design fails.

**Step 1: Identify your clinic's position in the delivery chain.**

Determine which provincial health department operates your clinic, which health district it falls under, and who the facility manager is. Then map the key dependencies: the provincial pharmaceutical depot (medicine supply), the district health management team (clinical oversight), the national Department of Health (policy, essential medicines list, staffing norms), and any community health worker teams linked to the facility. Each dependency is a potential point of failure.

**Step 2: Test medicine availability.**

Ask whether your clinic has experienced stockouts of essential medicines in the past six months. If it has, trace the cause: did the facility manager have the authority and budget to procure medicines directly, or did they depend on the provincial pharmaceutical depot? If the depot failed to deliver, could the facility manager source medicines from an alternative supplier? If the answer is no — if the facility manager had no procurement authority — you have identified an authority failure. The person responsible for patient care cannot control a critical input.

**Step 3: Check the Ideal Clinic scorecard.**

The national Department of Health's Ideal Clinic Realisation and Maintenance programme assesses clinics against a set of standards covering infrastructure, staffing, medicine availability, and clinical quality. Find out whether your clinic has been assessed, what its score is, and whether the score is publicly available. If the clinic has not been assessed, or if the results are not disclosed, you have identified an information failure.

**Step 4: Assess staffing against the norms.**

Compare your clinic's actual staffing against the Staffing Norms and Standards for Fixed Primary Health Care Facilities. Does the clinic have the prescribed number of professional nurses, enrolled nurses, and community health workers for its patient headcount? Are key clinical posts filled permanently or by agency staff and acting appointments? If the clinic consistently operates below the staffing norms, check whether the provincial department has the budget and authority to fill the posts — or whether a nationally imposed personnel ceiling prevents hiring.

**Step 5: Follow the healthcare money.**

Identify what proportion of your province's health budget comes from the equitable share versus conditional grants (such as the National Health Insurance Indirect Grant, the HIV/AIDS conditional grant, or the Health Facility Revitalisation Grant). For each conditional grant, check whether the funds are being spent on schedule or underspent. Underspending on conditional grants often signals a capability or authority problem — the province has the money but cannot execute the projects.

**Step 6: Look for enforcement triggers.**

Ask: if your clinic consistently fails its Ideal Clinic assessment, what happens automatically? If patients die from a preventable cause linked to stockouts or understaffing, what mandatory investigation and corrective action follows? If there is no automatic trigger — if consequences depend on ministerial discretion or media attention — the enforcement element is absent.

This diagnostic equips you to engage constructively with your clinic committee, district health council, or provincial health department — armed with specific structural observations rather than generalised dissatisfaction.
:::

## Implementation Pathway

The district-based PHC authority reform can be implemented primarily through amendments to the National Health Act and provincial health legislation. It does not require a constitutional amendment — health is a concurrent function under Schedule 4, and the Constitution does not prescribe the specific institutional architecture for health service delivery. The delegation of operational authority from provinces to district PHC authorities is a legislative and administrative restructuring, not a constitutional change.

### Phase 1: Legal and Institutional Foundation (Year 1-2)

The National Health Act must be amended to establish District PHC Authorities as semi-autonomous entities within provincial health departments, with prescribed governance structures, management qualifications, and delegated authorities. The amendment must specify the minimum delegated authorities — medicine procurement within framework contracts, staff recruitment within approved establishments, facility maintenance within defined thresholds — that cannot be withdrawn by provincial departments without National Treasury and NDoH approval.

The national framework contract system for essential medicines must be established. NDoH, working with National Treasury's procurement office, negotiates multi-year framework contracts with medicine suppliers. Districts order directly from contracted suppliers. The provincial pharmaceutical depot is restructured as a logistics service provider or, where depot capacity has collapsed, replaced by contracted logistics providers operating under the framework.

The community health worker employment framework must be finalised nationally — standardising terms of employment, training requirements, scope of practice, and remuneration across all provinces. Community health workers currently employed through NPOs must be transitioned to formal employment by the District PHC Authority within a defined timeline.

The OHSC's enforcement mandate must be strengthened through legislative amendment — giving the office binding enforcement powers with prescribed timelines for corrective action and automatic escalation mechanisms when compliance is not achieved.

### Phase 2: Pilot Districts (Year 2-3)

The district-based model should be piloted in six to eight districts across at least four provinces, selected to represent different governance contexts — well-performing metropolitan districts, moderately performing urban districts, and poorly performing rural districts. Pilot districts would operate under the full delegated authority framework, including direct medicine procurement, delegated staff recruitment, and ring-fenced PHC budgets.

The pilot must run for at least two years to generate meaningful outcome data. Independent evaluation — commissioned by the DPME, not by NDoH or the provincial departments — must compare pilot districts against control districts on defined health outcome indicators: immunisation coverage, antenatal care attendance, TB treatment completion, chronic disease management, medicine availability, and patient satisfaction.

If the NHI is being implemented in parallel, the pilot districts should serve as NHI contracting partners — testing the integrated funding model where the NHI Fund contracts with the District PHC Authority as a single entity rather than with individual facilities.

### Phase 3: National Rollout (Year 3-5)

Based on pilot evaluation results, the district-based model is rolled out nationally. As with the education reform, the rollout should be phased — districts with adequate institutional capacity transition first. Districts requiring more preparation receive transitional support from a national PHC Support Unit (modelled on the Education Support Unit proposed in [Fixing Basic Education](/articles/ra-2-1-fixing-basic-education)).

The rollout must be accompanied by a sustained investment in district health management capability — training programmes for district health managers, facility management development programmes, and health information system strengthening. The reform's success depends not just on redesigning the governance architecture but on building the human capital needed to operate within the new architecture effectively.

:::takeaway
The PHC reform is constitutionally feasible through legislative amendment to the National Health Act and provincial health legislation. The key design innovation is the District PHC Authority — a semi-autonomous management unit with delegated operational authority, ring-fenced funding, and direct medicine procurement capability. This design co-locates authority with accountability at the level closest to the patient, transforming the facility manager from a powerless administrator into an empowered manager operating within a supportive district framework.
:::

The structural parallel with the education reform in [Fixing Basic Education](/articles/ra-2-1-fixing-basic-education) is deliberate. Both education and healthcare are concurrent functions where authority is concentrated nationally, delivery is provincial, and accountability is split. Both require the same structural fix: delegating meaningful operational authority to the level closest to the service user — the school and the clinic — within a framework of national standards, ring-fenced funding, and automatic enforcement triggers. The next article in this series applies the same diagnostic approach to human settlements — a function where the fragmentation is even more severe and the delivery chain even longer.
