There is a real argument, backed by real theory and real evidence, that taxes can slow economic growth — and a serious case that South Africa cannot redistribute its way out of unemployment and poverty without first growing the economy that funds the redistribution. The logic is a virtuous cycle. Investment-friendly policy draws investment; investment creates businesses, jobs and activity; that activity widens the base from which the state draws resources; and a bigger base funds a bigger social wage — grants, schooling, clinics, infrastructure — than a stagnant economy ever could. This is the most important practical argument in South African economic policy, and this article gives it the full hearing it deserves, because too much of the left treats it as a trick and too much of the right treats it as a magic spell.

Here is where the evidence actually lands, and it disappoints both camps. The *mix* of taxes genuinely matters for growth — taxes on capital and high marginal income are more growth-harmful than taxes on consumption and property — and growth genuinely is the ultimate source of a country's capacity to redistribute. Those two findings are solid, and they vindicate part of the pro-growth case. But the strong form of the claim — that cutting taxes pays for itself through the sheer volume of new activity — is not supported for an economy like South Africa's, which sits well below the point where rate cuts could conceivably recover their own cost; the volume effect typically offsets *part* of the lost revenue, not all of it. And South Africa's investment is held back far less by tax rates than by electricity, logistics, skills, crime, and access to credit. The honest synthesis is this: make the tax mix pro-investment and treat growth as the engine of the resources social policy needs — but do not expect tax cuts alone to ignite the cycle while the deeper constraints bind. This is the fifth article in The Fiscal Levers, and it is the one where keeping the argument balanced matters most.

## The theoretical case, at full strength

Begin with the case for, made properly, because a fair test does not start by sneering at it. The previous articles established the disincentive principle: a tax discourages whatever it lands on. Follow that principle to the level of the whole economy and the pro-growth argument writes itself. Tax working, and you discourage some work. Tax hiring, and you discourage some hiring. Tax investing and starting businesses, and you discourage some of exactly the activities that produce growth. The discouragement is not free; economists give the cost a name — *deadweight loss* — and it is one of the more important ideas in public finance, so it is worth making plain.

Deadweight loss is the activity that never happens because the tax made it not worth doing. Imagine a person deciding whether to take on extra work, or an investor deciding whether to fund a new plant. At the margin, some of these deals are barely worth it — the reward only just exceeds the effort or the risk. Now add a tax that takes a slice of the reward. Some of those marginal deals tip from just-worth-it to not-worth-it, and they simply do not happen. Nobody earns the income, nobody collects the tax, the plant is not built, the job is not created. That foregone activity is the deadweight loss, and it is a real cost, not an accounting entry — value that the economy never gets to produce. A society that taxes productive effort heavily, and spends the proceeds badly, can genuinely tax itself toward stagnation. This is not a fringe view; it is mainstream economics, and anyone arguing about tax who waves it away is not arguing seriously.

Make it concrete in South African terms. A manufacturer weighing whether to add a second production line runs the numbers: the new line will earn a return, but corporate tax takes 27% of the profit, and the profit she eventually draws out as a dividend is taxed again at 20%. If the expected return was marginal to begin with — and in a slow-growing, high-cost economy many investments are — that combined bite can be enough to tip the decision from "build it" to "leave the money in the bank". The line is not built, the dozen jobs it would have created do not appear, and the workers who would have filled them stay unemployed. Multiply that single decision across thousands of firms and you can see how a tax system that falls heavily on investment and accumulation might quietly suppress the very activity a developing economy most needs. The pro-growth camp is pointing at something real when it points here. The question is not whether the effect exists — it does — but how large it is relative to everything else holding investment back.

## The empirical case, led by its strongest evidence

So much for theory. What does the evidence show? Here the pro-growth case has one genuinely robust empirical anchor, and intellectual honesty means leading with it rather than with the weaker claims that usually get top billing.

That anchor is the OECD's work on tax and growth — principally the 2008 study *Taxation and Economic Growth* by Åsa Johansson, Christopher Heady, Jens Arnold and colleagues, and the follow-up by Arnold and co-authors in *The Economic Journal* in 2011. Their central finding is not the crude "all taxes kill growth". It is that the *composition* of taxation matters: some taxes are more growth-harmful than others, and you can rank them. From most harmful to least, the hierarchy runs corporate income taxes, then personal income taxes, then consumption taxes like VAT, then recurrent taxes on immovable property. Corporate and high marginal personal income taxes sit at the damaging end because they fall most heavily on mobile capital and on the incentive to invest, accumulate and take risk; property and consumption taxes sit at the gentler end because they are harder to avoid and distort fewer decisions about whether to produce.

Notice how directly this connects to the previous article. [The Double Taxation of Capital](/articles/eg-3-4-the-double-taxation-of-capital) showed that South Africa taxes the returns to capital twice and incoherently — and capital taxes sit at the *growth-costly* end of the OECD hierarchy. So the worry that South Africa's treatment of capital is working against investment is not invented; it has empirical backing. The composition finding is the strongest card the pro-growth case holds, and it is a strong one. Hold it firmly, because we are about to see what it does and does not license.

:::expert
The OECD research team led by Jens Arnold and Åsa Johansson was careful, in both the 2008 working paper and the 2011 *Economic Journal* article, to state their finding precisely — and the precision is the point. They did not conclude that lower taxes mean higher growth. They concluded that a *revenue-neutral* shift in the tax mix — raising the same total revenue, but leaning more on property and consumption taxes and less on corporate and personal income taxes — was associated with higher long-run growth. That is a claim about *structure*, holding the overall tax burden constant, not a claim about *cutting* taxes. It is frequently misquoted in exactly the direction its authors guarded against: a finding that "you can raise the same money in a more growth-friendly way" gets repackaged as "lower taxes grow the economy". The two are different arguments, and only the first is what the evidence actually supports.
:::

## Growth for redistribution: reframing the oldest fight

Before the qualifications, give the moral core of the argument its due, because this is the part that should move you even if the econometrics leave you cold.

South Africa's greatest challenges — mass unemployment, deep poverty, a vast and rising social-grant bill, failing public services — all require one thing above all: *resources*. And resources come, ultimately, from a growing economy. The tired framing of South African politics pits "growth" against "redistribution" as if they were enemies, as if every rand directed to the poor were a rand stolen from growth and every rand of growth a betrayal of the poor. That framing is a trap. The truer relationship is growth *for* redistribution: the larger the economy, the larger the social wage it can sustainably fund. A grant system, a schooling system, a health system — all of them are easier to finance, expand and sustain in a country whose economy is growing than in one that is stagnant. Redistribution without growth is the division of a shrinking pie, which is a recipe for conflict and eventual collapse. Redistribution funded by growth is the only kind that lasts.

Put it in concrete South African terms. The social wage — the grants reaching more than 18 million people, the no-fee schools, the public clinics, the free basic water and electricity allocations — is the single largest thing the state does, and it is what stands between tens of millions of South Africans and destitution. Every rand of it has to be funded, year after year, and the funding has to grow at least as fast as the population that depends on it. A stagnant economy cannot do that: its revenue flatlines while the need rises, and the gap is closed by borrowing until the borrowing itself becomes unsustainable and the social wage is cut in a crisis — the worst possible way to do it, falling hardest on the poor in the moment they can least absorb it. A growing economy, by contrast, expands the revenue base in step with or ahead of the need, so the social wage can be sustained and even widened without a debt spiral. This is why the pro-growth argument is not, properly understood, an argument *against* the poor. The most pro-poor thing a South African government can do over the long run is to grow the economy that pays for the social wage. The disagreement worth having is not about whether to grow — it is about *how*.

And the arithmetic of growth is unforgiving in a way that should concentrate the mind. [The R7,000 Economy](/articles/eg-1-1-the-seven-thousand-rand-economy) showed how a sustained difference of even a couple of percentage points in the annual growth rate, compounded over a generation, is the difference between a country that transforms and one that falls behind. A South Africa growing at 1% a year and a South Africa growing at 4% a year are, after twenty-five years, almost different countries — and the high-growth version can fund a social wage the low-growth version can only dream of. For a country with South Africa's obligations to its poor, growth is not an optional extra or an ideological preference. It is the precondition for keeping the promises the Constitution makes.

## The virtuous cycle, laid out fairly

Put the pieces together and you have the mechanism the pro-growth case rests on, which deserves to be stated at its best rather than caricatured. Lower, simpler, more investment-friendly taxation encourages business formation and fixed investment. More firms and more investment mean more people employed and more economic activity. A broader base of activity recovers part of the revenue given up when rates were cut — more businesses paying some tax, more workers paying some tax, more spending generating VAT. And the larger pool of resources funds more redistribution, which, done well, raises the health, skills and spending power of the population, which feeds back into still more growth. Tax light enough to let the engine turn, the argument goes, and the engine generates more revenue than the high-rate, low-growth alternative ever could. This is a serious argument advanced by serious people, and the right response to it is not mockery. It is examination.

## Testing the case — where it holds and where it breaks

Now the examination, and the discipline here is to *qualify* the case, not demolish it. Four limits matter, and each one narrows the claim without erasing it.

**First, the self-financing question.** Does cutting taxes actually pay for itself? The idea rests on the Laffer curve — the genuine insight that beyond some rate, higher rates raise *less* revenue because they choke the activity being taxed. The Laffer curve is real as a concept. The error is in where economies actually sit on it. Essentially all serious evidence places economies like South Africa well to the *left* of the revenue-maximising peak, in the region where cutting rates loses revenue rather than recovering it. The strong-form claim that "tax cuts pay for themselves" is rejected by mainstream dynamic-scoring analysis, and it has been tested to destruction in the real world. The clearest case is the Kansas experiment: in 2012 and 2013, Governor Sam Brownback signed deep income-tax cuts — slashing the top rate by almost a third and cutting the tax on certain business profits to zero — and called it a "real live experiment" in supply-side economics. The promised growth surge never came. Kansas grew more slowly than its neighbours and the national average; revenues collapsed by hundreds of millions of dollars; roads, bridges and schools were cut; and in 2017 a Republican legislature repealed the cuts over Brownback's veto. The volume effect is real but partial: cutting a growth-harmful tax recovers *some* of the lost revenue through extra activity, rarely all of it, and never the full self-financing miracle the slogan promises.

In fairness to the other side, the Kansas story has a genuine counter-reading worth stating: analysts at the Tax Foundation argue that Kansas was badly *designed* rather than a clean test of rate cuts — the zero rate on pass-through business income, in particular, created an avoidance loophole that drained revenue without stimulating real activity, and a better-structured reform might have fared less badly. That is a fair point, and it sharpens rather than overturns the lesson: tax design matters enormously, and a sloppy cut can be worse than useless — but it is not evidence that a well-designed cut would have paid for itself either.

**Second, level versus composition.** The OECD finding was about the *mix* of taxes, and it is important not to inflate it into a claim about the *level*. Cross-country evidence that the overall level of taxation strongly suppresses growth is weak — and the standing refutation is the Nordic countries, which combine some of the highest tax-to-GDP ratios in the world (Denmark around 45%) with strong growth and world-leading innovation. If high taxes simply throttled growth, Denmark and Sweden would be basket cases; they are the opposite. So the defensible claim is narrow and specific: the *composition* of taxes matters, and the *quality of spending* matters — not "less tax, more growth" as a general law. A country can tax a lot and grow well if it taxes the right things and spends the money well; it can tax little and stagnate if it does the reverse.

**Third, South Africa's actual binding constraints.** Here is the point that should reorder the whole debate. Suppose you cut South Africa's corporate tax rate tomorrow. Would investment surge? Only if tax was the thing holding it back. And it mostly is not. Fixed investment in South Africa is gated by a stack of constraints that have nothing to do with the tax rate: years of load-shedding and unreliable, costly electricity; collapsing rail and port logistics that strand exports; a deep skills deficit; violent crime that raises the cost and risk of doing business; policy uncertainty; and — the argument this part is building toward — the allocation of credit. The IMF's Article IV consultations, National Treasury's own diagnoses and the SARB's analysis all point to these real-economy bottlenecks, not tax rates, as the binding constraints on investment. When those constraints bind, a tax cut does not call forth the investment the virtuous cycle assumes. It simply reduces revenue while the investor, still unable to get reliable power or move goods to port, stays on the sidelines. You cannot tax-cut your way past a broken grid.

The logic is worth spelling out because it is where so many tax debates go wrong. An investment decision clears only when *every* necessary condition is met — the financing, the power, the logistics, the skills, the security, the expected after-tax return. Tax affects the last of these. But if the binding constraint is the second or the third — if the firm genuinely cannot secure reliable electricity, or cannot get its containers to the port — then improving the tax term does nothing, because the deal was already blocked upstream. Easing a constraint that is not binding is wasted effort; it changes a number in a calculation whose answer was already "no" for a different reason. This is why a country can cut taxes and see no investment response at all: not because incentives do not matter, but because it loosened the wrong screw. South Africa's most credible diagnoses are close to unanimous that the tight screws are power, logistics, skills and crime — and, this part will argue, credit — not the corporate tax rate.

:::expert
Successive IMF Article IV consultations on South Africa, together with National Treasury's own economic-strategy documents and the Reserve Bank's analysis, have converged on a diagnosis that puts tax well down the list of what ails investment. The recurring shortlist is the reliability and cost of electricity, the state of the freight-rail and port system, the shortage of skills, violent crime, and policy and regulatory uncertainty. These institutions are not hostile to business or allergic to tax reform; several have argued for a more growth-friendly tax *mix*. But their consistent message is that the first-order constraints on fixed investment in South Africa are in the real economy, not the tax code — which means a serious growth strategy has to fix the grid and the ports before, or at least alongside, anything it does to tax rates. A tax cut offered as a substitute for fixing electricity is answering a question investors are not asking.
:::

**Fourth, the quality of spending.** The growth-funds-redistribution argument only works if the resources growth generates are actually spent well. A bigger pie feeds more people only if it is not first eaten by waste and theft. South Africa's problem here is partly allocative and deeply governance-related — the years of state capture documented elsewhere in this series are precisely the story of resources generated and then stolen or squandered. Neither higher taxes nor lower taxes help if the money is wasted: high taxes funding looted programmes is a tragedy, and low taxes starving functional ones is a different tragedy. The quality of the state's spending is a variable in the growth equation as surely as the rate of tax, and in South Africa it may be the more decisive one.

## Synthesis: the right pieces in the right places

So concede what is right in the pro-growth instinct, because a great deal of it is right. South Africa does need investment-led growth to fund its social obligations; the growth-versus-redistribution framing really is a trap; and a tax system that double-taxes capital and punishes business formation really is working against the investment the country needs. The pro-growth camp is correct that growth is not optional and correct that the tax treatment of capital is part of the problem.

But locate the binding constraint where the evidence actually puts it. It is rarely the tax rate. It is the real-economy bottlenecks — power, logistics, skills, crime — and, at the deepest level this part will reach, the allocation of credit. The virtuous cycle is real, but it is not ignited by tax cuts alone while the grid fails and the ports seize. It is ignited by fixing the constraints that actually bind and by directing productive credit toward new firms and new capacity.

There is a deeper reason to be wary of leading with tax cuts, beyond their poor record at self-financing. Revenue given up on a tax cut is certain and immediate; the growth it is supposed to buy is uncertain and delayed. A government that cuts first and waits for the growth to materialise has taken a real loss in exchange for a hoped-for gain — and if the growth does not come, because the binding constraint was elsewhere, it is left with a larger deficit and the same broken grid, now with less money to fix it. Sequencing matters. Fix the constraints that demonstrably block investment, let the investment and growth follow, and the broader base then makes room for a more growth-friendly tax mix funded from a position of strength rather than desperation. That ordering — constraints first, structure second — is the opposite of the "cut taxes and hope" instinct, and it is the one the evidence supports for a country in South Africa's position. That is why this part keeps faith with the hierarchy its source argues for: tax structure is a lower-order lever, and pulling it harder while the higher levers go untouched is effort misdirected. The next articles climb to those higher levers — [The Household Fallacy](/articles/eg-3-6-the-household-fallacy) on whether revenue is even the binding constraint, and [Where Money Comes From](/articles/eg-3-7-where-money-comes-from) and [The Real Lever](/articles/eg-3-8-the-real-lever) on where growth is actually lit.

So hold both halves of this together, because the temptation is always to drop one. Drop the pro-growth half and you are left defending a tax-heavy status quo that is genuinely failing to generate the investment South Africa needs, while pretending the country can redistribute a shrinking pie forever. Drop the sceptical half and you are left promising that a tax cut will conjure a boom, a promise that has failed everywhere it has been tested and would fail here while the grid is still dark. The mature position holds both: growth is non-negotiable, the tax *mix* and the quality of spending are real levers worth pulling, and the deepest constraints on growth lie outside the tax code altogether. That is not a fence-sitting conclusion; it is a redirection — away from the argument the country loves to have about tax rates, and toward the ones it mostly refuses to have, about why the lights go out, why the trains do not run, and where the new money in the economy actually goes.

## Practical Framework: The Virtuous-Cycle Pressure Test

:::takeaway
Taxes can slow growth, and South Africa does need to grow to fund redistribution — both are true. The robust evidence is that the tax *mix* matters (capital and high-income taxes are more growth-harmful than consumption and property taxes) and that growth is the ultimate source of redistributive capacity. The weak claims are that tax cuts pay for themselves (rejected; Kansas is the cautionary case) and that the tax *level* drives growth (the high-tax Nordics refute it). South Africa's investment is gated by power, logistics, skills, crime and credit far more than by tax rates — so the cycle is ignited by fixing real constraints and directing productive credit, not by tax cuts alone.
:::

Whenever someone argues "cut taxes to grow the economy", you can test the claim with four questions. A genuine growth-friendly reform answers them well; a revenue giveaway dressed in growth language fails them.

:::framework
**The Virtuous-Cycle Pressure Test**

Apply these four questions to any "cut taxes to grow" proposal.

**1. Which tax is being cut — and where does it sit on the growth-harm hierarchy?**

The OECD evidence ranks taxes by how much they harm growth: corporate income (most), then personal income, then consumption, then property (least). A cut to a high-harm tax (corporate, top marginal income) has a better growth case than a cut to a low-harm one. If the proposal cuts a low-harm tax in the name of growth, be sceptical.

**2. What share of the lost revenue would the volume effect actually recover — most, some, or little?**

The honest answer for an economy like South Africa's is "some". The Laffer curve is real, but South Africa sits well to the left of its peak, so cuts lose revenue. Treat any claim that a cut "pays for itself" as the red flag it is — Kansas tested that promise and it failed.

**3. Is the binding constraint on the targeted investment actually the tax?**

This is the decisive question. If the investment is really being held back by unreliable electricity, broken logistics, missing skills, crime, or lack of credit, then cutting its tax will not call it forth — it will just forgo revenue. Ask what is *actually* stopping the investment before assuming tax is the lever.

**4. Will the retained revenue be spent in ways that compound growth and fund the social wage — or lost to waste?**

Growth-funds-redistribution only holds if the money is spent well. A cut that leaves more money with productive investors can help; a cut that simply starves functional public services to fund consumption at the top does not. And neither tax level helps if the spending is looted.

A proposal that cuts a high-harm tax, makes honest claims about partial revenue recovery, targets a sector where tax is genuinely the binding constraint, and protects productive spending is a real growth reform. One that promises a self-financing miracle while the grid fails is a giveaway with a growth label.
:::
