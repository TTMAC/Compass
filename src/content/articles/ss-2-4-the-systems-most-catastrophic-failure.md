## The System Fails Its Most Vulnerable Survivors Most Completely

**South Africa has among the highest rates of gender-based violence in the world — and one of the lowest rates of conviction for GBV offences.**

This is not a coincidence. *Gender-based violence* is the crime category that exposes every structural weakness in the criminal justice system simultaneously. Every failure documented in this series so far — the investigation failures, the SAPS-NPA disconnect, the court delays, the geographic inequality, the remand detention crisis — converges on GBV survivors with devastating force. The system fails GBV survivors at every single stage: at the police station, where survivors face re-traumatisation, victim-blaming, and cases that are never properly opened; during investigation, where dockets go missing, forensic evidence is mishandled, and detectives lack specialised training; at prosecution, where cases are declined or withdrawn at rates far above other crime categories; and at sentencing, where even successful convictions often produce inadequate sentences. This is not an anecdotal problem. It is a systemic, measurable, and well-documented failure.

The statistics tell the story with brutal clarity. South Africa records one of the highest rates of sexual offences in the world — over 40,000 reported sexual offences per year in recent SAPS crime statistics, with victim surveys suggesting the true figure is several times higher. The femicide rate — women killed by intimate partners or family members — is estimated at approximately five times the global average. Domestic violence, while notoriously underreported, is pervasive across all socio-economic groups, geographic areas, and demographic categories. And the conviction funnel for these offences is catastrophically narrow: of all sexual offences reported, only a fraction result in arrests, a fraction of those result in prosecutions, and a fraction of prosecutions result in convictions. The system that is supposed to protect the most vulnerable people from the most devastating crimes is the system that fails them most completely.

## Every Structural Weakness, Concentrated in One Crime Category

This article walks you through each stage of that failure — not to catalogue despair, but to identify the specific structural points where intervention could make a difference. It also examines the Thuthuzela Care Centres, a model that demonstrates what is possible when the system is designed around the survivor's needs rather than the institution's convenience. Understanding both the failure and the exception is essential for demanding the reforms that Part 3 of this series will propose.

## The Scale: What the Numbers Actually Show

Before tracing the pipeline failures, you need to understand the scale of GBV in South Africa — and the gap between what the official statistics show and what the evidence tells us about the true prevalence.

SAPS annual crime statistics consistently record between 40,000 and 55,000 sexual offences per year. This figure includes rape, sexual assault, and other sexual offences as defined in the Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act of 2007. But **reporting rates for sexual offences are among the lowest of any crime category**. The Stats SA Victims of Crime Survey and other research consistently indicate that only a minority of sexual offences are reported to the police — estimates range from one in three to one in nine, depending on the study and the specific offence type. **If the true reporting rate is even one in three, the actual number of sexual offences per year exceeds 120,000. If it is one in nine, the figure exceeds 360,000.**

Domestic violence is even harder to quantify because it is not a separate crime category in SAPS statistics — domestic violence cases are recorded under the offence type (assault, assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm, murder, sexual offence) rather than under the context in which the violence occurred. The Domestic Violence Act of 1998 provides for protection orders, but the number of protection order applications is itself a fraction of actual domestic violence incidents. Research by the South African Medical Research Council and other institutions has estimated that one in three women in South Africa has experienced physical violence from an intimate partner, and one in five has experienced sexual violence from a partner.

*Femicide* — the killing of women because they are women, typically by intimate partners or family members — is the most extreme manifestation of GBV. **South Africa's femicide rate, based on mortuary studies and police data, is estimated at approximately 12 per 100,000 women — roughly five times the global average.** Every year, approximately 2,500 to 3,000 women are killed by current or former intimate partners. These are not cold statistics. Each number represents a person whose death was the endpoint of a pattern of violence that the criminal justice system was supposed to interrupt — and failed to.

:::takeaway
South Africa's GBV crisis is measured in staggering numbers: over 40,000 reported sexual offences per year (with true prevalence estimated at several times that figure), a femicide rate approximately five times the global average, and domestic violence affecting an estimated one in three women. But the crisis is not just the prevalence of violence — it is the system's failure to respond to it. The criminal justice pipeline for GBV offences is even more broken than the pipeline for crime generally, with case attrition at every stage that is higher, and conviction rates that are lower, than for other serious crime categories.
:::

## Stage One: The Police Station — Where Survivors Are Failed First

For many GBV survivors, the police station is their first point of contact with the criminal justice system. It is also, in a disturbing number of cases, the point at which the system first fails them.

The problem begins with the reception a survivor receives. Research by the Centre for the Study of Violence and Reconciliation (CSVR), Rape Crisis Cape Town, and other organisations has documented a pattern of secondary victimisation at police stations — survivors of sexual violence being disbelieved, blamed for the assault, asked intrusive and irrelevant questions about their sexual history or clothing, made to wait for hours before being seen, or told to return at a different time because the station is busy. For domestic violence survivors, the pattern is similar: officers minimising the violence, advising the complainant to "go home and sort it out," or treating domestic disputes as private matters rather than criminal offences.

The Domestic Violence Act imposes specific obligations on SAPS officers when they encounter domestic violence: they must inform the complainant of their rights, assist with obtaining a protection order, explain the available remedies, and, if necessary, arrange for the complainant to find shelter. Non-compliance with these obligations is a disciplinary offence. Yet study after study has found that compliance is inconsistent at best and absent at worst. Many station-level officers have not been adequately trained on the Domestic Violence Act's requirements. Many do not have copies of the relevant forms. And the institutional culture at many stations does not treat domestic violence as a priority crime requiring a structured response.

Even when survivors are received respectfully and cases are opened, the quality of the initial case registration often determines whether the case will survive the pipeline. For sexual offences, the initial medical examination is critical: the collection of forensic evidence (through a Sexual Assault Evidence Collection Kit, commonly known as a "rape kit") must occur within 72 hours of the assault to maximise the chances of DNA evidence recovery. Yet there is no guarantee that a survivor will be examined within this window — the distance to a medical facility with trained personnel, the availability of forensic kits, and the coordination between the police station and health services all introduce delays that can compromise evidence.

## Stage Two: Investigation — Where Dockets Disappear

If a GBV case survives the station-level filter and is opened, it enters the investigation stage — and here, the failures become even more acute.

SAPS has specialised Family Violence, Child Protection and Sexual Offences (FCS) units tasked with investigating sexual offences and serious cases of domestic violence. These units represent a recognition that GBV cases require specialised skills — interviewing traumatised survivors, managing forensic evidence, understanding the dynamics of intimate partner violence, and building cases that can withstand the rigours of prosecution. In principle, the FCS units should provide a level of investigation quality above the general detective service.

In practice, the FCS units are chronically understaffed and under-resourced. The number of FCS detectives relative to the caseload they handle is insufficient, and in some areas, FCS units have been amalgamated with general detective services, diluting the specialisation that was their reason for existence. FCS detectives, like general detectives, carry caseloads that make thorough investigation of each case impossible. The result is that many GBV cases receive the same superficial investigation that characterises the general pipeline — and for GBV cases, where the evidence is often more complex and the survivor more vulnerable, superficial investigation is even more likely to produce a case that cannot be prosecuted.

The forensic evidence chain is particularly critical for sexual offences — and particularly fragile. DNA evidence collected through rape kits must be properly stored, transported to SAPS forensic laboratories, and processed within timeframes that preserve its evidentiary value. **The forensic laboratory backlogs — with DNA processing times stretching to months or years — are devastating for sexual offence cases.** When DNA evidence is delayed, the prosecution loses its strongest corroborative tool, and cases that should have been straightforward become difficult or impossible to prove.

**The loss of case dockets — a systemic problem across SAPS — is particularly devastating for GBV survivors.** When a sexual offence docket is lost, the survivor's statement, the medical evidence, the forensic samples, the witness statements, and the entire record of the investigation disappear. The case cannot proceed. The survivor must either re-open the case (requiring them to relive the trauma of reporting) or accept that the system has simply erased their case from existence.

:::expert
The Centre for the Study of Violence and Reconciliation (CSVR) has conducted extensive research into the institutional and cultural barriers that prevent the criminal justice system from responding effectively to gender-based violence. Their analysis has identified a persistent pattern of what they term "institutional patriarchy" within SAPS — a set of unwritten norms, attitudes, and practices that treat GBV as a lesser category of crime, that place the burden of proof implicitly on the survivor rather than the system, and that reflect broader societal attitudes about gender, sexuality, and domestic relationships. The CSVR's research has found that this institutional culture is not confined to individual officers who hold sexist views — it is embedded in operational practices, resource allocation decisions, training curricula, and performance measurement systems that collectively deprioritise GBV relative to other crime categories. Their analysis has argued that addressing GBV investigation failure requires not just training individual officers but transforming the institutional systems that shape how GBV cases are classified, prioritised, investigated, and measured — a structural intervention rather than a behavioural one.
:::

## Stage Three: Prosecution — The Narrowing Funnel

For GBV cases that survive investigation and reach the NPA, the prosecution gateway is another major attrition point. The NPA's annual reports consistently show that sexual offence cases are declined or withdrawn at rates that exceed those for other serious crime categories. The reasons are instructive: insufficient evidence (a direct product of investigation failure), uncooperative or unavailable complainants (often a product of secondary victimisation, fear of retaliation, or the passage of time eroding the survivor's willingness to participate), and cases where the prosecution cannot meet the evidentiary standard required for conviction.

The NPA's Sexual Offences and Community Affairs (SOCA) unit is the specialised prosecution unit tasked with handling sexual offence cases. SOCA prosecutors have specialised training in sexual offences law, survivor-sensitive prosecution techniques, and the management of cases involving vulnerable witnesses. But SOCA's capacity is limited relative to the caseload — many sexual offence cases are prosecuted by general prosecutors who lack specialised training and who may be simultaneously handling dozens of other cases across multiple crime categories.

The withdrawal of charges by survivors is a particularly painful feature of the GBV prosecution pipeline. Survivors withdraw for many reasons: reconciliation with the perpetrator (particularly in domestic violence cases), fear of retaliation, exhaustion with a system that processes their case at a glacial pace, pressure from family or community to drop the matter, and the re-traumatisation of repeated court appearances and cross-examination. **Each withdrawal is recorded in the NPA's statistics as a case "resolved" — but each represents a failure of the system to provide the safety, support, and responsiveness that would enable the survivor to participate in the prosecution.**

## The Thuthuzela Care Centres: An Island of Good Practice

Against this landscape of systemic failure, the Thuthuzela Care Centres (TCCs) stand out as an island of relative good practice — a model that demonstrates what is possible when the system is designed around the survivor's needs.

TCCs are one-stop facilities, typically located at hospitals, that provide integrated services for sexual offence survivors: medical examination (including forensic evidence collection), police statement taking, counselling, and access to a dedicated prosecutor — all in a single location, at a single visit. The model was developed by the NPA's SOCA unit and is operated through partnerships between the NPA, SAPS, the Department of Health, and civil society organisations. There are currently over 50 TCCs across South Africa.

The TCC model addresses several of the pipeline failures that this article has documented. By co-locating services, it reduces the number of separate institutions that a survivor must navigate — instead of visiting the police station, then the hospital, then the court, then a social worker, the survivor accesses all of these services in one location. The dedicated prosecutors and investigators assigned to TCCs have specialised training and reduced caseloads relative to general prosecution services. The forensic evidence collection is conducted by trained medical professionals at the point of care, reducing the risk of evidence loss. And the counselling and support services help survivors navigate the emotional and practical demands of participation in the criminal justice process.

The results are measurable. **TCCs consistently achieve higher conviction rates for sexual offence cases than the general criminal justice system. Cases processed through TCCs have shorter cycle times, lower withdrawal rates, and higher rates of forensic evidence collection.** The TCC model has been recognised internationally as a best-practice approach to sexual offence case management.

:::takeaway
The Thuthuzela Care Centres demonstrate that the criminal justice system can respond effectively to sexual violence when services are integrated, specialised, and designed around the survivor's needs. TCCs achieve higher conviction rates, shorter case cycle times, and lower withdrawal rates than the general system. But with only 50-odd centres across the country, most survivors have no access to a TCC — the model's success highlights what is possible, but its limited coverage highlights how far the system is from delivering that possibility at scale.
:::

But the TCC model's success also highlights the scale of the challenge. With approximately 55 centres across a country of 60 million people, the vast majority of sexual offence survivors do not have access to a TCC. Rural areas, where GBV prevalence is high and police station resources are lowest, are particularly underserved. The TCC model demonstrates what works — but it has not been scaled to a level that would transform the system's overall performance on GBV.

## The Structural Drivers: Why the System Fails GBV Specifically

The pipeline failures documented above are not random. They reflect structural features of the criminal justice system that make it particularly ill-suited to handling GBV cases.

The first structural driver is the classification of domestic violence as "less serious" within the institutional hierarchy. Despite the Domestic Violence Act's provisions and the NPA's National Policy Guidelines on the handling of domestic violence cases, the operational reality at many stations and courts is that domestic violence is treated as a lower priority than other crime categories. Resource allocation, detective assignment, and prosecution attention all reflect this implicit classification — with the result that domestic violence cases receive less investigative effort, less prosecutorial attention, and less judicial time than their severity warrants.

The second structural driver is the absence of specialised courts in most jurisdictions. The Sexual Offences Courts — dedicated courts with specially trained magistrates, prosecutors, and support staff — were established in the late 1990s, scaled back during the mid-2000s, and partially reinstated after sustained advocacy. Where they exist, they produce better outcomes than general courts. But they cover only a fraction of jurisdictions, and the majority of sexual offence cases are heard in general magistrates' courts by judicial officers who may handle sexual offence cases alongside theft, assault, fraud, and traffic matters on the same court roll.

The third structural driver is the inadequacy of the protection order system. The Domestic Violence Act's protection order mechanism is supposed to provide survivors with an immediate legal remedy — a court order prohibiting the abuser from committing further violence, making contact with the survivor, or entering the shared residence. But protection orders are only as effective as their enforcement, and enforcement depends on the same police stations that are failing at every other stage of the GBV pipeline. Research has consistently found that SAPS enforcement of protection order breaches is inconsistent, that many breaches go unpunished, and that **the protection order system provides a sense of legal protection that does not correspond to actual physical protection**.

:::expert
Research by the Commission for Gender Equality and academic institutions including the University of Cape Town's Gender Health and Justice Research Unit has documented the systemic factors that drive the criminal justice system's failure on GBV. Their analyses have identified that the failure is not confined to any single institution or stage — it is a system-wide phenomenon that reflects the interaction between institutional weaknesses (understaffed FCS units, backlogged forensic labs, overloaded courts), legal framework gaps (the classification of domestic violence, the protection order enforcement failure), and the broader social context in which GBV occurs (patriarchal norms that normalise violence, economic dependence that prevents survivors from leaving abusive relationships, community pressure to resolve violence "privately"). This research has argued that effective reform requires simultaneous intervention at multiple points in the system — improving investigation quality alone will not help if courts remain overloaded, and improving courts alone will not help if survivors are failed at the police station. The structural reforms proposed in Part 3 of this series — the investigating magistracy, community courts, and the three-tier policing architecture — would address multiple failure points simultaneously, creating the conditions for meaningful improvement in GBV case outcomes.
:::

## The Survivor's Navigation Guide

If you or someone you know experiences gender-based violence, the criminal justice system should be your ally. In reality, you will need to navigate it actively, armed with knowledge of your rights, the services available, and the escalation paths when the system fails. This guide provides that practical knowledge.

:::framework
**The GBV Survivor's Navigation Guide**

**Immediate Safety:**
If you are in immediate danger, call the SAPS emergency number (10111) or the GBV Command Centre (0800 428 428, which operates 24 hours). If possible, go to a safe location — a neighbour, family member, or friend. If you need shelter, the GBV Command Centre can refer you to a shelter in your area.

**Reporting to the Police:**
You have the right to open a case at any SAPS station, at any time. You do not have to report at the station closest to where the incident occurred. When you arrive, ask for the station's Victim Friendly Room — stations are required to have a designated space for GBV complainants. You have the right to give your statement to an officer of the gender you prefer. If the station does not have a Victim Friendly Room or if you are treated disrespectfully, note the officer's name and service number and report the experience to the station commander and to IPID.

**Medical Examination and Forensic Evidence:**
For sexual offences, seek a medical examination as soon as possible — ideally within 72 hours. If there is a Thuthuzela Care Centre near you, go there — you can find TCC locations through the NPA website or the GBV Command Centre. If there is no TCC, any hospital casualty department can conduct an examination. Do not bathe, change clothing, or wash before the examination — this preserves forensic evidence. The medical examination is free of charge.

**Protection Orders:**
If you are experiencing domestic violence, you can apply for a protection order at any magistrate's court. The application is free. You do not need a lawyer — the clerk of the court is required to assist you with the application. An interim protection order can be granted the same day, without the respondent being present. The respondent is then served with the order and given an opportunity to respond before a final order is made. If the respondent breaches the protection order, this is a criminal offence — report it to the police immediately.

**Legal Representation:**
You have the right to be assisted by a legal practitioner throughout the criminal process. If you cannot afford a private attorney, contact Legal Aid South Africa or legal assistance organisations such as the Legal Resources Centre, Lawyers Against Abuse, or the Women's Legal Centre.

**Escalation When the System Fails:**
If the police station refuses to open your case, insist on speaking to the station commander. If the station commander is unresponsive, contact the provincial SAPS complaints line, file a complaint with IPID, or contact a legal assistance organisation. If your case is not being investigated, contact the investigating officer's supervisor and the station commander. If your protection order is not being enforced, report the breach and the enforcement failure to the station commander and the Civilian Secretariat for Police Service.

**Support Organisations:**
The following organisations provide support to GBV survivors: the GBV Command Centre (0800 428 428), Rape Crisis Cape Town (021 447 1467), People Opposing Women Abuse (POWA) (011 642 4345), Sonke Gender Justice, Lawyers Against Abuse, the Teddy Bear Clinic (for child survivors), and LifeLine South Africa (0861 322 322). Many of these organisations can assist with navigating the criminal justice system, accompany you to police stations and courts, and provide counselling.

**Engaging Parliament:**
If you want to advocate for systemic change, [Making Public Submissions](/articles/4-3-making-public-submissions) explains how to engage with Parliament on GBV legislation. Parliamentary portfolio committees regularly consider GBV-related legislation and policy, and public submissions can influence legislative outcomes.
:::
