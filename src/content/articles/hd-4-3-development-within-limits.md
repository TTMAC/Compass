## The Bottom Line, Up Front

South Africa needs industrial development. This is not a contested claim. A country with an expanded unemployment rate above 40 per cent, a manufacturing sector that has shrunk as a share of GDP for two decades, and infrastructure that is visibly deteriorating does not have the luxury of treating industrialisation as optional. The question is not whether South Africa should industrialise but how — and specifically, how to pursue industrial development in a way that does not poison the water its people drink, destroy the ecosystems that sustain agriculture and tourism, or create unhealthy living conditions in the communities that are supposed to benefit from growth.

Development that degrades the natural environment undermines its own goals. This is not an environmentalist slogan. It is a practical observation. A mining operation that contaminates the groundwater of surrounding communities has not delivered net development to those communities. A power station whose emissions cause respiratory disease in nearby towns has not improved human capabilities in those towns. An industrial process that destroys wetlands — which provide flood protection, water filtration, and fisheries — has not increased the productive capacity of the economy. Environmental degradation is not an abstract future cost. It is a present-day development failure that falls hardest on the poor.

South Africa's economy is resource-intensive and, in many respects, environmentally inefficient. Eskom's coal-fired power stations — beyond their well-documented reliability failures — produce significant air pollution. Mining operations have left a legacy of acid mine drainage, contaminated soil, and degraded landscapes across Gauteng, Mpumalanga, and the North West. Water systems are under stress from pollution, over-extraction, and failing municipal infrastructure. Biodiversity — South Africa is one of the world's seventeen megadiverse countries — is under pressure from habitat destruction, invasive species, and poor land management.

This means that South Africa cannot afford to be careless about the environmental dimensions of its development. But environmental responsibility should be understood as a component of good development strategy, not as a constraint on development. The countries examined in [When the Cycle Works](/articles/hd-4-1-when-the-cycle-works) — South Korea, Malaysia, Botswana — all industrialised aggressively. The lesson from their experience is not that industrialisation is dangerous but that industrialisation produces the best outcomes when accompanied by effective institutions, sound governance, and responsible management of natural resources.

This article introduces the metrics that attempt to capture the relationship between development and environmental impact — the Planetary Pressures-Adjusted HDI, the Sustainable Development Index, the ecological footprint — and applies them to South Africa. It examines countries that have achieved high human development at relatively low environmental cost, analysing what they did efficiently. It explores the energy modernisation challenge that South Africa faces, the economic opportunities in renewable energy and technological leapfrogging, and the practical nexus between water, energy, and food security.

The argument is that South Africa can and should pursue vigorous industrial development, economic growth, and infrastructure expansion — and that doing so with attention to environmental quality, biodiversity protection, and clean living conditions is not a concession to environmentalism but a requirement of effective development. The technologies, policies, and institutional frameworks needed to industrialise responsibly exist, are increasingly cost-competitive, and in some cases offer economic advantages over older, dirtier approaches.

## The Planetary Pressures-Adjusted HDI

In 2020, the United Nations Development Programme introduced a significant modification to the Human Development Index: the Planetary Pressures-Adjusted HDI, or PHDI. The PHDI adjusts the standard HDI downward based on a country's carbon dioxide emissions per capita and its material footprint per capita — two measures of the ecological cost at which development achievements are produced.

The methodology is conceptually straightforward. The standard HDI measures how much development a country achieves. The PHDI measures how efficiently a country achieves that development — how much environmental capacity it consumes per unit of human development. A country with a high HDI and a low ecological footprint receives a PHDI close to its HDI. A country with a high HDI and a high ecological footprint sees its PHDI adjusted downward.

The results are revealing. Norway, which has the world's highest HDI at 0.961, sees its PHDI drop to approximately 0.84 — a penalty of about 12 per cent. The United States, with an HDI of 0.921, sees a PHDI of approximately 0.77 — a penalty of over 16 per cent. Qatar, with an HDI of 0.855, suffers a PHDI penalty of approximately 30 per cent.

South Africa's PHDI presents a concerning picture. Its standard HDI of 0.713 is already moderate by global standards — the development achievement, before any adjustment, is underwhelming for a country with South Africa's resource endowment, institutional sophistication, and historical advantages. The PHDI adjusts this further downward to approximately 0.63 — a penalty of roughly 12 per cent. South Africa's PHDI penalty is comparable to those of countries with much higher development outcomes. South Africa pays an environmental cost similar to that of upper-income countries while achieving development outcomes closer to lower-middle-income countries. It gets the worst of both worlds — high environmental pressure and moderate development.

This is the efficiency problem. South Africa's development model is resource-inefficient. It produces relatively little human development per unit of resources consumed. The coal-fired electricity system is a significant factor, but the broader economic structure — energy-intensive mining, long-distance transport of goods across an economy with poor logistics, energy-inefficient buildings and industrial processes — compounds the problem. What this means for you is that the environmental cost of South Africa's development model is being paid without producing the development outcomes you should be receiving in return.

:::expert[The PHDI Methodology: A Narrow Lens on a Broad Problem]
The PHDI, while an important addition to the development measurement toolkit, has significant methodological limitations that researchers at the Oxford Poverty and Human Development Initiative, the Stockholm Resilience Centre, and South African environmental economists including James Blignaut at the University of Pretoria have identified. The most fundamental limitation is its narrow focus: it uses only two environmental indicators — CO2 emissions and material footprint — as proxies for all ecological impact. This means it misses the environmental dimensions that arguably matter most for a developing country like South Africa: biodiversity loss, water pollution, soil degradation, acid mine drainage, habitat destruction, invasive species, and the degradation of ecosystems that provide essential services to poor communities. A country could score well on CO2 emissions while devastating its rivers, destroying its forests, and driving species to extinction — and the PHDI would not capture this. Conversely, a country that industrialises with relatively high energy use but maintains clean water, healthy ecosystems, and biodiversity could be unfairly penalised. Second, it measures production-based rather than consumption-based impacts, which means that wealthy countries that have outsourced their manufacturing — and thus their pollution — to developing countries appear cleaner than they actually are. Third, for a developing country like South Africa, where the immediate environmental priorities are clean air in communities near industrial facilities, safe drinking water, and protection of the ecosystems that support agriculture and tourism, the PHDI's CO2-centric approach misses the point. Despite these limitations, the PHDI provides a useful signal: South Africa is paying a high environmental price for a moderate development outcome, and there is substantial room to improve the efficiency with which resources are converted into human capabilities.
:::

## The Sustainable Development Index

If the PHDI provides a modest adjustment to the HDI, the Sustainable Development Index — the SDI — provides a more radical reconceptualisation. Developed by Jason Hickel at the Institute for Environmental Science and Technology in Barcelona, the SDI measures development efficiency: the ratio of human development outcomes to ecological impact. It explicitly asks the question that the PHDI approaches more cautiously: which countries are achieving the most human development per unit of ecological impact?

The SDI's methodology rewards countries that achieve high life expectancy, high education levels, and decent income while maintaining a low ecological footprint. It penalises countries that achieve high development at the cost of excessive resource consumption. The results upend the conventional development rankings.

On the standard HDI, the top ten countries are dominated by wealthy European nations, Australia, and East Asian economies. On the SDI, the top performers include Costa Rica, Sri Lanka, Albania, and Georgia — countries that achieve relatively high human development at relatively low ecological cost.

South Africa's SDI ranking is poor for the same reason its PHDI is poor: high environmental impact relative to development outcomes. When you consider countries like Costa Rica or Sri Lanka outranking South Africa on development efficiency despite lower average incomes, the question that follows is: why is South Africa burning through resources and getting so little human development in return? The SDI, while useful as a diagnostic, has an important limitation that must be acknowledged: it rewards low resource use, which in many cases simply reflects poverty rather than efficiency. A country that uses few resources because it lacks industry, infrastructure, and economic activity is not a model to emulate — it is a country that has not yet developed. The goal for South Africa should not be to minimise its ecological footprint for its own sake but to ensure that its resource use translates effectively into human development. The question is not "how do we use less?" but "how do we get more development value from what we use?"

## South Africa's Environmental Challenges

To understand why South Africa's development is environmentally inefficient, it is necessary to examine the specific sources of its environmental impact — and to recognise that the most pressing environmental problems are those that directly harm people and ecosystems today.

### Energy, Pollution, and Environmental Health

South Africa's energy system presents multiple environmental challenges, the most immediately harmful of which is air pollution. Eskom's coal-fired power stations, concentrated in the Mpumalanga Highveld, produce sulphur dioxide, nitrogen oxides, particulate matter, and mercury — pollutants that directly damage human health. Research by the Centre for Environmental Rights and the organisation groundWork has documented that air quality in the Mpumalanga Highveld Air Priority Area regularly exceeds national ambient air quality standards. An estimated 20,000 premature deaths per year in South Africa are attributed to air pollution, with the burden concentrated in low-income communities near power stations and industrial facilities.

This is an environmental problem that demands attention not because of global atmospheric concerns but because South Africans are being made sick and are dying. If you live in the Mpumalanga Highveld — or know someone who does — this is not a policy discussion, it is a daily health reality. Children in eMalahleni grow up breathing polluted air. Communities near coal ash dumps face contaminated water supplies. Workers in coal mines face occupational health hazards. These are immediate, local, tangible harms to human development — the kind of environmental degradation that directly undermines the capabilities and freedoms that the human development framework centres.

Beyond air pollution, South Africa's mining legacy has left a trail of environmental damage. Acid mine drainage from abandoned mines in Gauteng contaminates surface and groundwater. Coal mining in Mpumalanga has destroyed wetlands, degraded water catchments, and scarred landscapes. Mining operations across the country have produced waste dumps that leach heavy metals into surrounding ecosystems. The regulatory framework — the National Environmental Management Act, the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act — exists on paper but enforcement has been inconsistent, and the financial provisions that mining companies are supposed to set aside for environmental rehabilitation are frequently inadequate.

Biodiversity loss is another dimension. South Africa is one of seventeen megadiverse countries globally, home to three of the world's thirty-six biodiversity hotspots (the Cape Floristic Region, the Succulent Karoo, and the Maputaland-Pondoland-Albany region). This biodiversity is not merely an aesthetic asset — it underpins ecosystem services worth billions of rands annually, including pollination, water purification, flood regulation, and soil formation. Habitat destruction from mining, agriculture, and urban expansion threatens these services. The South African National Biodiversity Institute has documented ongoing declines in the conservation status of multiple ecosystems.

### Material Footprint

Beyond energy-related pollution, South Africa's material footprint — the total mass of raw materials extracted from the environment to support economic activity — is high relative to its development level. Mining operations extract hundreds of millions of tonnes of material annually. Water consumption is high relative to availability — South Africa is classified as a water-stressed country, with per capita water availability below the global average and declining. Land degradation, driven by mining, commercial agriculture, and urban sprawl, reduces the productive capacity of ecosystems over time.

South Africa's material footprint per capita is approximately 13 tonnes — roughly twice the global average — reflecting the resource-intensive structure of the economy.

### Getting More Development from Resource Use

The core problem is the mismatch between South Africa's resource use and its development outcomes. Countries with comparable resource consumption levels — Poland, Malaysia, China — have substantially higher HDI scores. Countries with comparable HDI scores — Colombia, Brazil, Thailand — achieve them with substantially lower resource consumption. South Africa occupies an unusual and unfavourable position: high resource use, moderate development.

This is not inevitable — it is the product of an economic structure that has failed to convert resource extraction into broad-based human capabilities. The mining industry extracts enormous value from South African soil but the revenues have not been translated into the health, education, and infrastructure outcomes that a resource-rich country should achieve. The energy system consumes vast quantities of coal but delivers unreliable electricity at rising costs. The development model is inefficient — not because South Africa uses too many resources in some abstract sense, but because it gets too little human development in return.

:::expert[Environmental Justice: Who Bears the Cost of Pollution?]
Environmental justice scholars — including Patrick Bond at the University of Johannesburg, Thuli Makama at the Open Society Foundations, and researchers at the Centre for Environmental Rights — raise an important point about the distribution of environmental harm within South Africa. Industrial pollution, mining contamination, and poor waste management disproportionately affect poor and predominantly Black communities. The communities living near Eskom's power stations in Mpumalanga breathe the worst air. The communities downstream from mining operations drink the most contaminated water. The communities living near landfills and industrial waste sites face the highest exposure to toxic substances. Research by the Energy Research Centre at the University of Cape Town has shown that the top income decile in South Africa benefits most from industrial economic activity while the bottom five deciles bear most of the environmental costs — polluted air, contaminated water, degraded landscapes. This distributional reality means that environmental policy is development policy. Cleaning up air pollution near power stations is not an abstract environmental goal — it is a health intervention that directly improves the capabilities of the poorest South Africans. Enforcing water quality standards near mining operations is not regulatory burden — it is protection of communities' right to clean water. Managing industrial waste responsibly is not a cost to be minimised — it is a basic requirement of development that actually serves the people it claims to benefit. Environmental responsibility and pro-poor development are not in tension; they are the same agenda.
:::

## The "Sweet Spot" Countries

If South Africa's position represents an inefficient combination — high resource use, moderate development — there are countries that demonstrate what efficient development looks like: strong human development outcomes achieved through smart investment. Understanding how they achieved this is essential for designing South Africa's development pathway.

### Costa Rica

Costa Rica is frequently cited as a model of development efficiency. With a GDP per capita approximately one-third of South Africa's when adjusted for purchasing power, Costa Rica achieves an HDI of 0.806 — placing it firmly in the "very high human development" category. Life expectancy is 80 years. Mean years of schooling exceed 8.5. The under-five mortality rate is approximately 8 per 1,000 live births — compared to South Africa's 32.

How does Costa Rica achieve these outcomes? The answer is less about ecological footprint and more about development strategy.

First, health. Costa Rica established universal healthcare in 1941 through the Caja Costarricense de Seguro Social — the CCSS. The system provides comprehensive healthcare to the entire population, funded through social security contributions. Per capita health spending is approximately $1,000 — roughly half of what South Africa spends per capita when public and private spending are combined — but health outcomes are dramatically better. The efficiency comes from a focus on primary healthcare, preventive medicine, and community-based health services, rather than hospital-centric curative care.

Second, education. Costa Rica abolished its army in 1948 and constitutionally redirected military spending to education. Education spending has consistently exceeded 7 per cent of GDP. The result is a well-educated population that supports a diversified services economy, including medical tourism, software development, and ecotourism.

Third, energy. Costa Rica generates approximately 99 per cent of its electricity from renewable sources — primarily hydropower, with significant contributions from geothermal, wind, and solar. This is partly a deliberate policy choice and partly a reflection of geography: Costa Rica has abundant hydropower and geothermal resources and no domestic fossil fuel reserves. The result is cheap, reliable electricity that supports industrial and commercial activity.

Fourth, environmental stewardship. Costa Rica reversed deforestation — forest cover increased from approximately 21 per cent in 1987 to over 52 per cent by 2021 — through a pioneering Payment for Environmental Services programme that compensated landowners for preserving forests. This was smart economics: the forests support a tourism industry that is one of the country's largest foreign exchange earners, and they protect watersheds that supply water to cities and farms.

The lesson for South Africa is not that Costa Rica's specific policies can be transplanted. Costa Rica is a small country (5 million people), without the mining industry, inequality legacy, or spatial structure that characterise South Africa. The lesson is that investing efficiently in people — health, education, social protection — produces better development outcomes than simply increasing resource consumption. Costa Rica gets more development per dollar spent because it spends on the right things, not because it spends less. You can ask the same question of South Africa's budget: is the education spending reaching the children who need it most, or is it absorbed by administration and a wage bill that produces credentials without capability?

### Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka, despite decades of civil war that ended only in 2009, achieves an HDI of 0.782 — "high human development" — with per capita resource consumption far below South Africa's. Life expectancy is 76 years. Literacy is near-universal. Under-five mortality is approximately 7 per 1,000 live births.

Sri Lanka's development efficiency reflects a long history of investment in health and education, inherited from colonial-era welfare state policies and expanded after independence. Its health and education systems deliver outcomes that are remarkably good relative to the country's income level — a testament to consistent prioritisation and reasonably efficient delivery, rather than to any particular environmental strategy.

### What the Sweet Spot Countries Share

The common thread across Costa Rica and Sri Lanka is a development strategy that prioritised investment in human capabilities — health, education, social protection — and that invested efficiently. They built effective primary healthcare systems rather than expensive hospital complexes. They focused on education quality and access rather than prestige institutions. They achieved high life expectancy through preventive health and clean water rather than through the expensive curative interventions that characterise high-income health systems.

For South Africa, this suggests that the pathway to better development outcomes runs through efficiency — getting more human development per rand spent and per unit of resource consumed. South Africa does not need to consume less; it needs to convert what it consumes more effectively into human capabilities. And it needs to ensure that its industrial and economic activity does not create environmental damage that undermines those same capabilities.

## Energy Modernisation

South Africa's energy system needs modernisation — not primarily for environmental reasons, but because it is failing on its own terms. Eskom's coal fleet is old, unreliable, and increasingly expensive to operate. Load-shedding has cost the economy hundreds of billions of rands. The average age of Eskom's coal fleet exceeds 40 years, and the two new coal stations — Medupi and Kusile — have been plagued by construction delays, cost overruns, and design defects. An energy system built around aging coal infrastructure is not serving South Africa's development needs.

Approximately 86,000 people are directly employed in coal mining. An additional estimated 100,000 to 150,000 jobs depend indirectly on the coal value chain — transport, equipment, services, and the local economies that coal workers' spending supports. These jobs are concentrated in Mpumalanga province, where coal mining is the dominant economic activity in several municipalities. The towns of eMalahleni (formerly Witbank), Middelburg, and Secunda exist because of coal.

Energy modernisation must proceed with these realities firmly in view. The case for diversifying South Africa's energy system rests on several pillars — none of which require treating coal as morally objectionable.

First, energy security. An energy system that depends on a single fuel source is vulnerable. When coal supply chains are disrupted — by logistics failures, labour disputes, or quality problems — the entire system is at risk. Diversifying into solar, wind, gas, and potentially nuclear energy reduces this vulnerability. No sensible industrial strategy puts all its eggs in one basket.

Second, reliability. South Africa needs more generation capacity, urgently. Renewable energy projects can be built faster than coal plants — the REIPPPP has demonstrated that private developers can bring capacity online within two to three years of procurement, compared to the decade-plus timelines of Medupi and Kusile. Adding renewable capacity is the fastest route to ending load-shedding.

Third, cost. Solar and wind energy are now cheaper than new coal generation. The average price of electricity from the most recent REIPPPP bidding rounds was approximately R0.50 per kilowatt-hour — less than half the cost of electricity from Medupi and Kusile. In a country where electricity costs are a major burden on households and a constraint on industrial competitiveness, cheaper energy is a development priority.

Fourth, health. Reducing air pollution from coal combustion would prevent thousands of premature deaths per year and reduce the burden of respiratory disease on an already strained health system. This is an immediate, tangible health benefit for the communities — overwhelmingly poor — that live near power stations.

Fifth, economic diversification for coal regions. Mpumalanga's dependence on coal makes it economically fragile. As coal plants reach the end of their operational lives, the region needs alternative economic activity. Proactive diversification — into renewable energy manufacturing, agriculture, tourism, and other industries — is preferable to the economic collapse that would follow unmanaged decline.

None of these arguments, however, diminish the critical importance of baseload energy — reliable, dispatchable power that runs continuously regardless of weather or time of day. Industrialisation requires baseload. Steel mills, aluminium smelters, chemical plants, data centres, and manufacturing lines cannot operate on intermittent power. They need electricity that is available twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week, at predictable cost. No country has industrialised without abundant baseload energy, and no country will. Solar and wind are valuable additions to the energy mix, but they are variable by nature — the sun sets, the wind drops. Without firm baseload capacity to anchor the grid, an energy system built predominantly on renewables will struggle to support the heavy industrial activity that South Africa needs to create jobs at scale.

The baseload question is not whether South Africa needs it — it does — but what source provides it most effectively. Coal has served this function for decades but at rising financial, health, and environmental cost. Gas offers flexibility and lower emissions than coal but introduces import dependency unless domestic gas resources are developed. Nuclear energy offers the most instructive case for low-carbon baseload power.

France demonstrates what a deliberate nuclear strategy can achieve. Following the 1973 oil crisis, France made a strategic decision to invest heavily in nuclear energy to reduce its dependence on imported fossil fuels. Today, approximately 70 per cent of France's electricity comes from nuclear power plants. The result is that France has one of the lowest carbon intensities of electricity generation among industrialised nations — approximately 55 grams of CO2 per kilowatt-hour, compared to over 900 for South Africa's coal-dominated grid. French electricity is reliable, relatively affordable, and supports a fully industrialised economy — the fourth largest in Europe — without the air pollution and carbon burden that coal imposes. France did not deindustrialise to decarbonise; it decarbonised while maintaining industrial capacity, because nuclear provided the firm baseload that industry demands.

South Africa already operates the Koeberg nuclear power station near Cape Town — the only nuclear plant on the African continent — and has the institutional and technical foundations, however imperfect, to expand nuclear capacity. The question is whether the political will, regulatory capacity, and financial discipline exist to do so at a cost and timeline that serve development rather than becoming another Medupi — a sound concept destroyed by poor execution. Nuclear energy is capital-intensive and requires long construction timelines, rigorous safety regulation, and competent project management. South Africa's track record on large infrastructure projects is a legitimate concern. But the development case for nuclear baseload is strong: it provides the kind of reliable, dispatchable, low-pollution power that supports industrialisation, and it does so without the air quality and health costs that coal imposes on communities in Mpumalanga and beyond.

The energy mix that best serves South Africa's development is not a choice between renewables and baseload — it is both. Renewables for cost-effective, rapidly deployable generation. Baseload from a combination of existing coal (managed responsibly through its remaining operational life), gas (where feasible), and potentially expanded nuclear capacity for long-term, low-carbon industrial power. The goal is an energy system that is reliable enough to industrialise, affordable enough to support households and businesses, and clean enough not to poison the communities it is supposed to serve.

The Just Energy Transition Investment Plan — the JET-IP — outlines a framework for mobilising approximately $98 billion over five years to support energy modernisation, with substantial commitments from the European Union, the United States, the United Kingdom, France, and Germany through the Just Energy Transition Partnership announced at COP26 in 2021. This funding represents an unprecedented opportunity for infrastructure modernisation — new generation capacity, transmission upgrades, industrial diversification, and worker retraining.

But the word "effectively" carries enormous weight. South Africa's track record of deploying large-scale investment programmes is, as documented in [South Africa's Broken Cycle](/articles/hd-4-2-south-africas-broken-cycle), deeply problematic. The institutional capacity to manage a transition of this complexity — involving multiple government departments, state-owned enterprises, private sector partners, international funders, labour unions, and affected communities — has not been demonstrated. The risk is that transition funding follows the pattern of other large-scale South African development programmes: announced with fanfare, designed with sophistication, and implemented with incompetence or corruption.

The non-negotiable principle is that workers and communities must not bear the cost of energy modernisation. Coal workers in Mpumalanga did not choose the structure of South Africa's energy system. They took the jobs that were available. Any transition that destroys their livelihoods without providing viable alternatives is not a development programme — it is a transfer of costs from one group of South Africans to another. If you live in a coal-dependent region like Mpumalanga, this is not a distant policy question — it is about whether your town still exists in twenty years with a functioning economy or whether it follows the trajectory of hollowed-out mining towns from the North West to the Northern Cape.

:::expert[The Political Economy of Energy Modernisation in Mpumalanga]
Research by the Trade and Industrial Policy Strategies think tank, the Centre for Environmental Rights, and scholars including Saliem Fakir at the African Climate Foundation and Jesse Burton at E3G has identified several political economy dynamics that could derail energy modernisation in South Africa. First, the coal industry lobby — including mining companies, coal transporters, and coal-dependent municipalities — has strong incentives to delay modernisation and capture transition funding for coal-extending rather than diversifying investments. Second, Eskom's institutional culture remains oriented toward coal, and elements within the utility resist change as a threat to their institutional relevance and employment. Third, organised labour — particularly the National Union of Mineworkers — supports modernisation in principle but demands guarantees of employment and income security that may not be fiscally feasible in all cases. Fourth, the communities most affected — coal workers and their families in Mpumalanga — have limited voice in the policy process relative to the institutional actors that claim to represent their interests. The risk is that energy modernisation becomes a political negotiation among powerful institutional actors, with the actual affected communities — whose development outcomes are at stake — marginalised in the process. Effective governance of this process requires direct community participation, transparent allocation of resources, and independent monitoring of outcomes — precisely the governance capabilities that South Africa struggles to deliver.
:::

## Renewable Energy as Economic Opportunity

The shift toward renewable energy is not merely a substitution of one energy source for another. It is an economic opportunity that could address multiple dimensions of South Africa's development challenge simultaneously.

South Africa has extraordinary renewable energy potential. Solar irradiation in the Northern Cape exceeds 2,500 kilowatt-hours per square metre per year — among the highest in the world. Wind resources along the Eastern and Western Cape coastlines are sufficient for large-scale wind generation. The combination of abundant solar and wind resources means that South Africa could, in principle, generate a large share of its electricity from renewable sources at costs that are now lower than new coal generation and increasingly competitive with existing coal generation.

The Renewable Energy Independent Power Producer Procurement Programme — the REIPPPP — demonstrated what is possible. Between 2011 and 2021, the programme attracted over R210 billion in private investment, brought approximately 6,300 megawatts of renewable capacity online, and created an estimated 58,000 full-time equivalent jobs in construction and operations. The average price of electricity from REIPPPP projects has fallen dramatically with each bidding round, reflecting global trends in technology cost reduction.

The development case for renewable energy stands on economics and practical benefits — and the efficiency gap affects you directly: you pay more for electricity generated from aging, debt-ridden coal infrastructure than you would for electricity from modern renewables at current technology costs. First, cheaper electricity reduces costs for households and businesses, improving competitiveness and disposable income. Second, decentralised renewable energy — rooftop solar, community microgrids, off-grid systems — can provide electricity to communities that are poorly served by the centralised grid, improving energy access without the infrastructure cost of extending transmission lines. Third, renewable energy manufacturing — solar panels, wind turbine components, battery storage systems — represents an industrial opportunity for a country that needs to diversify its manufacturing base and create employment. Fourth, a cleaner energy system reduces the air pollution that causes an estimated 20,000 premature deaths per year in South Africa, with the health benefits concentrated in the low-income communities that live closest to power stations and industrial facilities.

The reinforcing cycle, rebuilt on a modernised energy system, would follow the same logic as the development cycles described in [When the Cycle Works](/articles/hd-4-1-when-the-cycle-works). Reliable, affordable energy enables development investment. Development investment improves human capabilities. Improved human capabilities support higher-value economic activity. Higher-value economic activity generates revenue. Revenue funds further development and energy investment. Each link reinforces the next — and a diversified, modern energy system provides a more resilient foundation than one dependent on aging coal infrastructure.

## The Water-Energy-Food Nexus

South Africa is a water-scarce country, and this scarcity is one of the most significant environmental constraints on its development. Average annual rainfall is approximately 450 millimetres — roughly half the global average. Distribution is highly uneven: the eastern regions receive adequate rainfall while the western regions are semi-arid to arid. Rainfall variability, periodic droughts, and the demands of a growing economy and population place increasing pressure on water resources.

The water-energy-food nexus describes the interconnection between three systems that are individually critical and collectively interdependent. Energy production requires water — Eskom's coal plants consume approximately 2 per cent of South Africa's total freshwater withdrawals for cooling. Water treatment and distribution require energy — pumping, purifying, and distributing water consumes approximately 2 per cent of South Africa's electricity. Food production requires both water (irrigation consumes approximately 60 per cent of South Africa's water) and energy (farming, processing, and transport are energy-intensive).

This interconnection means that stress in any one system cascades through the others — and you experience this cascade every time load-shedding triggers a boil-water advisory, or every time a drought year pushes up food prices in the same week your electricity bill increases. When Eskom load-sheds, water treatment plants lose power, and water supply is interrupted. When water supply is constrained by drought, agriculture suffers, food prices rise, and food security deteriorates. When energy prices rise due to Eskom's financial crisis, farming costs increase, food prices increase further, and the poorest households — which spend the highest proportion of their income on food and energy — are squeezed hardest.

Water scarcity is not a future problem — it is a present reality. The Day Zero crisis in Cape Town in 2018 demonstrated how close a major city can come to running out of water. Municipal water infrastructure across the country is deteriorating, with water losses (non-revenue water) exceeding 40 per cent in many municipalities. Water pollution from mining, agriculture, and inadequate sewage treatment reduces the usable supply further.

For the reinforcing cycle, the water-energy-food nexus represents both a constraint and an opportunity. The constraint is clear: a development pathway that depletes water resources and is vulnerable to drought is fragile. The opportunity is that integrated solutions — renewable energy that does not consume water for cooling (solar and wind, unlike coal), water-efficient agriculture (drip irrigation, drought-resistant crops), investment in water infrastructure maintenance, and pollution control near mining operations — can address multiple dimensions of the nexus simultaneously. Each of these interventions is good development policy in its own right; together, they build resilience.

## Responsible Development

Kate Raworth's Doughnut Economics framework provides a visual and conceptual model for the development challenge South Africa faces. The doughnut has two boundaries: a social foundation (the minimum level of human development that every person should achieve) and an ecological ceiling (the maximum level of environmental impact that is compatible with a healthy, functioning natural environment). The goal of development is to operate in the space between these boundaries — meeting everyone's basic needs while maintaining a clean, healthy, biodiverse environment.

Applied to South Africa, the picture is sobering — but the priorities are clear. Think about where you sit in this picture: are the basic needs that the social foundation describes — clean water, reliable electricity, decent schooling, physical safety — being met in your household, your street, your municipality? South Africa falls below the social foundation on multiple dimensions — life expectancy, education quality, employment, income, safety, housing, water and sanitation. These social deficits are the primary development failure. Simultaneously, it faces environmental pressures — air pollution, water contamination, biodiversity loss, land degradation — that directly harm the people the social foundation is supposed to protect.

The framework reframes the development question usefully. The question is not "how do we shrink the economy to reduce environmental impact?" — that would be absurd in a country with 40 per cent expanded unemployment. The question is "how do we meet the social foundation — jobs, health, education, housing, clean water — while managing our environmental challenges responsibly?" This requires development that is smart, efficient, and attentive to environmental quality — not development that is constrained or apologetic.

For South Africa specifically, the responsible development agenda suggests several priorities. First, meet the social foundation: this means jobs, functional healthcare, quality education, housing, clean water, and personal safety. These are the urgent priorities and they require economic growth, industrial development, and infrastructure investment. Second, clean up pollution: enforce air quality standards near industrial facilities, address acid mine drainage, ensure safe drinking water, and manage waste responsibly. This is not anti-development — it is pro-community. Third, protect biodiversity and ecosystems: South Africa's extraordinary natural heritage is an economic asset (supporting tourism, agriculture, fisheries, and water security) and must be managed as such. Fourth, modernise the energy system: for cost, reliability, and health reasons. Fifth, invest in water security: the binding environmental constraint in much of South Africa.

## The Case for Leapfrogging

South Africa does not have to follow outdated development models. The concept of leapfrogging — skipping intermediate stages of technological development by adopting the most advanced available technologies directly — is not hypothetical. South Africa has already done it in several domains.

Mobile banking is the most cited example. South Africa effectively leapfrogged the branch-banking model — the network of physical bank branches that developed countries built over centuries — by adopting mobile and digital banking at scale. Mobile money and digital payment platforms enable financial transactions for millions of South Africans who never had access to a physical bank branch. The infrastructure cost is a fraction of what a branch network would have required, and the service is often more accessible and convenient.

Solar energy offers a similar leapfrogging opportunity. Rather than relying exclusively on extending the centralised grid — a model that is expensive and slow — South Africa could accelerate the deployment of distributed solar energy systems. Rooftop solar for households and businesses, community solar installations, and off-grid solar systems for remote communities can provide electricity faster and cheaper than grid extension. The technology costs have fallen by approximately 90 per cent over the past decade, making solar competitive with or cheaper than grid electricity in most applications.

Distance education and digital learning represent another leapfrogging opportunity. South Africa's education quality problem is substantially a teacher quality problem — there are not enough well-trained teachers, particularly in mathematics, science, and languages, to staff every school adequately. Digital learning platforms, while not a substitute for human teachers, can supplement classroom instruction, provide access to high-quality content, and enable teacher professional development at scale. Countries like India (with the DIKSHA platform) and Bangladesh (with access to information programmes) have demonstrated that digital education can improve learning outcomes in low-resource settings.

Telemedicine can extend healthcare access to underserved communities without the capital cost of building new clinics. Electric vehicles can reduce transport costs and air pollution in cities. Precision agriculture can improve crop yields while reducing water and chemical inputs. In each case, the most advanced technology is increasingly the most cost-effective technology — the leapfrogging option is not just cleaner but economically rational.

The reinforcing cycle, built on modern technologies, has the potential to be both faster and more efficient than older development models. Solar electricity is cheaper than new coal electricity. Mobile banking is cheaper than branch banking. Digital education can scale faster than physical school construction. Telemedicine can reach communities faster than clinic construction. Each leapfrogging technology reduces the capital cost and time required for development investment, enabling more development per rand spent.

But leapfrogging is not automatic. It requires institutional capacity to identify and deploy appropriate technologies. It requires investment in digital infrastructure — broadband connectivity, which remains inadequate in rural South Africa. You can see the gap yourself: a South African entrepreneur in Cape Town can run a business on her phone with mobile banking, e-commerce, and digital marketing, while her counterpart in rural Limpopo still travels fifty kilometres to a bank branch because the connectivity does not exist for the same digital tools. It requires regulatory frameworks that enable new technologies rather than protecting incumbent industries. And it requires human capital — the engineers, technicians, and managers who can implement and maintain new systems.

The reinforcing cycle applies here as well. Improved education produces the human capital needed to deploy modern technologies. Modern technologies enable more efficient development investment. More efficient development investment produces better outcomes, which justify further investment in the education and infrastructure that support further leapfrogging. Each turn of the cycle makes the next easier — but the first turn requires deliberate investment and institutional commitment.

South Africa stands at a junction. The development models of previous decades — whether coal-dependent industrialisation or resource-extraction without environmental management — have produced underwhelming results: moderate human development at high environmental cost. The opportunity now is to industrialise using the most modern, efficient, and cost-effective technologies available — technologies that happen to be cleaner but are chosen because they are better. This requires the same institutional discipline, sustained commitment, and political courage that the case studies in [When the Cycle Works](/articles/hd-4-1-when-the-cycle-works) described. But it offers a development pathway that builds capability more efficiently and does not undermine its own foundations through environmental degradation.

What are your constitutional rights to development? How do you hold government accountable for development outcomes? And how do you participate in the decisions that shape the reinforcing cycle — for better or for worse — in your community, your province, and your country?

**The Responsible Development Scorecard**

Use this framework to evaluate whether South Africa's development trajectory is building human capabilities while maintaining environmental quality — pursuing industrial progress with environmental responsibility.

**Dimension 1: Energy modernisation.**
Key metric: Generation capacity added and load-shedding frequency; share of electricity from diversified sources.
Current: approximately 10 per cent renewable (growing); persistent load-shedding. Target: reliable electricity supply with diversified generation mix.
Source: Eskom annual reports; IPP Office quarterly reports; Department of Mineral Resources and Energy statistics.
What to ask: Is new generation capacity being added fast enough to end load-shedding? Is the energy mix diversifying for security and cost reasons? Is distributed generation (rooftop solar) being enabled or obstructed by regulation?

**Dimension 2: Environmental quality.**
Key metrics: Air quality measurements in industrial areas; water quality in mining-affected catchments; biodiversity conservation status; waste management performance.
Source: Department of Forestry, Fisheries and the Environment; South African National Biodiversity Institute; Department of Water and Sanitation.
What to ask: Is air quality improving in Mpumalanga and other industrial areas? Is acid mine drainage being managed? Are environmental regulations being enforced? Is biodiversity being protected as an economic and ecological asset?

**Dimension 3: Water security.**
Key metric: Blue Drop and Green Drop scores (drinking water and wastewater treatment quality).
Source: Department of Water and Sanitation regulatory reports.
What to ask: Are Blue Drop scores improving or declining? Are municipalities investing in water infrastructure maintenance? Is water loss (non-revenue water) being reduced? Water security is the binding environmental constraint in many parts of South Africa.

**Dimension 4: Social foundation gaps.**
Key metrics: Life expectancy, learning-adjusted years of schooling, expanded unemployment rate, MPI headcount.
Source: UNDP Human Development Report; Stats SA; World Bank Human Capital Index.
What to ask: Is South Africa closing its social foundation gaps? Meeting basic human needs — jobs, health, education, housing, clean water — is the primary development objective. If the social foundation is not being met, development is failing regardless of any other metric.

**Dimension 5: Industrial development and environmental management.**
Key metric: Manufacturing value added as share of GDP; employment trends in industrial sectors and coal-dependent regions; new employment in diversifying industries; environmental compliance rates.
Source: Stats SA Quarterly Labour Force Survey (by province and sector); Department of Trade, Industry and Competition; Presidential Climate Commission reports.
What to ask: Is South Africa industrialising and creating jobs? Are coal workers and communities being supported through economic diversification? Are new industries emerging in Mpumalanga and other transitioning regions? Is industrial development proceeding with adequate environmental management — or are environmental costs being externalised onto poor communities?
