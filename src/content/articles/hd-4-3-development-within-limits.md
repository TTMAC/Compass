## The Bottom Line, Up Front

Development that destroys the planet is not development. This is not an environmental platitude. It is a logical consequence of the definition of human development that this series has used from the beginning: the expansion of human capabilities and freedoms. A development pathway that degrades the ecological systems on which all human life depends — clean air, stable climate, fresh water, fertile soil, functioning ecosystems — does not expand human capabilities. It borrows against the capabilities of future generations to inflate the apparent capabilities of the present. And the bill, when it comes due, falls heaviest on the poor.

South Africa faces this tension more acutely than almost any other developing country. Its economy is among the most carbon-intensive in the world. Eskom's fleet of coal-fired power stations makes South Africa the fourteenth-largest emitter of carbon dioxide globally, despite ranking only thirty-third in GDP. Per capita carbon emissions are approximately 7.5 tonnes — comparable to several European countries, and four times the sub-Saharan African average. South Africa emits at developed-country levels without producing developed-country outcomes. It has the carbon footprint of Germany without the HDI of Germany, the material throughput of Poland without the life expectancy of Poland.

This means that South Africa cannot replicate the development pathway that the case studies in [When the Cycle Works](/articles/hd-4-1-when-the-cycle-works) described. South Korea's industrialisation was powered by coal and then oil. Malaysia's transformation relied on fossil-fuel-intensive manufacturing and palm oil plantations. Even Botswana's development was funded by a mining industry with a significant environmental footprint. The twentieth-century model of development — burn fossil fuels, industrialise, get rich, then clean up — is no longer available. The atmospheric carbon budget is spent. The climate system is already destabilised. A country that pursues carbon-intensive industrialisation in the 2020s and 2030s is not following in South Korea's footsteps. It is accelerating a crisis that will undermine the very development outcomes it seeks.

This article introduces the metrics that capture this tension — the Planetary Pressures-Adjusted HDI, the Sustainable Development Index, the ecological footprint — and applies them to South Africa. It examines the countries that have achieved high human development at low ecological cost, analysing what they did differently. It explores the just transition challenge that South Africa faces as it shifts away from coal, and the opportunities that renewable energy, technological leapfrogging, and the circular economy present for building the reinforcing cycle sustainably.

The argument is not that South Africa must choose between development and environmental sustainability. The argument is that, in the twenty-first century, development without environmental sustainability is not development at all — and that the technologies, policies, and institutional frameworks needed to achieve sustainable development exist, are increasingly cost-competitive, and in some cases offer advantages over the fossil-fuel-intensive pathway that previous generations of developing countries followed.

## The Planetary Pressures-Adjusted HDI

In 2020, the United Nations Development Programme introduced a significant modification to the Human Development Index: the Planetary Pressures-Adjusted HDI, or PHDI. The PHDI adjusts the standard HDI downward based on a country's carbon dioxide emissions per capita and its material footprint per capita — two measures of the ecological cost at which development achievements are produced.

The methodology is conceptually straightforward. The standard HDI measures how much development a country achieves. The PHDI measures how efficiently a country achieves that development — how much planetary capacity it consumes per unit of human development. A country with a high HDI and a low ecological footprint receives a PHDI close to its HDI. A country with a high HDI and a high ecological footprint sees its PHDI adjusted downward, reflecting the unsustainability of its development pathway.

The results are revealing. Norway, which has the world's highest HDI at 0.961, sees its PHDI drop to approximately 0.84 — a penalty of about 12 per cent, reflecting the ecological cost of its petroleum-funded development model. The United States, with an HDI of 0.921, sees a PHDI of approximately 0.77 — a penalty of over 16 per cent, reflecting its outsized material and carbon footprint. Qatar, with an HDI of 0.855, suffers a PHDI penalty of approximately 30 per cent, reflecting the extreme carbon intensity of its hydrocarbon-based economy.

South Africa's PHDI presents a peculiar and damning picture. Its standard HDI of 0.713 is already moderate by global standards — the development achievement, before any adjustment, is underwhelming for a country with South Africa's resource endowment, institutional sophistication, and historical advantages. The PHDI adjusts this further downward to approximately 0.63 — a penalty of roughly 12 per cent. But here is the critical point: South Africa's PHDI penalty is comparable to those of countries with much higher development outcomes. South Africa pays an ecological cost similar to that of upper-income countries while achieving development outcomes closer to lower-middle-income countries. It gets the worst of both worlds — high planetary pressure and moderate development.

This is the efficiency problem. South Africa's development model is carbon-inefficient. It produces relatively little human development per unit of carbon emitted or material consumed. The coal-fired electricity system is the primary culprit, but the broader economic structure — energy-intensive mining, long-distance transport of goods across an economy with poor logistics, energy-inefficient buildings and industrial processes — compounds the problem.

:::expert[The PHDI Methodology: Strengths and Limitations]
The PHDI, while an important addition to the development measurement toolkit, has methodological limitations that researchers at the Oxford Poverty and Human Development Initiative, the Stockholm Resilience Centre, and South African environmental economists including James Blignaut at the University of Pretoria have identified. First, it uses only two environmental indicators — CO2 emissions and material footprint — and excludes other dimensions of ecological impact such as biodiversity loss, water consumption, and land use change. This means that countries with low carbon emissions but severe environmental degradation in other dimensions may receive artificially high PHDI scores. Second, it measures production-based rather than consumption-based emissions, which means that countries that have outsourced their manufacturing to developing countries (and thus their emissions) appear more sustainable than they actually are. Third, it treats current emissions as the relevant metric, without adjusting for historical cumulative emissions — a significant omission for climate justice analysis, since the countries with the highest current development outcomes achieved them partly through centuries of emissions that are now in the atmosphere. Despite these limitations, the PHDI provides a useful corrective to the standard HDI by introducing the ecological dimension into development measurement. For South Africa specifically, it quantifies a reality that is intuitively obvious: the country is paying a high ecological price for a moderate development outcome, which means there is substantial room to improve development efficiency.
:::

## The Sustainable Development Index

If the PHDI provides a modest adjustment to the HDI, the Sustainable Development Index — the SDI — provides a more radical reconceptualisation. Developed by Jason Hickel at the Institute for Environmental Science and Technology in Barcelona, the SDI measures development efficiency: the ratio of human development outcomes to ecological impact. It explicitly asks the question that the PHDI approaches more cautiously: which countries are achieving the most human development per unit of ecological impact?

The SDI's methodology rewards countries that achieve high life expectancy, high education levels, and decent income while staying within planetary boundaries. It penalises countries that achieve high development at the cost of excessive resource consumption and emissions. The results upend the conventional development rankings.

On the standard HDI, the top ten countries are dominated by wealthy European nations, Australia, and East Asian economies. On the SDI, the top performers include Costa Rica, Cuba, Sri Lanka, Albania, and Georgia — countries that achieve relatively high human development at relatively low ecological cost. The wealthy countries that top the HDI — Norway, Switzerland, Australia, the United States — rank poorly on the SDI because their development achievements came at an ecological cost that, if replicated globally, would require multiple planet Earths.

South Africa's SDI ranking is poor for the same reason its PHDI is poor: high ecological impact relative to development outcomes. But the SDI provides an additional insight. It suggests that the development frontier — the set of human development outcomes that are achievable within planetary boundaries — is not as constrained as conventional thinking assumes. Costa Rica, with a per capita income a fraction of Norway's, achieves an HDI of 0.806 while consuming a fraction of Norway's ecological resources. If Costa Rica can reach "very high human development" within planetary limits, the question for South Africa is not whether sustainable development is possible but how to achieve it.

## South Africa's Ecological Footprint

To understand why South Africa's development is so ecologically expensive, it is necessary to examine the specific sources of its environmental impact.

### Carbon Emissions: The Coal Dependency

South Africa's carbon dioxide emissions are approximately 435 million tonnes per year, making it the fourteenth-largest emitter globally. Per capita emissions are approximately 7.5 tonnes, compared to a global average of approximately 4.7 tonnes and a sub-Saharan African average of approximately 0.9 tonnes. South Africa is responsible for approximately 1 per cent of global emissions — a disproportionate contribution for a country with approximately 0.8 per cent of the global population and approximately 0.4 per cent of global GDP.

The primary driver is Eskom's coal-fired power generation fleet. Approximately 80 per cent of South Africa's electricity is generated from coal, making the electricity system one of the most carbon-intensive in the world. A single unit of electricity generated by Eskom produces approximately 1.0 kilograms of CO2 — roughly ten times the emissions intensity of electricity generated from renewable sources and approximately four times the global average emissions intensity of electricity.

Beyond electricity, South Africa's industrial structure is energy-intensive. The mining sector, the chemicals sector (particularly Sasol's coal-to-liquids operations, which produce fuel from coal at an extraordinarily high emissions intensity), and the metals sector are major emitters. The transport sector, which relies almost entirely on liquid fossil fuels, contributes approximately 10 per cent of total emissions.

The consequence is that almost every economic activity in South Africa carries a larger carbon footprint than the same activity in most other countries. A South African factory powered by Eskom electricity is more carbon-intensive than an equivalent factory in Brazil (hydropower), France (nuclear), or Kenya (geothermal). A South African hospital, school, or university — powered by coal-fired electricity — has a larger carbon footprint per student or patient than equivalents in countries with cleaner energy systems. Even South Africa's social development achievements — the houses built through RDP, the schools connected to the grid, the clinics electrified — carry a carbon cost that would be dramatically lower if the electricity system were decarbonised.

### Material Footprint

Beyond carbon emissions, South Africa's material footprint — the total mass of raw materials extracted from the environment to support economic activity — is high relative to its development level. Mining operations extract hundreds of millions of tonnes of material annually. Water consumption is high relative to availability — South Africa is classified as a water-stressed country, with per capita water availability below the global average and declining. Land degradation, driven by mining, commercial agriculture, and urban sprawl, reduces the productive capacity of ecosystems over time.

The material footprint dimension of the PHDI captures this broader ecological impact. South Africa's material footprint per capita is approximately 13 tonnes — roughly twice the global average — reflecting the resource-intensive structure of the economy.

### The Emissions-Development Mismatch

The core problem is the mismatch between South Africa's ecological impact and its development outcomes. Countries with comparable per capita emissions — Poland, Malaysia, China — have substantially higher HDI scores. Countries with comparable HDI scores — Colombia, Brazil, Thailand — have substantially lower per capita emissions. South Africa occupies an unusual and unfavourable position: high emissions, moderate development. This is not inevitable — it is the product of an economic structure built around cheap coal that has now become neither cheap nor clean, and a development model that has failed to convert resource extraction into human capabilities.

:::expert[South Africa in the Global Carbon Budget: A Climate Justice Perspective]
Climate justice scholars — including Patrick Bond at the University of Johannesburg, Thuli Makama at the Open Society Foundations, and international scholars including Naomi Klein and Jason Hickel — raise an important distinction between South Africa's aggregate emissions and the distribution of those emissions within the country. Approximately half of South Africa's emissions come from Eskom, which provides electricity to the entire economy. But the benefits of that electricity are distributed highly unequally — wealthier households and businesses consume far more electricity per capita than poor households, many of which remain in energy poverty despite being connected to the grid. Research by the Energy Research Centre at the University of Cape Town has shown that the top income decile in South Africa is responsible for per capita emissions comparable to the European average, while the bottom five deciles have per capita emissions comparable to the least-developed countries. This distributional reality means that climate mitigation policy — carbon taxes, emissions standards, coal plant closures — has distributional consequences that must be managed. A carbon tax that raises electricity prices hurts poor households that already spend a disproportionate share of their income on energy. Coal plant closures that destroy jobs in Mpumalanga hurt communities that have no alternative employment. The just transition is not merely an environmental concept — it is a development concept, and it must be designed with the same attention to distributional equity that any development policy requires.
:::

## The "Sweet Spot" Countries

If South Africa's position represents the worst of both worlds — high ecological impact, moderate development — there are countries that represent the best of both: high development at low ecological cost. Understanding how they achieved this is essential for designing South Africa's development pathway.

### Costa Rica

Costa Rica is the most frequently cited example of sustainable development. With a GDP per capita approximately one-third of South Africa's when adjusted for purchasing power, Costa Rica achieves an HDI of 0.806 — placing it firmly in the "very high human development" category. Life expectancy is 80 years. Mean years of schooling exceed 8.5. The under-five mortality rate is approximately 8 per 1,000 live births — compared to South Africa's 32.

Costa Rica achieves these outcomes at an ecological footprint that is approximately one-third of South Africa's per capita. How?

First, energy. Costa Rica generates approximately 99 per cent of its electricity from renewable sources — primarily hydropower, with significant contributions from geothermal, wind, and solar. The country invested in renewable energy infrastructure decades before it became fashionable, driven partly by the absence of fossil fuel resources and partly by a deliberate policy choice to preserve its natural environment.

Second, health. Costa Rica established universal healthcare in 1941 through the Caja Costarricense de Seguro Social — the CCSS. The system provides comprehensive healthcare to the entire population, funded through social security contributions. Per capita health spending is approximately $1,000 — roughly half of what South Africa spends per capita when public and private spending are combined — but health outcomes are dramatically better. The efficiency comes from a focus on primary healthcare, preventive medicine, and community-based health services, rather than hospital-centric curative care.

Third, education. Costa Rica abolished its army in 1948 and constitutionally redirected military spending to education. Education spending has consistently exceeded 7 per cent of GDP. The result is a well-educated population that supports a diversified services economy, including medical tourism, software development, and ecotourism.

Fourth, environmental policy. Costa Rica reversed deforestation — forest cover increased from approximately 21 per cent in 1987 to over 52 per cent by 2021 — through a pioneering Payment for Environmental Services programme that compensated landowners for preserving forests. The country has committed to full decarbonisation by 2050.

The lesson for South Africa is not that Costa Rica's specific policies can be transplanted. Costa Rica is a small country (5 million people), without the mining industry, inequality legacy, or spatial structure that characterise South Africa. The lesson is that high human development does not require high ecological impact. The two can be decoupled — and Costa Rica demonstrates that the decoupling is not theoretical but achievable.

### Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka, despite decades of civil war that ended only in 2009, achieves an HDI of 0.782 — "high human development" — at per capita emissions of approximately 1.0 tonne, roughly one-seventh of South Africa's. Life expectancy is 76 years. Literacy is near-universal. Under-five mortality is approximately 7 per 1,000 live births.

Sri Lanka's development efficiency reflects several factors. Its economy is less resource-intensive than South Africa's — services and agriculture rather than mining and heavy industry. Its energy system, while not as renewable as Costa Rica's, is less carbon-intensive than South Africa's coal-dominated grid. And its health and education systems, inherited from colonial-era welfare state policies and expanded after independence, deliver outcomes that are remarkably good relative to the country's income level.

### Cuba

Cuba is the most controversial case in the sustainable development pantheon. Its HDI of approximately 0.764 is achieved at one of the lowest ecological footprints in the world for a country at that development level. Life expectancy is 78 years. Doctor-to-population ratios are among the highest globally. Literacy is universal. Infant mortality is lower than in the United States.

Cuba achieves these outcomes partly by choice (investment in health and education as deliberate policy) and partly by constraint (the US embargo has limited economic growth and, consequently, resource consumption). The political system — a one-party state with significant restrictions on civil liberties — makes Cuba an uncomfortable model for democratic South Africa. But the development efficiency is real: Cuba demonstrates that health and education outcomes approaching developed-country levels can be achieved at a fraction of developed-country ecological cost.

### What the Sweet Spot Countries Share

The common thread across Costa Rica, Sri Lanka, and Cuba is a development strategy that prioritised human capabilities — health, education, social protection — over material accumulation. They invested in people rather than in stuff. They built efficient health systems rather than expensive hospital complexes. They focused on primary education quality rather than prestige universities. They achieved high life expectancy through preventive health and clean water rather than through the expensive curative interventions that characterise high-income health systems.

For South Africa, this suggests that the pathway to sustainable development runs through efficiency — getting more human development per unit of resource consumed — rather than through austerity or degrowth. South Africa does not need to consume less; it needs to convert what it consumes more effectively into human capabilities.

## The Just Transition

South Africa's shift away from coal is not merely an environmental imperative. It is a development challenge of the first order, because coal is deeply embedded in the economy, the employment structure, and the social fabric of specific regions.

Approximately 86,000 people are directly employed in coal mining. An additional estimated 100,000 to 150,000 jobs depend indirectly on the coal value chain — transport, equipment, services, and the local economies that coal workers' spending supports. These jobs are concentrated in Mpumalanga province, where coal mining is the dominant economic activity in several municipalities. The towns of eMalahleni (formerly Witbank), Middelburg, and Secunda exist because of coal. Without coal, they face economic collapse.

Eskom employs approximately 40,000 people, the majority in coal-fired power stations. As these stations reach the end of their operational lives — the average age of Eskom's coal fleet exceeds 40 years — thousands of workers face displacement. The workers are typically semi-skilled or skilled in coal-specific technologies, and the transferability of their skills to other industries is limited without significant retraining.

The just transition is the framework for managing this shift in a way that does not create a new development deficit. The concept, which emerged from the labour movement, insists that the costs of environmental transition should not fall disproportionately on workers and communities that are least responsible for the environmental damage and least able to absorb the economic shock.

South Africa has established institutional mechanisms for the just transition. The Presidential Climate Commission, created in 2020, advises the government on climate change policy with an explicit mandate to address social and economic impacts. The Just Energy Transition Investment Plan — the JET-IP — outlines a framework for mobilising approximately $98 billion over five years to support the energy transition, with substantial commitments from the European Union, the United States, the United Kingdom, France, and Germany through the Just Energy Transition Partnership announced at COP26 in 2021.

The JET Partnership represents an unprecedented opportunity. The initial commitment of $8.5 billion — now expanded to approximately $98 billion in total investment need identified by the JET-IP — is the largest climate finance commitment to a single country. If deployed effectively, it could fund the decommissioning of coal plants, the construction of renewable energy capacity, the retraining of displaced workers, the economic diversification of coal-dependent regions, and the upgrade of South Africa's electricity transmission infrastructure.

But the word "effectively" carries enormous weight. South Africa's track record of deploying large-scale investment programmes is, as documented in [South Africa's Broken Cycle](/articles/hd-4-2-south-africas-broken-cycle), deeply problematic. The institutional capacity to manage a transition of this complexity — involving multiple government departments, state-owned enterprises, private sector partners, international funders, labour unions, and affected communities — has not been demonstrated. The risk is that just transition funding follows the pattern of other large-scale South African development programmes: announced with fanfare, designed with sophistication, and implemented with incompetence or corruption.

:::expert[The Political Economy of the Just Transition in Mpumalanga]
Research by the Trade and Industrial Policy Strategies think tank, the Centre for Environmental Rights, and scholars including Saliem Fakir at the African Climate Foundation and Jesse Burton at E3G has identified several political economy dynamics that could derail the just transition in South Africa. First, the coal industry lobby — including mining companies, coal transporters, and coal-dependent municipalities — has strong incentives to delay the transition and capture transition funding for coal-extending rather than coal-replacing investments. Second, Eskom's institutional culture remains oriented toward coal, and elements within the utility resist the transition to renewable energy as a threat to their institutional relevance and employment. Third, organised labour — particularly the National Union of Mineworkers — supports the transition in principle but demands guarantees of employment and income security that may not be fiscally feasible in all cases. Fourth, the communities most affected by the transition — coal workers and their families in Mpumalanga — have limited voice in the policy process relative to the institutional actors that claim to represent their interests. The risk is that the just transition becomes a political negotiation among powerful institutional actors, with the actual affected communities — whose development outcomes are at stake — marginalised in the process. Effective just transition governance requires direct community participation, transparent allocation of resources, and independent monitoring of outcomes — precisely the governance capabilities that South Africa struggles to deliver.
:::

## Renewable Energy and Development

The shift from coal to renewable energy is not merely a substitution of one energy source for another. It is a potential restructuring of the development model that could address multiple links in the vicious spiral simultaneously.

South Africa has extraordinary renewable energy potential. Solar irradiation in the Northern Cape exceeds 2,500 kilowatt-hours per square metre per year — among the highest in the world. Wind resources along the Eastern and Western Cape coastlines are sufficient for large-scale wind generation. The combination of abundant solar and wind resources means that South Africa could, in principle, generate all of its electricity from renewable sources at costs that are now lower than new coal generation and increasingly competitive with existing coal generation.

The Renewable Energy Independent Power Producer Procurement Programme — the REIPPPP — demonstrated what is possible. Between 2011 and 2021, the programme attracted over R210 billion in private investment, brought approximately 6,300 megawatts of renewable capacity online, and created an estimated 58,000 full-time equivalent jobs in construction and operations. The average price of electricity from REIPPPP projects in the most recent bidding rounds was approximately R0.50 per kilowatt-hour — less than half the cost of electricity from Eskom's new coal-fired stations at Medupi and Kusile.

The development implications of a renewable energy transition extend beyond electricity supply. First, decentralised renewable energy — rooftop solar, community microgrids, off-grid systems — can provide electricity to communities that are poorly served by the centralised grid, improving energy access without the infrastructure cost of extending transmission lines. Second, renewable energy manufacturing — solar panels, wind turbine components, battery storage systems — represents an industrial opportunity for a country that needs to diversify its manufacturing base and create employment. Third, reduced carbon emissions improve South Africa's access to climate finance, green bonds, and sustainability-linked investment — capital flows that are increasingly conditioned on environmental performance. Fourth, a cleaner energy system reduces the air pollution that causes an estimated 20,000 premature deaths per year in South Africa, with the health benefits concentrated in the low-income communities that live closest to power stations and industrial facilities.

The reinforcing cycle, rebuilt on renewable energy, would look different from the coal-powered cycle of the twentieth century — but the logic would be the same. Clean energy enables development investment. Development investment improves human capabilities. Improved human capabilities support higher-value economic activity. Higher-value economic activity generates revenue. Revenue funds further development and clean energy investment. Each link reinforces the next — but without the carbon debt that would undermine long-term sustainability.

## The Water-Energy-Food Nexus

South Africa is a water-scarce country, and climate change is making it scarcer. Average annual rainfall is approximately 450 millimetres — roughly half the global average. Distribution is highly uneven: the eastern regions receive adequate rainfall while the western regions are semi-arid to arid. Climate projections suggest that rainfall will become more variable, with more intense droughts and more intense flooding episodes — a pattern that disrupts agriculture, stresses water infrastructure, and threatens the water supply of cities.

The water-energy-food nexus describes the interconnection between three systems that are individually critical and collectively interdependent. Energy production requires water — Eskom's coal plants consume approximately 2 per cent of South Africa's total freshwater withdrawals for cooling. Water treatment and distribution require energy — pumping, purifying, and distributing water consumes approximately 2 per cent of South Africa's electricity. Food production requires both water (irrigation consumes approximately 60 per cent of South Africa's water) and energy (farming, processing, and transport are energy-intensive).

This interconnection means that stress in any one system cascades through the others. When Eskom load-sheds, water treatment plants lose power, and water supply is interrupted. When water supply is constrained by drought, agriculture suffers, food prices rise, and food security deteriorates. When energy prices rise due to Eskom's financial crisis, farming costs increase, food prices increase further, and the poorest households — which spend the highest proportion of their income on food and energy — are squeezed hardest.

Climate change intensifies every dimension of the nexus. Higher temperatures increase water evaporation from reservoirs and increase crop water requirements. More variable rainfall makes agricultural planning more difficult and increases the frequency of water supply emergencies. Sea level rise threatens coastal infrastructure, including the desalination plants that several cities are considering as an alternative water source.

For the reinforcing cycle, the water-energy-food nexus represents both a constraint and an opportunity. The constraint is obvious: a development pathway that depletes water resources, depends on coal-fired energy, and is vulnerable to climate shocks is not sustainable. The opportunity is that integrated solutions — renewable energy that does not consume water (solar and wind, unlike coal and nuclear), water-efficient agriculture (drip irrigation, drought-resistant crops), and closed-loop food systems (reduced waste, local production) — can address multiple dimensions of the nexus simultaneously.

## Development Within Planetary Boundaries

Kate Raworth's Doughnut Economics framework provides a visual and conceptual model for the development challenge South Africa faces. The doughnut has two boundaries: a social foundation (the minimum level of human development that every person should achieve) and an ecological ceiling (the maximum level of environmental impact that the planet can sustain). The goal of development is to operate in the space between these boundaries — meeting everyone's basic needs without exceeding the planet's capacity.

Applied to South Africa, the picture is sobering. South Africa falls below the social foundation on multiple dimensions — life expectancy, education quality, employment, income, safety, housing, water and sanitation. Simultaneously, it exceeds the ecological ceiling on carbon emissions, material footprint, and several other environmental dimensions. South Africa is, in Raworth's framework, both socially deficient and ecologically excessive — failing its people while degrading its planet.

The doughnut framework reframes the development question. The question is not "how do we grow the economy?" — growth that pushes South Africa further beyond the ecological ceiling while failing to lift it above the social foundation is not progress. The question is "how do we meet the social foundation while returning to within the ecological ceiling?" This requires a fundamentally different approach to development — one focused on redistribution, efficiency, and qualitative improvement rather than quantitative expansion of material throughput.

For South Africa specifically, the doughnut analysis suggests several priorities. First, decarbonise the energy system — the single intervention that would most significantly reduce South Africa's ecological overshoot. Second, improve the efficiency of health and education spending — getting more development per rand spent, which improves social outcomes without requiring additional material throughput. Third, redistribute income and opportunity — reducing the inequality that keeps millions below the social foundation while the economy as a whole exceeds ecological limits. Fourth, invest in regenerative agriculture and water management — addressing the resource base on which food security and rural livelihoods depend.

:::expert[Applying the Doughnut to South Africa: Research and Practice]
Research applying the Doughnut Economics framework to South Africa — conducted by the Doughnut Economics Action Lab in collaboration with South African researchers, and by environmental economists at the Universities of Cape Town, Stellenbosch, and Pretoria — reveals a country that is one of the worst performers globally on the ratio of social achievement to ecological cost. South Africa breaches multiple ecological boundaries (CO2 emissions, material footprint, ecological footprint) while falling short on multiple social foundations (health, education, income, equality, jobs, social equity). Only a handful of countries share this dubious distinction — they tend to be resource-extracting middle-income nations with high inequality. The research also identifies leverage points — interventions that could simultaneously improve social outcomes and reduce ecological impact. The most significant leverage point is the energy transition: replacing coal-fired electricity with renewable energy improves air quality (health), reduces emissions (ecological ceiling), creates jobs (employment), and reduces energy costs in the long term (income). The second most significant is water management: investing in water infrastructure reduces waterborne disease (health), supports agriculture (food security), and reduces the energy cost of water treatment (emissions). The doughnut framework is not yet widely used in South African policy circles, but its integrated approach — treating social and ecological outcomes as simultaneously binding constraints rather than trade-offs — offers a more useful conceptual model than the conventional growth-first approach.
:::

## The Case for Leapfrogging

South Africa does not have to follow the high-carbon development path. The concept of leapfrogging — skipping intermediate stages of technological development by adopting the most advanced available technologies directly — is not hypothetical. South Africa has already done it in several domains.

Mobile banking is the most cited example. South Africa effectively leapfrogged the branch-banking model — the network of physical bank branches that developed countries built over centuries — by adopting mobile and digital banking at scale. Mobile money and digital payment platforms enable financial transactions for millions of South Africans who never had access to a physical bank branch. The infrastructure cost is a fraction of what a branch network would have required, and the service is often more accessible and convenient.

Solar energy offers a similar leapfrogging opportunity. Rather than extending the centralised coal-fired grid to every household — a model that is expensive, slow, and carbon-intensive — South Africa could accelerate the deployment of distributed solar energy systems. Rooftop solar for households and businesses, community solar installations, and off-grid solar systems for remote communities can provide electricity faster, cheaper, and cleaner than grid extension. The technology costs have fallen by approximately 90 per cent over the past decade, making solar competitive with or cheaper than grid electricity in most applications.

Distance education and digital learning represent another leapfrogging opportunity. South Africa's education quality problem is substantially a teacher quality problem — there are not enough well-trained teachers, particularly in mathematics, science, and languages, to staff every school adequately. Digital learning platforms, while not a substitute for human teachers, can supplement classroom instruction, provide access to high-quality content, and enable teacher professional development at scale. Countries like India (with the DIKSHA platform) and Bangladesh (with access to information programmes) have demonstrated that digital education can improve learning outcomes in low-resource settings.

Telemedicine can extend healthcare access to underserved communities without the capital cost of building new clinics. Electric vehicles can reduce transport emissions and fuel costs. Precision agriculture can improve crop yields while reducing water and chemical inputs. In each case, the most advanced technology is increasingly the most cost-effective technology — the leapfrogging option is not just environmentally preferable but economically rational.

The reinforcing cycle, built on leapfrogging technologies, has the potential to be both faster and more sustainable than the fossil-fuel-powered cycle of the twentieth century. Solar electricity is cheaper than coal electricity. Mobile banking is cheaper than branch banking. Digital education can scale faster than physical school construction. Telemedicine can reach communities faster than clinic construction. Each leapfrogging technology reduces the capital cost and environmental footprint of the development investment, enabling more development per rand spent and per tonne of carbon emitted.

But leapfrogging is not automatic. It requires institutional capacity to identify and deploy appropriate technologies. It requires investment in digital infrastructure — broadband connectivity, which remains inadequate in rural South Africa. It requires regulatory frameworks that enable new technologies rather than protecting incumbent industries. And it requires human capital — the engineers, technicians, and managers who can implement and maintain new systems.

The reinforcing cycle applies here as well. Improved education produces the human capital needed to deploy leapfrogging technologies. Leapfrogging technologies enable more efficient development investment. More efficient development investment produces better outcomes, which justify further investment in the education and infrastructure that support further leapfrogging. Each turn of the cycle makes the next easier — but the first turn requires deliberate investment and institutional commitment.

South Africa stands at a junction. The twentieth-century development path — burn coal, industrialise, get rich, clean up later — is closed. The climate will not permit it, and the economics no longer favour it. The twenty-first-century development path — renewable energy, digital technology, efficient institutions, human capability — is open. It is not easy. It requires the same institutional discipline, sustained commitment, and political courage that the case studies in [When the Cycle Works](/articles/hd-4-1-when-the-cycle-works) described. But it offers something the fossil-fuel path cannot: a reinforcing cycle that strengthens rather than undermines its own foundations, a development pathway that builds capability without borrowing against the future.
