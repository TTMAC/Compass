## The Only Municipal Function That Funds Everything Else

Electricity is different from every other municipal function analysed in this series. It is the only function where the municipality's financial survival depends on the service itself — not just delivering it, but profiting from it. Electricity surpluses represent 25 to 30 per cent of total municipal income on average, and in some rural municipalities the figure reaches 40 per cent. When you pay your electricity bill, a significant portion of what you pay is not funding the electricity network. It is funding libraries, road maintenance, general administration, and councillor salaries. This cross-subsidy model is the structural feature that defines everything about municipal electricity governance — its political economy, its incentive structure, and its trajectory of decline.

## The Electricity Value Chain

To understand how the system works and why it is failing, trace the value chain from policy to plug.

At the top sits the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy, which sets national energy policy — the Integrated Resource Plan that determines South Africa's generation mix, the regulatory framework for licensing, and the strategic direction of the sector. DMRE does not directly touch municipal reticulation, but its decisions on generation capacity, renewable energy procurement, and market structure shape the environment within which municipalities operate.

The National Energy Regulator of South Africa occupies the next layer. NERSA licenses electricity distributors (including municipalities), approves tariff structures, regulates Eskom's bulk pricing, and oversees the emerging competitive electricity market. NERSA's decisions on Eskom's Multi-Year Price Determination directly determine the input cost that municipalities face — the price at which they buy electricity from Eskom before adding their own distribution surcharge. For 2025/26, NERSA approved a 12.74 per cent increase in Eskom's tariff. For 2026/27, following a court-ordered redetermination, NERSA approved 9.01 per cent for municipal customers. These annual increases, compounding over years, steadily squeeze the margin between what municipalities pay Eskom and what they can charge consumers.

Eskom dominates the middle of the chain. Eskom generates roughly 80 per cent of South Africa's electricity, operates the national transmission grid through the newly separated National Transmission Company of South Africa (which commenced trading as an Eskom subsidiary on 1 July 2024), and distributes electricity directly to approximately 6.9 million customers in areas where municipalities do not reticulate. About 42 per cent of Eskom's generated electricity is routed through municipalities for onward distribution. The remainder goes to Eskom's direct customers: industry, mines, residential areas not served by municipalities, and exports.

At the reticulation level, 177 municipalities hold NERSA distribution licences and supply electricity to consumers within their areas. These municipalities buy electricity in bulk from Eskom, distribute it through their own network of substations, transformers, and cables, and bill consumers at a tariff that includes a surcharge above the Eskom bulk price. That surcharge is the cross-subsidy — the revenue margin that municipalities use to fund other services.

The value chain, then, runs: DMRE (policy) → NERSA (regulation) → Eskom (generation and transmission) → Municipality (reticulation and billing) → Consumer. It is a simpler chain than water — fewer handoff points, clearer institutional boundaries. But the structural problems are no less severe. They are merely different in kind.

Where water's core structural problem is conflation (the regulator is also the provider), electricity's core structural problem is resource absence — not the absence of revenue (electricity generates substantial revenue), but the absence of any mechanism that ring-fences that revenue for the function that produces it. The revenue generated by the function is systematically extracted to fund other functions. Where water fails because the municipality cannot regulate itself, electricity fails because the municipality cannot afford to maintain the infrastructure that generates its revenue — because the revenue is siphoned off to pay for everything else. In the language of the [five failure patterns](/articles/ra-1-2-why-institutions-fail), this is an absence pattern: a critical RAARICLE™ element — the ring-fencing of resources to the function — simply does not exist.

:::takeaway
Electricity is the only municipal function where the municipality's financial model depends on profiting from the service. Electricity surpluses fund 25 to 40 per cent of total municipal revenue. This cross-subsidy model creates a perverse incentive: municipalities need electricity revenue to survive, but they extract so much revenue that the electricity infrastructure deteriorates — which eventually destroys the revenue base itself.
:::

## RAARICLE™ Diagnosis

### Responsibility — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

The responsibility assignment for municipal electricity reticulation is clearer than for most functions. Schedule 4 Part B of the Constitution lists electricity reticulation as a local government function. The municipality that holds a NERSA distribution licence is unambiguously responsible for distributing electricity within its licensed area.

The tension arises at the boundary between Eskom and municipal responsibility. In many parts of the country, Eskom distributes electricity directly to consumers — particularly in rural areas, informal settlements, and areas that were historically outside municipal boundaries. The result is that within a single municipal area, some consumers receive electricity from the municipality and others receive it from Eskom. The municipality has no control over Eskom's distribution infrastructure within its boundaries, cannot influence maintenance or investment decisions, and cannot address outages in Eskom-supplied areas. When residents complain about electricity failures, they often complain to the municipality regardless of who actually supplies them — creating an accountability mismatch.

The Eskom unbundling process adds further complexity. In December 2025, the Minister approved Eskom's revised unbundling strategy targeting full implementation by 2030, which includes the creation of a National Electricity Distribution Company of South Africa. The relationship between NEDCSA and municipal distributors remains undefined. Will NEDCSA absorb Eskom's current direct distribution areas and leave municipal distribution untouched? Will it become a competitor to municipalities? Will it become the entity that takes over distribution in failing municipalities? These questions are unresolved, and until they are answered, the responsibility map for electricity distribution in South Africa remains incomplete.

A rating of 3 reflects a function where responsibility is assigned in principle (the NERSA licence is clear) but where the boundary between municipal and Eskom responsibility creates friction, and where the unbundling process introduces uncertainty about the future architecture.

### Accountability — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

The municipal council is the accountability point for electricity reticulation. Councillors approve tariffs, approve the budget that funds electricity operations, and oversee the municipal entity or department that manages the distribution network. When the lights go out, residents hold the council accountable — if the municipality supplies them.

The accountability mechanism works better for electricity than for water, primarily because electricity has a clearer financial trail. Every unit of electricity purchased from Eskom is metered. Every unit sold to a consumer should be metered. The difference between purchase and sale — the distribution losses — is a measurable indicator of network efficiency and governance quality. Councillors who pay attention to their annual reports can see whether losses are increasing or decreasing.

But the cross-subsidy model distorts accountability in a specific way. When electricity revenue subsidises other municipal functions, the council has a financial incentive to maintain or increase the electricity surplus — not to invest in the electricity network. A council that approves a larger capital expenditure budget for electricity infrastructure is reducing the surplus available for other services. A council facing budget pressure will almost always choose to defer electricity infrastructure maintenance rather than cut spending on other visible services. The accountability relationship runs through the budget, and the budget creates an incentive to underfund the function that generates the revenue.

NERSA provides a layer of regulatory accountability through its tariff approval process. In November 2025, the Gauteng High Court ruled that NERSA's previous process for approving municipal tariff hikes was unconstitutional — it lacked adequate public participation and transparent timelines. The court ordered a reformed process: Eskom tariff increases communicated to municipalities by 31 January, municipal tariff applications by 30 March, public participation on each application, and NERSA decisions by 5 May. For 2025/26, all 177 active municipal distributors submitted cost-of-supply studies for the first time — a milestone in tariff transparency. This is regulatory accountability in development, not regulatory accountability that has arrived.

### Authority — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

Apply the 30-day authority test. A municipal manager identifies that the distribution network in a specific suburb is experiencing frequent outages due to ageing transformers. Can the municipal manager fix this within 30 days?

The municipal manager can authorise emergency repairs if the budget allows and if procurement can be completed within the required MFMA procedures. For emergency procurement, some flexibility exists. But replacing ageing transformers is not an emergency — it is a capital investment that requires budget allocation, procurement, and project management. The budget for capital expenditure in electricity must compete with other capital needs, and the council controls the budget allocation. If the council has prioritised other spending, the transformers wait.

The municipal manager cannot change the tariff to fund the investment — tariff changes require NERSA approval through a process that takes months. The municipal manager cannot borrow to fund infrastructure investment without council approval and compliance with the Municipal Finance Management Act's borrowing provisions. The municipal manager cannot procure electricity from an independent power producer to supplement supply during the maintenance period without navigating the regulatory approvals that, even after the Electricity Regulation Amendment Act of 2024, remain complex for most municipalities.

The authority picture is better than for water (rating 2) because the municipality does control its own distribution network, does have budgetary authority over electricity operations, and does have some flexibility in emergency situations. But the 30-day test reveals that the critical decisions — tariff levels, capital budgets, independent power procurement — all require approvals from NERSA, the council, or both, on timelines that exceed 30 days.

The Electricity Regulation Amendment Act, which came into force on 1 January 2025, is the most significant authority expansion in the sector's recent history. The Act explicitly enables municipalities to procure from independent power producers, reduces licensing requirements for small facilities (under 100 kW need only register with NERSA, not obtain a full licence), and provides for the South African Wholesale Electricity Market targeted for launch in April 2026. Cape Town has led the way — procuring 300 MW of renewable energy from IPPs via 20-year power purchase agreements and, in February 2026, issuing a tender to appoint an electricity trader to aggregate supply from multiple producers. But Cape Town is an outlier. Most municipalities lack the legal, financial, and technical capability to navigate independent power procurement. The authority exists in law. The capability to exercise it does not.

:::expert
The Electricity Regulation Amendment Act of 2024 represents a structural shift in South Africa's electricity market architecture. The Act introduces the concept of a Transmission System Operator, enables open wholesale and retail electricity markets, and streamlines licensing for small-scale generation. NERSA awarded NTCSA a Market Operator Licence as a step toward establishing the competitive market. For municipalities, the Act's most significant provision is the explicit enablement of independent power procurement — removing the ministerial determination requirement that previously constrained municipal procurement. However, the Act's transformational potential depends on implementation. The South African Wholesale Electricity Market's draft trading rules include a "revenue neutrality" provision — municipalities can charge only for distribution wire usage, not profit on energy when customers buy from private traders. If implemented as drafted, this could reduce municipal electricity income by up to 60 per cent, fundamentally destroying the cross-subsidy model. The Act creates new authority for municipalities to procure power but simultaneously threatens the revenue model that currently funds municipal operations.
:::

### Resources — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

The resource picture for municipal electricity is defined by a single structural fact: electricity surpluses are not ring-fenced for electricity operations and infrastructure. Every rand of surplus — the difference between what the municipality charges consumers and what it pays Eskom plus its own operating costs — flows into the general municipal account. From there, it funds whatever the council decides it should fund.

Municipal markups above the NERSA-approved bulk tariff range from 20 to 150 per cent. Johannesburg's indigent households pay 49 to 76 per cent more than Eskom-supplied counterparts for the same electricity. Cape Town's 2025/26 tariff analysis found that roughly 10 per cent of the old electricity tariff had been subsidising unrelated services. These are not marginal leakages. They are the financial architecture of municipal government in South Africa.

The consequences for the electricity infrastructure are severe. The maintenance backlog for municipal electricity distribution has reached R32 billion and grows by R2.5 billion annually. Thirty-nine per cent of municipalities allocate 1 per cent or less of their budgets to infrastructure maintenance, against a National Treasury recommendation of 8 per cent. Distribution networks average approximately 40 years old. Some Johannesburg assets are up to 90 years old. City Power, Johannesburg's electricity entity, reported an annual loss of R4.3 billion for the period ending 30 June 2025.

Distribution losses — the electricity that enters the network but is never billed — compound the problem. Well-managed urban municipalities achieve losses as low as 5 per cent. The international benchmark for efficient management is below 10 per cent. But South African municipalities average approximately 18 per cent, and poorly performing municipalities exceed 25 per cent. Johannesburg's losses grew from 11 per cent in 2007 to 18 per cent in 2017. Ekurhuleni went from 8 to 20 per cent. Tshwane went from 8 to 20 per cent. Non-technical losses alone cost Tshwane R1.6 billion in 2023. These are not natural losses from physics — they are the signature of degrading infrastructure, illegal connections, and weak revenue management.

Municipalities collectively owe Eskom more than R110 billion in unpaid bulk purchases — a figure that grew 27 per cent year-on-year and is up from R4.3 billion in 2014. Meanwhile, municipalities collected only 61.5 per cent of billed electricity revenue between July 2023 and March 2024. The financial model is broken at every point: municipalities underspend on maintenance, lose electricity through leaking networks and theft, fail to collect what they bill, and owe their bulk supplier an ever-growing debt.

The resource rating of 2 reflects a system where revenue exists — electricity is a revenue-generating function, unlike most municipal services — but where the cross-subsidy model systematically extracts that revenue for other purposes, leaving the electricity infrastructure chronically underfunded. The function that generates the municipality's income is being starved of the investment it needs to continue generating that income.

:::takeaway
Municipal electricity distribution is caught in a resource death spiral: surpluses are extracted to fund other services, infrastructure maintenance is deferred, networks deteriorate, distribution losses increase, revenue falls, Eskom debt grows, and the surplus available for extraction shrinks. The R32 billion maintenance backlog, R110 billion debt to Eskom, and average 18 per cent distribution losses are symptoms of a model that consumes its own capital base.
:::

### Information — Rating: 3 (Moderate Tension)

The information architecture for municipal electricity is better than for most functions, primarily because electricity is a metered commodity. Bulk purchases from Eskom are metered. Consumer usage is (or should be) metered. The difference — distribution losses — is calculable. Financial data flows through mSCOA. NERSA requires cost-of-supply studies as part of the tariff approval process. The basic data infrastructure exists.

The gaps are in operational information. Many municipalities do not have accurate asset registers for their distribution networks — they cannot tell you the age, condition, or maintenance history of every transformer, substation, and cable segment in their network. Without this data, maintenance cannot be planned; it can only be reactive. Network failures are addressed when they occur rather than prevented through planned replacement cycles.

Real-time monitoring of the distribution network — SCADA systems that track voltage levels, load patterns, fault locations, and network configuration — is available in well-managed municipalities and metros but absent in many smaller municipalities. Without real-time monitoring, fault identification and response are slow, losses are harder to detect and locate, and the municipal manager has no dashboard showing the health of the network.

The information picture is improving. The requirement for all municipalities to submit cost-of-supply studies — achieved for the first time in 2025/26 — forces a level of financial transparency that did not previously exist. NERSA's reformed tariff process, mandated by the Gauteng High Court, requires public participation on each municipality's tariff application, which means the information must be published. But operational information — network condition, maintenance records, loss analysis by area — remains weak in most municipalities.

### Capability — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

Municipal electricity distribution requires qualified electrical engineers to design, maintain, and manage distribution networks. The Engineering Profession Act requires that engineering work be performed or supervised by persons registered with the Engineering Council of South Africa. Municipal electrical engineering work should be overseen by registered Professional Engineers or Professional Engineering Technologists.

In practice, most municipalities do not have properly accredited officials in electrical engineering. Only 141 accredited civil engineers are employed across all municipalities — the figure for electrical engineers is comparable or worse. Rand West Local Municipality has no electrical engineer at all. Lesedi Municipality has two electrical engineers, neither registered with a professional body. Buffalo City Metro — a large urban municipality — had its district engineer for electricity position vacant for 80 months. Six years and eight months without a qualified electrical engineer in a metro that serves hundreds of thousands of electricity consumers.

The capability crisis extends beyond engineering. Municipal electricity departments need financial managers who understand the cost structures of electricity provision, technicians who can maintain substations and transformers, meter readers who can identify tampering, and project managers who can oversee infrastructure investment. The SAICE Infrastructure Report Card 2022 — the most recent available — graded local electricity distribution at D, the lowest category above outright failure.

The coming energy transition makes the capability gap more acute, not less. Municipalities must now manage rooftop solar connections (5.6 GW of distributed solar capacity installed nationally by May 2024, up 143 per cent from 2.3 GW in July 2022), navigate independent power procurement (requiring legal, financial, and technical expertise), adapt to a competitive wholesale market, and manage bidirectional power flows on networks designed for one-way distribution. These are not capabilities that most municipalities possess. Cape Town can appoint an electricity trader and manage a 300 MW IPP procurement programme because it has qualified staff. Most municipalities cannot.

### Legitimacy — Rating: 4 (Functional)

Electricity reticulation is a Schedule 4 Part B function — clearly assigned to local government by the Constitution. The NERSA distribution licence provides a specific legal mandate. The democratic chain from voter to councillor to electricity department is visible. Citizens understand that the municipality is responsible for their electricity supply (where the municipality distributes, not Eskom).

The legitimacy is functional. The question, as with water, is whether the institutional architecture can deliver on a mandate that is constitutionally clear but operationally compromised.

### Enforcement — Rating: 2 (Severe Gap)

NERSA is the regulatory authority for municipal electricity distribution. It issues licences, approves tariffs, and has the power to attach conditions to licences. In theory, a municipality that fails to maintain its distribution network, that accumulates unsustainable losses, or that fails to meet licence conditions could face regulatory consequences up to licence revocation.

In practice, NERSA has never revoked a municipal distribution licence. The political consequences of doing so — leaving a municipality's consumers without a licensed distributor — make this an effectively unusable sanction. NERSA's enforcement is limited to tariff approval (it can refuse to approve a tariff increase, but this punishes the municipality's revenue rather than its performance) and licence conditions (which are monitored but rarely enforced with binding consequences).

The Auditor-General audits municipal electricity as part of the MFMA audit cycle, but the AG's focus is financial compliance, not operational performance. A municipality can receive a clean audit while its distribution network is falling apart — because the audit measures whether the money was spent according to regulations, not whether the infrastructure is maintained.

There are no automatic enforcement triggers for municipal electricity. No threshold of distribution losses that triggers mandatory intervention. No infrastructure condition standard that, when breached, requires a prescribed response. No consequence for a municipality that fails to employ a qualified electrical engineer for 80 months. The enforcement architecture is entirely discretionary — NERSA can act, the AG can report, the provincial government can intervene under Section 139 — but none of these actions are automatic, pre-defined, or binding.

The municipality's debt to Eskom illustrates the enforcement vacuum. Municipalities collectively owe Eskom more than R110 billion. Eskom cannot cut off supply to a municipality without risking the health and safety of residents — an action that would almost certainly be challenged constitutionally. So the debt grows, year after year, with no mechanism that compels payment or triggers consequences for non-payment.

## The Root Cause

The electricity diagnosis differs from water in one critical respect: the core structural problem is not conflation but absence — specifically, the absence of any mechanism that ring-fences electricity revenue for electricity infrastructure. The municipality is not simultaneously regulator and provider in the way that the WSA/WSP model conflates these roles. NERSA regulates. The municipality distributes. The regulatory separation exists — imperfectly, with weak enforcement, but it exists.

The root cause is the interaction of three structural failures.

The first is resource absence. The cross-subsidy model extracts electricity revenue to fund other municipal functions, and no institutional mechanism prevents this extraction. This is not a bug — it is the design. Municipalities were explicitly expected to use electricity surpluses to cross-subsidise services for which there is no direct revenue (like roads, libraries, and parks). The problem is that the cross-subsidy was never calibrated against the infrastructure maintenance requirement. Nobody designed a rule that said "you may extract surplus only after investing 8 per cent of asset value in maintenance." The extraction was unlimited, and the infrastructure paid the price. In the [five failure patterns](/articles/ra-1-2-why-institutions-fail) taxonomy, this is a textbook absence: the Resources element of the RAARICLE™ framework is structurally compromised because the institutional architecture that would protect it — ring-fenced revenue, minimum maintenance thresholds, infrastructure renewal obligations — was never built.

The second is authority constraint. Municipalities cannot set tariffs without NERSA approval. They cannot procure independent power without navigating complex regulatory processes. They cannot restructure their distribution operations without council approval and compliance with multiple legislative frameworks. The municipality is responsible for distribution but does not fully control the key parameters that determine whether distribution succeeds or fails.

The third is the distributed energy transition. Rooftop solar is growing at 18.4 per cent annually. The Electricity Regulation Amendment Act enables a wholesale market where consumers can buy from private traders. The draft trading rules include revenue neutrality provisions that could reduce municipal electricity income by up to 60 per cent. The cross-subsidy model that municipalities depend on is being eroded by technology and regulation simultaneously. A municipality that loses its electricity surplus loses a quarter to a third of its total income — with no replacement revenue source in sight.

These three failures are converging on a crisis. The absence of ring-fenced revenue has degraded the infrastructure. Authority constraints have prevented municipalities from adapting. The energy transition is eliminating the revenue model. The result will be municipalities with broken distribution networks, shrinking revenue, growing Eskom debt, and no capacity to invest in the infrastructure that their consumers and the broader economy depend on.

:::expert
The OECD's 2025 review of South Africa's energy sector recommended that the government consider transferring electricity distribution operations of struggling municipalities to third parties via concession agreements with independent companies. The OECD's analysis identified the same structural problem that the RAARICLE™ framework diagnoses: municipalities lack the capability and the financial incentives to maintain distribution infrastructure, because the cross-subsidy model extracts revenue that should fund maintenance. The concession model would separate the distribution infrastructure (which remains publicly owned) from the distribution operation (which is managed by a professionally run entity under a long-term contract). The concessionaire would invest in infrastructure maintenance and network upgrades in exchange for a regulated return — ensuring that the infrastructure receives the investment it needs while the municipality continues to receive a concession fee that replaces the cross-subsidy. This model is used in multiple countries for utility distribution and addresses the RAARICLE™ resource and capability elements simultaneously.
:::

## The Reform Blueprint

### Ring-Fence Electricity Revenue for Electricity

The first reform mirrors the water blueprint: electricity revenue must be ring-fenced for electricity operations and infrastructure. Every rand collected through electricity tariffs must fund electricity — distribution, maintenance, debt service, and infrastructure investment — before any surplus flows to the general municipal account.

This reform is existentially threatening to municipal finances as currently structured. If electricity surpluses can no longer cross-subsidise other services, municipalities must either find alternative revenue sources, reduce spending on other services, or both. This is why the reform has been resisted for decades — every mayor and municipal manager understands that ring-fencing electricity revenue means cutting spending elsewhere.

But the alternative is worse. The current model is consuming the infrastructure that generates the revenue. Within a decade, many municipal distribution networks will have deteriorated to the point where they cannot reliably distribute electricity. At that point, the cross-subsidy disappears anyway — not because it was reformed but because the asset base that generated it collapsed. Ring-fencing now, while the infrastructure can still be saved, is cheaper than rebuilding after collapse.

The implementation mechanism is the same as for water: a binding National Treasury regulation under the MFMA mandating that electricity trading services revenue is ring-fenced, with a defined minimum percentage (8 per cent of asset value) allocated to infrastructure maintenance before any surplus transfer. The transition should be phased — perhaps over three years — to give municipalities time to adjust their budgets and identify alternative revenue sources.

### Regional Distribution Entities

The second reform addresses the capability crisis. Most of South Africa's 177 municipal electricity distributors are too small to attract and retain qualified electrical engineers, manage complex procurement processes, invest in network modernisation, or navigate the energy transition. The same logic that argues for regional water utilities argues for regional electricity distribution entities.

The Eskom unbundling process creates a natural opportunity. The planned National Electricity Distribution Company of South Africa could become the vehicle for consolidating distribution — not by absorbing all municipal distribution (which would face fierce political resistance) but by offering a professionally managed alternative for municipalities that cannot sustain their own distribution capability. A municipality that cannot employ a qualified electrical engineer for six years should not be operating a distribution network. The distribution function should transfer to a regional entity that has the scale, capability, and financial structure to manage it professionally.

For larger municipalities and metros that have the capability to manage distribution effectively, the reform should allow continued municipal operation — but under stricter regulatory oversight, with ring-fenced revenue, and with automatic enforcement triggers for infrastructure maintenance standards.

### Managed Transition from the Cross-Subsidy Model

The third reform addresses the elephant in the room: if electricity surpluses no longer fund other municipal services, what does? This is not a question that the electricity reform can answer alone — it is a question about the entire municipal revenue model. But the electricity reform must include a transition plan.

The transition should include: a phased reduction of the cross-subsidy over three to five years, allowing municipalities to adjust; an increase in property rates to partially replace electricity surplus revenue (property rates are the municipality's own revenue source and are more sustainable than electricity surcharges); a revision of the equitable share formula to compensate municipalities that lose electricity surplus revenue due to ring-fencing; and support for municipalities to develop alternative revenue sources, including the distribution wire fee that the wholesale market will create.

The coming wholesale electricity market, if designed correctly, creates a new revenue opportunity for municipalities: the distribution network itself becomes a regulated asset, and the municipality charges a regulated wire fee for access. This is the model used in most liberalised electricity markets globally. The municipality does not profit from the sale of energy (which private traders and generators will do) but from providing the distribution infrastructure that enables the sale. The wire fee replaces the cross-subsidy — and because it is a regulated fee based on the cost of maintaining the infrastructure, it creates an incentive to invest in the network rather than extract from it.

### Streamlined Independent Power Procurement

The fourth reform makes the authority expansion of the Electricity Regulation Amendment Act real for all municipalities, not just Cape Town. Most municipalities cannot navigate independent power procurement because they lack the legal, financial, and technical expertise. The reform should include: a standardised procurement framework (template request for proposals, standard power purchase agreement terms, standard grid connection agreements) developed by National Treasury and NERSA; a national procurement support unit that municipalities can access for technical assistance; and pre-approved IPP frameworks that municipalities can call off without repeating the full procurement process.

The goal is to reduce the capability barrier to independent power procurement so that a municipality with a competent municipal manager (even without a specialised energy procurement team) can access renewable energy at competitive prices. This addresses both the authority constraint (simplified regulatory path) and the resource constraint (independent power at prices that may be lower than Eskom's trajectory of annual increases).

### Automatic Enforcement Triggers

The fifth reform replaces discretionary enforcement with automatic triggers, following the same principle established in the [water reform blueprint](/articles/ra-4-1-fixing-water-and-sanitation).

When distribution losses exceed 15 per cent for two consecutive years, the independent regulator must require a loss reduction plan with binding annual targets. If losses exceed 25 per cent, operational management of the distribution network must transfer to the regional distribution entity.

When a municipality fails to employ a qualified electrical engineer for more than 12 months, the regional distribution entity must be appointed to provide engineering oversight at the municipality's cost — funded from the ring-fenced electricity account.

When infrastructure maintenance spending falls below 4 per cent of asset value for two consecutive years, the municipality must submit a remediation plan to NERSA within 60 days. If the plan is not implemented within 180 days, NERSA must attach conditions to the distribution licence requiring minimum maintenance spending.

When municipal debt to Eskom exceeds six months of bulk purchase costs, an automatic payment arrangement must activate — with the municipality's equitable share allocation reduced by the debt service amount and paid directly to Eskom by National Treasury.

These triggers are not punitive. They are protective — they protect the infrastructure from the political dynamics that currently sacrifice it. They protect consumers from a system that degrades their electricity supply to fund other municipal spending. And they protect municipalities from the long-term consequences of short-term budget decisions.

### Anti-Capture Architecture

Political capture in electricity takes a specific form: tariff suppression before elections (keeping prices low to win votes while infrastructure deteriorates) and patronage appointments to electricity departments (placing political loyalists rather than qualified engineers in management positions). The structural separation of distribution into a regional entity or the ring-fencing of revenue addresses the first vector. Protected appointments — requiring ECSA registration for technical positions, with appointment processes verified by the independent regulator — address the second.

Commercial capture — procurement fraud in infrastructure projects — is addressed by mandatory e-procurement, automated price benchmarking against NERSA-published reference costs, and the standardised procurement frameworks proposed above.

Labour capture — resistance to performance management and bloated non-technical headcount — is addressed by the professional management requirement for regional distribution entities and by outcome-based performance contracts for electricity department management.

Regulatory capture — the risk that NERSA becomes too close to the entities it regulates — is mitigated by the reformed tariff process (with mandatory public participation), separate funding for the regulator, and the automatic enforcement triggers that remove regulatory discretion.

Information capture — management filtering distribution loss data or infrastructure condition reports before they reach the council or the regulator — is addressed by automated metering infrastructure that reports distribution data directly to NERSA's monitoring systems without passing through municipal management.

:::framework
**The Municipal Electricity Diagnostic Framework**

Use these five steps to assess how electricity reticulation works in your area and identify the structural problems behind supply failures:

**Step 1: Determine who distributes your electricity.**

Not all South Africans receive electricity from their municipality. In many areas, Eskom distributes directly. Check your electricity bill — if it comes from Eskom, your municipality has no control over local distribution. If it comes from the municipality, your council is responsible for the local network. This distinction determines who to hold accountable for outages and maintenance.

**Step 2: Measure the cross-subsidy leakage.**

Municipalities that distribute electricity typically charge a surcharge above the NERSA-approved tariff. This surplus is meant to fund other municipal services. Request your municipality's annual financial statements and check what percentage of electricity revenue funds electricity infrastructure maintenance versus what percentage is transferred to the general municipal budget. If infrastructure maintenance spending is declining while surcharges increase, the system is eating itself.

**Step 3: Check distribution losses.**

Your municipality's annual report should disclose electricity distribution losses — both technical losses (from ageing infrastructure) and non-technical losses (from illegal connections and meter tampering). Distribution losses above 10% indicate serious infrastructure or governance problems. Losses above 20% signal a system approaching collapse.

**Step 4: Assess the independent power procurement pathway.**

Since the Electricity Regulation Amendment Act of 2024, municipalities can procure power from independent producers. Check whether your municipality has initiated any independent power procurement processes. If not, ask why — the barriers may be regulatory (NERSA approval), capability (no qualified staff to manage procurement), or political (resistance to competition with Eskom).

**Step 5: Evaluate the capability base.**

Check your municipality's organogram for qualified electrical engineers, the age profile of the distribution network, and the capital expenditure budget for electricity infrastructure. A municipality without qualified engineers managing an ageing network with a shrinking capital budget is on a predictable path to distribution failure.

With these five steps, you can diagnose whether your municipality's electricity problems are caused by Eskom bulk supply failures, local network neglect, revenue leakage, or capability collapse — and target your advocacy at the right structural problem.
:::

## Implementation Pathway

### Phase 1: Revenue Protection and Regulatory Reform (Year 1)

Issue a binding National Treasury regulation mandating ring-fenced electricity trading services accounts. Publish a three-year transition schedule for municipalities to reduce cross-subsidy extraction. Require all municipalities to submit infrastructure condition assessments and maintenance plans to NERSA within six months. Enact the automatic enforcement triggers for distribution losses and maintenance spending. Publish standardised independent power procurement templates for municipal use. These actions require no new primary legislation — they can be achieved through MFMA regulations, NERSA licence conditions, and ministerial directives.

### Phase 2: Structural Separation and Capability Building (Years 2-3)

Pilot the regional distribution entity model in two or three areas — one metro with a failing municipal electricity entity (such as Johannesburg's City Power), one district area with multiple small municipalities that lack engineering capability, and one area where Eskom direct distribution overlaps with municipal distribution. The pilot tests whether consolidation achieves economies of scale, whether the concession or utility model attracts qualified professionals, and whether ring-fenced revenue is sufficient to fund infrastructure rehabilitation.

Simultaneously, align the municipal distribution reform with Eskom's unbundling timeline. The creation of NEDCSA provides a natural vehicle for absorbing distribution in areas where municipalities cannot sustain the function. The relationship between NEDCSA, regional distribution entities, and municipal distributors must be defined before 2030 — when full unbundling is targeted.

### Phase 3: Market Integration and New Revenue Model (Years 3-5)

As the South African Wholesale Electricity Market matures, transition municipal distribution revenue from the cross-subsidy model to the regulated wire fee model. Municipalities that maintain their distribution networks receive a regulated return on infrastructure investment — creating an incentive to invest rather than extract. Municipalities that cannot maintain their networks transfer distribution to a regional entity that can.

Fully integrate independent power procurement into the municipal energy mix. Support municipalities with technical assistance to manage distributed energy (rooftop solar connections, bidirectional power flows, battery storage). Ensure that the new market architecture does not leave low-income consumers behind — the transition must include protections for indigent households who currently benefit from the cross-subsidy through free basic electricity allocations.

The end state is a distribution sector with clear responsibility (licensed distributors, whether municipal or regional), professional capability (qualified engineers managing the networks), ring-fenced resources (wire fees fund infrastructure), real-time information (automated monitoring reported to the regulator), and automatic enforcement (licence conditions with pre-defined consequences). As explored in [Infrastructure Foundations](/articles/eg-2-2-infrastructure-foundations), reliable electricity distribution is not just a municipal service — it is the foundation of economic participation. Getting this architecture right is not optional.
