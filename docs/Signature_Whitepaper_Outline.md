## Section 1 — Executive Summary (2 pages)

Structure as four blocks:

1. **The problem** — the most consequential problems governments face are composite: strategic *and* security-adjacent *and* economic *and* human. They are routinely mis-diagnosed because single-domain advisors produce single-domain reports.
2. **The method** — the Integrated Statecraft Audit: a four-pillar diagnostic that treats the state as one system and produces one integrated diagnosis.
3. **The case** — South Africa diagnosed across the four pillars. Headline finding (draft): *SA's composite crisis cannot be solved by any single ministry or any single reform, because the failures in each pillar are now load-bearing for the failures in the others.*
4. **The implication** — governments and funders need integrated diagnoses before they can commission effective integrated programmes. This paper is the template for both.

Include one signature graphic: the four-pillar audit diagram with interdependency arrows.

## Section 2 — Introduction: Why Composite Problems Defeat Siloed Advisors (2–3 pages)

### 2.1 The composite-problem category
Define composite problems: problems that are simultaneously strategic, security-adjacent, economic, and human, where intervention on any one pillar fails unless the others move with it.

Examples (one paragraph each):
- Post-conflict reconstruction (Ukraine, Syria, Ethiopia).
- Middle-income traps with governance decay (Brazil, Argentina, South Africa).
- Fragile-state recovery (Haiti, Lebanon, Yemen).
- Strategic competition under fiscal stress (France, UK, Japan).

### 2.2 The market failure
Why the current advisory market mis-serves composite problems:
- Strategy houses (McKinsey et al.) default to corporate frames; public-interest literacy thin.
- Think tanks produce analysis but rarely execution.
- Boutique specialists go deep on one pillar; cross-pillar integration is somebody else's problem.
- The Big Four have breadth but no partner owns the systemic outcome.

### 2.3 What the paper will do
The paper introduces a method, applies it to one case, and derives implications. It is not a policy prescription — it is a demonstration of how composite problems should be diagnosed before prescriptions are written.

## Section 3 — The Method: The Integrated Statecraft Audit (3–4 pages)

The firm's core IP. Must be precise enough to be credible and generic enough to travel.

### 3.1 Intellectual lineage
One paragraph acknowledging influences: Carnegie Commission's five-volume integrated diagnosis (1932); systems thinking (Meadows); institutional economics (North, Acemoglu & Robinson); human development (Sen, UNDP HDI).

### 3.2 The four pillars
Each described in one paragraph with diagnostic questions:

1. **Grand Strategy** — does the state have a coherent theory of its place in the world, and are its institutions aligned to act on it?
2. **Safety & Security** — does the state hold a monopoly on legitimate violence within its borders, and is that monopoly exercised accountably?
3. **Economic Growth & Development** — does the economy generate broad-based productivity growth, and are the institutions that govern it inclusive?
4. **Human Development** — do citizens have the health, education, and material security to exercise agency?

### 3.3 The interdependency matrix
A 4×4 matrix showing how each pillar reinforces or undermines the others. The method's distinctive move: the diagnosis is not complete until every cell is populated.

Signature graphic: the matrix with live examples.

### 3.4 The audit sequence
Six steps:
1. Pillar-by-pillar diagnosis using standard indicators.
2. Interdependency mapping — how failures in each pillar propagate.
3. Identification of load-bearing failures — the ones whose resolution would unlock the others.
4. Identification of sequencing constraints — what must move first, second, third.
5. Programme-design implications — institutional architecture required to act on the diagnosis.
6. Accountability design — who owns the systemic outcome, on what timeframe, measured how.

### 3.5 What the audit is not
Explicit disclaimers to pre-empt criticism:
- Not a forecast. Not a ranking. Not a scorecard. Not a prescription.
- Not race-neutral where the data are racially distributed — diagnosis follows the evidence.
- Not politically neutral on institutional design — it takes a clear position on what works and what does not.

## Section 4 — Case Application: The Republic of South Africa (12–14 pages)

The bulk of the paper. Each pillar gets roughly equal weight.

### 4.1 Framing
One page. Why South Africa is the archetypal composite-problem case:
- Formally democratic, operationally dysfunctional.
- An HDI of 0.713 that masks two radically divergent development realities.
- A state that spends at OECD levels but delivers at fragile-state levels in many domains.
- Every major pillar in visible decline; none can be fixed without the others moving.

### 4.2 Pillar 1 — Grand Strategy (≈ 3 pages)

**Diagnostic questions.** Does South Africa have a coherent grand strategy? Are its foreign policy, defence posture, and domestic state-capacity architecture aligned to a theory of its place in the world?

**Findings to develop:**
- Foreign policy oscillation between non-alignment rhetoric and de facto alignments that contradict it (BRICS+, ICJ case, Lady R incident, US AGOA risk).
- Hollowing of the Department of International Relations and Cooperation and the SA National Defence Force.
- Absence of a published National Security Strategy that binds foreign policy, defence, and intelligence posture.
- Decline of state capacity: the cadre deployment model as a grand-strategic choice with grand-strategic consequences.

**Signature data:** defence spending as share of GDP (declined from ≈ 4% in 1989 to ≈ 0.7% in 2024); diplomatic posts understaffed and under-resourced; NDP targets for state capacity consistently missed.

### 4.3 Pillar 2 — Safety & Security (≈ 3 pages)

**Diagnostic questions.** Does the state hold a monopoly on legitimate violence? Is policing effective, accountable, and trusted?

**Findings to develop:**
- Homicide rate of ≈ 45 per 100,000 — 6× the global average, comparable to active-conflict zones.
- SAPS clearance rates in single digits for most serious categories.
- Organised crime penetration of critical infrastructure (construction mafia, illegal mining, extortion).
- Private security industry 3× the size of SAPS, signalling market exit from state provision.
- Gender-based violence as a compounding security and development crisis.

**Signature data:** SAPS annual report; ISS homicide data; private security council registrations; femicide rates.

### 4.4 Pillar 3 — Economic Growth & Development (≈ 3 pages)

**Diagnostic questions.** Does the economy generate broad-based productivity growth? Are the institutions that govern it inclusive or extractive?

**Findings to develop:**
- Per-capita GDP growth near zero for more than a decade.
- Expanded unemployment above 40%, youth unemployment above 60%.
- SOE collapse (Eskom, Transnet, Post Office, SAA, Denel) as a productivity drag equivalent to several percentage points of GDP.
- Extractive-institutional features: rent-seeking in procurement, BEE capture by a narrow elite, regulatory uncertainty deterring investment.
- Fiscal path: debt-to-GDP above 73%, debt service now larger than most departmental budgets.

**Signature data:** Stats SA QLFS; Treasury Budget Review; World Bank governance indicators; Eskom energy-availability factor time series.

### 4.5 Pillar 4 — Human Development (≈ 3 pages)

**Diagnostic questions.** Do citizens have the capabilities — health, education, material security — to exercise agency?

**Findings to develop:**
- HDI of 0.713 disaggregates into two realities; IHDI drops to 0.468.
- PIRLS 2021: 81% of Grade 4 learners cannot read for meaning.
- Life expectancy recovering post-HIV but still ≈ 8–10 years below comparable MIC peers.
- 30.4 million below the upper-bound poverty line; 18.2 million below the food poverty line.
- Public health system servicing 84% of population with under half the health spend.

**Signature data:** UNDP HDR; PIRLS; Stats SA poverty series; MRC burden-of-disease.

**Link to prior GovCompass work:** `hd-1-4-the-problem-that-has-no-name` — the unnamed structural deprivation of the black majority as the central undiagnosed problem.

### 4.6 Interdependencies — where the failures compound (≈ 1–2 pages)

The distinctive section. Work the 4×4 interdependency matrix live:

- **Strategy × Security**: without a published national security strategy, SAPS and SANDF optimise against incompatible objectives.
- **Security × Economy**: crime's direct cost to GDP (insurance, private security, foregone investment) ≈ 10% of GDP.
- **Economy × Human Development**: unemployment is the binding constraint on poverty reduction; grants prevent catastrophe but not immiseration.
- **Human Development × Security**: under-educated, unemployed youth as the recruitment base for organised crime.
- **Human Development × Strategy**: a state that cannot educate its citizens cannot staff its diplomatic service, its technical ministries, or its intelligence agencies at the level grand strategy requires.
- **Strategy × Human Development**: cadre deployment compromises the delivery of basic education, health, and housing.

**Finding to surface:** no single pillar's failures can be resolved without progress on the others, and there is currently no institution in South Africa accountable for the system as a whole.

## Section 5 — From Diagnosis to Programme Design (3 pages)

Demonstrates that the method does not stop at diagnosis. Shows what the audit *implies* about programme design without prescribing specific policies (which would require a client engagement).

### 5.1 Load-bearing failures
From the SA diagnosis, identify 3–5 failures whose resolution would unlock others. Draft candidates:
- Restoration of the state's capacity to appoint on merit (cadre deployment reversal).
- A published, binding National Security Strategy that aligns foreign policy, defence, intelligence, and policing.
- Learning outcomes in the bottom two school quintiles as the single highest-leverage development intervention.
- SOE reform with enforceable governance, not restructuring theatre.

### 5.2 Sequencing
Which must move first. The method's sequencing discipline: some interventions are prerequisites for others and must not be attempted in parallel.

### 5.3 Institutional architecture
The method's signature move: no programme succeeds without a named institution owning the systemic outcome. What this would require in the SA case — a Delivery Unit in the Presidency with budget, authority, and accountability for cross-pillar outcomes, modelled on (but more powerful than) the UK Prime Minister's Delivery Unit under Blair.

### 5.4 Accountability design
Targets, timeframes, consequences. The method requires all three; SA's NDP had only the first.

## Section 6 — Implications (2 pages)

Three audiences, one page each (or half a page each):

### 6.1 For governments
Composite problems require composite diagnoses. Any single-domain reform launched without a cross-pillar audit is at risk of being cancelled by developments in other pillars.

### 6.2 For development finance institutions and funders
The programmes most likely to fail are those designed against a single-pillar theory of change. Funders should require cross-pillar diagnoses before committing multi-year programmes.

### 6.3 For the advisory profession
The composite-problem category is under-served. The demand will grow as more states enter conditions resembling SA's — formally functional, operationally composite-failing. The advisory market will need to restructure to meet it.

## Section 7 — Methodological Appendix (2 pages)

Transparency that distinguishes research from opinion.

- Data sources, with date accessed.
- Indicators used for each pillar, with justification.
- Definitions (what counts as a "load-bearing failure," "institutional architecture," "sequencing constraint").
- Known limitations (the audit is a diagnostic tool, not a forecast; quantitative and qualitative findings triangulated but not statistically modelled).
- Caveats on the SA case specifically: data vintage, ongoing developments not yet captured, political sensitivities acknowledged.
