Foreword: Why Reform, Not Just Monitoring
Johannesburg is a city of profound contradictions. It generates nearly 16% of South Africa's GDP, yet hundreds of thousands of its residents endure burst pipes that go unrepaired for weeks, load-shedding compounded by a collapsing local grid, and roads that have become dangerous obstacle courses. The CoJ has one of the country's largest municipal budgets — and among the worst service delivery records.
The first playbook in this series equips communities to monitor, document, and escalate. This playbook goes further. It is for those who believe that documenting failures is necessary but not sufficient — that lasting change requires changing the systems, incentives, policies, and power relationships that produce those failures in the first place.
The Reform Mandate
This playbook is for cross-functional civil society organisations — coalitions, NGOs, trade unions, professional bodies, faith communities, activist networks — who are prepared to:
  •  Analyse root causes, not just symptoms
  •  Build lasting coalitions across sectors and communities
  •  Engage policy processes to change the rules
  •  Apply strategic pressure through multiple channels simultaneously
  •  Sustain campaigns over years, not weeks
The Difference Between Monitoring and Reform Advocacy
Monitoring asks: Did the municipality fix the pothole?
Reform advocacy asks: Why are 40% of potholes still unrepaired after 30 days, and what must change structurally so that this is no longer possible?
Monitoring holds actors accountable for individual failures.
Reform advocacy changes the environment in which those actors operate.
1. Theory of Change
1.1 What We Mean by Reform
Reform in the metro context operates at three levels simultaneously. Durable change requires progress at all three:
Level
What Changes
Examples
Policy & By-law Reform
The written rules that govern how the city operates, procures, plans, and spends
Amended Procurement By-law, revised Tariff Schedule, updated Integrated Development Plan commitments
Institutional Reform
How departments, SOEs, and officials are organised, incentivised, and held accountable
Performance contracts for City Power CEO, independent oversight board for Pikitup, ward-level service SLAs with teeth
Political & Cultural Reform
The norms, relationships, and power dynamics that enable or block change
Transparent Ward Councillor performance scorecards, community veto rights over capital projects, anti-corruption culture shift
1.2 The Reform Hypothesis
The CSRA's working theory of change is:
Theory of Change Statement
IF cross-functional civil society actors build an organised, evidence-based, multi-sector coalition
AND that coalition uses coordinated pressure across legal, political, media, and community channels
AND engages constructively with reform-minded actors inside government
THEN it becomes politically and institutionally impossible for the City to continue operating as it does
AND structural reforms are adopted that improve service delivery outcomes for all residents.
Key assumptions: (a) There are reform-minded actors within CoJ worth engaging. (b) Public pressure matters to elected officials facing elections. (c) Evidence changes what is politically possible over time.

2. Coalition Organisation
2.1 The Civil Society Reform Alliance (CSRA) Model
The CSRA is a coalition of organisations — not a new organisation itself. It operates on a federated model: member organisations retain full autonomy but commit to shared priorities, joint actions, and coordinated messaging within agreed campaigns. This model maximises reach while minimising turf conflicts.
Coalition Founding Principle
The CSRA is stronger than the sum of its parts only if members act with genuine strategic discipline.
This requires: (a) a binding coalition charter, (b) clear decision-making protocols, (c) explicit conflict-of-interest rules,
and (d) the maturity to subordinate organisational interests to shared campaign goals during joint actions.

2.4 Membership Obligations & Charter Commitments
Every member organisation signs the CSRA Coalition Charter, committing to:
Non-partisan engagement — no electoral endorsements under the CSRA banner
Evidence-based advocacy — all public claims are substantiated by documented evidence
Transparency — financial relationships with government, business, or political parties disclosed annually
Conflict-of-interest management — member organisations that are government contractors must recuse from relevant campaign decisions
Shared messaging discipline — public statements on joint campaigns approved by the Communications Council before release
Proportional contribution — each member contributes capacity (time, expertise, resources) proportional to its size
Constructive tone — criticism of the City is substantive and solution-oriented, never personal or defamatory
3. Cross-Functional Skills for Reform Advocacy
3.1 Skills That Set Advocacy Apart From Monitoring
Reform advocacy requires all the skills described in the companion monitoring playbook, plus a distinct set of strategic and influence competencies:
Skill Domain
Why It Matters for Reform
How to Build It
Campaign Strategy
Without strategic discipline, energy dissipates into reactive complaint. Campaigns need a clear theory of change, milestones, and exit conditions.
Dedicated Campaign Leads trained in advocacy strategy (use ActionAid, Oxfam, or Civicus toolkits). Recruit experienced campaigners from NGOs.
Policy Analysis & Drafting
Reform requires engaging the actual text of policies, by-laws, tariff schedules, and budget documents — and producing counter-proposals.
Partner with academic institutions. Recruit retired municipal officials. Commission technical policy reviews.
Strategic Litigation
Courts can compel compliance, establish precedent, and unlock records that change public understanding.
Maintain a panel of public interest attorneys. Partner with OUTA, CER, and Section27. Build a litigation fund.
Investigative Research
Reform advocacy needs the full story — not just symptoms but procurement records, audit trails, beneficial ownership of contractors.
Partner with media (Daily Maverick's Scorpio unit, amaBhungane). Train team in PAIA and open-source investigation.
Coalition Management
Multi-stakeholder coalitions are slow and messy. Managing them without destroying them is a distinct professional skill.
Invest in facilitation training. Use professional neutral facilitators for high-stakes coalition decisions.
Political Economy Analysis
Understanding who benefits from the status quo and why is essential to designing effective reform strategies.
Use Drivers of Change analysis. Map financial flows, procurement networks, political career incentives.
Media & Narrative Strategy
Reform requires shifting public narrative — the dominant story about what is normal, acceptable, and possible.
Embed a Communications Strategist (not just a press officer). Build relationships with senior journalists and editors.
Community Organising
Elite advocacy without a community base is fragile. Sustained pressure requires organised constituencies.
Invest in community organising training. Partner with experienced CBOs. Pay community organisers fairly.
Fundraising & Sustainability
Long reform campaigns require multi-year funding. Financial dependence on one funder is a strategic vulnerability.
Diversify: grants, membership fees, earned income (training, research), individual donors, diaspora funding.

4. Campaign Process & Operating Rhythms
Reform campaigns operate on longer time horizons than monitoring. The CSRA uses a nested process: Annual Strategic Cycle, Quarterly Campaign Cycle, Monthly Operational Cycle, and Weekly Execution Cycle — with each level feeding the next.
4.1 Annual Rhythm — Strategic Planning Cycle
Purpose
Set campaign priorities for the year, assess the political landscape, align the coalition, secure funding, and publish the Annual Reform Agenda.
Period
Activity
January: Landscape Review
Research Unit publishes the annual 'State of Reform' report: what changed last year, what didn't, why. Political Economy Analysis updated. Power map refreshed.
February: Priority-Setting Retreat
2-day retreat with all Steering Committee members and CWG Leads. Review Theory of Change. Select 3–5 priority reform campaigns for the year. Assign resources. Approve Annual Reform Agenda document.
March: IDP & Budget Engagement
All CWGs submit evidence-based inputs to CoJ's IDP public participation process. Legal team reviews draft budget for reform-relevant allocations. Political Liaisons brief sympathetic councillors.
April: Coalition Renewal
Annual member organisation re-commitment. New members onboarded. Coalition Charter reviewed and signed. Conflict-of-interest disclosures collected.
June: Mid-Year Strategic Review
Full Steering Committee reviews campaign progress against annual milestones. Adjust priorities if political context has shifted significantly. Budget reforecast.
September: Forward Planning
Begin planning for following year. Identify major political events (elections, CoJ audit release, IDP cycle) that will shape 2026 strategy. Begin funder engagement for next year.
November: Annual Report Preparation
Compile Annual Impact Report. Document what changed, what the CSRA contributed to it, what didn't work. Publish before year end — radical transparency about failures as well as wins.

Rapid Response Window
The first 24 hours after a major public development (audit release, infrastructure collapse, corruption exposure, Council vote) are the most influential.
The CSRA must be able to:
  •  Publish a substantive public statement within 4 hours
  •  Have a spokesperson available for media within 6 hours
  •  File a formal complaint or PAIA request within 48 hours if warranted
  •  Publish a full analysis briefing within 5 working days
Pre-draft response templates for the most likely scenarios. Assign a designated rapid-response duty officer on rotation.
5. Campaign Design
5.1 Selecting Reform Campaigns
Not every issue warrants a full reform campaign. Use the following selection criteria to prioritise:
Criterion
Question to Ask
Minimum Threshold
Impact Potential
If this reform succeeds, how many people benefit, and how significantly?
Must affect 50,000+ residents or set metro-wide precedent
Winnability
Is there a realistic pathway to achieving the reform within 2–3 years given current political conditions?
At least one plausible pathway through law, politics, or public pressure
Evidence Base
Do we have or can we obtain the evidence to make a credible, substantiated case?
Primary data or verified secondary data exists or can be obtained via PAIA
Coalition Relevance
Can we mobilise meaningful coalition support across at least 3 member categories?
At least 3 different types of member organisations willing to actively contribute
Organisational Capacity
Do we have (or can we acquire) the campaign skills and resources to sustain a 2-3 year campaign?
CWG roles fully staffed and funded for minimum 12 months before launch
5.2 Campaign Architecture
Each approved campaign must be built on a Campaign Architecture Document covering:
Element
Content
Problem Definition
Specific, evidence-based statement of the reform needed: what is the policy/institution/practice that must change?
Root Cause Analysis
Why does this problem persist? Who benefits from it continuing? What has prevented reform so far?
Theory of Change
Specific causal logic: what must change for the reform to happen, and how will the campaign cause that change?
Reform Objective
SMART objective: specific policy/regulatory/institutional change, with measurable indicator of success, 2–3 year horizon
Target Actors
Primary decision-maker who must act (e.g., CoJ Executive Mayor, City Power Board, specific Council Committee)
Pressure Points
Specific vulnerabilities in the target actors that the campaign will leverage (electoral accountability, reputational risk, legal compliance obligation, financial incentive)
Tactic Mix
Which of the four pathways (legal, political, media, inside-outside) will be used, in what sequence, with what resources?
Milestones & Exit Conditions
What does partial success look like? What does full success look like? What conditions would cause us to suspend or abandon?
Risk Register
Key risks to the campaign: legal retaliation, coalition breakdown, co-optation, loss of public interest
Resource Plan
Staff time, legal budget, research budget, community organising budget, media budget

7.3 Policy Brief — Standard Structure
The Policy Brief is the CSRA's primary tool for shifting what decision-makers believe is technically and politically feasible. It must be evidence-based, solution-oriented, and readable in under 10 minutes:
Section
Content (max length)
The Problem (half page)
Specific, evidence-based description of the reform needed. Lead with impact on residents — numbers, specific cases. No jargon.
Why It Persists (half page)
Honest analysis of the political economy: who benefits from the status quo? What has been tried before and why it failed?
The Reform Proposal (1 page)
Specific, actionable change requested. Reference comparable reforms in other cities. Address the most obvious objections.
The Evidence Base (1 page)
Key data points, research citations, PAIA-obtained documents. Every factual claim has a source.
The Case for Action (half page)
What changes if this reform is adopted? Quantify benefits. What is the cost of continued inaction?
Who Must Act (quarter page)
Specific decision-maker(s), their legal powers, and the specific action required of them.
Call to Action (quarter page)
What should the reader do? Be specific: sign, attend, vote, contact, fund.

8. Political Engagement Strategy
8.1 Principles of Non-Partisan Political Engagement
The CSRA engages political actors strategically without becoming partisan. This is not political neutrality — it is political discipline. Reform requires engaging power, and power in CoJ is held by elected officials and political parties.
The Non-Partisan Engagement Test
Before any political engagement, ask three questions:
  1. Is this engagement driven by the reform objective or by political affinity?
  2. Are we willing to apply the same pressure, the same scrutiny, and the same criticism to ALL parties?
  3. Would this engagement compromise our independence in the eyes of the communities we represent?
If any answer is 'no' — do not proceed without Steering Committee sign-off.
8.2 Engagement with the Council
Register all CWGs as regular participants in relevant Council committee public participation processes
Submit written and oral deputations to the Executive Mayor's Committee, Finance Committee, and relevant MMC portfolios on reform issues
Track all Council minutes for reform-relevant motions; respond publicly within 48 hours to significant votes
Maintain relationships with the multi-party caucus chairs, seeking broad support for non-partisan reform motions
Engage the Speaker's office on procedural rights of public participation
8.3 Engagement with CoJ Executive & Administration
Maintain a formal, documented relationship with the City Manager's office and relevant Group Heads
Request quarterly technical briefings from relevant SOE CEOs (City Power, Johannesburg Water, Pikitup)
Participate in any CoJ technical working groups related to reform campaigns, as long as independence is preserved
All engagement with CoJ officials is documented, summarised, and shared with the relevant CWG within 48 hours
Never accept hospitality, gifts, or consulting contracts from CoJ or its SOEs — independence is non-negotiable
8.4 Electoral Engagement
In the period before local government elections, the CSRA escalates accountability activities:
Publish Councillor Reform Scorecard 6 months before elections
Host public ward forums in partnership with WATs: candidates present reform positions
Publish voter education guides: what to demand from your councillor on each reform campaign
Monitor election promises and publish accountability tracker post-election
The CSRA does NOT endorse candidates or parties — it holds all accountable
9. Legal Strategy
9.1 Strategic Litigation Philosophy
The CSRA uses litigation strategically — not as a first resort, but as a lever that no other tool can replace. Courts can compel disclosure, stop harmful processes, establish legal precedent, and impose accountability on officials who believe they are untouchable.
9.2 Legal Toolkit
Legal Tool
Application in Reform Campaigns
PAIA Applications
Primary tool for obtaining procurement records, audit management letters, meeting minutes, and technical reports that CoJ refuses to voluntarily disclose
High Court Review (PAJA)
Challenge irrational or unlawful administrative decisions: procurement awards, tariff increases, development approvals. PAJA (Promotion of Administrative Justice Act) provides the legal basis.
Constitutional Litigation
When fundamental rights are violated at scale: right of access to water (s.27), right to an environment not harmful to health (s.24), right of access to information (s.32)
Public Protector Complaints
For maladministration, improper conduct, or corruption by CoJ officials. Free, accessible, and produces binding remedial action directives.
Information Regulator
When PAIA requests are refused without lawful grounds. The Information Regulator can compel disclosure and impose penalties.
AGSA Referrals
Request the Auditor-General to investigate specific accounting irregularities or material misstatements in CoJ financial statements
National Treasury
Report material irregular, fruitless, and wasteful expenditure for MFMA compliance intervention
SCA / Constitutional Court
Appeal of adverse High Court decisions in cases with constitutional significance or public interest
9.3 PAIA Strategic Programme
PAIA requests are the CSRA's most used legal tool. The Research & Evidence Unit runs a rolling PAIA programme:
Maintain a PAIA Request Register — all requests, responses, timelines, and outcomes tracked centrally
Set quarterly PAIA targets per CWG: what records are needed to advance each campaign?
All PAIA refusals are reviewed by the Legal Sub-committee within 5 working days for appeal potential
PAIA responses that contain significant evidence are published (with legal clearance) within 10 working days
Chronic non-responses are escalated to the Information Regulator and publicised via media partners
PAIA Programme Targets (Annual)
Minimum 50 PAIA requests filed per year across all CWGs
100% of responses reviewed within 5 working days of receipt
80% of refused requests to receive a formal appeal or Information Regulator complaint
All significant PAIA disclosures to be published as public research bulletins
10. Coalition Sustainability
10.1 Financial Sustainability
Reform campaigns fail when funding dries up. The CSRA maintains a diversified funding model:
Funding Stream
Description
Foundation Grants
Multi-year grants from international foundations (Open Society Foundation, Ford Foundation, Heinrich Böll Stiftung). Target 40% of budget but no single funder >30%.
Membership Contributions
Proportional annual contributions from member organisations — amount based on organisation's annual budget. Target 20% of CSRA operating budget.
Research & Advisory
Paid research commissions from media partners, academic institutions, and other CSOs. Not from government or corporations with CoJ interests.
Individual Donations
Small-donor programme via the CSRA public website. Builds citizen ownership and political independence.
Diaspora Fundraising
Targeted fundraising in Johannesburg diaspora communities (UK, Australia, USA). Tax-deductibility via NPO registration.
Government Grants
DSD NPO funding accepted only for service delivery activities, never for advocacy campaigns. No CoJ funding — independence is non-negotiable.
10.2 Coalition Cohesion
Coalitions fracture over strategy, credit, resources, and politics. The CSRA manages cohesion through:
Annual coalition retreat — relationship-building, not just strategy
Clear credit attribution — all publications name contributing organisations
Conflict resolution protocol — neutral third-party facilitated mediation for inter-member disputes, resolved within 30 days
Strategic patience — members with different speeds and risk appetites need explicit space to opt out of specific tactics without leaving the coalition
Win sharing — when reforms are achieved, celebrate collectively and publicly attribute to the coalition, not just the most visible organisation
10.3 Succession & Institutional Memory
All strategy documents, power maps, stakeholder relationship records, and PAIA histories are stored in the institutional repository — not in individuals' heads or personal devices
Minimum 2-person knowledge overlap for all critical functions — no single points of failure
Annual succession planning review: who are the next generation of campaign leads?
After each concluded campaign, a Learning Report is produced and published — documenting what worked, what failed, and why

Appendix C: Key Reform-Relevant Legislation
Legislation
Relevance to Reform Campaigns
Constitution, 1996 (s.24, 27, 32, 33, 195)
Constitutional rights to environment, water, information, just admin action, and principles of public administration. Foundation of all constitutional litigation.
Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000
Governs service delivery, IDPs, community participation, and performance management. Core source of reform obligations.
Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003
Governs financial management, procurement, and accountability. Source of legal basis for procurement reform campaigns.
Promotion of Access to Information Act 2 of 2000
Primary tool for obtaining government records. Every campaign should have a PAIA strategy.
Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000
Enables judicial review of unlawful or irrational administrative action by CoJ and its SOEs.
Public Audit Act 25 of 2004
Governs the AGSA. Enables referrals of material irregularities for investigation and recovery action.
Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act 12 of 2004
Makes certain persons (including directors of SOEs) liable for failing to report corrupt transactions.
Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998
Governs how Council and Ward Committees are constituted and how power is distributed within the metro.
Electricity Regulation Act 4 of 2006
Governs NERSA's oversight of electricity distributors including City Power. Enables regulatory complaints and licence challenges.
Water Services Act 108 of 1997
Governs provision of water and sanitation services. Enables complaints about failures to meet minimum standards.
