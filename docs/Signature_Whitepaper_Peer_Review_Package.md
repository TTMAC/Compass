## 2. Briefing Note for Reviewers

*One page. Sent with the manuscript to accepting reviewers.*

### The paper in 200 words

The paper introduces the Integrated Statecraft Audit, a method for diagnosing composite national problems — problems that are simultaneously strategic, security-adjacent, economic, and human. The method has four pillars, a 4×4 interdependency matrix, and a six-step audit sequence. It applies the method to South Africa, producing a pillar-level diagnosis, a populated interdependency matrix, five load-bearing failure identifications, a three-phase sequencing proposal, and an institutional architecture recommendation. It concludes by deriving implications for governments, development-finance institutions and funders, and the advisory profession. The paper is explicitly a method paper and a case demonstration, not a prescription for South African reform.

### Where the paper sits in the literature

The paper draws on systems thinking (Meadows), institutional economics (North; Acemoglu and Robinson), the capabilities approach (Sen), and the integrated-diagnosis tradition of the Carnegie Commission (1929–1932). It does not claim novelty in its components. It claims novelty in the explicit architecture — specifically, the move from integrated diagnosis to integrated programme design to integrated accountability design, and the sequencing discipline that treats some interventions as prerequisites for others.

### What the paper is meant to accomplish

1. Introduce the audit as Sentinel Quadrant's proprietary method.
2. Demonstrate the method on a globally legible case.
3. Establish the firm as the authoritative voice on composite national problems.

### Limitations the author is aware of

- The audit is a diagnostic tool, not a forecast. Its interdependency claims are evidence-based but not causally estimated.
- Data vintage is a standing limitation; several indicators carry a reporting lag.
- The sequencing recommendations reflect an institutional-economic prior that is defended in the text but not formally tested.
- The paper takes clear positions on institutional design (e.g., that cadre deployment compromises state capacity) because the evidence supports them. Some readers will find these positions politically loaded; the author's view is that evidence-responsive analysis must follow where the evidence leads.

## 3. Review Framework — Questions

*For reviewers who want a structured starting point. Organised from "big picture" down to "fine detail." A reviewer who addresses even three or four of these questions produces a highly useful review.*

### Framing and method (Sections 1–2)

1. Does the **composite-problem framing** hold up under scrutiny, or does it collapse into something else? Is the distinction between composite and single-domain problems analytically real, or rhetorical?
2. Is the **Integrated Statecraft Audit** method well-specified enough that a practitioner could apply it to a different country without the author's guidance? Where are the gaps?
3. What would you change about the **four-pillar structure**? Are there pillars missing, pillars whose boundaries should be redrawn, or pillars that should be consolidated?

### Case application (Section 3)

4. Within your domain of expertise, are the **pillar-level findings** factually accurate and fairly characterised? What has the paper got wrong?
5. Are there **key data points or analytical dimensions the paper has missed** that should be included before publication?
6. Does the **interdependency analysis (§3.6)** ring true to your experience of how pillar failures actually propagate? Which transmission mechanisms are overstated or understated?

### Programme design and implications (Sections 4–5)

7. Is the **load-bearing-failure argument** defensible? Are there other candidates the paper should have considered alongside or instead of the five it identifies?
8. Does the **sequencing logic** (Enabler → Capability → Outcome) hold up against the political economy of South African reform? Is it too tidy?
9. Are the **implications** for governments, DFIs/funders, and the advisory profession overstated, understated, or well-calibrated?

### Tone and positioning

10. Does the paper read as **non-partisan** to a reader outside the firm's ideological priors? Where does it tilt?
11. Does it avoid the standard **consulting-document failure modes** — jargon, motivational close, hedged claims, unsupported optimism?
12. Are there passages where the firm's **commercial interest** in diagnosing a market opportunity is allowed to distort the analysis of that opportunity's scale?

### Practical (optional)

13. Is there a published dataset, paper, or authoritative source we have not cited that we should?
14. Is there an institutional reader or outlet you would recommend for the launch push?

## 5. Candidate-Reviewer Shortlist

*Five reviewers across the four pillars plus one cross-cutting / method reviewer. The profiles are categorical; specific names are suggestions for the founder to refine based on existing relationships and independence.*

### Pillar 1 — Grand Strategy
**Profile.** Senior foreign-policy or defence analyst with published work on South African strategic posture. Capable of assessing whether the paper's characterisation of SA grand strategy (defence, diplomacy, strategic coherence, cadre deployment) is fair.
**Candidate profiles to consider.** Senior staff at the South African Institute of International Affairs (SAIIA); senior researchers at ISS Africa's peace and security programme; a former DIRCO Director-General or Ambassador; retired senior SANDF officer with public track record; authors of the 2014 Defence Review.

### Pillar 2 — Safety & Security
**Profile.** Criminologist, policing scholar, or security practitioner with deep data literacy on SA crime statistics. Must be able to assess the detection-rate argument and the organised-crime penetration claim.
**Candidate profiles to consider.** ISS Africa crime and justice team (e.g., Gareth Newham, Lizette Lancaster); academic criminologists at UCT (Anine Kriegler) or Wits; senior retired SAPS investigator; NPA research unit.

### Pillar 3 — Economic Growth & Development
**Profile.** Development economist with strong SA track record; comfortable with fiscal, labour-market, and productivity arguments; ideally with experience inside Treasury, SARB, or a major research institute.
**Candidate profiles to consider.** Senior researchers at Economic Research Southern Africa (ERSA); Bureau for Economic Research (Stellenbosch); Intellidex; a former Treasury DDG; senior staff at SARB's research unit; faculty at GSDPP (UCT) or WITS School of Governance.

### Pillar 4 — Human Development
**Profile.** Education or social-policy specialist able to assess the PIRLS analysis, the poverty characterisation, and the IHDI argument. Must be comfortable with the language/literacy disaggregation.
**Candidate profiles to consider.** Nic Spaull (RESEP / Stellenbosch); Servaas van der Berg (Stellenbosch); Martin Gustafsson (DBE / Stellenbosch); senior World Bank human-development economist working on SA; HSRC education researcher.

### Method / Cross-cutting
**Profile.** Someone whose professional work is integrated-strategy framework assessment — a DFI portfolio manager, a multilateral programme designer, a senior public-sector strategy partner, or an academic in public administration with systems-thinking credentials.
**Candidate profiles to consider.** Senior staff at the Mo Ibrahim Foundation or the Hewlett Foundation; African Development Bank strategy unit; World Bank SA country team lead; senior McKinsey or Oliver Wyman public-sector partner; Harvard Kennedy School faculty with building-state-capability background (Andrews, Pritchett lineage).

### Selection criteria (apply to shortlist)

1. **Direct domain expertise** matching the pillar or method.
2. **Public profile** sufficient that named acknowledgement (if consented) adds credibility.
3. **Independence** — no prior commercial relationship with Sentinel Quadrant or the founder.
4. **Track record of substantive peer review** — avoid reviewers who accept and disappear.
5. **Willingness to disagree** — avoid reviewers whose institutional position requires them to endorse the paper's conclusions.

### Anti-criteria (reasons to decline a reviewer)

- A known advocate for any political party cited in the paper.
- Anyone whose employment depends on the government bodies the paper diagnoses critically.
- Anyone whose work the paper substantially builds on without citing — invite only after adding the citation.
