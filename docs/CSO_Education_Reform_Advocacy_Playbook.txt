0  PHILOSOPHY AND CONTEXT
Civil society occupies a fundamentally different position from government in education reform. Where government has authority, civil society has legitimacy. Where government has budget, civil society has trust. Where government moves slowly, civil society can move fast. This playbook is about using those advantages deliberately and in coalition.
Core Belief  Government cannot fix a broken education system alone — not because officials lack ability, but because fixing it requires pressure from the outside, voice from communities, evidence from independent researchers, and legal accountability through courts and commissions. Civil society provides all four.
0.1  The Concurrent Function Problem
South Africa's Constitution places Basic Education in Schedule 4 as a concurrent national and provincial function. The Department of Basic Education (DBE) writes policy; nine Provincial Education Departments (PEDs) deliver it. This split creates a gap — the space between policy intent and classroom reality — that government has structural difficulty seeing. Civil society lives in that gap.

1  COALITION ORGANISATION
A coalition is not a committee. A committee reports to a hierarchy; a coalition operates on mutual interest and trust. The structures here are light enough to move fast and strong enough to take difficult decisions.

1.3  Coalition Agreements and Accountability
⚠ CAUTION  Coalitions collapse when trust breaks. Trust breaks when members free-ride, take credit, or act outside agreed mandates. The agreements below are non-negotiable.
Membership Agreement (signed at joining)
Organisations join in good faith; they may disagree on tactics but not on the coalition's core reform objectives.
No member may enter into formal engagement with the target department on behalf of the coalition without CCT approval.
Research and evidence produced within the coalition is jointly owned; attribution must credit the coalition, not just the producing organisation.
Members declare conflicts of interest (e.g., receiving government funding that creates dependence on the target institution).
Withdrawal from a campaign requires 30 days written notice; departing members do not take campaign materials or contacts.

2.2  Avoiding Skills Silos
Assign each CWG a skills mentor — an experienced advocate who has run similar campaigns and can coach the team on blind spots.
Run a skills audit at campaign inception: use a simple heat map (Green = strong, Amber = developing, Red = gap) and source the red cells before launch.
Cross-train deliberately: researchers should attend community hearings; community organisers should read budget documents; lawyers should do media interviews.
💡 TIP  The most dangerous gap in most civil society campaigns is financial analysis. Rights organisations can argue constitutional violations; few can prove the money was available and deliberately not spent. Embed a budget analyst in every CWG.
2.3  Managing Expert Voice vs Community Voice
Principle  The expert's job is to make the community's experience legible to power — not to replace community voice with expert voice. A lawyer's affidavit is more powerful when it is grounded in a parent's testimony. A researcher's data is more compelling when it can be narrated by a Grade 4 learner who can't read.
Every major submission or campaign document must include direct testimony from affected learners, parents, or teachers — not just research findings.
Community members must be briefed on and consent to how their stories will be used before they appear in any public communication.
When government invites 'expert inputs', invite a community representative to give the opening testimony before the researcher presents data.

4.2  Digital Security Principles
⚠ CAUTION  Civil society organisations working on politically sensitive campaigns face real digital security risks. This is not paranoia — it is professional practice.
Never discuss litigation strategy, confidential sources, or pre-publication evidence on WhatsApp or standard email. Use Signal or encrypted email.
All coalition members complete a basic digital security induction (EFF's Surveillance Self-Defence guide, freely available online).
Campaign devices (especially those used in field monitoring) should have remote-wipe capability enabled.
PAIA requests and court papers are public documents once filed — but legal strategy documents must be attorney-client privileged and stored accordingly.
Field monitoring data containing learner or community member personal information is subject to POPIA. Obtain informed consent before collecting.

5.2  The Accountability Scorecard
Why This Matters  A published scorecard transforms abstract advocacy into a specific, recurring accountability moment. Government knows it is coming. Media knows when to ask questions. Communities know how to judge progress. It is the coalition's most powerful recurring artifact.
Scorecard Design Principles
Use government's own commitments as the measure — Annual Performance Plan targets, Budget Vote speeches, court orders, signed agreements. Do not invent standards they can dispute.
RAG rating: Green = commitment met with evidence; Amber = partial delivery or delayed; Red = no delivery or deterioration.
Publish it before the government publishes its own APR. Do not let them set the narrative first.
Make it visual. A one-page graphic that can be shared on social media is more powerful than a 40-page technical report.
Include a trend line. Is the system improving, stagnant, or declining? Year-on-year comparison is the most powerful accountability tool.

5.3  The Field Monitoring Report
Field monitoring is the foundation of civil society's evidentiary advantage. Government reports on what it plans to deliver; civil society reports on what actually exists at school level.
Minimum Field Monitoring Protocol
Advance notice: Monitor schools in a defined circuit or district. Notify the DBE/PED 48 hours in advance — this prevents 'we didn't know you were coming' as a defence, and tests whether advance notice alone causes improvement.
Standardised checklist: Every monitor uses the same KoboToolbox form covering: textbook availability per subject and grade, teacher presence vs timetable, functional toilets, water access, library or reading corner, LTSM display and use.
Photographic evidence: Photograph (with consent where learners are present) empty classrooms, missing books, broken infrastructure. Metadata timestamps the evidence.
Learner and parent testimony: Short verbal testimony (recorded with consent) from a parent or learner on the day. Ask: 'What is the biggest problem at this school right now?'
Verification: Within 5 working days, send findings to the school principal and the circuit manager for factual correction. Their response (or silence) is part of the record.
Publication: Field monitoring reports go public only after the verification step. Include the government's response — it demonstrates the coalition acted in good faith.
6  CIVIL SOCIETY CAMPAIGN CASE STUDIES

6.1  Campaign A — Mother-Tongue Literacy in Foundation Phase
Problem Statement
PIRLS 2021 found that 81% of South African Grade 4 learners cannot read for meaning. The crisis is concentrated in schools using African languages as medium of instruction in Grades 1–3 but switching to English by Grade 4 — before learners have achieved literacy in any language.
Coalition Composition
Campaign Lead: PRAESA (Project for the Study of Alternative Education in South Africa)
Research: Stellenbosch ReSEP, University of the Western Cape Linguistics
Community Voice: Pan South African Language Board community networks; rural Eastern Cape CBO
Legal: SECTION27 (Section 29 language rights; DBE Home Language Policy compliance)
Parliamentary: Children's Rights Committee monitoring; Portfolio Committee submissions
Media: Ground Up education reporters; SABC educational broadcasting
Campaign Lifecycle Position
Phases 3–5 (Demand through Negotiation). Demand letters sent; DBE has acknowledged the problem in writing. Coalition is in negotiation over a revised Home Language Policy implementation plan with binding timelines.
Key Artifacts in Play
Evidence Pack: PIRLS data reanalysed by home language cohort, province, and quintile
Demand letter to DBE citing Section 29(2) language rights obligations
Parliamentary submission to the Basic Education Portfolio Committee
Field Monitoring Reports from 40 Foundation Phase schools across 5 provinces
Community testimony: 12 recorded parent testimonies from Limpopo and Eastern Cape
6.2  Campaign B — School Safety and Gender-Based Violence
Problem Statement
School-related gender-based violence (SRGBV) affects an estimated 1 in 3 learners, with girls and LGBTQ+ learners disproportionately affected. The DBE National School Safety Framework exists but is largely unimplemented at school level. Most schools have no functioning GBV reporting mechanism.
Coalition Composition
Campaign Lead: People Opposing Women Abuse (POWA) + Shukumisa Coalition
Research: Centre for the Study of Violence and Reconciliation (CSVR)
Community: Grass-roots women's organisations in Gauteng, KZN, and Limpopo
Legal: Women's Legal Centre (Labour Court precedents; educator misconduct regulations)
Digital: VAWNet SA (online reporting tool for learners)
Media: Bhekisisa health journalism centre; Spotlight (Health Justice Initiative)
Specific Campaign Tactics
Shadow implementation review: field-tested DBE's own School Safety Framework against 60 schools — found 7% had a functional safety committee.
Freedom of Information campaign: PAIA requests to all 9 PEDs for sexual misconduct disciplinary records for the past 5 years. Refusals treated as suppression of evidence.

7  COALITION HEALTH AND SUSTAINABILITY
Coalitions die in four ways: burnout, funding dependency, internal conflict, and winning without knowing it. This section addresses all four.
7.1  Preventing Burnout
Reality Check  Education reform advocacy is emotionally heavy work. You are confronted daily with evidence of children failing, rights violated, and promises broken. Without deliberate care, your most committed activists will leave within 18 months.
Weekly check-ins include an energy check — not a performance metric, a human moment.
No campaign deliverable takes priority over a team member's mental health crisis. Full stop.
Celebrate wins deliberately: even small wins — a parliamentary question asked, a document released under PAIA, a school that now has books — are celebrated in the group.
Rotation of difficult roles: field monitoring in damaged communities, direct survivor testimony sessions, and adversarial negotiations should be rotated, not left to the same person.
Annual coalition retreat (1–2 days): separate from planning. Reflect, reconnect, rest.
7.2  Managing Funding Dependency
Never allow more than 40% of coalition operating budget to come from a single funder.
Restricted funding (for specific campaigns) must not drive campaign priority. Campaigns are driven by reform needs; funders are invited to support them.
Maintain an unrestricted operating reserve equivalent to 6 months of core team costs.
Develop earned revenue where possible: training programmes, research contracts, consulting to sister organisations.
💡 TIP  Funders follow credibility. The best fundraising strategy is doing excellent, visible campaign work. A coalition that publishes a rigorously evidenced Accountability Scorecard every quarter will attract funders — not the other way around.
7.3  Managing Internal Conflict
Coalition conflict is normal. The question is whether it is productive (disagreement on tactics that sharpens strategy) or destructive (personality clashes, organisational competition, values misalignment).
Tactical disagreements go to the CCT for facilitated resolution within one week. Unresolved tactical disputes are escalated to the CSG.
Values misalignment (e.g., a member taking positions inconsistent with the coalition's mandate) triggers a mediated conversation and, if unresolved, a CSG decision on membership.
Organisational competition (e.g., two members competing for the same grant) is disclosed and quarantined from campaign decision-making.
No coalition communication or artifact is used for individual organisational fundraising without explicit CCT approval.
7.4  Knowing When You've Won
The Trap  Coalitions that win but do not know it keep campaigning in a mode of urgency that alienates government partners and burns out members. Define your win condition in the Campaign Charter and honour it when it is achieved.
A win is: a legal remedy granted, a policy changed, a budget line restored, a commitment made and verifiably delivered, or a measurable improvement in learner outcomes attributable to the campaign.
When a CWG achieves its win condition, hold a public celebration AND an internal debrief. Document what worked. Archive the campaign.
Distinguish between: winning the battle (government acts), winning the campaign (commitment is delivered), and winning the war (systemic change embedded in law, budget, and practice). Most civil society campaigns win battles and campaigns. Wars require decades.
